## Case 3493: DuPont Buffalo, Hot Work on Vinyl Fluoride Tank

**Incident date:** November 9, 2010
**Location:** DuPont Yerkes Plant, Buffalo, New York
**Report:** CSB Case Study 2011-01-I-NY (published 2011)
**Hazard type:** Flammable vapor ignition (hot work)
**Outcome:** 1 killed (contract welder), 1 injured (foreman)

### What happened

A contract welder began cutting on an out-of-service vinyl fluoride (VF) slurry tank. VF vapors had migrated into the tank through two pathways: a cracked mechanical seal on a flash tank overflow line loop, and an unblinded equalizer line connecting the out-of-service tank to an adjacent in-service unit. The cutting arc ignited the vapor. The welder died; the foreman standing outside was knocked down and injured.

### Root causes and findings

The hot work permit was reviewed and signed by a DuPont service department employee who had no knowledge of the vinyl fluoride system or the connected piping. Neither the permit issuer nor the welder tested the atmosphere inside the tank before work began. The process hazard analysis (PHA) for the VF system had not identified the cracked seal loop as a potential ignition pathway. No management of change (MOC) review was conducted when the equalizer line was left unblinded during the shutdown. DuPont's contractor safety program did not require Mollenberg-Betz (the welding contractor) to conduct its own hazard review before beginning hot work. OSHA cited both DuPont and Mollenberg-Betz.

### Key lessons

Hot work permits must be issued by people with direct process knowledge of every connected system, not administrative designees. Isolation must be verified by physical blinding, not valve position alone. Atmosphere testing inside confined or enclosed spaces is mandatory before ignition sources are introduced. PHAs must trace all interconnected piping, including normally inactive loops and equalizer lines.

### Recommendations

Four recommendations to DuPont: revise hot work permit procedures to require issuer competency in the process, require pre-job atmosphere testing inside all tanks, require blinding rather than valve isolation for hot work, and update PHA to address interconnected piping pathways.

## Case 3495: Oil and Gas Storage Tank Safety Study

**Incident dates:** 1983 to 2010 (26 incidents reviewed; 3 in depth)
**Location:** Multiple states; focused on Carnes MS, Weleetka OK, and New London TX
**Report:** CSB Investigative Study 2011-H-1 (September 2011)
**Hazard type:** Flammable vapor exposure, public access hazard
**Outcome:** 44 fatalities, 25 injured across the 26-incident dataset; all victims were members of the public

### What happened

Three incidents anchor the study. At Delphi Oil in Carnes, Mississippi (2009), a 16-year-old and an adult were killed when they climbed onto an open-hatch production tank and were overcome by hydrocarbon vapors. At Three MG Family in Weleetka, Oklahoma (2009), three children died after entering an unguarded tank battery area. At MC Production in New London, Texas (2010), two boys were killed at an open storage tank near a residential neighborhood. In each case, tanks were accessible to the public, hatches were open, no warning signs were posted, and no perimeter fencing existed.

### Root causes and findings

State regulations in Mississippi, Oklahoma, and Texas either did not require perimeter fencing, or required fencing only above a minimum tank size threshold that excluded most field tanks. API standards at the time did not mandate warning signage visible to the public. No federal regulation governed public access to oil and gas production facilities. Operators generally treated tank battery fencing and signage as optional.

### Key lessons

Inherently safer tank design can reduce vapor accumulation at open hatches. Regulatory frameworks that rely on operator discretion for perimeter security routinely fail to protect the public, especially in rural areas where tanks are near homes and play areas. Warning signs, locked hatches, and fence heights above climbable thresholds are low-cost controls that operators had not adopted absent regulatory mandate.

### Recommendations

CSB called on EPA and state regulators in MS, OK, and TX to require perimeter fencing, locked access hatches, and standardized warning signs at all oil and gas storage facilities, regardless of tank size. API was urged to update its standards to include these requirements.

## Case 3496: Donaldson Enterprises, Fireworks Disposal Explosion

**Incident date:** April 8, 2011
**Location:** Waikele Self Storage, Waipahu, Hawaii
**Report:** CSB Report 2011-06-I-HI (January 2013)
**Hazard type:** Explosive detonation (commercial fireworks)
**Outcome:** 5 killed (DEI employees), 1 minor injury (supervisor outside building)

### What happened

Donaldson Enterprises Inc. (DEI) had contracted with VSE Corporation to dispose of three shipments of seized contraband commercial display fireworks on behalf of the U.S. Treasury's Equitable Sharing Program. DEI modified its disposal process twice during the job. The approved method was water-immersion dissolution of fireworks as intact units. DEI first shifted to manual disassembly to speed the work, then began accumulating loose explosive components, including black powder and aerial shells, in cardboard boxes stacked inside a storage magazine. The accumulated mass detonated and destroyed the building, killing everyone inside.

### Root causes and findings

DEI had no prior experience disposing of commercial display fireworks. VSE awarded the subcontract to DEI without conducting a safety review of DEI's capabilities or proposed methods. Neither VSE nor the government oversight agencies confirmed that DEI's modified disposal procedures were reviewed or approved. No management of change process existed to evaluate the shift from water immersion to manual disassembly and bulk accumulation. Eleven findings covered subcontract oversight failures, lack of competency verification, absence of regulatory guidance for this type of disposal, and the progressive escalation of risk as the process changed on-site without review.

### Key lessons

Government-contracted disposal of hazardous materials requires competency verification of all subcontractors before work begins, not after. Any change to an approved energetic material disposal method must be reviewed by qualified personnel and cannot be left to field-level improvisation. Regulatory frameworks governing this type of disposal work had gaps that allowed an unqualified contractor to take on increasingly dangerous operations without triggering oversight.

### Recommendations

Recommendations to the FAR Council, Treasury Office of Procurement and Property Enforcement, TEOAF, VSE, NFPA, EPA, and ATF. Core recommendations: require safety competency verification for energetic material disposal subcontracts, establish regulatory standards for surplus/seized fireworks disposal, and mandate MOC processes for any deviation from approved disposal methods.

## Case 3497: Carbide Industries, Electric Arc Furnace Explosion

**Incident date:** March 21, 2011
**Location:** Carbide Industries LLC, Louisville, Kentucky (Rubbertown district)
**Report:** CSB Case Study 2011-05-I-KY (February 2013)
**Hazard type:** Overpressure, mechanical failure (electric arc furnace)
**Outcome:** 2 killed (workers in control room), 2 injured

### What happened

An electric arc furnace (EAF) used to produce calcium carbide overpressured and exploded at approximately 5:40 p.m. Debris penetrated the double-pane reinforced glass window of the control room located only 12 feet from the furnace. The two workers inside were killed. Two others outside were injured. In the four months before the incident, 26 work orders had been written for water leaks from the furnace cover, a known pathway for water-gas reactions that generate pressure.

### Root causes and findings

The CSB identified normalization of deviance as the central factor. Furnace "blows," explosive pressure releases from water intrusion, had occurred repeatedly and were treated by plant personnel as a normal operating condition rather than as precursors requiring corrective action. None of the prior events was formally investigated. No corrective action had been taken on the furnace cover water leak work orders before the fatal event. The facility operated below PSM thresholds and was not subject to OSHA 1910.119. NFPA 86, the applicable standard for ovens and furnaces, contained no specific requirements for Class A electric arc furnaces of this type, leaving a regulatory gap. The control room's proximity to the furnace amplified the consequences.

### Key lessons

Recurring abnormal events that are "managed around" without investigation are warning signs of impending catastrophic failure. Mechanical integrity programs must track and resolve open work orders; accumulation of unresolved items on safety-critical equipment is itself a leading indicator. Facility siting must account for potential blast effects from nearby high-energy equipment, even below PSM applicability thresholds.

### Recommendations

R1: NFPA, develop a standard specifically addressing EAF design and operation. R2 and R3: Carbide Industries, implement a mechanical integrity program for the furnace and modify facility design to increase the distance or protection between the furnace and occupied control areas.

## Case 3498: AL Solutions, Titanium/Zirconium Dust Explosion

**Incident date:** December 9, 2010
**Location:** AL Solutions, New Cumberland, West Virginia
**Report:** CSB Case Study 2011-3-I-WV (July 2014)
**Hazard type:** Combustible metal dust explosion
**Outcome:** 3 killed (James Eugene Fish, Jeffrey Scott Fish, Steven Swain), 1 contractor injured

### What happened

Workers in a blending and pressing room were processing wet-milled titanium and zirconium scrap when a loud noise was heard, followed by a dust explosion and fire. The three employees in the room were killed. The process involved milling scrap metals wet, blending the resulting powder, pressing it into compacts, and drying the compacts in an oven. Titanium and zirconium dusts are pyrophoric and highly combustible when dry, and the accumulation of fine metal dust in the work area created an explosive atmosphere.

### Root causes and findings

Workers had not received training on the combustible and pyrophoric hazards of titanium and zirconium dusts. Management had not recognized or communicated these hazards to employees or contractors. OSHA's West Virginia area office had not conducted an inspection under its National Emphasis Program (NEP) for combustible dust at this facility despite prior incidents. At the time of the incident, no specific OSHA standard for combustible metal dusts existed, leaving enforcement reliant on the general duty clause.

### Key lessons

Combustible metal dust (NFPA 484 scope) requires the same layer of protection analysis as grain, coal, or wood dust. Wet milling does not eliminate the hazard when downstream dry blending and pressing steps reintroduce fine dry particles. Regulatory gaps for combustible metals (distinct from combustible dust generally) allowed known hazards to persist without standard-driven corrective action. OSHA's NEP must be implemented proactively at facilities handling combustible metals.

### Recommendations

Reiterated 2006-1-H-1 (prior CSB recommendation to OSHA to issue a combustible dust standard). New recommendations R1 to R4 to AL Solutions covering hazard recognition training, written safe work procedures for combustible metal processes, and implementation of dust control and housekeeping programs.

## Case 3499: Hoeganaes Corporation, Three Iron Dust Incidents

**Incident dates:** January 31, March 29, and May 27, 2011
**Location:** Hoeganaes Corporation, Gallatin, Tennessee
**Report:** CSB Case Study 2011-4-I-TN (December 2011)
**Hazard type:** Combustible metal dust flash fires, hydrogen explosion
**Outcome:** 5 killed, 3 injured across three incidents

### What happened

Three separate incidents occurred at this iron powder manufacturing plant within five months. On January 31, workers restarted a bucket elevator after a jam; the restart dispersed accumulated iron dust, which ignited in a flash fire. Two workers died. On March 29, an igniter was being replaced on a band furnace; iron dust lofted during the maintenance and ignited. One worker was injured. On May 27, a corroded hydrogen supply pipe in a below-grade trench developed a 3 by 7 inch hole. A forklift chain used to remove a trench cover sparked, igniting hydrogen. The hydrogen explosion lofted iron dust accumulated on elevated beams and rafters, producing secondary flash fires throughout the building. Three workers were killed and two were injured.

### Root causes and findings

Hoeganaes had commissioned combustibility testing of its iron dust in 2009 and again in 2010. Both tests confirmed the dust was combustible. No corrective action was taken to control dust accumulation or to modify ignition-source controls. The hydrogen piping inspection program had not identified the corroded pipe segment before it failed. Dust housekeeping on elevated structural members was absent, leaving large quantities of deposited dust available as secondary fuel. Nine findings covered dust hazard awareness, housekeeping programs, hydrogen system integrity, and regulatory gaps.

### Key lessons

Combustibility test results must trigger action. Knowing a material is combustible and taking no mitigation is worse than not knowing, because knowledge creates legal and moral accountability. Secondary dust explosions from overhead accumulations are often more destructive than primary events. Hydrogen piping in buried or enclosed trenches requires periodic integrity inspection.

### Recommendations

R1 to R9 to OSHA, the International Code Council (ICC), TOSHA (Tennessee), and Hoeganaes. Core recommendations: OSHA to issue a combustible dust standard, Hoeganaes to implement comprehensive dust housekeeping and hydrogen system inspection programs.

## Case 3501: Bayer CropScience, Methomyl Runaway Reaction

**Incident date:** August 28, 2008
**Location:** Bayer CropScience LP, Institute, West Virginia
**Report:** CSB Report 2008-08-I-WV (January 2011)
**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway, toxic release potential
**Outcome:** 2 killed (inside residue treater unit), 8 treated for possible toxic exposure, 40,000+ residents sheltered-in-place for more than 3 hours

### What happened

At approximately 10:35 p.m., a runaway chemical reaction occurred inside a 4,500-gallon methomyl residue treater pressure vessel. The unit had been shut down for an extended period for a distributed control system (DCS) upgrade and residue treater replacement. During restart, out-of-specification methomyl-solvent mixture was fed to the residue treater before the vessel had been properly pre-filled and heated. Equipment malfunctions during the restart went unaddressed. A minimum temperature safety interlock that would have halted the reaction had been bypassed, a practice that had become normalized over time. The runaway reaction overwhelmed the pressure relief system and the vessel failed, killing two workers. The facility is adjacent to MIC (methyl isocyanate) storage, creating the potential for a release similar in character to Bhopal. The MIC air monitor was non-operational at the time of the incident.

### Root causes and findings

The CSB identified five causes. First, the pre-startup safety review (PSSR) was not applied with sufficient rigor before the post-outage restart. Second, operators had not been adequately trained on the new DCS interface. Third, equipment malfunctions during the restart sequence were not properly resolved before feed was introduced. Fourth, out-of-spec methomyl-solvent feed was charged to the vessel before pre-fill and pre-heat conditions were met. Fifth, the bypassed minimum temperature interlock removed the last automatic safeguard against runaway. The proximity of MIC storage amplified the public consequence potential and drew community attention to the broader facility safety culture.

### Key lessons

PSSR following extended outages must be rigorous, not abbreviated. Operator training on new control systems must be validated before live restart, not learned during startup. Safety interlocks must never be bypassed as a routine operating practice. Facility siting in proximity to communities and to other hazardous chemical inventories compounds the consequence of any single release.

### Recommendations

Multiple recommendations to Bayer CropScience covering PSSR rigor, interlock bypass management, operator requalification after DCS changes, and MIC monitoring reliability. Recommendations also addressed West Virginia emergency response planning for facilities with catastrophic release potential.

## Case 3502: Hot Work Safety Bulletin, Seven Key Lessons

**Coverage period:** 1990 to 2010 (11 incidents reviewed)
**Report:** CSB Safety Bulletin 2009-01-SB (February 2010)
**Hazard type:** Flammable vapor ignition (hot work on and around tanks)
**Outcome:** 60+ fatalities in the covered period; incidents reviewed include PCA Tomahawk WI (3 killed Jul 2008), TEPPCO Garner AR (3 killed May 2009), Partridge-Raleigh MS (3 killed Jun 2006), ConAgra Boardman OR (1 killed Feb 2009), Motiva Delaware City DE (1 killed Jul 2001), Bethune Point FL (2 killed Jan 2006), Pennzoil Rouseville PA (5 killed Oct 1995), and four others

### What happened

This bulletin synthesizes 11 incidents in which workers died while performing hot work (welding, cutting, grinding) on or near tanks containing or previously containing flammable or combustible materials. Seven of the 11 incidents involved no gas testing before or during hot work. Four involved improper testing methods or misread results. The anchor case, PCA Tomahawk (Wisconsin, July 2008), involved three contract workers killed during cutting on a storage tank believed to be empty; residual flammable material was not identified before work began.

### Root causes and findings (common across incidents)

Hot work permits were either absent or treated as administrative checkboxes rather than as active hazard controls. Atmosphere testing was skipped, delegated to workers without proper equipment, or conducted in locations that did not represent the actual hazard zone. Workers and supervisors did not understand how flammable vapors accumulate and travel inside tanks or in surrounding areas. Contractors were permitted to begin work without facility-level review of the specific hazard conditions. Supervision during the work was absent or ineffective.

### Seven lessons

1. Use alternatives to hot work whenever possible. 2. Analyze all hazards before issuing a permit. 3. Monitor the atmosphere continuously during work. 4. Test the area surrounding the tank, not just the interior. 5. Use written permits signed by a qualified issuer. 6. Train all workers and supervisors on hot work hazards. 7. Actively supervise contractor hot work from start to finish.

### Key takeaway

Hot work is among the most consistently lethal operations in industrial settings, and the recurring cause is systematic bypass of established controls, not exotic or unpredictable failure modes.

## Case 3503: Goodyear Houston, Heat Exchanger Ammonia Release

**Incident date:** June 11, 2008
**Location:** Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company, Houston, Texas
**Report:** CSB Case Study 2008-06-I-TX (January 2011)
**Hazard type:** Overpressure, toxic gas release (ammonia)
**Outcome:** 1 killed (body found approximately 1:20 p.m., hours after emergency declared over), 6 injured (5 ammonia exposure, 1 while evacuating)

### What happened

On June 10, maintenance personnel replaced a rupture disk on a refrigeration heat exchanger. During the work, the isolation valve between the heat exchanger shell and the rupture disk and relief valve assembly was left in the closed position when the job was completed. The error was not caught during post-maintenance inspection. The following morning, an operator closed the block valve on a pressure control valve and connected steam for pipe cleaning operations. Steam heated the liquid ammonia trapped in the isolated heat exchanger shell, which had no overpressure protection whatsoever. Pressure climbed until the shell ruptured at approximately 7:30 a.m. The release and structural collapse injured workers in the area. Emergency response was declared over before a full worker accounting was completed. The victim, a member of the emergency response team, was not reported missing at the muster because her absence was attributed to a response role. Her body was found in the rubble more than five hours later.

### Root causes and findings

The maintenance closeout procedure did not verify valve position after disk replacement. No pre-job or post-job checklist confirmed that isolation valves were returned to their operating positions. The decision to apply steam to a connected line without confirming the status of adjacent equipment was made without a hazard review. ASME Code requires that pressure relief protection be confirmed as active any time relief devices are removed from service; this requirement was not implemented. Goodyear had not conducted evacuation drills for four years, and the emergency accountability system did not cross-check against all facility personnel, allowing the victim to go unaccounted.

### Key lessons

Maintenance isolation valve positions must be verified as part of work closeout, not assumed. Any operation that introduces heat to a system connected to refrigerant must confirm that relief protection is active. Worker accountability during emergencies must be independently verified against a complete personnel list, not rely on self-reporting or role assumptions.
