## 3504 - Imperial Sugar Refinery Explosion and Fire

**Date:** February 7, 2008
**Location:** Port Wentworth, Georgia
**Facility type:** Sugar refining and packaging (food manufacturing)
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2008-05-I-GA, September 2009
**Outcome:** 14 killed, 36 injured; two packing buildings and multiple silos destroyed

### What happened

Workers were running normal production at the Imperial Sugar refinery when a series of explosions tore through the packaging buildings. The primary explosion originated inside an enclosed steel belt conveyor running beneath the sugar storage silos. An overheated bearing ignited sugar dust that had accumulated inside the conveyor housing. That initial explosion dispersed accumulated sugar dust throughout the connected packing buildings, triggering a chain of secondary explosions. The fires and structural collapses trapped workers throughout the facility.

### Why it happened

The conveyor housing had been enclosed with steel panels roughly two years before the incident to contain dust and reduce housekeeping demands. The enclosure was not fitted with explosion relief vents. When the primary deflagration occurred inside the enclosed conveyor, the pressure had no relief path and destroyed the structure. More broadly, sugar dust had accumulated to hazardous levels on elevated surfaces, equipment tops, and inside structures throughout the plant. The facility had experienced small fires and minor explosions over many years. Rather than treating these events as warnings, supervisors and workers came to view them as normal. No formal combustible dust hazard analysis had been completed. The emergency evacuation system was inadequate for a facility of this size and layout.

### Causes and contributing factors

The immediate physical cause was an overheated bearing igniting accumulated sugar dust inside an unvented enclosed conveyor. Root causes included: equipment not designed or maintained to prevent dust accumulation; no engineering controls on the conveyor enclosure to limit explosion consequences; decades-long normalization of minor dust fires; no process hazard analysis specific to combustible dust; and inadequate emergency evacuation capability. The hazard of sugar dust explosions had been documented in the literature since at least 1925, but that knowledge was not applied at this facility.

### PSM gaps

Process hazard analysis, mechanical integrity, operating procedures, housekeeping program, emergency planning, process safety information (dust explosion characteristics never formally evaluated).

### Key lesson

Combustible dust accumulation is a slow-moving, normalized hazard. Facilities that have experienced small fires without serious consequence are at elevated risk precisely because those near-misses erode the sense of urgency. Equipment modifications (such as enclosing a conveyor) that reduce routine nuisance can dramatically increase explosion severity if not engineered with dust explosion venting.

## 3505 - T2 Laboratories Explosion

**Date:** December 19, 2007
**Location:** Jacksonville, Florida
**Facility type:** Specialty chemical manufacturing (methylcyclopentadienyl manganese tricarbonyl, MCMT)
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2008-3-I-FL, September 2009
**Outcome:** 4 killed, 32 injured (4 employees, 28 members of the public); multiple businesses condemned; debris found 1 mile away

### What happened

T2 Laboratories was producing its 175th batch of MCMT, a gasoline additive, when the reactor underwent a violent runaway reaction. During the metalation step, the cooling system failed. Without cooling, the reaction temperature climbed uncontrolled. The reactor reached pressures far beyond its design limits and burst with an explosion equivalent to roughly 1,400 pounds of TNT. The blast destroyed the facility, damaged surrounding buildings, and sent fragments more than a mile from the site.

### Why it happened

T2 had never formally recognized that MCMT synthesis could undergo a runaway reaction. The process relied on a single cooling water supply with no backup. When that supply failed, there was no redundant system, no high-temperature alarm, and no operator procedure for loss of cooling. The reactor's relief system had been sized only for normal overpressure scenarios, not for the much larger vapor generation rate of a runaway reaction. Because the hazard was never identified, no protective layers were ever installed.

### Causes and contributing factors

The root cause was that T2 Laboratories did not identify or characterize the reactive chemical hazard in MCMT synthesis. Contributing factors included: single-point failure in cooling with no redundancy; a relief system undersized for runaway conditions; no high-temperature or cooling-loss alarm; and no emergency response procedure for loss of cooling. The CSB found that reactive hazard awareness is not consistently taught in undergraduate chemical engineering programs and recommended that AIChE and ABET address this gap in curricula.

### PSM gaps

Process hazard analysis (reactive chemistry), process safety information (thermal hazard data, runaway reaction screening), mechanical integrity (cooling system redundancy), operating procedures (loss-of-cooling emergency).

### Key lesson

Producing a product successfully many times does not mean the process is safe. Reactive hazards require experimental characterization (calorimetry) and dedicated scenario analysis. Single-point failure in a critical safety utility is a known risk pattern that no amount of operating experience substitutes for addressing.

## 3506 - Xcel Energy Cabin Creek Hydroelectric Plant Fire

**Date:** October 2, 2007
**Location:** Georgetown, Colorado
**Facility type:** Hydroelectric power generation (utility)
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2008-01-I-CO, August 2010
**Outcome:** 5 killed, 3 injured; buildings condemned; Xcel Energy challenged the CSB's investigative authority in federal court before the report was released

### What happened

A subcontractor crew was recoating the interior of a large steel penstock (water pipe) at the Cabin Creek facility. Workers were using an airless sprayer with methyl ethyl ketone (MEK) as a flushing solvent inside the confined, enclosed penstock. MEK vapor accumulated in the space. A static discharge or other ignition source ignited the vapor. The resulting fire blocked the single entry/exit point, trapping workers inside the penstock. Five workers died from the fire and smoke.

### Why it happened

The penstock was a permit-required confined space, but neither Xcel Energy nor the contractor (RPI Coating) treated it as one. No permit was issued. No atmospheric monitoring for flammable vapor was conducted before or during work. The entry point was the only egress, and no confined space rescue team was standing by. The project had been awarded to RPI on the basis of low price despite RPI having a documented history of safety violations. No pre-job hazard evaluation was performed to identify the fire and entrapment risks from solvent use inside the space.

### Causes and contributing factors

Root causes: the penstock was misclassified and not managed as a permit-required confined space; no flammable vapor monitoring was performed during solvent use; contractor selection prioritized cost over safety performance; no competent confined space rescue was arranged. Contributing factors included scheduling and financial pressure on the project, inadequate training for both Xcel and contractor personnel, and a gap in OSHA confined space rules that did not specify a lower explosive limit threshold for halting work.

### PSM gaps

Contractor management (selection criteria, oversight), permit-to-work system, confined space hazard recognition, pre-job hazard analysis, emergency planning (rescue capability).

### Key lesson

Confined space misclassification is a recurring precursor in contractor fatalities. Owner companies that delegate confined space work without verifying contractor competency and safety management system quality share direct responsibility for outcomes. Low-bid contractor selection without minimum safety performance requirements systematically transfers risk to the least-capable workforce.

## 3507 - Little General Store Propane Explosion

**Date:** January 30, 2007
**Location:** Ghent, West Virginia
**Facility type:** Convenience store with propane supply (retail and propane distribution)
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2007-04-I-WV, September 2008
**Outcome:** 4 killed (2 firefighters, 2 propane technicians), 6 injured; store leveled; ambulance destroyed

### What happened

A Ferrellgas crew arrived at the Little General Store to change out an 1,000-gallon propane tank. The existing tank had a defective liquid withdrawal valve. When the crew connected to remove the tank, the defective valve released liquid propane rather than vapor. The propane tank was positioned against the exterior back wall of the store building, a placement that had been in violation of NFPA 58 standards for more than a decade. Liquid propane flashed to vapor and entered the store through wall penetrations. Neither the crew nor store employees evacuated the building. First responders arrived before the area was cleared. Propane vapor ignited inside the store, causing an explosion that killed four people and destroyed the structure.

### Why it happened

Ferrellgas had inspected the installation repeatedly without identifying the non-compliant tank placement against the building wall. The junior technician performing the tank change-out had not received training on how to handle a defective liquid withdrawal valve and was working without adequate supervision. Emergency responders in the area had not received training on propane emergency procedures, specifically that vapor accumulation requires immediate evacuation of the building, not entry.

### Causes and contributing factors

The non-compliant tank placement allowed propane vapor to enter the building. The defective valve had not been identified or corrected through years of inspections. An untrained technician attempted to handle an emergency situation without support. Responding firefighters entered or remained near the building without recognizing the vapor accumulation hazard. The CSB identified failures in propane industry inspection practice, technician qualification standards, and emergency responder training for propane incidents.

### PSM gaps

Mechanical integrity (valve inspection and replacement), training (technician qualification, emergency response), contractor supervision, operating procedures (defective equipment protocols), emergency planning.

### Key lesson

Long-standing code violations that have not caused an incident become normalized and invisible. Inspection programs must have defined acceptance criteria and clear escalation paths when deficiencies are found. Emergency responders need propane-specific training; general fire response instincts (enter the building, search for the source) can be fatal in a vapor accumulation event.

## 3508 - Barton Solvents Static Spark Fire

**Date:** October 29, 2007
**Location:** Des Moines, Iowa
**Facility type:** Chemical distribution (solvents)
**Report:** CSB Safety Advisory Case Study No. 2008-02-I-IA, September 2008
**Outcome:** 1 worker minor injury, 1 firefighter heat illness; warehouse destroyed; prolonged business interruption
**Note:** Only a CSB Safety Advisory Case Study was published for this incident, not a full investigation report.

### What happened

A worker at Barton Solvents was filling a 300-gallon steel tote with ethyl acetate from a bulk storage tank. The fill hose and nozzle were not bonded to the tote, and the tote was not grounded. As ethyl acetate flowed through the equipment, static electricity built up. A static discharge ignited the ethyl acetate vapors. The fire spread rapidly through the packaging area and into the main warehouse, destroying the building and its contents.

### Why it happened

Bonding and grounding is the standard engineering control for preventing static ignition during flammable liquid transfer. The fill equipment used in this operation was not bonded or grounded. Top-filling without a dip tube (a pipe that delivers liquid to the bottom of the container) increases static generation. The packaging area had no fire suppression system, and there was no physical separation between the packaging operation and bulk flammable liquid storage. When the packaging area ignited, fire spread rapidly to the larger inventory.

### Causes and contributing factors

Absence of bonding and grounding on fill equipment was the direct cause. Contributing factors included top-filling practice without a dip tube, no fire suppression in the packaging area, no physical barrier between packaging and bulk storage, and insufficient distance between ignition-risk areas and high-inventory storage. The case study highlights that static hazards in solvent transfer are well understood and avoidable with standard controls.

### PSM gaps

Process hazard analysis, process safety information (static ignition hazards), mechanical integrity (bonding/grounding equipment), facility siting (separation of packaging from bulk storage), operating procedures.

### Key lesson

Static ignition in flammable liquid transfer is a fully understood and preventable hazard. Bonding and grounding equipment is inexpensive and reliable. When those controls are absent, a single transfer operation can ignite an entire warehouse. Separation of packaging operations from bulk storage limits the consequences when ignition does occur.

## 3509 - Valero McKee Refinery Fire

**Date:** February 16, 2007
**Location:** Sunray, Texas
**Facility type:** Petroleum refining
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2007-05-I-TX, July 2008
**Outcome:** 4 injured (3 with serious burns); refinery shutdown approximately 2 months; more than $50 million direct damage; secondary release of 2.5 tons of chlorine from cooling tower biocide containers

### What happened

A propane deasphalting (PDA) unit at the McKee refinery had a piping control station that had been out of service for roughly 15 years. This dead-leg section of pipe had no freeze protection. On a cold night, water in the dead-leg froze and cracked an elbow. When temperatures warmed the following day, the ice plug melted and the cracked elbow released liquid propane at an estimated rate of 4,500 pounds per minute. The propane cloud ignited. The fire engulfed a pipe rack whose structural steel had not been fireproofed to the distances required for high-pressure flammable releases. The rack collapsed, extending the fire. In a nearby cooling tower, chlorine biocide containers were heated to failure by the fire, releasing chlorine gas.

### Why it happened

Valero had a corporate standard requiring remotely operated shutoff valves (ROSOVs) in units handling high-pressure flammables. The PDA unit had no ROSOVs. Once the pipe failed, operators had no way to isolate the release from a safe distance. The facility had no formal freeze protection program for dead-leg piping. The process hazard analysis for the PDA unit had not identified the freeze risk or the ROSOV gap. Fireproofing on the pipe rack was designed to a standard that did not account for the proximity of the high-pressure PDA unit. Chlorine biocide in the cooling tower had not been evaluated for interaction with nearby fire scenarios; inherently safer water treatment alternatives were available.

### Causes and contributing factors

Root causes: no freeze protection program for dead-leg piping; ROSOVs absent from the PDA unit despite a Valero corporate standard requiring them; PDA unit PHA did not identify freeze hazard or ROSOV gap; inadequate fireproofing criteria for pipe rack near high-pressure flammables. Contributing factors: deluge valve for fire suppression located too close to the fire to be operated safely; chlorine biocide storage in the fire exposure zone.

### PSM gaps

Process hazard analysis, mechanical integrity (dead-leg freeze protection, fireproofing adequacy), process safety information, inherently safer design (chlorine biocide), management of change (ROSOV gap not corrected when identified in corporate standard).

### Key lesson

Out-of-service equipment and dead-leg piping retain physical hazards even when not in operation. Freeze protection programs must include abandoned or inactive lines. Remote isolation capability is essential for high-pressure hydrocarbon releases; without it, operators cannot stop the flow once a large release begins.

## 3510 - CAI/Arnel Ink Facility Explosion

**Date:** November 22, 2006
**Location:** Danvers, Massachusetts
**Facility type:** Ink and paint manufacturing
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2007-03-I-MA, May 2008
**Outcome:** 10 injured; 24 homes and 6 businesses destroyed; more than 300 residents evacuated for several days; approximately 51,000 pounds of nitrocellulose caught fire following the explosion

### What happened

Workers at CAI had been mixing ink overnight in a 2,000-gallon mixing tank. An operator left the facility with a steam valve open, continuing to heat the tank contents through the night. The ventilation system in the building was shut off each evening as standard practice. Flammable vapor from the overheated ink accumulated in the closed building overnight. At 2:46 a.m., an ignition source (never determined) ignited the accumulated vapor. The resulting explosion destroyed the CAI building and damaged or destroyed dozens of surrounding structures. A fire at the adjacent Arnel Company warehouse ignited a large nitrocellulose inventory.

### Why it happened

CAI had no automated temperature controls or high-temperature alarms on the mixing tank. There were no written operating procedures specifying how to shut down the tank at end of shift. Turning off building ventilation in the evening was routine practice. CAI's flammable material inventory exceeded the OSHA PSM threshold, but CAI management was unaware that the PSM regulation applied to their facility. No process hazard analysis had ever been conducted. The local fire department had not inspected the facility in more than four years, and operating permits had lapsed.

### Causes and contributing factors

Root causes: no automated temperature safeguards on the mixing tank; ventilation routinely disabled overnight; no written procedures; CAI management unaware of PSM applicability and therefore had never implemented any systematic hazard controls. Contributing factors: fire department inspection gap allowed permit lapses and unidentified hazards to persist; Massachusetts state licensing law did not specify inspection frequency requirements for facilities handling flammables.

### PSM gaps

Process hazard analysis, process safety information, operating procedures, mechanical integrity (automated temperature controls), emergency planning, management awareness of regulatory applicability.

### Key lesson

PSM applicability is determined by inventory thresholds, not by whether management believes the process is hazardous. Facilities handling large quantities of flammable materials that have never conducted a PHA are operating without any systematic barrier analysis. Disabling ventilation routinely in a building where flammable mixing occurs is a recognized precursor to vapor accumulation events.

## 3511 - Environmental Quality (EQ) Hazardous Waste Facility Fire

**Date:** October 5, 2006
**Location:** Apex, North Carolina
**Facility type:** Hazardous waste treatment, storage, and disposal (TSDF)
**Report:** CSB Case Study No. 2007-01-I-NC, April 2008
**Outcome:** Approximately 30 people sought medical attention (13 first responders); 3,300 residences evacuated for 2 days; no fatalities; building and contents destroyed; no confirmed offsite contamination

### What happened

A small fire started in the EQ facility's oxidizer storage bay, where the inventory included unspent aircraft oxygen generators and pool chlorination chemicals. The fire spread rapidly to an adjacent bay storing hundreds of 55-gallon drums of flammable liquids. The building collapsed under the fire load. Because the fire quickly became uncontrollable and the hazmat inventory was unknown to first responders, firefighters adopted a defensive-only posture and focused on protecting exposures. Local authorities ordered evacuation of the surrounding neighborhood due to uncertainty about what was burning and what might be released.

### Why it happened

The EQ facility had no fire or smoke detection system and no automated fire suppression. There were no fire-rated barriers between the oxidizer bay and the flammable liquid bay, so a fire in one could travel freely to the other. EQ had not provided written information about its hazardous inventory to the local fire department or the Local Emergency Planning Committee (LEPC), as required by community right-to-know regulations. Because first responders did not know what chemicals were present, they could not make informed decisions about suppression strategy or evacuation scope.

### Causes and contributing factors

Absence of fire detection, suppression, and fire barriers allowed a small fire to become a facility-destroying event. Failure to share hazmat inventory information with the LEPC and fire department meant emergency responders arrived without critical information. RCRA hazardous waste regulations at the time did not require fire suppression systems in TSDFs, creating a regulatory gap for facilities with high fire risk.

### PSM gaps

Emergency planning (no hazmat information shared with responders), facility siting (incompatible chemical bays adjacent), process hazard analysis, mechanical integrity (no suppression or detection).

### Key lesson

Emergency responders cannot protect the community if they do not know what is stored at nearby facilities. LEPC reporting requirements exist precisely for this reason, and compliance is not optional. Storing oxidizers and flammable liquids in adjacent, unseparated bays is an inherently unsafe arrangement that virtually guarantees fire escalation if either bay ignites.

## 3512 - Synthron Runaway Reaction Explosion

**Date:** January 31, 2006
**Location:** Morganton, North Carolina
**Facility type:** Acrylic polymer manufacturing (specialty chemicals)
**Report:** CSB Case Study No. 2006-04-I-NC, July 2007
**Outcome:** 1 killed (worker severely burned, died 5 days later), 14 injured (2 seriously); facility destroyed; two churches and a nearby house condemned; window glass broken up to one-third of a mile

### What happened

A batch of acrylic polymer was being produced at Synthron. The production superintendent decided to scale the batch 12 percent larger than the standard recipe. He put nearly all of the additional monomer into the initial charge rather than holding it back as a feed. This change increased the initial heat release rate to approximately 2.3 times the standard recipe. The condenser was already operating at about 75 percent of its rated capacity due to fouling that had persisted for years without cleaning. The combined effect overwhelmed the condenser. The reaction temperature climbed uncontrolled, generating vapor that vented through the reactor manway (which had only 4 of 18 clamps installed). The vapor ignited and the resulting explosion destroyed the building.

### Why it happened

Synthron had never conducted a process hazard analysis and had no formal recognition that acrylic polymerization could undergo a runaway reaction. All management personnel, including the superintendent who changed the recipe, had less than one year of experience at Synthron. Recipe modifications were made informally, without any management of change review. No automated temperature alarm or interlock existed. The condenser had been fouled for years without a cleaning program. The parent company, Protex International, provided no safety oversight or reactive chemistry support to the Synthron operation.

### Causes and contributing factors

Root causes: management and workforce did not recognize runaway reaction hazard; no PHA; no MOC process for recipe changes; no automated safeguards. Contributing factors: condenser operating well below rated capacity due to long-term fouling; manway not fully secured; entire management team inexperienced; parent company provided no process safety support.

### PSM gaps

Process hazard analysis (reactive chemistry), management of change (recipe scaling), mechanical integrity (condenser fouling, manway clamp verification), process safety information (runaway reaction characterization), training.

### Key lesson

Recipe modifications are process changes and must go through management of change review. Scaling a batch or changing charge sequence can dramatically alter heat release rates in ways that overwhelm existing cooling capacity. Reactive hazard recognition requires deliberate effort, not just experience; inexperienced management teams are particularly vulnerable when no corporate safety oversight exists.

## 3513 - Partridge-Raleigh Oilfield Hot Work Explosion

**Date:** June 5, 2006
**Location:** Raleigh, Mississippi
**Facility type:** Oil and gas production (stripper well operation)
**Report:** CSB Case Study No. 2006-07-I-MS, June 2007
**Outcome:** 3 killed (foreman and 2 maintenance workers), 1 seriously injured (welder survived because he was wearing a safety harness); tanks destroyed

### What happened

Workers at a Partridge-Raleigh oil production site were connecting piping between three storage tanks. Before welding on tank 4, that tank was cleaned of flammable residue. Tanks 2 and 3 still contained hydrocarbon residue and vapor. An open-ended pipe from tank 3 was left uncapped and ran near the welding work area on tank 4. Welding on tank 4 ignited vapors that had migrated from the open pipe on tank 3. Fire flashed back into tank 3 and then propagated to tank 2, which exploded. Tank lids were found between 50 and 250 yards from the site. Three workers died; the welder was thrown by the explosion but survived because his safety harness kept him from falling from the tank structure.

### Why it happened

No gas detector was used to check the atmosphere before or during welding. Workers had checked for vapor by "flashing" a lit propane torch near openings, a method that is itself an ignition source. There was no hot work permit system. Partridge-Raleigh had no written safety program. The interconnection between the three tanks meant that cleaning one tank did not isolate it from flammable vapors in adjacent tanks. Workers used a ladder as a makeshift work platform rather than appropriate scaffolding or fall protection.

### Causes and contributing factors

No atmospheric testing before hot work; interconnected tanks not recognized as creating a shared vapor hazard; no hot work permit; no written safety program; improvised work platforms and inadequate fall protection. The CSB noted that the oil and gas production sector, particularly small stripper well operations, commonly lacks the safety infrastructure present in larger facilities. The Mississippi State Oil and Gas Board had authority to observe operations but did not refer safety deficiencies to OSHA.

### PSM gaps

Operating procedures (hot work controls), process safety information (interconnected tank vapor behavior), training, management of change, contractor oversight, emergency planning.

### Key lesson

Hot work on tanks that share vapor spaces or interconnected piping requires treating all connected vessels as potential hazard sources, not just the one being worked on. Checking for flammable vapor with a lit torch is not an atmospheric test; it is an ignition source. Hot work permit systems exist to enforce the sequence of gas testing, isolation, and hazard verification before any flame or spark source is introduced.
