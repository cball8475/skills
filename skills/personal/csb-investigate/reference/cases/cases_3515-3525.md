### BP Texas City Refinery Explosion and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3515 | March 2007
- Company | Location | Industry: BP Products North America Inc. | Texas City, Texas | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: March 23, 2005
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Raffinate (light hydrocarbon liquid, primarily C5/C6 isomers); flammable vapor cloud
- What happened: During startup of the ISOM isomerization unit after a maintenance turnaround, operators overfilled the raffinate splitter tower for more than three hours. A faulty level indicator showed the tower level dropping when it was actually rising. Relief valves opened and discharged flammable liquid to an undersized blowdown drum, which overfilled and vented liquid through an atmospheric blowdown stack directly to grade. A large flammable vapor cloud formed, drifted to a running diesel pickup truck approximately 25 feet away, and ignited, triggering an explosion and fire. All 15 fatalities occurred in or near temporary office trailers sited close to the unit.
- Consequences: 15 killed, 180 injured, more than $1.5 billion in financial losses, 43,000 people under shelter-in-place order, homes damaged up to 0.75 miles away
- Sequence: Tower level indicator reads false low, operators fill tower without withdrawing liquid for 3+ hours, relief valves open, blowdown drum overfills, flammable liquid geyser exits atmospheric stack, vapor cloud ignites at idling truck
- Immediate cause: Liquid overflow from an atmospheric blowdown stack created a large flammable vapor cloud that ignited
- Contributing factors:
  - Faulty tower level indicator (showed declining level during actual overfill) and failed redundant high-level alarm
  - Blowdown drum undersized; atmospheric stack not connected to a flare (prior OSHA citation withdrawn; upgrade proposals rejected for cost)
  - Shift turnover did not communicate the critical status of a partially filled tower
  - Operators fatigued from 12-hour shifts for 29 or more consecutive days
  - Inadequate board operator training; simulator and central training staff cut by 70 percent
  - Extra board operator not assigned despite a staffing assessment recommending one for ISOM startups
  - Unit started with known malfunctions in level indicator, sight glass, and pressure control valve
  - Occupied trailers permitted close to an active hazardous process unit
  - Eight prior ISOM blowdown stack releases and repeated high-level events during startups were never investigated
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Cost-cutting and production pressure from BP Group executives impaired process safety at Texas City
  - BP Board lacked effective oversight of major accident hazard prevention; no board member assigned responsibility for process safety
  - Misleading reliance on personal injury rate as a proxy for process safety health
  - Deficient mechanical integrity program allowed run-to-failure of critical equipment
  - "Check the box" compliance culture where paperwork was signed without requirements being met
  - No reporting and learning culture; workers feared retaliation; lessons from prior incidents not captured
  - Safety campaigns focused on personal safety metrics rather than process safety management systems
  - Repeated audits and surveys identified deep-seated problems, but management response was consistently too little, too late
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Level indicator read falsely low (failed to provide correct process information)
  - Redundant high-level alarm did not activate
  - No automatic high-level shutdown on the tower
  - Blowdown drum too small to handle relief valve discharge; no flare
  - No requirement for nonessential personnel to clear the area during startups
  - Pre-startup safety review (PSSR) policy not effectively implemented
- Key findings:
  - The level control valve was left closed during filling, and no liquid was removed from the tower for over three hours; the control board display did not clearly show the flow imbalance
  - Prior incidents warning of blowdown stack dangers were ignored from 1992 onward; an upgrade to a flare was proposed in 2002 but rejected for a cheaper alternative
  - Trailer siting placed up to 150 contractors in the blast zone
  - 23 workers died at Texas City in the 30 years before this incident, but the personal injury rate gave a misleading picture of safety performance
  - The Baker Panel (independent review commissioned by BP) confirmed systemic safety culture and management system deficiencies across all North American BP refineries
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - BP Group Executive Board: commission independent safety culture review of all North American refineries (Baker Panel); establish board member accountability for process safety
  - BP Texas City: replace atmospheric blowdown stacks with flare-connected systems; enforce trailer siting policy; implement shift turnover requirements; add extra board operator to high-hazard startups
  - OSHA: strengthen PSM standard to address process safety indicators beyond injury rates; improve oversight of corporate-level safety management systems
  - API: develop guidance on process safety performance metrics and blowdown system design
- PSM elements implicated: process safety information, PHA, mechanical integrity, operating procedures/safe operating limits, training, MOC, facility siting, incident investigation, emergency planning
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Operators made errors during a unit startup and did not notice the tower filling up.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A decade of cost-cutting had degraded every layer of defense simultaneously, from faulty instruments that were never fixed, to a blowdown stack upgrade that was cancelled to save money, to a training department cut by 70 percent, to a culture where workers did not feel safe raising concerns. No single error caused this, but the organizational environment made the disaster nearly inevitable.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Verify that personal injury rate is not the only metric used to judge process safety health; add lagging and leading process safety indicators.
  - Confirm that all atmospheric blowdown or vent stacks handling large flammable inventories are routed to a flare or knockout system, not open to atmosphere.
  - Before any unit startup, require a documented pre-startup walkdown that verifies all instrumentation is functional and that nonessential personnel have cleared the area.

### Bethune Point Wastewater Plant Methanol Tank Explosion and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3516 | March 2007
- Company | Location | Industry: City of Daytona Beach | Daytona Beach, Florida | Municipal wastewater treatment
- Incident date: January 11, 2006
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE (hot work ignition)
- Materials: Methanol (highly flammable liquid, stored in aboveground tank); methanol vapor
- What happened: Maintenance workers were performing hot work (cutting torch) on a roof above a methanol storage tank. Methanol vapors venting from the tank vent were ignited by the torch flame, which flashed back through the vent into the tank and caused an internal explosion. The blast fractured the PVC methanol piping attached to the tank, releasing approximately 3,000 gallons of methanol that fed a large fire. Two employees were killed and a third was severely burned.
- Consequences: 2 fatalities, 1 severely injured, significant damage to the wastewater plant
- Sequence: Cutting torch used on roof above tank vent, vapors ignite, flame travels back through vent, tank explodes internally, PVC piping fails, 3,000 gallons methanol released, large fire ensues
- Immediate cause: Hot work performed directly above an active methanol tank vent ignited vapors and flashed back into the tank
- Contributing factors:
  - Piping in the methanol system was PVC rather than steel; PVC shattered catastrophically when the tank exploded, releasing the bulk of the fuel
  - Aluminum flame arrester on the tank vent had corroded (methanol corrodes aluminum) and was not functioning correctly; no maintenance program required inspection of the arrester
  - No program existed at the city to evaluate hazards of non-routine maintenance tasks
  - The operation and maintenance manual did not require flame arrester inspection or testing
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - The City of Daytona Beach did not implement adequate hot work controls at the WWTP; specifically, there was no assessment of the hazard of working above or near the methanol system while using a torch
  - The City's hazard communication program did not effectively communicate the flammable hazards of methanol to workers at the plant
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Flame arrester corroded and non-functional
  - No hot work permit process adequate to identify the vapor hazard above the tank
  - PVC piping used in a flammable liquid service where steel is required
  - No maintenance inspection requirement for flame arresters
- Key findings:
  - Florida public employees at the time were not covered by the federal OSHA standard, and the state had no equivalent program; the City had little third-party safety oversight
  - The engineering firm that designed the advanced wastewater treatment process (Camp Dresser & McKee) specified PVC piping for methanol service and an aluminum flame arrester, both of which were inappropriate for the service conditions
  - Similar methanol systems at wastewater plants nationwide use the same hazardous design features
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Governor and Legislature of Florida: extend OSHA-equivalent coverage to Florida public employees
  - City of Daytona Beach: implement hot work permit program; replace PVC methanol piping with steel; establish flame arrester inspection program
  - NFPA: clarify that NFPA 30 (Flammable and Combustible Liquids Code) applies to wastewater treatment methanol systems
  - OSHA: issue guidance targeting flammable liquid hazards at publicly owned wastewater treatment plants
  - Methanol Institute and Water Environment Federation: communicate hazards of methanol in wastewater applications to member utilities
  - Camp Dresser & McKee: notify other municipal clients who received similar PVC methanol piping designs
- PSM elements implicated: process safety information, operating procedures/safe operating limits, mechanical integrity, PHA, training, emergency planning
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers did hot work in the wrong place and didn't know there was a flammable tank nearby.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A municipal utility with no process safety culture, no hot work permit system, and a design from an engineering firm that specified the wrong materials for a flammable liquid application. Public sector facilities often fall outside OSHA jurisdiction and receive no equivalent regulatory oversight, leaving basic safeguards to chance.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Confirm that all flame arresters on flammable liquid tank vents are inspected on a defined schedule and are constructed of a material compatible with the stored liquid.
  - Verify that hot work permits require a survey of what is directly above, below, and within 35 feet of any torch or spark-producing work, including tank vents.
  - Check that flammable liquid piping materials conform to codes for the service; PVC is not acceptable for most flammable liquids.
- Source file(s) read: Bethune_Final_Report.pdf (ID 17nXS_BJC7XFGON03X16CkfJ1kBRe26Rf)

### Formosa Plastics Corporation Vinyl Chloride Monomer Explosion

- Archive id | Final report date: 3517 | March 2007
- Company | Location | Industry: Formosa Plastics Corporation, USA (Illiopolis, IL subsidiary) | Illiopolis, Illinois | Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) manufacturing
- Incident date: April 23, 2004
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Vinyl chloride monomer (VCM), a flammable gas (LEL 3.6 percent, UEL 33 percent) and known human carcinogen
- What happened: During reactor cleaning operations at the Formosa-IL PVC plant, an operator cleaning one reactor inadvertently opened the bottom valve of an adjacent operating reactor that was full of pressurized, heated VCM. The valve opening required bypassing a pressure interlock. A massive VCM release resulted, which ignited and caused a large explosion and fire. Two operators and the shift supervisor who remained to manage the release did not evacuate and were killed. Three other workers were severely injured.
- Consequences: 5 fatalities, 3 severely injured, most of the reactor facility and adjacent warehouse destroyed, community evacuation for 2 days, PVC resin warehouse fire, environmental impact from VCM release. Plant permanently shut down.
- Sequence: Operator bypasses interlock on adjacent operating reactor, opens bottom valve, VCM floods reactor building, vapor ignites, explosion destroys reactor facility, workers remaining to manage release are killed
- Immediate cause: Inadvertent opening of the bottom valve of an operating reactor, releasing its full inventory of pressurized VCM
- Contributing factors:
  - Reactor bottom valve control panels were not clearly labeled; panel layout allowed confusion between adjacent reactors
  - Staffing reductions reduced the number of experienced operators per shift
  - A similar incident at the Baton Rouge FPC USA facility in 2003 was not recognized as relevant to Illiopolis; no cross-facility lessons-learned process existed
  - A near-identical incident at Illiopolis in February 2004, two months before, was investigated but corrective actions were not implemented before this fatal incident
  - Emergency evacuation procedures were ambiguous; no large-release drill had been conducted in more than 10 years
  - The deluge fire suppression system covered only the valve panel area, not the zone where VCM would concentrate
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Borden Chemical (the prior owner) did not implement 1992 PHA recommendations to redesign the interlock bypass to reduce misuse, and accepted the procedural interlock as adequate despite high consequences
  - Formosa-IL did not recognize the applicability of the 2003 Baton Rouge incident and took no preventive action
  - Formosa-IL did not implement corrective actions from the February 2004 on-site incident before the fatal event
  - Formosa-IL and FPC USA relied on a written procedure as the sole safeguard for a hazard with catastrophic potential
  - FPC USA lacked written guidelines for matching safeguard rigor to risk level and had no comprehensive interlock management standards
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Pressure interlock bypass was accessible and insufficiently protected
  - Human factors in reactor panel layout contributed to valve confusion
  - Deluge system did not cover the area where VCM accumulated
  - Emergency evacuation procedures were unclear on when to evacuate versus stay to manage a release
  - No cross-facility incident learning system to share lessons from similar events at sister plants
- Key findings:
  - At least three prior incidents at FPC USA PVC facilities involving inadvertent reactor discharge were not adequately shared or acted upon
  - The hazard of opening the reactor bottom valve was analyzed as "severe" in the 1999 PHA, but the interlock controlled only by procedure and training was accepted as sufficient
  - FPC USA had no written standard for interlocks at its PVC facilities
  - The Vinyl Institute (trade association) had not developed industry-wide guidance on preventing inadvertent reactor discharge
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Formosa USA: install engineering controls (key locks, redundant interlocks, or positive barriers) to prevent bypass of operating reactor bottom valves; redesign control panels to reduce human error; require large-release evacuation drills; implement corporate lessons-learned sharing across all PVC plants
  - Vinyl Institute: develop industry guidance on safeguarding reactor discharge valves and matching safeguards to risk
  - NFPA and EPA: address PVC/VCM reactor hazard controls in relevant standards and regulations
  - CCPS (AIChE): incorporate VCM reactor safeguard cases in guidelines for hazard evaluation procedures
- PSM elements implicated: PHA, mechanical integrity, operating procedures/safe operating limits, MOC, incident investigation, training, emergency planning, process safety information
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A worker opened the wrong valve and didn't know there was a dangerous reactor nearby.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The company had been warned by its own PHAs and by nearly identical incidents at sister plants, but never elevated the safeguard from a written procedure to an engineered barrier. A culture that treated prior near-misses as closed items rather than signals of systemic vulnerability set the conditions for this fatality.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - For any valve whose inadvertent operation could release a large hazardous inventory, confirm that the safeguard is an engineered barrier (key interlock, positive lock-out), not just a procedure.
  - When a serious incident or near-miss occurs at one company site or a sister facility, require a formal applicability review at all similar units before closing the investigation.
  - Verify that large-release emergency drills are conducted at a defined frequency; a drill gap of more than 10 years is a red flag.
- Source file(s) read: Formosa_IL_Report.pdf (ID 16TDiohrcxVqjVnRJXJzLLdMNpQAQT-lA)

### DPC Enterprises Glendale Chlorine Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3518 | February 2007
- Company | Location | Industry: DPC Enterprises, L.P. | Glendale, Arizona | Chlorine repackaging
- Incident date: November 17, 2003
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Chlorine gas; sodium hypochlorite (bleach) decomposition products (chlorine, oxygen)
- What happened: At DPC's chlorine repackaging facility, excess chlorine vented to a batch caustic scrubber completely depleted the scrubbing caustic solution. The over-chlorinated bleach then underwent a decomposition reaction, releasing a cloud of chlorine and other toxic gases for approximately six hours into the surrounding neighborhood. Operators discovered the event during a quality control test but could not stop emissions until additional caustic was injected six hours later.
- Consequences: 16 people sought medical evaluation (5 residents, 11 Glendale police officers), 1.5 square miles of Glendale and Phoenix evacuated, no fatalities; 11 police officers were exposed because they entered the chlorine zone without adequate protective equipment
- Sequence: High chlorine vent rate to scrubber, operators continue chlorine flow during QC testing (contrary to procedure), caustic depleted, bleach decomposes, chlorine cloud released for 6 hours, emergency responders partially exposed
- Immediate cause: Excess chlorine vented to scrubber while operators continued adding chlorine during quality control testing, depleting caustic and triggering over-chlorination decomposition
- Contributing factors:
  - Site practice deviated from procedure by continuing chlorine flow to the scrubber during quality control sampling; management did not detect or correct this deviation
  - Operators were not trained on the consequences of over-chlorination or the sensitivity of the scrubber to this condition
  - Published industry guidance from The Chlorine Institute did not specify the composition, quantity, or duration of emissions during over-chlorination events, delaying stabilization
  - Police officers were sent into the chlorine-impacted area without hazmat briefing or equipment checks due to poor integration between fire and police command structures
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - DPC's safeguard against scrubber over-chlorination consisted of a single procedural control, which was not commensurate with the risk given the potential for a large community toxic release
  - DPC's hazard assessment process had not evaluated the consequences of procedural deviation or identified the need for engineering safeguards
  - DPC's internal PSM/RMP audit program did not detect deficiencies in operating procedures, training, or process safety information
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No automatic chlorine shutoff tied to scrubber oxidation-reduction potential (ORP) alarm
  - No automatic or remote caustic injection to interrupt decomposition
  - No secondary scrubber downstream to treat over-chlorination emissions
  - Sole safeguard was a written procedure that was routinely violated without detection
- Key findings:
  - DPC had been warned by prior over-chlorination events at its own facilities but had not added engineering barriers
  - The facility was regulated under EPA's Risk Management Program, but the RMP had not prompted the company to evaluate engineering safeguards commensurate with community consequence
  - 11 police officers were exposed to chlorine because the police department had not been integrated into incident command and officers lacked adequate APR equipment or training in hazmat self-protection
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - DPC Enterprises: install automatic chlorine shutoff on ORP alarm; install automatic caustic injection or redundant scrubber; revise SOPs to include consequence warnings and operating limits; independent PSM/RMP audit
  - Glendale Fire Department: integrate police department into hazmat incident command; conduct joint drills
  - Glendale Police Department: ensure APR availability and training for first responders near chemical facilities
  - The Chlorine Institute: publish guidance specifying emissions composition and duration during over-chlorination events
- PSM elements implicated: PHA, mechanical integrity, operating procedures/safe operating limits, training, process safety information, emergency planning, incident investigation
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers didn't follow procedure and let too much chlorine into the scrubber.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A hazard that required an engineered barrier was protected only by a procedure that was routinely skipped. DPC's own audit program never caught the deviation. The community bore the consequences of a corporate safety management system that had not sized its safeguards to the risk.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - For any scrubber or abatement device treating a toxic gas stream, confirm that there is at least one automatic (non-procedural) safeguard to prevent over-loading, such as an interlock shutting off feed on ORP or level alarm.
  - Audit whether site practice for safety-critical steps matches the written procedure; deviations that are normalized over time are often invisible to management.
  - Confirm that your community emergency plan includes coordinated response roles for police and that officers who may enter a hazmat perimeter are trained and equipped.
- Source file(s) read: DPC2-_Final.pdf (ID 1MO341ADH1rCVLcJv6kBNAPOzPS3h1Ef1)

### CSB Combustible Dust Hazard Study

- Archive id | Final report date: 3519 | November 2006
- Company | Location | Industry: Industry-wide study (not a single-site incident) | United States, multiple states | General industry (aluminum, plastics, pharmaceuticals, wood, grain, food, rubber, and others)
- Incident date: Study published November 2006; covers incidents from 1980 to 2005; catalyzed by three catastrophic explosions in 2003 (West Pharmaceutical Services, CTA Acoustics, Hayes Lemmerz International)
- Hazard type: combustible dust
- Materials: Wide variety, including polyethylene dust, fiberglass resin dust, aluminum dust, wood dust, grain dust, rubber dust, pharmaceutical powders
- What happened: Following 14 worker fatalities in three separate catastrophic dust explosions in 2003, the CSB initiated a broad hazard investigation of combustible dust in American industry. The study found 281 combustible dust incidents between 1980 and 2005, killing 119 workers and injuring 718. The study analyzed regulatory gaps, industry standards, and the common causal factors across incidents. It concluded that the hazard is serious and widespread, yet no comprehensive federal OSHA standard addressed it.
- Consequences: Across the 281 incidents: 119 killed, 718 injured, extensive property damage across 44 states and multiple industries. Three 2003 incidents alone killed 14 workers. Seven catastrophic events with multiple fatalities occurred in the prior decade.
- Sequence (common pattern across incidents): Combustible dust accumulates on elevated surfaces or inside equipment, a primary explosion or disturbance lofts the accumulated dust, secondary dust cloud ignites, a larger secondary explosion follows propagating through ducting or building spaces
- Immediate cause: Ignition of suspended combustible dust clouds, often triggered by a primary event such as a small fire, equipment spark, or mechanical impact
- Contributing factors:
  - Lack of housekeeping programs to remove dust accumulations from elevated surfaces, roofs, and concealed spaces
  - Material safety data sheets (MSDSs) frequently omit dust explosion warnings; CSB found that nearly half of MSDSs for combustible particulate materials contained no explosion warning
  - No comprehensive federal OSHA standard for combustible dust in general industry (contrast with OSHA Grain Handling Standard, which effectively reduced grain dust explosions after issuance)
  - NFPA voluntary consensus standards (NFPA 654, 484, and others) are comprehensive and authoritative but adoption and enforcement by state and local fire codes is inconsistent
  - Local fire code officials rarely inspect industrial facilities for dust hazards and lack familiarity with the hazard
  - Dust explosion potential can arise through processing and handling that was not anticipated at the time of MSDS preparation
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - No mandatory federal standard requiring hazard assessment, engineering controls, housekeeping, or worker training specific to combustible dust in general industry
  - OSHA's Hazard Communication Standard did not clearly apply to combustible dusts, leaving employers without unambiguous guidance
  - Decentralized and inconsistent U.S. fire code system made uniform enforcement of NFPA standards infeasible
  - Employers often unaware that a material creates a dust explosion hazard when processed or handled differently from its bulk form
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Dust collection and housekeeping systems inadequate or absent
  - No deflagration suppression or venting designed per NFPA 68/69
  - Dust collectors sited inside buildings rather than outside, per NFPA guidance
  - Ignition control programs absent or inadequate
- Key findings:
  - The three 2003 incidents that catalyzed the study would have been prevented or significantly mitigated had the facilities complied with relevant NFPA voluntary standards
  - OSHA's grain handling standard demonstrates that a specific OSHA standard can materially reduce the frequency and severity of dust explosions in a targeted industry
  - Local fire inspection largely ignores industrial dust hazards; the NFPA standard framework is effective on paper but enforcement is absent in practice
  - MSDSs are an unreliable tool for communicating combustible dust explosion risk
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - OSHA: issue a comprehensive combustible dust standard for general industry covering hazard assessment, engineering controls (venting, suppression, isolation), housekeeping, and training; conduct a special emphasis inspection program targeting high-risk industries as an interim measure
  - OSHA: revise the Hazard Communication Standard to explicitly apply to combustible dusts and require MSDSs to include explosion hazard information and safe handling guidance
  - ANSI: amend Z400.1 to require dust-specific MSDS content
- PSM elements implicated: PHA, process safety information, mechanical integrity, operating procedures/safe operating limits, training, inherently safer design, emergency planning
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers didn't sweep up dust and then a spark set it off.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): No federal standard required any facility handling combustible particulates to identify the hazard, design for it, or train workers on it. Voluntary NFPA standards existed and were effective where applied, but their adoption depended on patchwork state and local fire code enforcement that rarely reached industrial floors. The regulatory gap allowed the hazard to remain hidden in plain sight across hundreds of facilities.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Survey all areas of your facility for dust accumulation on elevated surfaces, cable trays, roof trusses, and inside ventilation ducts; any layer more than 1/32 inch on a significant surface area is a secondary explosion hazard.
  - For any powdered or granular material you process or handle, check whether its MSDS addresses combustible dust explosion potential; if not, obtain test data (Kst, MIE, MEC) and apply applicable NFPA standards.
  - Confirm that dust collectors serving combustible dust applications are located outside or equipped with deflagration venting, suppression, or isolation per NFPA 654/68/69.

### Valero Delaware City Refinery Nitrogen Asphyxiation Incident

- Archive id | Final report date: 3520 | November 2006
- Company | Location | Industry: Valero Energy Corporation (Delaware City refinery, acquired from Premcor 2005) | Delaware City, Delaware | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: November 5, 2005
- Hazard type: asphyxiation
- Materials: Nitrogen gas (oxygen-depleted atmosphere inside and near a hydrocracker reactor being purged with nitrogen)
- What happened: During a maintenance turnaround, a Matrix boilermaker contractor tried to retrieve a roll of duct tape from a distribution tray inside a hydrocracker reactor that was being purged with nitrogen. After several unsuccessful attempts with a wire hook, he entered or fell into the reactor and was overcome by oxygen deprivation. His co-worker and crew foreman immediately climbed into the reactor to rescue him, was also overcome, and both died. Properly equipped emergency responders recovered both victims, who were pronounced dead at the hospital.
- Consequences: 2 fatalities (both contractor employees, Matrix Service Industrial Contractors)
- Sequence: Reactor on nitrogen purge with open manway, first victim tries to retrieve tape from near manway, falls in or intentionally enters, collapses on distribution tray, foreman sees victim inside and climbs in to rescue him without breathing equipment, both overcome by nitrogen asphyxiation
- Immediate cause: Entry into an oxygen-deficient (near-zero oxygen) reactor without supplied air breathing equipment
- Contributing factors:
  - Safe work permits did not accurately reflect the nitrogen purge status for the boilermaker crew; the nitrogen purge box was marked "N/A" on their permit even though the reactor was purging
  - Required pre-job joint site visit between Valero operator and Matrix foreman was not conducted before the permit was approved
  - Required nitrogen purge barricade and warning signs were not in place at the manway at the time of the incident
  - Training did not adequately address the hazard of an oxygen-deficient atmosphere outside the confined space, near the manway opening, where nitrogen can accumulate in calm weather conditions
  - Training did not emphasize that oxygen deprivation acts within one to two breaths with no sensory warning, and that victims cannot hold their breath long enough to exit safely
  - The powerful human instinct to rescue a co-worker overrode the foreman's training and experience
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Valero's safe work permit procedures were not effectively implemented; critical nitrogen purge information was omitted from one crew's permit and no site walk was conducted
  - Industry guidance (API, ASSE, NIOSH confined space standards) did not adequately address the risk of asphyxiation outside a confined space near an open nitrogen purge vent
  - Training programs at both Valero and Matrix did not communicate that an oxygen-depleted atmosphere may be present in open air directly above or around a venting confined space opening
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Nitrogen purge barricade and warning signs not installed as required
  - Boilermaker crew permit incorrectly marked nitrogen purge as not applicable
  - Pre-job site walk not conducted
  - No fresh-air designation on the boilermaker permit despite proximity to nitrogen vent
  - Confined space entry permit required but not obtained before first victim entered the reactor
- Key findings:
  - 39 percent of confined space fatalities involve would-be rescuers; both this fatality and the foreman's death fit this pattern
  - The risk of asphyxiation from nitrogen accumulation outside the confined space opening is not addressed in OSHA 1910.146 (Permit-Required Confined Spaces) or in most industry guidance
  - Rapidly incapacitating effects of nitrogen (unconsciousness in one to two breaths) are not widely understood even by experienced refinery workers
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Valero Delaware City: refresher training on permit preparation; audit nitrogen purge procedures at all Valero US refineries
  - Valero Energy Corporation: revise nitrogen hazard awareness training across all US refineries to address outside-confined-space asphyxiation risk
  - Matrix Service Industrial Contractors: confined space refresher training emphasizing nitrogen hazard outside openings and safe rescue protocols
  - API: revise API 2217A (Inert Confined Spaces guidelines) to address outside-opening asphyxiation hazard
  - American Society of Safety Engineers: revise ANSI/ASSE Z117.1 confined spaces standard to address outside-opening asphyxiation and rapid incapacitation
  - Compressed Gas Association: issue safety alert on inert gas hazards near confined space openings
- PSM elements implicated: operating procedures/safe operating limits, training, mechanical integrity, contractor management, process safety information, incident investigation
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A worker climbed into a tank to get something and a co-worker tried to save him.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The permit system failed to communicate the nitrogen purge to the crew doing the work, and neither party performed the required site walk that would have revealed the hazard. The underlying gap was that no one, from the industry standards to the site-level training, had taught workers that the hazard extends beyond the vessel opening into the open air around it.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - When any vessel or equipment is being purged with nitrogen or other inert gas, require that a physical barricade and oxygen-alarm monitoring be placed around all open vents, manways, or openings, regardless of whether formal confined space entry is planned.
  - Confirm that confined space entry permits explicitly require the work crew to identify the nitrogen purge status of all nearby equipment, not just the vessel they are entering.
  - Include in training a clear message that inert gas rescuers become victims; only supplied-air-equipped personnel may enter and retrieve an asphyxiated victim.
- Source file(s) read: Valero_Case_Study.pdf (ID 1hwm6BYIoFeI96hSJKXUcm5pXUE3Z_sXC)

### Formosa Plastics Corporation Propylene Explosion and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3521 | June 2006
- Company | Location | Industry: Formosa Plastics Corporation, USA | Point Comfort, Texas | Olefins / petrochemicals manufacturing
- Incident date: October 6, 2005
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Liquid propylene (stored at 216 psig), propylene vapor cloud
- What happened: A forklift operator towing a trailer in the Olefins II unit backed through a non-designated area and the trailer snagged a drain valve protruding from a strainer in a liquid propylene piping system, pulling the valve and attached pipe out and leaving a 1.9-inch opening. Pressurized liquid propylene rapidly escaped and vaporized, forming a large flammable vapor cloud. Operators could not reach the manual valves to isolate the release because the advancing vapor cloud forced them back. The cloud ignited at approximately 3:07 pm, causing an explosion that knocked down and burned two operators. Fires burned for five days.
- Consequences: 16 workers injured (2 burned operators, 14 with minor injuries including scrapes and smoke inhalation), community shelter-in-place ordered for Point Comfort, elementary school evacuated, more than 20 residents sought medical evaluation, Olefins II unit shut down for 5 months
- Sequence: Forklift trailer catches on unprotected valve, valve pulled from strainer leaving 1.9-inch opening, liquid propylene flashes to vapor, large vapor cloud forms, operators unable to reach isolation valves, cloud ignites, explosion and pool fire, structural steel (unprotected from fire) collapses at 30 minutes, crimping flare header and causing further equipment failures, fire burns 5 days
- Immediate cause: Mechanical impact from a forklift trailer on an unprotected process valve, releasing pressurized liquid propylene
- Contributing factors:
  - No physical vehicle impact protection on propylene piping that protruded into accessible areas; administrative controls (speed limits, permits) did not address where vehicles could operate within the unit
  - The facility siting analysis had rated vehicle impact frequency as very low and accepted administrative safeguards as adequate, without requiring physical protection
  - The structural steel supporting the flare header and pressure relief valves was not fireproofed, even though API 2218 (1988) recommended fireproofing structural steel supporting critical safety systems; the plant design was recycled from an earlier plant built before the API guidance was issued and never updated
  - No remotely actuated isolation valve upstream of the propylene pumps; operators could not shut off feed from the control room quickly enough to prevent escalation
  - Workers in the unit were not required to wear flame-resistant clothing (FRC), contrary to NFPA 2113 guidance for environments with flash fire risk
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Formosa's hazard reviews (HAZOP, siting analysis, pre-startup safety review) did not fully address vehicle impact protection for specific process piping or the need for remote emergency isolation
  - The recycled Kellogg plant design was never updated to incorporate post-1988 API fireproofing guidance for structural steel supporting critical safety systems
  - Formosa did not require FRC for unit operators despite prior flash fire incidents from hydrocarbon leaks
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No vehicle impact guards on protruding propylene piping
  - Structural steel supporting relief valves and flare header unprotected against fire
  - No remote isolation valve upstream of propylene pumps; no remote pump control from control room
  - No FRC requirement for field operators in flammable liquid/gas service
- Key findings:
  - The Olefins II unit was an essentially identical copy of an Olefins I design originally from the mid-1980s; Kellogg sold the same design multiple times without updating it for newer standards
  - API fireproofing guidance (API 2218, 1988) predated the Olefins II purchase agreement, yet the design was not updated
  - The area where the impact occurred was large enough for a vehicle to easily enter but was not a designated vehicle route and had no physical demarcation
  - OSHA subsequently cited Formosa for not requiring FRC, which Formosa contested
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Formosa Plastics USA: revise PHA, siting analysis, and PSSR procedures to more fully address vehicle impact, remote isolation, and passive fire protection; require FRC for all unit operators
  - Kellogg, Brown, and Root (KBR): revise plant design procedures to ensure current safety standards are applied to reused designs; notify other clients with similar Olefins units
  - CCPS (AIChE): add vehicular traffic protection and remote equipment isolation to Guidelines for Hazard Evaluation Procedures
- PSM elements implicated: PHA, facility siting, mechanical integrity, inherently safer design, MOC, process safety information, operating procedures/safe operating limits, training
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A forklift driver hit a pipe and caused a leak that exploded.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): An outdated plant design that had never been updated for two decades of improved safety practices, combined with hazard reviews that failed to ask the right questions about vehicle impact and emergency isolation. The company recycled the design without recycling the lesson of improving it.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Physically survey your facility for process piping or valves that protrude into areas where vehicles, forklifts, or mobile equipment operate, and install pipe bollards or barriers.
  - Confirm that structural steel supporting critical safety lines (flare headers, relief valve discharge piping, emergency isolation valves) is fireproofed per API 2218.
  - Check that emergency remote isolation capability exists for large inventories of flammable liquids or gases, and verify that operators can initiate isolation from the control room without entering the affected area.

### Praxair St. Louis Flammable Gas Cylinder Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3522 | June 2006
- Company | Location | Industry: Praxair Distribution, Inc. | St. Louis, Missouri | Industrial and specialty gas distribution
- Incident date: June 24, 2005
- Hazard type: fire
- Materials: Propylene (liquefied gas in cylinders), propane, acetylene
- What happened: During a heat wave with air temperatures of 97 degrees F and intense direct sunlight, returned propylene cylinders in an outdoor storage area at Praxair's St. Louis filling and distribution facility were heated until a cylinder's relief device opened, venting propylene. The gas ignited (most likely from static discharge) at approximately 3:20 pm, starting a fire that spread to adjacent cylinders by radiant heat. Within minutes, cylinders began exploding and rocketing out of the facility into a surrounding residential and commercial neighborhood. Firefighters could not actively fight the fire because of rocketing cylinders; they established a five-block perimeter until the flammable gas was consumed over about five hours.
- Consequences: 1 community fatality (asthma attack triggered by smoke and fumes, attributed by medical examiner), no facility worker fatalities, extensive community property damage, cylinders and parts found up to 800 feet away, a burned-out commercial building, broken windows and structural damage to residences, asbestos from ruptured acetylene cylinders scattered over a one-third-mile by one-mile area; approximately 8,000 cylinders consumed
- Sequence: Heat wave plus direct sunlight heats returned propylene cylinders beyond relief valve set point, relief opens and vents propylene, ignition (probable static), fire spreads to adjacent cylinders by radiation, fire reaches propane and acetylene cylinders, cylinders rupture and rocket into community, fire consumed by burning gas inventory over 5 hours
- Immediate cause: Propylene cylinders overheated by direct sunlight and ambient heat, causing relief devices to open and vent fuel that ignited
- Contributing factors:
  - Propylene relief valves have a smaller margin between vapor pressure and minimum relief set point than propane cylinders, making propylene particularly susceptible to spontaneous venting on hot days (propylene valves may open at as low as 149 degrees F; propane valves at 158 degrees F)
  - Relief valves in service show variability in opening pressure and can open below the minimum CGA setpoint; repeated openings may reduce set pressure further
  - No fixed deluge system or fire monitors to cool cylinders in the event of a fire
  - No barriers between cylinder groups to contain a spreading fire and prevent domino effects
  - No area flammable gas detectors to provide early warning of a venting cylinder before ignition
  - Similar incidents had occurred at Air Liquide (Phoenix 1997), Airgas (Tulsa 2003), and Praxair (Fresno 2005), indicating a recurring industry pattern without corrective systemic action
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Industry standards for propylene CG-7 relief valves did not provide an adequate margin between normal vapor pressure and minimum relief set point to prevent spontaneous venting under foreseeable summertime temperatures
  - Gas repackaging facilities handling propylene did not universally adopt best practices for fire mitigation, barriers, and gas detection that could have limited the incident
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No deluge or fixed fire suppression to cool cylinders once a fire started
  - No barriers to contain cylinders or limit fire spread
  - No gas detectors to alarm before ignition
  - Relief device opening pressure margin insufficient for high-temperature outdoor storage of propylene
- Key findings:
  - Propylene fires at gas repackaging plants have occurred with notable frequency despite much lower propylene cylinder counts versus propane; the physics of propylene relief valve design explains this pattern
  - Three nearly identical prior incidents (1997, 2003, 2005) preceded this event with no industry-wide corrective action
  - Firefighting was effectively impossible once rocketing cylinders began, underscoring the need for fixed automated suppression as the primary line of defense
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Compressed Gas Association (CGA): communicate best practices for cylinder storage to member gas repackaging facilities; revise CG-7 relief valve standards for propylene to require a greater margin between vapor pressure and minimum set point (comparable to propane), and require valves to tolerate multiple openings within setpoint tolerance or provide an indicator when a valve has operated
- PSM elements implicated: process safety information, PHA, mechanical integrity, facility siting, emergency planning, inherently safer design
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Cylinders were left in the sun and got too hot.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A propylene-specific relief valve design standard that provided less margin than propane, combined with the industry's failure to learn from three prior nearly identical events. The facility had no engineering barriers to slow fire spread once the first cylinder vented, so a single cylinder failure cascaded to thousands.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - For outdoor propylene or LPG cylinder storage areas, verify that fixed cooling water (deluge or monitor) is installed and that there are physical barriers separating cylinder groups to limit fire spread.
  - Review relief valve maintenance records for propylene cylinders; valves that have operated previously should be replaced or recertified before returning to service.
  - Confirm that your emergency response plan for an outdoor gas cylinder fire specifically addresses the danger of rocketing cylinders and the appropriate standoff distance for firefighters.

### Marcus Oil and Chemical Tank Explosion and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3523 | June 2006
- Company | Location | Industry: Marcus Oil and Chemical | Houston, Texas | Polyethylene wax processing
- Incident date: December 3, 2004
- Hazard type: overpressure / mechanical failure
- Materials: Polyethylene wax and extracted hydrocarbons (including hexane); oxygen-contaminated nitrogen inerting gas; compressed air
- What happened: Marcus Oil processed polyethylene wax using nitrogen to protect product quality and to pressurize tanks for wax transfer. When the onsite nitrogen generator could not keep pace with demand, operators installed a permanent connection between the compressed air system and the nitrogen distribution system, not recognizing that compressed air would contaminate the nitrogen with up to 18 percent oxygen. Tank 7, a 50,000-pound pressure vessel that had been improperly altered (a 24-inch opening had been cut into the head for steam pipe installation, then welded closed using a defective weld that fused less than 25 percent of the plate thickness), failed catastrophically at its operating pressure of 67 psig. The defective weld separated, rapidly depressurizing the tank; sparks from the plate striking concrete ignited the ejected wax and hydrocarbon vapor, the flame flashed back into the oxygen-enriched tank, and an internal deflagration propelled the 50,000-pound vessel 150 feet into an adjacent warehouse. The warehouse and nearby equipment caught fire, burning for nearly seven hours.
- Consequences: 3 firefighters slightly injured, local residents sustained minor injuries from flying glass and debris, structural damage and glass breakage up to 0.25 miles away, 120-pound steel plate found 900 feet away, a 20-pound plate embedded in a building wall 300 feet from the property line, significant facility damage
- Sequence: Operators connect compressed air to nitrogen system to maintain transfer pressure, nitrogen in storage vessels reaches 18 percent oxygen, Tank 7 (with defective weld) pressurized to 67 psig, weld fails, tank rapidly depressurizes, ejected wax and hydrocarbon vapor ignite from sparks, flame flashes back, internal deflagration propels 50,000-lb vessel 150 feet, large wax fire burns 7 hours
- Immediate cause: Catastrophic failure of a defective pressure vessel weld in the presence of an oxygen-enriched inerting gas, producing conditions for internal deflagration
- Contributing factors:
  - Tank 7 and three identical tanks had been altered (24-inch openings cut and welded closed) without following ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code or National Board Inspection Code (NB-23); welders were not qualified and no hydrostatic testing was performed after alteration
  - No manufacturer markings, design documentation, or safe operating pressure could be located for Tank 7 or three other vessels; the city demolished the fire-damaged building within 18 hours, destroying remaining records
  - None of the five wax processing tanks and neither nitrogen storage vessel was equipped with a pressure relief device as required by the ASME Code
  - Texas (unlike most states) had not adopted the ASME Code for pressure vessels or NB-23 for repairs and alterations, leaving Marcus Oil without a mandatory regulatory backstop
  - Management had not assessed the hazard of adding compressed air to the nitrogen inerting system; the modification was treated as a simple operational convenience
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - No management of change evaluation of the compressed air to nitrogen connection; operators and management were not aware that this action would produce an oxygen-enriched gas sufficient to support combustion
  - Pressure vessel alteration and repair were performed outside any recognized code or inspection regime
  - Texas's absence of pressure vessel regulations meant no third-party inspection at any point in the facility's life
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No pressure relief devices on process vessels or nitrogen storage tanks
  - Vessel alterations not inspected, tested, or certified
  - No oxygen monitoring or interlocks on the nitrogen distribution system
  - No MOC review of the nitrogen-to-air connection
  - Regulatory vacuum in Texas prevented any mandatory code compliance
- Key findings:
  - The CSB tested gas from the nitrogen storage vessel and found 18 percent oxygen (normal nitrogen inerting should be less than 8 percent oxygen); the generator output was 4 to 8 percent oxygen, but the compressed air connection raised the mixture well into the combustion-supporting range
  - Weld penetration was less than 25 percent of plate thickness, and contained excessive porosity; the weld would have failed a hydrostatic test at 150 percent of operating pressure
  - Six additional states beyond Texas do not require NB-23 repairs and alterations compliance; 11 states do not require ASME Code compliance for new vessels
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Marcus Oil: repair all altered pressure vessels to NB-23; install pressure relief devices on all vessels; train personnel on nitrogen system hazards and oxygen contamination risks
  - City of Houston: amend building ordinance to require NB-23 for pressure vessel alterations, not just new vessels; require ASME compliance for all new pressure vessels; enforce pre-operation inspections
- PSM elements implicated: process safety information, MOC, mechanical integrity, operating procedures/safe operating limits, PHA, training
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A tank blew up because it was made wrong and the air got into the nitrogen.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A facility operating with no formal safety management system, in a state with no pressure vessel regulations, made field modifications to vessels and piping without any engineering review. The combination of a defective vessel and an oxygen-contaminated inerting gas removed the two barriers that could have prevented the explosion independently.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Verify that any connection between an inert gas system and compressed air or any oxygen source is physically impossible (no valve, no coupling, no cross-connection) and document this as part of your process safety information.
  - Confirm that all pressure vessels have been fabricated and altered to a recognized code (ASME Section VIII) by qualified welders, and that post-repair hydrostatic test records exist and are retained.
  - Ensure that all pressure vessels above 15 psig are equipped with a properly sized, certified pressure relief device and that the device is included in your inspection and maintenance program.

### MFG Chemical Inc. Toxic Chemical Vapor Cloud Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3524 | April 2006
- Company | Location | Industry: MFG Chemical, Inc. | Dalton, Georgia | Specialty chemical manufacturing (toll manufacturing of triallyl cyanurate)
- Incident date: April 12, 2004
- Hazard type: reactive runaway
- Materials: Allyl alcohol (highly toxic and flammable), cyanuric chloride, triethylamine catalyst, allyl chloride (toxic reaction byproduct)
- What happened: MFG Chemical attempted its first production-scale batch of triallyl cyanurate (TAC), a specialty chemical crosslinking agent, having previously performed only small-scale laboratory trials. Operators added all reactants and the catalyst simultaneously to the reactor rather than controlling the addition sequence and rate. A runaway exothermic reaction occurred, causing the reactor to overpressure beyond its rupture disc, and releasing approximately 35,000 pounds of allyl alcohol and allyl chloride as a toxic vapor cloud into the community. More than 200 families were evacuated and 154 people, including 15 emergency responders, required decontamination and treatment. One MFG worker suffered chemical burns.
- Consequences: 1 worker with chemical burns, 154 people requiring decontamination and medical treatment (including 15 police and ambulance personnel), 200+ families evacuated, vapor cloud reached a nearby creek (environmental impact), extensive community disruption
- Sequence: All reactants and catalyst added at once, runaway reaction begins, reactor temperature and pressure exceed design, rupture disc activates, allyl alcohol and allyl chloride vent to atmosphere without scrubbing, toxic cloud spreads into community, emergency responders enter without adequate equipment and become exposed
- Immediate cause: Runaway exothermic reaction caused by simultaneous addition of all reactants and catalyst without rate control
- Contributing factors:
  - MFG did not conduct an adequate evaluation of reactive chemistry hazards before attempting the first production batch; published accident histories and technical literature on TAC synthesis were not reviewed
  - Small-scale laboratory tests used controlled addition sequences not replicated at production scale; the scale-up process did not include evaluation of how the larger batch would behave if addition order was changed
  - Lyondell Chemical (allyl alcohol supplier) did not clearly communicate to MFG that they would be subject to EPA Risk Management Program requirements before receiving the allyl alcohol shipment
  - GP Chemical (the process developer/buyer) and MFG did not share critical process safety information with each other; no toll manufacturing safety review was conducted
  - The emergency vent from the reactor discharged directly to atmosphere with no scrubber or containment
  - Whitfield County's emergency response plan lacked a shelter-in-place or effective evacuation plan and did not provide timely notification to residents
  - Emergency responders entered the toxic cloud without adequate personal protective equipment or air monitoring; the sole decontamination station was more than five miles from the evacuation zone perimeter
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - MFG did not develop the PSM standard hazard analysis, pre-startup review, or emergency response elements required by OSHA PSM and EPA RMP before initiating production
  - MFG and GP Chemical did not follow CCPS best practices for toll manufacturing safety, which require the process developer to verify that the toll manufacturer has addressed all hazards before production
  - Georgia had not established clear regulatory responsibility for oversight of EPCRA requirements and did not audit the Whitfield County Emergency Operations Plan
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No scrubber or containment on the reactor emergency vent
  - No PHA or pre-startup safety review conducted
  - Emergency response plan absent or inadequate at county and facility level
  - Process chemistry not fully characterized before scale-up
  - No controlled addition system to prevent simultaneous reactant addition
- Key findings:
  - Thermal stability studies commissioned before production showed that the reaction mixture could runaway under certain conditions, but the full implications for production-scale addition sequence control were not acted upon
  - GP Chemical had conducted small-scale trials but had not shared all process safety information with MFG
  - Lyondell's product stewardship documentation warned of RMP requirements but the message did not reach MFG management in a way that prompted action before the incident
  - The Dalton Fire Department incident command allowed inadequately protected MFG employees to reenter the toxic cloud; incident command also failed to direct all unprotected responders to maintain a safe distance
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - MFG Chemical: conduct full PSM PHA and pre-startup review before resuming TAC production; install scrubber on emergency vent; develop emergency response procedures with training; adopt CCPS toll manufacturing guidelines
  - GP Chemical: share complete process safety information with toll manufacturers; ensure PSM/RMP compliance before shipping hazardous materials to a toll facility
  - Lyondell Chemical: improve product stewardship process to verify RMP compliance of allyl alcohol recipients
  - City of Dalton and Whitfield County: develop shelter-in-place and evacuation plans; train and equip hazmat response teams; locate decontamination stations close to the hazard zone
  - State of Georgia: assign clear responsibility for EPCRA oversight and audit local emergency plans
  - Synthetic Organic Chemical Manufacturers Association: develop guidance for members on toll manufacturing safety responsibilities
- PSM elements implicated: PHA, process safety information, operating procedures/safe operating limits, training, contractor management, emergency planning, MOC, incident investigation
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers didn't know how to run the reaction and it got out of control.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A small company took on toll manufacturing of a reactive chemical without implementing any of the safety management system elements required by law. The process developer and raw material supplier did not verify that the manufacturer was ready. And when the release happened, emergency responders without adequate equipment amplified the casualty count by entering the hazard zone.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Before scaling up any reactive chemistry process from lab to production, conduct formal thermal hazard testing (accelerating rate calorimetry or differential scanning calorimetry) and verify that the production control system can replicate the controlled addition sequence used in the lab.
  - Any reactor emergency vent or rupture disc discharge should terminate in a scrubber, flare, or containment vessel; venting directly to atmosphere is not acceptable for toxic or reactive materials.
  - When engaging a toll manufacturer, verify that they have conducted a site-specific PHA and have adequate emergency response plans before the first shipment of a hazardous material.

### Sterigenics International Ethylene Oxide Explosion

- Archive id | Final report date: 3525 | March 2006
- Company | Location | Industry: Sterigenics International, Inc. | Ontario, California | Medical product sterilization services
- Incident date: August 19, 2004
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Ethylene oxide (EO), a flammable and highly toxic gas used for medical product sterilization; catalytic oxidizer (open-flame emissions control device)
- What happened: Maintenance personnel at Sterigenics's Ontario, California facility needed to access a sterilization chamber (Chamber 7) that had been used for sterilization and was still undergoing the aeration phase in which EO is vented and treated. To enter the chamber before the scheduled end of aeration, a maintenance supervisor used a password to override the computer safeguards controlling the sterilization cycle sequence, which prematurely opened the chamber door. This caused the ventilation system to evacuate EO from the chamber at high concentration to the catalytic oxidizer, an open-flame device used to comply with California air quality rules. The EO ignited in the oxidizer and the flame traveled back through ventilation ducting to the sterilizer chamber, where approximately 50 pounds of EO ignited and exploded. Four workers were injured by flying glass from control room windows; neither the control room nor its windows were designed to be blast resistant.
- Consequences: 4 workers injured (all from flying control room window glass), extensive facility damage, neighboring businesses evacuated, facility operations disrupted for 9 months, no fatalities
- Sequence: Maintenance supervisor uses password to bypass cycle safety interlock, chamber door opens prematurely, backvent system routes concentrated EO to catalytic oxidizer, EO ignites at oxidizer, flame travels back through ducting to sterilizer chamber, approximately 50 pounds of EO detonates, control room windows fail, glass injures 4 workers
- Immediate cause: Override of safety interlock permitted premature evacuation of concentrated EO to an open-flame oxidizer, causing ignition and flashback
- Contributing factors:
  - Maintenance supervisor did not understand that bypassing gas washes (the safety interlock) during a maintenance procedure on an empty chamber could lead to an explosion; he believed the chamber was sufficiently empty
  - Engineering controls (sensor interlocks) did not prevent an explosive EO concentration from reaching the oxidizer during maintenance bypass
  - Sterigenics management did not implement company-wide engineering control recommendations issued after prior similar incidents at other Sterigenics facilities
  - The Process Hazard Analysis at the Ontario facility had not identified any explosion scenarios involving the oxidizer; PHA revalidations addressed the potential narrowly but did not require additional engineering controls
  - California Air Resources Board (CARB) continued to require open-flame oxidizers for EO backvent treatment even after the federal EPA suspended the requirement in 2001 specifically to eliminate the explosion safety problem; no CARB requirement addressed safe design of these systems
  - The control room was not blast or shatter resistant, even though it was located adjacent to the sterilization area
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Engineering controls at the facility did not prevent an explosive concentration of EO from reaching the oxidizer, and management did not implement recommended engineering upgrades issued after prior incidents
  - The maintenance supervisor did not understand the explosion hazard associated with the bypass procedure; training had not communicated this specific risk
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Computer interlock bypassed by password to allow premature door opening
  - No EO concentration monitoring in the sterilizer chamber or oxidizer inlet to detect an explosive mixture before ignition
  - No post-ignition deflagration detection and damage control devices on the oxidizer
  - Control room windows not blast or shatter resistant
  - PHA did not identify oxidizer explosion scenario
- Key findings:
  - Sterigenics had received company-wide engineering control recommendations following earlier oxidizer-related incidents at its own facilities, but management did not implement them at Ontario
  - NIOSH and NFPA had issued guidance recommending chamber concentration monitoring connected to alarms and interlocks; this guidance was not followed at Ontario
  - Federal EPA suspended the NESHAP rule requiring EO backvent treatment in 2001 specifically because of explosion safety concerns with oxidizers; California continued to require the same oxidizers and four other states followed
  - The explosion would have caused fatalities if workers had been present in the sterilization area at the time
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Sterigenics International: audit all EO sterilization facilities using catalytic oxidizers; implement real-time EO concentration monitoring connected to alarms and interlocks; install deflagration detection and suppression on oxidizers; restrict password access to personnel with full process hazard training; redesign control room windows to be blast resistant
  - NFPA: revise NFPA 56F (EO sterilization facilities standard) to require concentration monitoring and oxidizer safety controls
  - NIOSH: publish guidance alerting the sterilization industry to the oxidizer explosion hazard
  - California Air Resources Board: require sterilization facilities to demonstrate that EO emission control devices are designed and operated to prevent explosions
- PSM elements implicated: PHA, process safety information, mechanical integrity, operating procedures/safe operating limits, training, MOC, incident investigation, emergency planning, facility siting
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A maintenance worker bypassed a safety system to open a chamber early and caused an explosion.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The company had received explicit engineering recommendations from its own prior incident reviews but had not implemented them. The maintenance supervisor had password access to override interlocks without the training needed to understand the explosion hazard that this created. The regulatory environment compelled use of an open-flame oxidizer without requiring safe design. Every layer was thin.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Restrict access to any interlock bypass (whether physical key or software password) to personnel who have been trained specifically on the hazard that the interlock is preventing; document this in your management of change and training records.
  - Any open-flame device (oxidizer, thermal oxidizer, flare) that receives process streams containing flammable gases should have a real-time concentration or combustibility monitor upstream that will shut off flow before an explosive mixture can reach the flame.
  - Review whether control rooms are sited and constructed to protect occupants from a credible explosion in adjacent process areas; blast-resistant windows and doors are a basic facility siting requirement where this risk exists.
