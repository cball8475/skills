## Case 3527 - Giant Industries, Jamestown Refinery

**Date:** April 8, 2004
**Location:** Jamestown, NM
**Facility type:** Petroleum refinery, HF alkylation unit
**Hazard type:** Flammable vapor / VCE
**Incident type:** Uncontrolled release during maintenance
**Regulatory programs triggered:** OSHA PSM, EPA RMP
**Casualties:** 0 killed, 6 injured
**Property damage:** Greater than $13 million
**CSB document type:** Case Study
**CSB case number:** 2004-09-I-NM

**Incident summary:** During planned maintenance on a pump in the HF alkylation unit, workers failed to verify that a manual plug valve was fully closed before breaking the pump seal. The valve collar had been repositioned incorrectly during an earlier repair, so the visible wrench position no longer corresponded to actual valve position. Operators used wrench orientation (perpendicular to pipe equals closed) as their only means of confirming isolation. With the valve actually open, breaking the pump connection released alkylate at approximately 150 psig and 350 degrees Fahrenheit. The hot liquid flashed and partially vaporized, igniting and injuring six workers. Lockout/tagout procedures were not effectively applied to verify double-block-and-bleed isolation prior to opening the pump.

**Root causes:** This is a Case Study and does not identify formal root causes. Key causal factors were: (1) the plug valve collar was repositioned without a management of change review, making the visual position indicator unreliable; (2) operators had no independent means to verify valve closure; (3) LOTO procedures did not require confirmed pressure-zero verification; and (4) breakdown maintenance was the norm, with 23 pump seal work orders submitted in the prior year alone, suggesting the pump's repeated failure was never addressed systematically.

**Contributing causes:** No formal contributing causes assigned. Relevant factors include inadequate pre-task hazard review, reliance on a single unreliable indicator for valve position, and absence of a safe work permit system that required a second verification step.

**Key PSM failures:** Management of change (valve collar repositioning undocumented), mechanical integrity (pump with repeated seal failures not investigated), operating procedures (no requirement to verify zero energy before breaking containment), and incident investigation (prior pump failures did not trigger corrective action).

**Recommendations summary:** Giant Industries was advised to implement a formal MOC program for all equipment changes, require physical pressure verification before breaking any pressurized system, and address the root causes of recurring pump seal failures through a formal mechanical integrity program.

**Regulatory violations noted:** No citations listed in the Case Study. OSHA PSM compliance gaps identified in MOC, mechanical integrity, and LOTO elements.

**Lessons for practitioners:** Visual position indicators on plug valves can be repositioned and made unreliable without triggering any alarm. Any maintenance procedure that breaks containment on a pressurized, hazardous-material line must require confirmed zero-energy verification through direct measurement, not visual inference.

## Case 3530 - CTA Acoustics

**Date:** February 20, 2003
**Location:** Corbin, KY
**Facility type:** Manufacturing, automotive acoustic insulation (fiberglass and phenolic resin binder)
**Hazard type:** Combustible dust
**Incident type:** Dust explosion, multiple secondary explosions
**Regulatory programs triggered:** None (SIC code exclusion from OSHA PSM; facility not covered by EPA RMP)
**Casualties:** 7 killed, 37 injured
**Property damage:** Severe; plant largely destroyed
**CSB document type:** Investigation Report
**CSB case number:** 2003-09-I-KY

**Incident summary:** An oven temperature controller on Line 405 malfunctioned, causing the oven to overheat. Workers opened the oven doors to investigate, and combustible phenolic resin-coated fiberglass material inside ignited. The initial fire spread outside the oven and ignited a dust cloud in the work area, producing a fireball and pressure wave. Secondary dust explosions then propagated through the building, killing seven workers and injuring 37. Phenolic resin dust had accumulated on overhead structural steel, above ceiling tiles, and on equipment surfaces throughout the production area. Borden Chemical supplied the phenolic resin binder and had data indicating its explosive properties but did not communicate that hazard to CTA.

**Root causes:** (1) CTA management did not establish programs to identify, evaluate, or control combustible dust hazards; (2) CTA's cleaning procedures were inadequate to prevent dangerous dust accumulation on elevated surfaces; (3) CTA's incident investigation process failed to identify combustible dust as a hazard after prior fires in the facility; (4) Borden Chemical did not warn CTA of the explosive potential of the resin dust generated during production; (5) building design (open overhead structure with many dust-collection surfaces, no suppression) allowed dust to accumulate and secondary explosions to propagate.

**Contributing causes:** Absence of a dust hazard analysis, lack of housekeeping standards for elevated surfaces, no suppression or explosion venting in production areas, and an inadequate MSDS that omitted explosion hazard data.

**Key PSM failures:** Hazard identification, process hazard analysis, mechanical integrity (oven temperature controller), operating procedures, and management of change. No formal PSM program existed.

**Recommendations summary:** CTA was directed to implement a comprehensive combustible dust management program. Borden Chemical was directed to revise its MSDS and proactively communicate explosion hazard data to customers. CSB issued a Safety Bulletin on combustible dust hazards.

**Regulatory violations noted:** No OSHA general industry standard specifically covered combustible dust explosion prevention at the time. OSHA subsequently issued a combustible dust National Emphasis Program.

**Lessons for practitioners:** Supplier hazard communication gaps can be fatal. Customers using reactive or flammable powders or residues must independently assess explosion potential and not rely solely on MSDS data. Prior fires or near-misses involving dust must trigger a formal dust explosion hazard analysis.

## Case 3531 - West Pharmaceutical Services

**Date:** January 29, 2003
**Location:** Kinston, NC
**Facility type:** Pharmaceutical rubber component manufacturing
**Hazard type:** Combustible dust
**Incident type:** Dust explosion, collapse of suspended ceiling
**Regulatory programs triggered:** None (facility not covered under OSHA PSM; EPA RMP not applicable)
**Casualties:** 6 killed, 38 injured (including 2 firefighters)
**Property damage:** Severe; production building destroyed
**CSB document type:** Investigation Report
**CSB case number:** 2003-07-I-NC

**Incident summary:** West Pharmaceutical Services used a polyethylene-based antitack agent in a dip process to prevent rubber stoppers from sticking together. The dip process generated fine polyethylene dust that was drawn by ventilation into the space above a 10.5-foot suspended acoustic tile ceiling in the production building. Over time, a large quantity of dust accumulated in that concealed space. On January 29, 2003, an ignition event (never definitively identified) ignited the dust above the ceiling. The resulting explosion destroyed the suspended ceiling and the production building, killing six workers and injuring 38, including two firefighters who responded to the scene.

**Root causes:** (1) West's engineering assessment processes did not identify that the antitack agent would generate explosible dust accumulations above the suspended ceiling; (2) West's management systems did not include periodic hazard reviews to identify new or changing process hazards; (3) West did not review the MSDS for the polyethylene antitack agent to determine whether it was combustible; (4) West did not communicate combustible dust hazards to employees even after a prior fire involving the antitack dust.

**Contributing causes:** Suspended ceiling created a concealed space that was not routinely inspected or cleaned. Ventilation system directed dust-laden air into the concealed space. No explosion protection or suppression was installed above the ceiling.

**Key PSM failures:** Process hazard analysis, management systems for ongoing hazard identification, MSDS review, employee hazard communication, and housekeeping program design.

**Recommendations summary:** West was directed to conduct a combustible dust PHA, develop housekeeping standards for concealed spaces, and revise chemical hazard communication programs. CSB expanded its combustible dust Safety Bulletin to include this incident.

**Regulatory violations noted:** No specific OSHA standard covered concealed-space combustible dust at the time.

**Lessons for practitioners:** Ventilation systems that move process air into concealed spaces can accumulate hazardous quantities of combustible dust invisibly. Any antitack, release, or coating agent used in powder or aerosol form must be evaluated for combustible dust explosion potential before use. Suspended ceilings in manufacturing environments must be treated as confined spaces requiring inspection and cleaning.

## Case 3532 - Isotec (Sigma-Aldrich subsidiary)

**Date:** September 21, 2003
**Location:** Miami Township, OH
**Facility type:** Specialty gas and isotopically labeled compound manufacturer
**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway
**Incident type:** Liquid nitric oxide detonation inside vacuum-jacketed piping
**Regulatory programs triggered:** OSHA PSM, EPA RMP
**Casualties:** 0 killed, 1 injured, 2,000+ community residents evacuated
**Property damage:** Significant structural damage; large CO storage vessel displaced
**CSB document type:** Case Study
**CSB case number:** 2004-11-I-OH

**Incident summary:** Isotec produced liquid nitric oxide (NO) packaged in cylinders. On September 21, 2003, a leak developed in the process piping inside the vacuum-jacketed transfer line. Operators discovered a gaseous NO release and closed an isolation valve to stop the leak. This action trapped liquid NO inside the vacuum-jacketed annular space. Liquid NO at elevated pressure is a powerful oxidizer and can detonate. The confined liquid detonated, producing a blast that launched a fragment into a nearby 52,000-pound liquid CO storage vessel, displacing it off its supports. The explosion prompted evacuation of more than 2,000 residents in the surrounding area, which had transitioned from farmland to a residential neighborhood with more than 500 homes adjacent to the facility.

**Root causes:** This is a Case Study and does not assign formal root causes. Key causal factors were: (1) no adequate PHA had been conducted on the liquid NO transfer system, including the hazard of liquid NO accumulation inside the vacuum jacket; (2) three prior NO detonation incidents at Isotec had not been fully investigated or resolved; (3) land use changes had placed a large residential population adjacent to the facility without updated emergency planning; and (4) facility emergency planning did not reflect the scale or complexity of off-site hazard consequences.

**Contributing causes:** Isolation valve procedure created a trap condition for liquid NO. Emergency response planning had not kept pace with community growth around the facility.

**Key PSM failures:** Process hazard analysis (liquid NO detonation not identified as a credible scenario), incident investigation (three prior detonations not corrected), emergency planning, and facility siting.

**Recommendations summary:** Sigma-Aldrich and Isotec were advised to conduct a comprehensive PHA including liquid NO confinement scenarios, correct all outstanding findings from prior incident investigations, and update community emergency response plans in coordination with local authorities.

**Regulatory violations noted:** PSM and RMP compliance gaps identified in PHA and incident investigation elements.

**Lessons for practitioners:** When isolating a release of a reactive cryogenic liquid, the act of closing a valve can create a more dangerous trapped condition than the original leak. Prior incidents at the same facility involving the same substance must be fully resolved before the next incident.

## Case 3533 - Technic Inc.

**Date:** February 7, 2003
**Location:** Cranston, RI
**Facility type:** Metal finishing and electroplating chemical manufacturer
**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway
**Incident type:** Explosion inside a ventilation duct from reactive silver compound deposits
**Regulatory programs triggered:** OSHA PSM (silver compounds above threshold), EPA RMP
**Casualties:** 0 killed, 1 critically injured
**Property damage:** Significant duct and building damage
**CSB document type:** Investigation Report
**CSB case number:** 2003-10-I-RI

**Incident summary:** Technic used a vacuum collection system (VCS) to exhaust vapors from multiple production processes sharing a common duct. The duct collected vapors from silver cyanide and silver nitrate plating processes as well as organic solvent vapors. Over time, explosive silver compounds (likely silver acetylide or silver nitride) deposited inside the duct. On February 7, 2003, an employee attempting to clear a blockage tapped the duct wall with a hammer. The mechanical shock detonated the sensitive silver compound deposits inside the duct. The explosion critically injured the employee and damaged the building. Silver compounds are known to form shock-sensitive secondary explosive deposits when silver ions are exposed to acetylene, ammonia, or other reactive vapors.

**Root causes:** (1) Technic did not conduct a process safety review when the VCS was designed and installed; the engineering hazard of combining silver compound vapors with organic vapors in a common duct was never evaluated. (2) Technic had no management of change process; when the VCS was later modified (additional connections, flow changes), no safety review was triggered.

**Contributing causes:** Equipment was allowed to degrade (blockage formed) without a scheduled inspection and cleaning program for the ducts. Workers had no documented procedure for clearing duct blockages.

**Key PSM failures:** Process hazard analysis (reactive deposits in shared ventilation not evaluated), management of change (no MOC for VCS additions or modifications), mechanical integrity (no inspection or cleaning schedule for duct system), and operating procedures (no safe procedure for duct blockage clearance).

**Recommendations summary:** Technic was directed to implement a PSM program with PHA for all processes, develop an MOC system, establish a duct inspection and cleaning schedule, and develop written procedures for hazardous maintenance tasks.

**Regulatory violations noted:** OSHA PSM compliance deficiencies in PHA, MOC, mechanical integrity, and operating procedures.

**Lessons for practitioners:** Common exhaust ducts that combine vapors from incompatible chemical processes can accumulate shock-sensitive or explosive deposits that are invisible from outside. Any shared ventilation system in a chemical facility must be evaluated for reactive deposit formation, especially where silver, copper, or other transition metals are combined with organic or nitrogen-containing vapors.

## Case 3534 - D.D. Williamson and Company

**Date:** April 11, 2003
**Location:** Louisville, KY
**Facility type:** Caramel color production (food ingredient manufacturer)
**Hazard type:** Overpressure / mechanical failure
**Incident type:** Feed tank explosion, aqua ammonia release
**Regulatory programs triggered:** OSHA PSM (aqua ammonia above threshold), EPA RMP
**Casualties:** 1 killed, multiple others at risk from ammonia release
**Property damage:** Severe; production building heavily damaged
**CSB document type:** Investigation Report
**CSB case number:** 2003-11-I-KY

**Incident summary:** A 2,200-gallon stainless steel feed tank, fabricated in 1977, was used to hold caramel color liquid that was fed to a spray dryer. The tank had no ASME pressure vessel rating and no pressure relief device. On April 11, 2003, caramel color liquid in the tank overheated. A 1-inch vent valve plugged with hardened caramel, preventing pressure relief. The tank failed catastrophically, killing one employee who was struck by the blast. The explosion also ruptured a line to a 26,000-pound aqua ammonia tank, releasing ammonia into the facility and surrounding area. The tank had been in continuous service for 26 years without any formal pressure vessel inspection or documentation of its design limits.

**Root causes:** (1) D.D. Williamson had no formal engineering programs to identify and meet applicable pressure vessel requirements for equipment in service; (2) the company had no hazard analysis systems to identify the overpressure scenario in the spray dryer feed process; (3) no operating procedures or training addressed the hazards of overheating viscous materials in a sealed vessel without relief protection.

**Contributing causes:** The vent valve design was susceptible to plugging with the process material. No high-temperature alarm or interlock was in place to alert operators or shut down heating before pressure could build. The adjacent aqua ammonia system compounded the consequences of the vessel failure.

**Key PSM failures:** Process hazard analysis, mechanical integrity (no inspection, no ASME certification, no relief device), operating procedures, training, and process safety information (no documented design basis for the vessel).

**Recommendations summary:** D.D. Williamson was directed to audit all pressure-containing equipment for code compliance and relief protection, implement a formal PHA program, and develop written operating procedures including limits and emergency responses for all spray dryer feed systems.

**Regulatory violations noted:** OSHA PSM compliance deficiencies in process safety information, PHA, mechanical integrity, and operating procedures. Possible ASME and local boiler and pressure vessel code violations.

**Lessons for practitioners:** Pressure vessels without relief devices are fundamentally unsafe regardless of the material they contain. Viscous or high-solids fluids can plug relief paths, eliminating the last line of defense. Any vessel that can be heated must have a documented maximum allowable working pressure, a functioning relief device, and a high-temperature shutdown interlock.

## Case 3535 - Third Coast Industries

**Date:** May 1, 2002
**Location:** Friendswood, TX (unincorporated Harris County)
**Facility type:** Petroleum product repackaging and blending (lubricants, solvents, automotive products)
**Hazard type:** Flammable vapor / VCE
**Incident type:** Fire and explosions, total facility loss
**Regulatory programs triggered:** OSHA PSM (flammable liquids above threshold), EPA RMP
**Casualties:** 0 killed, 0 injured (facility was closed for the holiday)
**Property damage:** Total loss of facility
**CSB document type:** Investigation Report
**CSB case number:** 2002-04-I-TX

**Incident summary:** On May 1, 2002 (a holiday), a fire started at an unknown ignition source near packaging line 4. Because the facility was unoccupied, the fire was not discovered until it had grown beyond control. The fire propagated through the facility via drum ruptures, failure of a large blend tank, and failure of a tank wagon. Burning liquid spread across the floor and outside the building. No automatic suppression system was installed. The entire facility was destroyed. The facility was located in an unincorporated area of Harris County with no adopted fire code requiring fire protection for flammable liquid storage or processing.

**Root causes:** (1) Third Coast did not conduct a fire protection analysis and did not recognize its exposure to total-loss fire scenarios; (2) the facility lacked an adequate automatic fire suppression system capable of controlling a flammable liquid fire; (3) the facility had no containment dikes, spill control, or drainage systems to prevent burning liquid from spreading.

**Contributing causes:** Location in an unincorporated area with no applicable fire code removed regulatory pressure to install fire protection. No fire detection system was in place to provide early warning. The facility operated without a formal process hazard analysis.

**Key PSM failures:** Process hazard analysis (fire spread scenarios not evaluated), mechanical integrity (no suppression), facility siting and fire protection, and emergency planning.

**Recommendations summary:** Third Coast was advised to install automatic suppression, provide secondary containment for flammable liquid areas, and conduct a PHA. CSB also recommended that Harris County adopt a fire code for unincorporated areas.

**Regulatory violations noted:** OSHA PSM compliance deficiencies. Harris County had no fire code covering flammable liquid facilities in unincorporated areas.

**Lessons for practitioners:** Facilities located outside incorporated municipal boundaries may face no enforceable fire code requirements. PSM-covered facilities with flammable liquids must implement fire protection independent of local code enforcement. A fire that starts during off-hours in an unmonitored facility with no suppression will almost certainly result in total loss.

## Case 3536 - Catalyst Systems

**Date:** January 2, 2003
**Location:** Gnadenhutten, OH
**Facility type:** Organic peroxide production (benzoyl peroxide, BPO)
**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway
**Incident type:** Thermal decomposition of BPO during vacuum drying
**Regulatory programs triggered:** OSHA PSM (organic peroxide above threshold)
**Casualties:** 0 killed, 1 minor injury
**Property damage:** Dryer destroyed; building damage
**CSB document type:** Case Study
**CSB case number:** 2003-01-I-OH

**Incident summary:** Catalyst Systems produced benzoyl peroxide (BPO), an organic peroxide used as a polymerization initiator and bleaching agent. On January 2, 2003, operators used a double-cone vacuum dryer to dry approximately 200 pounds of 98 percent BPO. The dryer had been purchased in 2001. The dryer's temperature controller was set using the self-accelerating decomposition temperature (SADT) data for 1-pound bags of BPO as the safety limit, not the SADT for a 200-pound batch. Because SADT decreases with increasing mass (larger batches are more prone to runaway), the temperature limit was set far too high for the quantity being processed. The BPO likely overheated and underwent runaway thermal decomposition, destroying the dryer and injuring one employee.

**Root causes:** This is a Case Study and does not assign formal root causes. Key causal factors were: (1) no PSM program existed at the facility despite processing quantities of BPO exceeding OSHA PSM thresholds; (2) no PHA had been conducted for the drying process; (3) no reactive hazard evaluation had been performed for BPO, including recognition that SADT is mass-dependent; (4) no prestartup safety review was conducted when the dryer was purchased and put into service; (5) no preventive maintenance program covered the dryer; (6) no written operating procedures specified safe temperature limits for specific batch sizes.

**Contributing causes:** Vendor documentation and operating instructions did not clearly address SADT dependence on batch size. No reactive chemicals expertise was available or sought.

**Key PSM failures:** Every element. The facility operated outside PSM compliance despite being covered by the standard.

**Recommendations summary:** Catalyst Systems was directed to develop a full PSM program, conduct a PHA for BPO drying, evaluate SADT for all processing scales, implement written procedures and training, and establish a prestartup review process for new equipment.

**Regulatory violations noted:** Failure to implement OSHA PSM program across all required elements.

**Lessons for practitioners:** SADT is not a fixed property of a chemical; it depends on quantity, geometry, and heat transfer. Safety limits derived from small-scale data cannot be applied directly to large-batch operations. Any facility processing organic peroxides above OSHA PSM thresholds must have a reactive hazard evaluation and a PSM program in place before operations begin.

## Case 3538 - Kaltech Industries

**Date:** April 25, 2002
**Location:** Manhattan, NY
**Facility type:** Furniture refinishing and manufacturing
**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway
**Incident type:** Incompatible chemical waste reaction, drum explosion in confined basement
**Regulatory programs triggered:** EPA hazardous waste regulations (RCRA); OSHA Hazard Communication Standard
**Casualties:** 0 killed, 36 injured
**Property damage:** Damage to a 10-story occupied residential building
**CSB document type:** Investigation Report
**CSB case number:** 2002-02-I-NY

**Incident summary:** Kaltech Industries occupied the basement and first floor of a 10-story residential building in Manhattan. Workers were consolidating waste solvents and chemicals from smaller containers into a 55-gallon drum. An unlabeled metal carboy was included in the consolidation; it contained concentrated nitric acid, a strong oxidizer. Lacquer thinner, a flammable organic solvent, was also added. The mixing of concentrated nitric acid with organic solvent initiated a vigorous chemical reaction, generating heat, gas, and vapor. Pressure built inside the sealed drum until it failed explosively. The blast in the confined basement space injured 36 people, some in the residential floors above, and caused structural damage throughout the building.

**Root causes:** (1) Kaltech had no hazard communication program; chemical containers were not labeled, workers had no training on chemical hazards, and there was no system to prevent mixing of incompatible materials. (2) Kaltech had no hazardous waste management program meeting EPA RCRA requirements; waste was stored and consolidated without tracking, labeling, or compatibility evaluation.

**Contributing causes:** The unlabeled container was the proximate enabling condition. The confined basement geometry amplified the overpressure effects. Outdated New York City fire codes did not adequately address chemical storage and waste handling in mixed-use buildings. Inspections by the Fire Department and other agencies had not identified the hazardous conditions.

**Key PSM failures:** PSM did not apply (furniture refinishing is not a covered process). Applicable regulations were OSHA HazCom and EPA RCRA, both of which were violated.

**Recommendations summary:** Kaltech was directed to implement a HazCom program and RCRA-compliant waste management. CSB recommended that New York City update fire and chemical safety codes for mixed-use buildings and that regulatory agencies improve coordination of inspections for chemical hazards in occupied buildings.

**Regulatory violations noted:** OSHA Hazard Communication Standard violations (no labels, no SDS access, no training). EPA RCRA violations (unlawful storage and handling of hazardous waste).

**Lessons for practitioners:** Unlabeled chemical containers in workplaces that handle multiple products are a direct path to catastrophic incompatible mixing. Any consolidation of chemical waste streams requires a compatibility check before combining. Businesses operating in occupied residential buildings face off-site consequence risks that go far beyond the workplace itself.
