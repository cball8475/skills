## Case 3540 - BLSR Operating Vapor Cloud Fire

**Incident date:** January 13, 2003
**Location:** Rosharon, Texas
**Industry:** Oil and gas E&P waste disposal
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2003-06-I-TX, September 2003

**Hazard type:** Flammable vapor, vapor cloud fire
**Materials involved:** BS&W (basic sediment and water) containing gas condensate (benzene, toluene, xylenes), diesel engines as ignition sources
**Outcome:** 3 killed (2 immediately, 1 died March 2), 4 seriously injured, 2 vacuum trucks destroyed, unloading pad heavily damaged

**PSM tags:** hazard communication, process safety information, contractor management, mechanical integrity (ignition source control), incident investigation

**Easy story:**
Two vacuum trucks arrived at the BLSR mud disposal pad to unload BS&W collected from Noble Energy gas condensate storage tanks. The BS&W was classified on manifests as oilfield waste, but it contained flammable hydrocarbon condensate that nobody in the chain, not Noble Energy, not the hauler T&L, not BLSR, had identified or communicated as a flammable material. When the drivers opened the drain valves, liquid splashed across the open pad and hydrocarbon vapors began accumulating. One truck's diesel engine ingested the vapors and began to race uncontrollably. The resulting deflagration and pool fire engulfed both trucks and the surrounding area.

**Systemic reframe:**
The hazard was invisible to everyone because no party in a three-company chain applied basic hazard identification to the material being moved. Noble Energy characterized the BS&W based on its origin (tank bottoms), not its actual composition. T&L hauled it without requiring an MSDS or checking flammability. BLSR accepted it without any safe-handling requirements. Running diesel engines sat feet away from open liquid unloading with no recognition that they were ignition sources. The three fatalities were the predictable end of a complete absence of process safety information flowing through a multi-company operation.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- Do manifests and MSDSs for all incoming waste streams accurately reflect the actual chemical composition, including flammable or reactive components mixed in during collection?
- Do diesel-powered vehicles (vacuum trucks, compressors) have a written protocol for shutting down before valve opening during liquid unloading?
- Is there a contractor/transporter requirement that hazard communication documents must be received and reviewed before material is accepted at the facility?
- Are disposal facility workers trained to recognize signs of diesel engine vapor ingestion (racing RPM) and to treat it as an immediate emergency?

## Case 3543 - BP Amoco Polymers Thermal Decomposition

**Incident date:** March 13, 2001
**Location:** Augusta, Georgia
**Industry:** Polymer manufacturing
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2001-03-I-GA, June 2002

**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway (thermal decomposition), pressure vessel failure
**Materials involved:** Amodel polyphthalamide plastic (partially reacted), hot oil at 370 degrees C
**Outcome:** 3 killed, vessel cover blown off, hot plastic ejected up to 70 feet, fire requiring hours to extinguish

**PSM tags:** PHA, process safety information, operating procedures, MOC, incident investigation, mechanical integrity

**Easy story:**
A startup at the Augusta nylon plastics plant was aborted 12 hours before the explosion, sending an unusually large amount of partially reacted Amodel polymer into a catch tank (KD-502). Workers returned the next morning to open the tank for cleaning. What they did not know was that the plastic had been thermally decomposing inside the sealed vessel, producing gas that foamed the material and pressurized the space above it. All vents were plugged by the expanded plastic. When workers removed roughly half of the 44 cover bolts, the built-up pressure blew the cover violently off the tank, killing all three workers.

**Systemic reframe:**
The engineering hazard, that hot partially reacted Amodel can decompose and generate pressure if confined, was known in Amoco's research organization but never made it into a formal process hazard analysis or equipment opening procedure. Near-miss events, including a prior polymer pod bursting and waste plastic fires, had each been treated as isolated nuisances rather than signals of an uncontrolled reactive hazard. The procedure for opening the catch tank assumed the vessel would be at atmospheric pressure; no one had designed a safe method for confirming that assumption before breaking containment. When the diversion time for the aborted run was extended from 30 to 50 minutes without a formal MOC review, the amount of reactive material sent to the tank increased substantially, pushing the system into failure territory.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- Do equipment opening procedures for any vessel that contains reactive or thermally sensitive material include a verified step to confirm pressure absence before any bolts are removed?
- Are near-miss events, such as unexpected foaming, container bulging, or waste fires, captured in a formal incident investigation system that looks for reactive hazard signals?
- Has a systematic reactivity hazard review been applied to all processes that handle partially reacted intermediates or materials with known decomposition temperatures?
- When batch times or charge quantities change, does the MOC process include evaluation of thermal runaway potential?

## Case 3544 - Concept Sciences Hydroxylamine Explosion

**Incident date:** February 19, 1999
**Location:** Hanover Township, Lehigh County, Pennsylvania
**Industry:** Specialty chemical manufacturing
**Report:** CSB Report No. 1999-13-C-PA, March 2002 (Case Study, not a full Investigation Report; case studies do not include formal root cause analysis or safety recommendations)

**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway (explosive decomposition)
**Materials involved:** Hydroxylamine (HA) free base solution at elevated concentration, potassium sulfate, potassium hydroxide
**Outcome:** 5 killed (4 CSI employees, 1 adjacent business manager), 14 injured, 10 buildings damaged in Lehigh Valley Industrial Park, blast equivalent to roughly 700 lbs TNT

**PSM tags:** PHA, process safety information, facility siting, MOC, mechanical integrity (safeguard implementation)

**Easy story:**
Concept Sciences (CSI) was producing its first commercial batch of 50 wt-percent hydroxylamine solution using vacuum distillation. HA is a reactive chemical with a known explosive hazard above 70 wt-percent, a threshold stated in CSI's own MSDS. During the distillation, the HA concentration in the charge tank climbed to 86 wt-percent because the process design routinely drove concentration well past the hazard threshold. The still was shut down at 7:45 pm to clean crystals from the condenser. Twenty-nine minutes later, the highly concentrated HA in the charge tank explosively decomposed, destroying the facility and damaging ten surrounding buildings in a mixed-use industrial park.

**Systemic reframe:**
The most important fact in this case is that CSI knew the 70 percent explosive threshold, documented it in their MSDS, and then designed a commercial process that regularly exceeded it. A one-page "What If" PHA was the only hazard review performed, and it did not address crystal formation, high concentrations, or the gap between the process design maximum and the known hazard threshold. Safeguards identified even in that limited review, including remote controls, blowout walls, and shielding, were never installed. The facility sat in a multi-tenant light industrial park next to other businesses, dramatically amplifying the consequences of a reactive chemical process that had no adequate containment or siting buffer.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- For any reactive chemical process, is there a written comparison between process operating concentrations or temperatures and the known hazard thresholds (decomposition onset, explosive limits)?
- Have safeguards identified in the PHA actually been installed and verified, with a tracking mechanism to close open items before startup?
- Is the facility siting appropriate for the worst-case energy release of the chemicals being processed, considering neighboring occupancies?
- Does the PHA address what happens when normal operations (such as crystal formation or batch variation) push concentrations toward hazard boundaries?

## Case 3545 - Bethlehem Steel COG Condensate Fire

**Incident date:** February 2, 2001
**Location:** Burns Harbor, Indiana (Lake Michigan shore)
**Industry:** Integrated steel manufacturing
**Report:** CSB Report No. 2001-02-I-IN, January 2002

**Hazard type:** Flammable liquid fire
**Materials involved:** Coke oven gas (COG) condensate (benzene 22.4%, toluene 16.8%, xylenes 13.7%, flash point 29 degrees F), coke oven gas
**Outcome:** 2 killed (1 Bethlehem Steel millwright, 1 Onyx contractor supervisor), 4 injured

**PSM tags:** mechanical integrity, operating procedures, MOC, incident investigation, contractor management, process safety information

**Easy story:**
Workers at the Burns Harbor coke plant set out to remove a cracked 10-inch valve from a coke oven gas deadleg, a section of piping connected to the main COG header but no longer in service since a furnace was decommissioned in 1992. What they did not know was that pipe insulation removed during a 1999 demolition project had never been replaced. Without insulation, the drain lines in the COG piping froze during the winter of 2000-2001, preventing condensate from draining. Flammable hydrocarbon condensate, with a flash point of 29 degrees F, had been accumulating in the dead piping system. Three warning events in January 2001, including COG releases and condensate encounters, were not communicated to the maintenance crew assigned to the February 2 valve removal job. When flammable condensate released during the removal, it ignited from an infrared heat lamp or space heater being used for warmth, engulfing the work area.

**Systemic reframe:**
A 1992 decommissioning left a deadleg connected to a live hazardous service. A 1999 demolition project removed insulation without recognizing the thermal protection it provided to freeze-sensitive drain lines. Each change degraded the safety state of the system incrementally, and no management of change review caught either degradation. Three workers encountered clear warning signs of a hazard in January and the information never reached the February work crew. The lack of purge procedures, the bypass of Lockout/Blue Flag/Tag policies, and the presence of active ignition sources in the work area compounded a situation where the hazard had been building undetected for years.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- Is there a formal program to identify and eliminate or protect deadlegs in hazardous service piping, including piping connected to decommissioned equipment?
- When demolition or construction removes thermal insulation from process piping, is there a system to ensure replacement or alternative protection before the next cold season?
- Do pre-job safety briefings include a review of recent incidents or near-misses in the same area, so that warning information reaches the crew doing the work?
- Are ignition source controls (no open flames, no electric heat lamps) explicitly required and verified before breaking containment on any piping in flammable service?

## Case 3546 - Tosco Avon Refinery Naphtha Fire

**Incident date:** February 23, 1999
**Location:** Martinez, California
**Industry:** Petroleum refining
**Report:** CSB Report No. 99-014-I-CA, March 2001

**Hazard type:** Flammable liquid fire, autoignition
**Materials involved:** Petroleum naphtha (highly flammable mixture, flash point well below ambient, also contained benzene)
**Outcome:** 4 killed, 1 critically injured, significant damage to crude fractionator unit

**PSM tags:** operating procedures, mechanical integrity, MOC, safe work practices (line breaking, lockout/tagout, blinding)

**Easy story:**
On February 10, maintenance discovered a pinhole leak in naphtha piping at the 112-foot level of the crude fractionator, which was running at full production. Over the next 13 days, workers made repeated attempts to isolate and drain the line so it could be repaired, but excessive corrosion and plugging inside the pipes made isolation impossible. No one escalated the situation to a formal hazard review or considered shutting the unit down. On February 23, a maintenance supervisor directed workers to make two hacksaw cuts into the naphtha piping to drain it. When they opened a flange at the second cut, naphtha released from the first cut location and autoignited on hot fractionator surfaces at that elevation. The resulting fire engulfed five workers positioned at multiple heights on the tower structure.

**Systemic reframe:**
This incident grew from 13 days of improvised nonroutine maintenance on a live, pressurized flammable system without any structured hazard review. The decision to cut into naphtha piping while the unit ran was effectively an on-the-spot field call by a supervisor, not an engineered procedure with identified controls. The safety management oversight system had not audited line breaking, lockout/tagout, or blinding procedures in the three years before the incident, leaving a vacuum in which unsafe practices developed unchecked. The root cause of the excessive corrosion, operating the desalter beyond design parameters with excess water in the crude, had also never been subjected to a MOC review, so the degraded piping condition was invisible to the safety management system.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- Is there a formal decision point that requires escalation (unit shutdown consideration, engineering review, written nonroutine work authorization) when routine repairs cannot be completed and an improvised approach is being considered on live hazardous piping?
- How often are line breaking, hot work, lockout/tagout, and blinding procedures audited in the field, and is that audit frequency documented?
- When a process parameter is routinely operated outside design limits (such as desalter water content), is a MOC review required, including corrosion rate reassessment?
- Do emergency procedures and training address fire scenarios where workers are positioned at multiple elevations on tall process structures?

## Case 3547 - Morton International Runaway Chemical Reaction

**Incident date:** April 8, 1998
**Location:** Paterson, New Jersey
**Industry:** Specialty chemical and dye manufacturing
**Report:** CSB Report No. 1998-06-I-NJ

**Hazard type:** Reactive runaway (thermal decomposition)
**Materials involved:** ortho-nitrochlorobenzene (o-NCB), 2-ethylhexylamine (2-EHA), Automate Yellow 96 Dye intermediate
**Outcome:** 9 injured (2 seriously), community contaminated with dye and o-NCB, extensive plant damage, blowout panels and window frames expelled

**PSM tags:** PHA, process safety information, incident investigation, MOC, operating procedures

**Easy story:**
During production of Yellow 96 Dye in a 2,000-gallon reactor kettle, the batch was started at a higher temperature than normal, steam was left on too long, and cooling was initiated too late. The primary exothermic reaction between o-NCB and 2-EHA accelerated beyond the kettle's heat removal capacity and drove the temperature up to 195 degrees C, the onset of decomposition for Yellow 96. A secondary runaway decomposition reaction rapidly over-pressured the kettle, blew off the hatch, and released flammable vapors that ignited into a fireball above the building, while the dye mixture contaminated the surrounding area.

**Systemic reframe:**
Neither the 1990 nor the 1995 process hazard analysis for Yellow 96 addressed the reactive hazard of the dye intermediate. Process safety information did not document the decomposition onset temperature, the point at which a runaway becomes unavoidable. A 1989 recommendation from an Amoco researcher for additional reactive hazard testing and safety devices was never implemented. When Morton scaled up from 1,000-gallon to 2,000-gallon kettles in 1996 without a MOC review, the heat transfer area per gallon decreased by 10 percent, narrowing the safety margin on heat removal exactly when it mattered most. Prior temperature excursion incidents during Yellow 96 production had been observed but never formally investigated, so the pattern of operating near the decomposition threshold was invisible.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- Does process safety information for every exothermic batch reaction document the decomposition onset temperature and the conditions (temperature, concentration, contaminants) that could trigger a secondary runaway?
- When reactor vessel size changes, does the MOC review include a heat transfer adequacy calculation comparing cooling capacity per unit volume before and after the change?
- Are temperature excursions during batch reactions captured as incidents and investigated for reactive hazard significance, not just logged as deviations?
- Have PHA teams for exothermic processes included chemists or engineers with formal reactive hazard training, not just process operations knowledge?

## Case 3548 - Herrig Brothers Farm Propane Tank BLEVE

**Incident date:** April 9, 1998
**Location:** Albert City, Buena Vista County, Iowa
**Industry:** Agriculture (turkey farm)
**Report:** CSB Report No. 98-007-I-IA

**Hazard type:** BLEVE (Boiling Liquid Expanding Vapor Explosion), overpressure and mechanical failure
**Materials involved:** Liquid propane and propane vapor
**Outcome:** 2 volunteer firefighters killed, 7 emergency personnel injured, farm buildings damaged by tank fragments

**PSM tags:** mechanical integrity, process safety information (emergency responder training), facility siting (piping protection), incident investigation

**Easy story:**
A teenager driving an ATV struck unprotected aboveground propane piping running between an 18,000-gallon storage tank and a set of vaporizers. The liquid line was severed at the tank connection. The excess flow valve, a safety device designed to stop flow when a line breaks, failed to activate because the downstream pipe diameter was too small to allow enough flow velocity to trigger it. Propane ignited at a direct-fired vaporizer roughly 37 feet away. Fire engulfed the storage tank. About 18 minutes after the first firefighters arrived and positioned themselves alongside the tank, believing the sides to be the safe position, the tank underwent a BLEVE. Tank fragments were scattered in all directions, including to the sides. Both firefighters positioned to the sides were killed.

**Systemic reframe:**
Two independent protection layers both failed before a single firefighter arrived: the aboveground piping had no vehicle impact protection, and the excess flow valve was installed in a system where the downstream piping diameter physically prevented it from ever activating. The state fire marshal had no record of this system and no record of an inspection since installation in 1988, when it was already not in compliance with NFPA 58. The firefighter fatalities were then compounded by training guidance that incorrectly told responders the sides of a BLEVE tank were safe positions, when in fact fragment trajectories from BLEVEs are not limited to the end caps.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- For aboveground propane piping exposed to vehicle traffic, is there physical vehicle impact protection (bollards, concrete barriers, setback), and is it inspected annually?
- Are excess flow valves sized to activate under the actual downstream pipe flow conditions, not just rated for the tank connection size?
- Have local emergency responders been briefed on current BLEVE science, specifically that tank fragments can travel in all directions including to the sides?
- Is there a mechanism for the state or local authority having jurisdiction to maintain a record of all farm-scale propane installations and to confirm current compliance when installations change?

## Case 3549 - Union Carbide Nitrogen Asphyxiation

**Incident date:** March 27, 1998
**Location:** Hahnville, Louisiana (Taft/Star Manufacturing Plant)
**Industry:** Chemical manufacturing (ethylene oxide, ethylene glycol)
**Report:** CSB Report No. 98-05-I-LA, February 1999 (Summary Report)

**Hazard type:** Nitrogen asphyxiation
**Materials involved:** Nitrogen gas (inert, colorless, odorless, undetectable by human senses)
**Outcome:** 1 killed (Worker B, 23 years service), 1 seriously injured (Worker A, 32 years service)

**PSM tags:** operating procedures, process safety information, safe work practices (confined/semi-confined space, nitrogen hazard control), training

**Easy story:**
During a turnaround shutdown, nitrogen was being continuously injected into reactors to protect new catalyst from moisture. The nitrogen vented out through an open 48-inch horizontal pipe where the oxygen feed mixer had been removed for cleaning. Workers arrived at that pipe opening to perform a black light inspection of the pipe flange, and one placed a black plastic sheet over the pipe end to shade the area from sunlight. The plastic sheet inadvertently trapped the flowing nitrogen, creating an oxygen-deficient enclosure around both workers. Neither worker had any way to detect the hazard; nitrogen is completely undetectable without instruments. Worker A, who had supervised the nitrogen injection the previous evening, did not know the gas was venting 150 feet away and several stories below at the inspection location. Both workers were overcome in seconds. Worker B died; Worker A survived after hospital treatment.

**Systemic reframe:**
The hazard was created by the unrecognized combination of two concurrent activities: nitrogen injection into equipment and a black light inspection at a vent location. No procedure addressed controlling the hazard of temporary enclosures near nitrogen-venting equipment, and no nitrogen hazard warnings or signs were posted at or near the open pipe. The invisibility of nitrogen is not an obscure fact, it is the defining property that makes nitrogen asphyxiation hazards uniquely lethal, and it was not incorporated into the work controls around this turnaround activity. The worker who inadvertently created the enclosure had been directly involved in establishing the nitrogen injection the night before, yet still had no awareness that a 150-foot, multi-story separation meant the gas was actively flowing at his work location.

**Monday-morning checks:**
- During turnarounds where nitrogen is being injected into equipment, is there a written protocol that identifies all downstream vent and opening locations and posts hazard warnings at each?
- Do work permits for inspection or maintenance tasks near nitrogen-containing or nitrogen-blanketed equipment explicitly address the hazard of creating even temporary partial enclosures (tarps, plastic sheeting, scaffolding curtains)?
- Are workers entering or working around any open equipment connection during nitrogen injection required to use oxygen-monitoring instruments continuously, not just before entry?
- Is there a turnaround coordination mechanism to ensure workers at downstream locations are aware of what is flowing through interconnected equipment upstream?
