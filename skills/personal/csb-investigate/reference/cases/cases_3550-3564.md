### Sierra Chemical Company High-Explosives Accident

- Archive id | Final report date: 3550 | Final report date not printed in document; investigation initiated January 1998, report published 1998 (Report No. 98-001-I-NV)
- Company | Location | Industry: Sierra Chemical Company | Mustang (Kean Canyon), Nevada | Explosives manufacturing
- Incident date: January 7, 1998
- Hazard type: overpressure / mechanical failure (high-explosive detonation)
- Materials: TNT (2,4,6-trinitrotoluene), PETN (pentaerythritol tetranitrate), Comp-B (TNT and RDX mixture), Pentolite (TNT and PETN mixture)
- What happened: Two explosions occurred in rapid succession at the Kean Canyon booster manufacturing plant. The first explosion initiated in Booster Room 2, most probably when an operator restarted a mixing pot that contained solidified base-mix explosive left overnight, causing detonation by impact or friction from the mixer blade against the solidified mass. The resulting shock wave destroyed Booster Room 2 and, approximately 3.5 seconds later, debris or burning material initiated a second explosion in the adjacent PETN storage building.
- Consequences: 4 fatalities, 6 injuries; both production buildings destroyed; extensive property damage; no offsite impact noted
- Sequence:
  1. Operator leaves 50 to 100 lb of base-mix explosive in mixing pot overnight
  2. Next morning, operator restarts mixer without inspecting or clearing the solidified mix
  3. Mixer blade contacts solidified explosive; detonation by impact, shear, or friction
  4. Explosion destroys Booster Room 2, propels debris toward adjacent PETN building
  5. Second explosion in PETN building occurs 3.5 seconds later
  6. Combined blast kills four workers and injures six
- Immediate cause: Mechanical initiation of solidified high-explosive mix in Booster Room 2 mixing pot when the pot motor was restarted with the blade embedded in hardened material
- Contributing factors:
  - Unsafe work practice of leaving explosive mix in the pot overnight
  - No written procedures for Booster Room 2 operations
  - Managers held the incorrect belief that, absent a blasting cap, their materials were nearly impossible to detonate
  - Reclaimed DoD demilitarized explosive materials sometimes contained foreign objects, adding contamination risk
  - Insufficient intra-plant separation distances between explosive operations
  - Buildings not designed or sited to prevent propagation between rooms
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Process hazard analysis was inadequate: no PHA was conducted for Booster Room 2; PHA for Booster Room 1 excluded workers and on-site supervisors and ignored siting and human-factors issues
  - Training programs relied almost entirely on informal on-the-job instruction and were ineffective; managers and workers lacked basic knowledge of explosive sensitivity to mechanical initiation
  - Written operating procedures were absent or not in the language of the workforce (primarily Spanish-speaking); no procedures existed for Booster Room 2
  - Employee participation element of PSM was non-functional; workers' safety concerns were not gathered or resolved
  - No management of change system to review process changes such as the use of substitute raw materials or building modifications
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No procedure requiring inspection or clearing of the mixing pot before restart
  - No engineering interlock to prevent mixer start with embedded blade
  - PHA never conducted for Booster Room 2
  - Quantity-distance guidelines for building separation were not applied between process areas
  - Pre-startup safety review element not practiced
- Key findings:
  - Seismic data confirmed Booster Room 2 exploded first, followed by the PETN building 3.5 seconds later
  - Multiple credible initiation scenarios existed, with solidified-mix restart considered most probable
  - The facility was OSHA PSM-covered, yet nearly all PSM elements were deficient or absent
  - Demilitarized DoD explosive materials sold commercially contained foreign objects not screened before use
  - Language barrier (Spanish-speaking workforce, English-only safety materials) was a systemic failure
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Sierra Chemical to Sierra and other explosives manufacturers: conduct PHAs with worker and supervisor involvement; write procedures covering all phases; provide materials in workers' language; require explosives safety certification for workers and line managers; analyze process changes under MOC; conduct pre-startup reviews; audit PSM elements; strengthen employee participation
  - Institute of Makers of Explosives: develop and disseminate training guidelines; distribute CSB report to members; develop screening guidelines for reclaimed explosive materials
  - Nevada OSHA: increase inspection frequency at explosives manufacturing facilities
  - Department of Defense: develop program to certify that demilitarized explosives sold commercially are free of foreign objects
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, Operating procedures, Training, Employee participation, Management of change, Pre-startup safety review, Mechanical integrity, Incident investigation, Compliance audits, Process safety information
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers left explosive mix in a pot overnight and it blew up when they turned it on the next morning.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A facility operating under OSHA PSM with virtually none of its required elements in place. No PHAs for the most dangerous work area, no written procedures, training conducted in the wrong language by word of mouth, managers who did not understand the sensitivity of the explosives they manufactured, and buildings packed too close together so one explosion guaranteed a second. The overnight solidification of the mix was a known and recurring condition; the hazard was invisible because no one had ever formally analyzed it.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. Before restarting any mixer or agitator that has been idle, verify the vessel is clear of solidified or hardened material; never reenergize with the blade embedded in a set batch.
  2. Confirm that PHAs cover every process area where hazardous materials are handled, including areas added or modified since the last review, and that workers from those areas are on the PHA team.
  3. Verify that all safety-critical procedures and hazard warnings are available in the primary language(s) of the workforce performing the work.

### Improving Reactive Hazard Management (CSB Hazard Investigation)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3553 | October 2002 (Report No. 2002-H-1; Hazard Investigation approved September 17, 2002)
- Company | Location | Industry: U.S. Chemical Safety Board (study spanning multiple companies and sectors) | United States, national scope | Chemical manufacturing, refining, bulk storage
- Incident date: Study period 1980 to 2001 (not a single incident)
- Hazard type: study/bulletin (reactive hazard investigation)
- Materials: Wide range including hydroxylamine, ammonium nitrate, monomers, peroxides, organic acids, metal powders, and other reactive chemicals across more than 40 chemical classes
- What happened: The CSB conducted a two-year hazard investigation into uncontrolled chemical reactions at U.S. industrial sites. Triggered by the 1998 Morton International runaway reaction in Paterson, NJ, and the 1999 Concept Sciences hydroxylamine explosion, the study examined 167 serious reactive incidents from 1980 to 2001, identified systemic gaps in regulatory coverage, and called on OSHA, EPA, NIST, and industry associations to overhaul how reactive hazards are managed.
- Consequences: The 167 incidents studied caused 108 fatalities and hundreds of millions of dollars in property damage; nearly 50 incidents impacted the public through evacuation or injury. The investigation itself had no casualties; it documented the cumulative toll.
- Sequence:
  1. CSB investigates Morton International (1998) and Concept Sciences (1999) reactive accidents
  2. CSB launches full hazard investigation, searching more than 40 data sources
  3. 167 reactive incidents documented (1980 to 2001); 108 deaths, multiple public evacuations
  4. Analysis reveals that over 50 percent of incidents involved chemicals not covered by OSHA PSM or EPA RMP
  5. Analysis shows reactive hazards are inadequately rated by NFPA system and missed by existing regulations
  6. CSB issues 18 recommendations to OSHA, EPA, NIST, CCPS, ACC, SOCMA, and NACD
- Immediate cause: N/A (study, not a single incident)
- Contributing factors (systemic, across studied incidents):
  - OSHA PSM Standard covered only 137 listed chemicals; only 38 based on reactivity; thousands of reactive chemicals in common use are unregulated
  - EPA RMP did not specifically cover reactive hazards
  - More than 60 percent of incidents with known causes involved inadequate hazard identification or PHA
  - Nearly 50 percent involved inadequate procedures for storage, handling, or processing
  - Reactive hazards of involved chemicals were documented in publicly available literature in over 90 percent of cases, yet companies did not consult that literature
  - No comprehensive national database tracked reactive incidents or their root causes
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Regulatory gap: neither OSHA PSM nor EPA RMP captured reactive hazards from combinations of chemicals or process-specific conditions; coverage triggered only by listed substances
  - Industry guidance gap: no specific codes or standards from major trade associations addressed reactive hazard management throughout a chemical's lifecycle
  - Data gap: OSHA and EPA had no functional tracking of reactive incidents; lessons learned were unavailable to industry
  - Company-level failures: over 60 percent of incidents with causal information showed inadequate hazard identification or PHA; reactive hazard test data not shared across industry
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Process hazard analyses did not explicitly address reactive hazard scenarios
  - PSI compilations did not require chemical reactivity test data or literature surveys
  - No requirement to evaluate reactive potential of chemical combinations
  - Incident reporting systems did not categorize reactive events separately
- Key findings:
  - 167 serious reactive incidents from 1980 to 2001; 108 fatalities; average of 5 deaths per year; 6 injury incidents per year
  - 36 percent of incidents involved chemical incompatibility; 35 percent runaway reactions; 10 percent impact or thermally sensitive materials
  - Storage and process equipment other than reaction vessels involved in over 65 percent of incidents
  - Over 90 percent of incidents involved reactive hazards documented in publicly available literature
  - NFPA instability ratings are insufficient as a sole criterion for determining PSM coverage
  - Companies generally did not use available computational tools (CHETAH, Chemical Reactivity Worksheet) to identify reactive hazards
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - OSHA: amend PSM to cover reactive hazards based on objective criteria beyond listed chemicals; require reactivity test data in process safety information; augment PHA to explicitly address reactive hazard scenarios; track reactive incidents in inspection databases
  - EPA: revise RMP to explicitly cover catastrophic reactive hazards with public impact potential; add "reactive incident" as a reportable release event in 5-year accident histories
  - NIST: develop and maintain a publicly available database of reactive hazard test data
  - CCPS: publish comprehensive guidance on reactive hazard management systems for both chemical manufacturers and bulk storage/handling users
  - ACC, SOCMA, NACD: expand voluntary codes (Responsible Care, Responsible Distribution) to require specific reactive hazard management programs; cooperate with NIST on test data database
- PSM elements implicated: Process safety information, Process hazard analysis, Operating procedures, Training, Management of change, Incident investigation, Compliance audits (regulatory coverage of all elements)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Chemicals that can react dangerously are falling through regulatory gaps because the rules only cover chemicals on a specific short list.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A regulatory framework built around individual listed substances rather than reactive behavior. Thousands of chemicals with well-documented reactive hazards remained outside PSM and RMP. Companies did not consult publicly available literature that described the hazards. No national tracking system existed, so the same incidents repeated without institutional learning. The study found that reactive accidents were not rare edge cases but a steady, predictable toll that a better-designed regulatory and management system could substantially reduce.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. When reviewing PHAs, explicitly add a "reactive hazard" deviation node: what happens if two streams mix unexpectedly, if temperature rises beyond normal, or if an unintended catalyst enters the system?
  2. For any process involving reactive chemicals, document a literature search (Bretherick, NOAA Chemical Reactivity Worksheet) in the process safety information file, not just the MSDS.
  3. Verify that incident reporting categories at your facility include "reactive event" so near-misses with uncontrolled reactions are tracked separately and trigger reactive hazard review.

### Silver Eagle Refinery Flash Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3554 | No final report in archive; only a January 30, 2009 investigative update statement. Note: CSB investigation was ongoing at the time of the available documents.
- Company | Location | Industry: Silver Eagle Refinery | Woods Cross, Utah | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: January 12, 2009
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Light naphtha (low-boiling hydrocarbon mixture) stored in an atmospheric floating-roof tank (Tank 105)
- What happened: On the evening of January 12, 2009, a large vapor cloud was released from Tank 105, an atmospheric storage tank containing approximately 440,000 gallons of light naphtha. Vapor escaped through atmospheric vents on the west side of the tank. The vapor cloud found an ignition source (believed to be a utility room with a gas heater or a conventional refrigerator electrical outlet) approximately 230 feet west of the tank farm, producing a flash fire that spread across the area.
- Consequences: 4 workers (2 refinery operators, 2 contractors) suffered serious burns and were hospitalized; 2 structures damaged (a shed and a laboratory approximately 140 and 160 feet from the tank); no fatalities reported in the available update
- Sequence:
  1. Tank 105 receives multiple hydrocarbon streams including feed from the No. 1 crude unit pre-flash accumulator, a recent process change
  2. Nitrogen purging of equipment was also routing liquid into Tank 105
  3. Vapor escapes through atmospheric vents on west side of Tank 105
  4. Vapor cloud disperses up to 230 feet from tank
  5. Cloud contacts ignition source (probable utility room appliance)
  6. Flash fire engulfs four workers in the area
- Immediate cause: Ignition of a large vapor cloud released from Tank 105 containing light naphtha
- Contributing factors:
  - A recent unreviewed process change directed feed from the No. 1 crude unit pre-flash accumulator directly to Tank 105; this stream contained lighter fractions than the tank's floating-roof seal was designed to handle
  - History of vapor leaks from Tank 105 both before and after previous tank repairs
  - Possible mismatch between the floating-roof seal design and the volatility of materials being stored
  - An occupied laboratory was sited in close proximity to the tank farm
  - Facility siting standards for occupied structures near hazardous process equipment may not have been followed
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Management of change: routing feed from a different process unit to Tank 105 was a recent change that may not have been formally evaluated for its effect on vapor emissions and seal compatibility
  - Facility siting: an occupied laboratory exposed workers to flash-fire hazard; this mirrors the BP Texas City finding where trailers sited near hazardous equipment multiplied casualties
  - Mechanical integrity: recurring vapor leaks from Tank 105 were known and not resolved
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Floating-roof seal not suited to light hydrocarbon service (under investigation at time of update)
  - No effective barrier or distance between tank farm vapor release area and occupied structures
  - Process change review that would have flagged increased vapor generation in Tank 105
- Key findings (from investigative update, not a final report):
  - Vapor was released from atmospheric vents on the tank roof, not a catastrophic seal failure
  - The process change directing lighter, less-processed streams to Tank 105 was identified as a key factor
  - The flash fire spread up to 230 feet from the tank
  - Facility siting of the laboratory relative to the tank farm is under scrutiny
  - More than 40 interviews were conducted and hundreds of pages of records gathered
- Recommendations (recipient to action): No formal recommendations issued in the available documents (update only); CSB intended to produce recommendations at conclusion of investigation
- PSM elements implicated: Management of change, Mechanical integrity, Facility siting, Process hazard analysis, Process safety information
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A leaking tank caught four workers in a flash fire.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): An unreviewed process change directed lighter, more volatile hydrocarbon streams into a storage tank whose floating-roof seal may not have been rated for that service. Repeated, known vapor leaks from the tank were not corrected. When the vapor cloud formed, an occupied laboratory was close enough to be in the hazard zone, mirroring the lesson from BP Texas City about siting people near high-consequence equipment.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. When routing a new or different feed stream to an existing storage tank, evaluate whether the tank's vapor controls (floating-roof seal type, vent sizing) are appropriate for the new material's volatility before the change is made.
  2. Treat repeated small vapor leaks from a storage tank as a precursor signal demanding engineering resolution, not a routine maintenance item.
  3. Audit the siting of occupied structures (labs, control rooms, trailers) relative to tanks and process units that hold large inventories of flammable liquids.

### INDSPEC Chemical Corporation Oleum Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3555 | September 2009 (Case Study No. 2009-01-I-PA)
- Company | Location | Industry: INDSPEC Chemical Corporation | Petrolia, Pennsylvania | Specialty chemicals (resorcinol manufacturing)
- Incident date: October 11, 2008
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Oleum (fuming sulfuric acid, mixture of H2SO4 and SO3); sulfuric acid mist generated when oleum reacts with atmospheric moisture
- What happened: On a Saturday afternoon, an INDSPEC operator used an emergency power supply to transfer oleum from a pressure vessel to a process tank. Unlike the normal power supply, the emergency supply was not wired into the DCS and bypassed the high-high level automatic shutoff. The operator believed he had stopped the pump before leaving but had not. The process tank overfilled, oleum flooded the exhaust ventilation system, and a sulfuric acid mist cloud filled the storage building and drifted into the surrounding community. Approximately 2,500 residents were evacuated or sheltered in place from three towns.
- Consequences: 2,500 residents evacuated or sheltered in place from Petrolia, Bruin, and Fairview; 1 emergency responder suffered minor injury from sulfuric acid inhalation; no fatalities; cleanup lasted until approximately 2:00 a.m. the following day
- Sequence:
  1. Saturday morning: operator called in to prepare soda ash and transfer oleum
  2. Operator uses both normal and emergency power supplies simultaneously to run two transfer pumps concurrently (weekend efficiency practice for nearly 28 years)
  3. Operator stops normal-supply pump via DCS but incorrectly believes he has also stopped the emergency-supply pump
  4. Operator leaves facility at 2:15 p.m.; emergency-supply pump continues running
  5. Process tank 1502 reaches high-high level at 2:18 p.m.; no automatic shutdown activates on emergency supply circuit
  6. Oleum overfills tank, enters ventilation duct, and sulfuric acid mist escapes to building exterior by 3:12 to 4:30 p.m.
  7. Emergency declared; evacuation ordered at 5:14 p.m.; vessel 612 empties by 5:50 p.m.
  8. Pump de-energized remotely at 7:00 p.m.; emergency ended at 2:00 a.m.
- Immediate cause: Oleum process tank overfilled because a transfer pump powered by the emergency supply lacked the automatic high-high level shutoff present on the normal supply, and the operator left without confirming the pump was off
- Contributing factors:
  - Emergency power supply installed circa 1980 as a temporary measure, never properly documented in drawings or procedures; became de facto permanent after the wiring was upgraded in the 1980s
  - DCS could not monitor or control a pump on the emergency supply; operator could not see pump status from control room
  - Audible alarm in the oleum storage building was out of service for months; no program to test it
  - PHAs in 2005 and 2008 did not identify the emergency power supply as a hazard because it was not shown in P&IDs or written procedures; only operators knew of it
  - Operators routinely ran two pumps concurrently on weekends to maximize efficiency; this "normalized" the non-standard practice
  - Weekend operations occurred without supervisors or engineers present
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Management of change: the 1980 temporary installation and its later hardening into permanent wiring were never formally evaluated; hazard controls were not applied to the emergency supply
  - Process safety information: the emergency power supply was absent from P&IDs and operating procedures, making it invisible to PHAs and management
  - PHA failure: because PSI was incomplete, both 2005 and 2008 PHAs could not evaluate the emergency supply scenario; the only safeguards identified for overfill were operator-dependent
  - Normalization of deviance: operators trained each other on a non-standard practice for nearly 28 years without management awareness; the practice became entrenched and invisible
  - Safeguard hierarchy not enforced: the facility relied on operator action (least reliable control) instead of engineering controls (most reliable) for a high-consequence transfer hazard
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Automatic high-high level shutdown not connected to the emergency power supply
  - DCS visibility of emergency-supply pump status absent
  - Audible alarm in oleum building out of service
  - No written procedure for using the emergency power supply
  - No requirement to verify pump stoppage before leaving the area
- Key findings:
  - The emergency power supply was installed as a temporary workaround in the 1980s and remained functionally unchanged for nearly 28 years
  - Supervisors, managers, and engineers were unaware of the emergency supply's use as a routine oleum transfer method
  - PHA teams never evaluated the emergency supply scenario because it was excluded from process safety documentation
  - Combining two transfer pumps simultaneously was a normalized weekend practice developed to save time but never formally evaluated for risk
  - The facility hierarchy of controls ranked administrative measures equal to engineering controls, contrary to good practice
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - INDSPEC (implied from lessons learned): ensure all temporary changes are evaluated as if permanent, with updated drawings, procedures, training, and hazard analysis; document all modes of operation in P&IDs; ensure PHA teams have access to accurate process safety information; apply equally robust engineering safeguards to all operating modes
- PSM elements implicated: Management of change, Process safety information, Process hazard analysis, Operating procedures, Training, Mechanical integrity (alarm maintenance)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): An operator left a pump running that should have been off and the tank overflowed, releasing a toxic acid cloud.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A workaround created in 1980 that was never formally evaluated, never written down, and never connected to the safety instrumentation. It lived only in operator oral tradition for almost three decades. When PHAs were conducted in 2005 and 2008, the workaround did not exist on any drawing or in any procedure, so it was never analyzed. The hazard was fully hidden from the management system. On the day of the incident, a single operator lapse in a scenario the PHA had never considered was all it took to evacuate three towns.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. Audit all workarounds and non-standard operating practices to confirm they are reflected in P&IDs, procedures, and PHAs; if a practice is not in the documented system, it cannot be analyzed.
  2. Require that all automatic safety functions (high-high level shutoffs, alarms, interlocks) apply equally to every power source or operating mode that can drive the same hazard.
  3. Verify that periodic alarm testing is required and documented; an undetected failed alarm is an absent safeguard.

### Sonat Exploration Company Catastrophic Vessel Overpressurization

- Archive id | Final report date: 3556 | Final report published 2000 (Report No. 1998-002-I-LA)
- Company | Location | Industry: Sonat Exploration Company | Pitkin, Louisiana | Oil and gas production
- Incident date: March 4, 1998
- Hazard type: overpressure / mechanical failure
- Materials: Well fluid (crude oil, natural gas, and water at high pressure); flammable hydrocarbon gases released on vessel failure
- What happened: Workers were purging a new pipeline by flowing well fluid from the Temple 24-1 well through the Temple 22-1 Common Point Separation Facility for approximately 60 minutes. A third-stage separator vessel (originally a storage tank, rated for atmospheric pressure only, with no pressure-relief device) was exposed to purge gas pressures potentially as high as 800 psig when two outlet block valves on the separator and two bypass line block valves were closed, trapping high-pressure gas. The vessel failed catastrophically, releasing flammable gas that ignited immediately.
- Consequences: 4 fatalities (workers killed instantly by massive trauma); 2 survivors uninjured; separator destroyed; 4 personal vehicles and a backhoe destroyed; oil and water storage tanks damaged; significant property damage to facility
- Sequence:
  1. Production team plans to start up the Temple 24-1 well and purge its pipeline to the facility using well fluid
  2. Purge proceeds for approximately 60 minutes
  3. Block valves on the third-stage separator outlet and downstream bypass line are closed (reason undetermined)
  4. High-pressure purge gas cannot vent; separator vessel is exposed to up to 800 psig
  5. Vessel rated only for atmospheric pressure (0 psig); no pressure-relief device present
  6. Catastrophic vessel failure; flammable gas cloud ignites
  7. Fireball and fire destroy facility equipment and kill four workers
- Immediate cause: Catastrophic overpressurization of an atmospheric-rated separator vessel that lacked pressure-relief protection, when block valves were closed and trapped high-pressure purge gas
- Contributing factors:
  - Vessel lacked an inlet valve, preventing isolation from the high-pressure bypass line
  - No written operating procedures addressing valve alignment during purging
  - Workers and contractors lacked training in purging hazards
  - Sonat operated similar third-stage separators without adequate pressure relief at other facilities for over a year before this incident
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - No formal engineering design review or hazard analysis process: the facility was designed and built without engineering drawings or hazard analyses; no formal design review would have been able to identify the pressure-protection gap
  - No pressure-relief systems on third-stage separators: ANSI/API Spec 12J requires pressure protective devices on all separators regardless of size or pressure; Sonat did not comply and did not have a program to audit compliance across its facilities
  - Absence of written operating procedures: workers had no written guidance on valve alignment during purging, a critical operation
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No pressure-relief device on the third-stage separator
  - No inlet isolation valve on the separator
  - No engineering drawings or formal design review for the facility
  - No hazard analysis that would have identified the overpressurization scenario
  - No written operating procedures for purging or startup
  - OSHA PSM Standard did not apply to oil and gas production facilities (regulatory gap)
- Key findings:
  - The third-stage separator was rated for 0 psig but was exposed to purge gas at up to 800 psig
  - No pressure-relief device of any kind was installed on the vessel
  - The facility was designed and constructed without producing engineering drawings
  - Sonat operated similar unprotected vessels at multiple other facilities, indicating a systemic rather than site-specific failure
  - The reason the block valves were closed remains undetermined
  - OSHA PSM did not cover oil and gas production, leaving this facility outside regulatory process safety requirements
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - El Paso Energy (successor to Sonat): implement formal engineering design review and hazard analysis for all oil and gas production facilities following good engineering practice; implement a program to ensure all potentially over-pressurizable equipment has adequate pressure-relief systems and audit compliance; develop written operating procedures for all facilities covering at minimum purging and startup, with worker and contractor training
  - American Petroleum Institute: develop and issue recommended practice guidelines for safe startup and operation of oil and gas production facilities covering design review, written procedures, training, and pressure-relief requirements; communicate CSB findings to membership
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, Process safety information (engineering drawings), Mechanical integrity (pressure-relief devices), Operating procedures, Training, Management of change, Pre-startup safety review
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers pressurized a tank that was only designed for atmospheric conditions, and it exploded.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A facility built without engineering drawings, without a design review, and without hazard analysis. A vessel that looked like and was called a storage tank was actually in separator service and could be exposed to wellhead pressures exceeding 800 psig. Because there were no drawings and no formal design process, nobody formally asked: what happens if this vessel gets pressurized? A required engineering standard (API 12J) mandated pressure relief on all separators; Sonat ignored it and had the same gap at multiple facilities. The PSM standard, which would have required hazard analyses, simply did not apply to oil and gas production.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. Verify that every vessel in your facility that can be exposed to a pressurized source has a pressure-relief device rated for the maximum credible inlet pressure, not just its normal operating pressure.
  2. Before any purging, startup, or commissioning operation on a new or modified system, confirm in writing that all valve alignments are correct and that no isolated dead-end sections can trap high-pressure fluid.
  3. Confirm that engineering drawings exist and are current for all process equipment; if drawings are absent, that is itself a process safety gap requiring immediate correction.

### Emergency Shutdown Systems for Chlorine Transfer (CSB Safety Bulletin)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3558 | June 2007 (Safety Bulletin No. 2005-06-I-LA)
- Company | Location | Industry: CSB safety bulletin comparing DPC Enterprises (Festus, Missouri, 2002) and Honeywell International (Baton Rouge, Louisiana, 2005) | National scope | Chemical manufacturing and distribution (chlorine users)
- Incident date: Bulletin issued June 2007; anchor incidents August 14, 2002 (DPC) and August 11, 2005 (Honeywell)
- Hazard type: study/bulletin (toxic gas release; chlorine)
- Materials: Chlorine (Cl2), transferred from railcars via hoses to facility receiving systems
- What happened: The bulletin compares two chlorine hose-rupture incidents to illustrate the life-safety difference between a functional and a failed emergency shutdown system (ESS). At DPC Enterprises in 2002, a chlorine transfer hose made with the wrong braid material (stainless steel instead of Hastelloy C) rapidly corroded and ruptured. The ESS valves failed to close despite detector activation and operator button pushes; 48,000 pounds of chlorine were released. At Honeywell in 2005, a hose ruptured due to wire corrosion; the ESS worked correctly, stopping the release in under one minute.
- Consequences: DPC 2002: 48,000 lb chlorine released; 63 residents sought medical evaluation; 3 hospitalized; hundreds evacuated or sheltered in place; vegetation killed. Honeywell 2005: minor release lasting less than one minute; several contractors treated and released; no offsite impact.
- Sequence (DPC 2002):
  1. Chlorine railcar hose contains wrong braid material; chlorine degrades stainless steel braid
  2. Hose ruptures; chlorine released
  3. Chlorine detectors activate; operators push emergency shutdown button
  4. ESS valves fail to operate (inadequately maintained and tested)
  5. 48,000 lb chlorine released into community
- Sequence (Honeywell 2005, controlled outcome):
  1. Chlorine transfer hose ruptures due to wire corrosion
  2. Chlorine detector alerts control room; supervisor sees escaping chlorine, sounds alarm
  3. Control room operator remotely closes ESS valves
  4. Release stopped in under one minute; no offsite impact
- Immediate cause (DPC): ESS valves not maintained or tested to ensure operability; excess flow valve (internal to railcar) also failed to close; wrong hose material allowed rapid corrosion
- Contributing factors:
  - DPC did not require employees to verify valve closure when operating the emergency shutdown (daily dry-cycle of the button did not confirm actual valve movement)
  - No quality assurance verification of hose braid material before placing in chlorine service
  - Approximately 30 percent of bulk chlorine users contacted relied solely on excess flow valves, which FRA has warned will not close during hose-rupture leak rates
  - DOT regulations required ESS on tank-truck chlorine transfers but not railcar transfers (regulatory gap)
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Testing and maintenance gap: ESS components were not periodically tested to verify operability under actual demand conditions
  - Material verification failure: neither manufacturer nor DPC confirmed hose material before service; no QA program for chlorine hose materials
  - Regulatory coverage gap: DOT covered ESS for tank trucks but not railcars; OSHA PSM and EPA RMP are performance-based and do not specify chlorine railcar ESS requirements
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - ESS valves did not function when activated (maintenance deficiency)
  - Excess flow valve did not close (as expected for leak rate below EFV trip threshold)
  - No materials verification program for incoming chlorine hoses
  - No DOT requirement for railcar chlorine transfer ESS
- Key findings:
  - Two incidents with identical initiating event (hose rupture) produced radically different outcomes solely due to ESS reliability
  - At least five chlorine hose failures resulting in releases were recorded since 1994; hose failure is a foreseeable, recurring event
  - Approximately 30 percent of bulk chlorine users relied only on excess flow valves as protection against hose rupture
  - The Chlorine Institute required ESS for member railcar unloading systems (Pamphlet 85) but DOT did not require it by regulation
  - NTSB had previously recommended DOT require railcar transfer ESS; DOT response classified as "Open-Unacceptable" as of bulletin publication
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - US Department of Transportation: expand regulatory coverage to require remotely operated emergency isolation devices for chlorine railcar unloading; require periodic maintenance and operational testing of ESS
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical integrity (ESS testing and maintenance), Process hazard analysis, Operating procedures (ESS activation), Training, Process safety information (material specifications)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A chlorine hose broke and 48,000 pounds of chlorine gassed a neighborhood because the emergency shutoff didn't work.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): An emergency shutdown system that was operated in a "dry run" daily but never verified to actually close. The difference between the DPC disaster and the Honeywell near-miss was not the hose failure; it was whether the ESS had been maintained and tested to confirm it would actually function when needed. A regulatory gap left railcar chlorine unloading without a mandate for ESS, while tank-truck unloading was covered.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. For every critical safety system (ESS, emergency shutdown valves, interlock), replace "press the button" operational tests with "verify the actuator actually moved" tests; demand confirmation of function, not just input.
  2. Establish a materials verification program for any safety-critical component (hoses, valves, gaskets) that contacts corrosive or toxic process streams; verify material identity before placing in service.
  3. Identify any transfers of highly toxic materials where the only physical safeguard is an excess flow valve; upgrade to include remotely operable isolation valves as a minimum.

### Hazards of Nitrogen Asphyxiation (CSB Safety Bulletin)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3560 | June 2003 (Safety Bulletin No. 2003-10-B)
- Company | Location | Industry: CSB safety bulletin; statistical review of 85 incidents from 1992 to 2002 across industrial plants, laboratories, and medical facilities in the United States
- Incident date: Study period 1992 to 2002 (not a single incident)
- Hazard type: study/bulletin (asphyxiation)
- Materials: Nitrogen gas (N2); compressed breathing air (in mix-up incidents)
- What happened: The CSB published this safety bulletin following a 1998 nitrogen asphyxiation investigation in Hahnville, Louisiana, to highlight the breadth and persistence of nitrogen asphyxiation hazards across industry. The bulletin analyzed 85 nitrogen asphyxiation incidents from 1992 to 2002, identified patterns of failure including confined-space entry, nitrogen-air mix-ups, and inadequate monitoring, and provided guidance on prevention.
- Consequences: 85 incidents documented; 80 deaths; 50 injuries from 1992 to 2002; approximately 10 percent of fatalities occurred during attempted rescues
- Sequence (common patterns across incidents):
  1. Nitrogen present in or near a confined space as an inerting or purging agent, or accumulated as a leak
  2. Personnel enter or approach the space without atmospheric testing or without recognizing the nitrogen hazard
  3. Oxygen-deficient atmosphere causes rapid incapacitation; victim cannot detect the hazard or self-rescue
  4. Rescuers without proper PPE enter and are also overcome (accounts for approximately 10 percent of fatalities)
- Immediate cause: Oxygen displacement by nitrogen to levels below safe limits, combined with failure to detect or anticipate the hazard before entry
- Contributing factors:
  - Widespread assumption that nitrogen is harmless because it is 78 percent of normal air
  - No odor, color, or sensory warning of nitrogen-enriched atmosphere
  - Interchangeable couplings and poor labeling allowing nitrogen to be mistaken for compressed air
  - Inadequate or absent atmospheric monitoring before and during confined-space entry
  - Over 60 percent of victims were working in or next to a confined space; contractor workers were involved in 42 of 85 incidents and accounted for over 60 percent of fatalities
  - No retrieval system in place to rescue victims without rescuers entering the hazardous atmosphere
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Knowledge gap: personnel did not understand that nitrogen displaces oxygen without detectable sensory cues
  - Training deficiency: confined-space programs did not adequately address nitrogen-specific risks, especially for contractors
  - Equipment and labeling failures: nitrogen and breathing-air lines had compatible fittings in some facilities; cylinders and piping inadequately labeled
  - Procedural gaps: nitrogen purging and confined-space entry procedures did not integrate atmospheric monitoring requirements
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Continuous atmospheric monitoring (oxygen sensors) not deployed
  - Retrieval systems (harnesses and lifelines) not in place
  - Supplied-air or SCBA not required for entry into nitrogen-purged spaces
  - Fitting compatibility controls not enforced to prevent nitrogen-air cross-connection
  - Contractor training and oversight insufficient
- Key findings:
  - 80 deaths and 50 injuries from nitrogen asphyxiation in U.S. workplaces from 1992 to 2002
  - 62 percent of incidents occurred in chemical plants, refineries, food processing, and other industrial sites
  - Approximately 10 percent of deaths were rescuers who entered without proper respiratory protection
  - Mix-up of nitrogen and breathing air accounted for 12 of 85 incidents and approximately 20 percent of fatalities
  - Nitrogen is used in virtually all industries and the hazard recurs in laboratories, medical facilities, and transportation as well as manufacturing
  - 42 of 85 incidents involved contractors; contractor fatalities exceeded 60 percent of total
- Recommendations (recipient to action): No formal numbered recommendations in the safety bulletin; guidance directed at all employers using nitrogen: implement continuous atmospheric monitoring in confined and semi-enclosed spaces; ensure fresh-air ventilation before and throughout work; install retrieval systems; supply and verify uninterrupted breathing air; prevent nitrogen-air mix-up through incompatible fittings and clear labeling; train employees and contractors on nitrogen asphyxiation hazards and confined-space entry requirements
- PSM elements implicated: Operating procedures (confined-space entry), Training, Mechanical integrity (monitoring equipment), Contractor management
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers suffocated in spaces filled with nitrogen because they couldn't smell it coming.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Nitrogen's invisibility is its primary hazard and is the common thread across 80 deaths. Workers and rescuers alike entered nitrogen atmospheres because there was no sensory warning. The deaths clustered around three failure modes: entering without testing, using nitrogen mistakenly instead of breathing air, and untrained rescuers repeating the victim's mistake. Contractors, who rotate through many worksites and may receive minimal site-specific training, accounted for the majority of fatalities.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. Before any work in, on, or adjacent to equipment that has been nitrogen-purged, require a documented atmospheric oxygen test by a competent person, even if the space is not normally classified as a confined space.
  2. Make nitrogen and breathing-air delivery connections physically incompatible; do not allow the same fitting on both systems; label all lines clearly and durably.
  3. Establish a standing rule that no one may enter an oxygen-deficient space to rescue a victim without donning supplied-air equipment first; train this rule repeatedly because the instinct to help overrides caution.

### Management of Change (CSB Safety Bulletin)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3562 | August 2001 (Safety Bulletin No. 2001-04-SB)
- Company | Location | Industry: CSB safety bulletin covering two incidents: Equilon Enterprises (Anacortes, Washington, 1998) and CONDEA Vista Company (Baltimore, Maryland, 1998)
- Incident date: Equilon: November 25, 1998; CONDEA Vista: October 13, 1998
- Hazard type: study/bulletin (flammable vapor release and fire; reactive chemical explosion)
- Materials: Equilon: heavy petroleum oil and volatile petroleum vapor in a delayed coking drum. CONDEA Vista: aluminum catalyst residue (sludge containing aluminum and aluminum chloride) reacting with water and steam to produce hydrogen and hydrogen chloride.
- What happened: The bulletin uses two 1998 incidents to demonstrate the importance of systematically managing operational deviations, not just planned equipment changes. At Equilon, a storm-caused power outage left a coking drum partially filled and required non-standard emptying. Without written procedures or engineering involvement, workers opened the drum while a pressurized pocket of hot volatile petroleum remained in the core, causing a fireball. At CONDEA Vista, a new catalyst type created unexpected sludge in a reactor; an improvised water-addition plan and verbal steam-injection instruction led to an explosion when the night shift applied steam continuously rather than in a brief burst.
- Consequences: Equilon: 6 fatalities (process supervisor and 5 operators and contractors caught in fire). CONDEA Vista: 4 injuries (2 employees, 1 contractor with first and second-degree burns; 1 contractor with back injury); approximately $13 million in property damage.
- Sequence (Equilon):
  1. Storm knocks out power and steam for extended period; coking drum is partially filled
  2. Tarry oil solidifies in piping, blocking normal steam injection route
  3. 1996 incident of similar type occurred; internal team recommended writing procedures for partially filled drums; task never completed
  4. No engineers present; supervisor and operators decide to open drum based on misleading external temperature readings
  5. Core of drum contains pressurized hot volatile petroleum not evident from surface temperature
  6. Bottom head lowered; hot petroleum vapor erupts; six workers caught in fire
- Sequence (CONDEA Vista):
  1. Process changed from aluminum chloride catalyst to powdered aluminum; sludge clogs reactor outlet
  2. Chemist and engineer develop water-addition plan to dissolve sludge; bench-scale test does not capture scale-up risks
  3. Temperature sensor in stagnant bypass line gives misleading readings; chemist and engineer conclude process is stable and leave
  4. Verbal instruction at shift handover misinterpreted: night supervisor orders continuous steam injection instead of brief burst
  5. Steam reacts with aluminum and aluminum chloride residue; reactor explodes
- Immediate cause: Equilon: opening a drum containing undiscovered pressurized hot petroleum vapor. CONDEA Vista: continuous steam injection into a sludge containing reactive aluminum compounds.
- Contributing factors:
  - Equilon: no written procedures for partially filled drums despite a 1996 recommendation; no engineering review of the non-standard emptying plan; misunderstanding of temperature sensor limitations (surface reading did not reflect hot core)
  - CONDEA Vista: bench-scale test did not account for scale-up of reactive energy; misleading temperature sensor location; verbal-only instruction created miscommunication between shifts; no written procedure for the non-standard operation
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Absence of MOC systems covering operational deviations and variances, not just physical alterations: neither company had a process for managing abnormal situations as "changes" requiring formal review
  - Failure to complete recommended corrective actions: Equilon's internal team in 1996 identified the need for procedures and the task was never finished
  - Failure to apply multidisciplinary expertise: engineers were absent from both the Equilon drum-opening decision and the CONDEA Vista scale-up evaluation
  - Written procedures not required for non-standard operations: both incidents involved improvised actions with verbal instructions only
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No written procedure for cooling and emptying partially filled coking drums (Equilon)
  - No formal hazard analysis of the drum-opening plan (Equilon)
  - No written procedure or formal authorization for water and steam addition to reactor (CONDEA Vista)
  - Temperature sensor placement gave misleading indication of process condition (both incidents)
  - Authorization and approval process for abnormal operational changes absent
- Key findings:
  - Neither incident required rapid emergency response; time was available for careful analysis; both were avoidable with more structured decision-making
  - MOC policies that cover only physical changes (equipment, interlocks) miss the operational deviations that cause accidents
  - Field personnel need defined process variable limits and training to recognize when deviations trigger MOC review
  - Multidisciplinary and specialized expertise is essential when analyzing deviations from normal operations
  - Absence of written instructions increased miscommunication risk at CONDEA Vista shift handover
- Recommendations (recipient to action): The bulletin is prescriptive guidance rather than a numbered-recommendation report. Core direction: review MOC policies to ensure they cover operational variances; define safe limits for process variables and train personnel to recognize significant deviations; apply hazard analysis techniques to deviations; authorize changes at a level matching their risk; communicate all non-standard procedures in writing; conduct periodic audits of MOC program effectiveness
- PSM elements implicated: Management of change, Operating procedures, Training, Process hazard analysis, Employee participation, Incident investigation
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers improvised a non-standard operation without engineers or written procedures and people died.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Both facilities had management-of-change systems that applied only to planned, physical alterations. When an abnormal situation arose (partial drum fill, clogged reactor), the MOC system did not recognize it as a "change." Decisions were made informally by whoever was available, without documentation, without hazard analysis, and without the engineering expertise needed to see the hidden hazard. The Equilon incident is especially instructive because the gap was identified internally in 1996; the corrective action was never completed, and the same scenario killed six workers two years later.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. Extend your MOC system to cover operational variances and abnormal situations, not just physical changes; any departure from the written procedure should be treated as a change requiring at minimum a documented review.
  2. When an abnormal situation requires a non-standard operation, require written approval at a management level commensurate with the hazard, and ensure the written plan is communicated at shift handover.
  3. Before relying on a process sensor to make a safety-critical decision (open a vessel, declare a system cool), understand where the sensor is located and what it can and cannot see; misleading readings are a recurring accident precursor.

### Positive Material Verification: Prevent Errors During Alloy Steel Systems Maintenance (CSB Safety Bulletin)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3564 | October 12, 2006 (Safety Bulletin No. 2005-04-B)
- Company | Location | Industry: BP Texas City Refinery | Texas City, Texas | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: July 28, 2005 (RHU fire; note: the devastating Isom explosion at the same facility occurred the same year, on March 23, 2005)
- Hazard type: study/bulletin (flammable vapor / VCE; material failure)
- Materials: Hydrogen gas at approximately 3,000 psi and 600 degrees F in the Resid Hydrotreater Unit (RHU); carbon steel elbow installed in place of required 1.25 percent chrome low-alloy steel; failure mechanism is high-temperature hydrogen attack (HTHA)
- What happened: During a scheduled heat exchanger maintenance overhaul in February 2005, a maintenance contractor accidentally switched a carbon steel pipe elbow with an alloy steel elbow during disassembly and reassembly. Carbon and alloy steel elbows were dimensionally identical and visually indistinguishable. The carbon steel elbow was not resistant to HTHA, while the alloy steel was designed for the service conditions. After approximately 3 months of operation (fewer than 3,000 hours), the carbon steel elbow was severely decarburized and fractured. Escaping hydrogen ignited, producing a large fireball that burned for two hours.
- Consequences: 1 minor injury (no fatalities from this incident); reported $30 million in property damage; shelter-in-place ordered for nearby residents
- Sequence:
  1. Winter 2005 scheduled maintenance: maintenance contractor removes three pipe elbows from RHU heat exchanger assembly for cleaning
  2. Elbows are dimensionally identical but have different material specifications: one is carbon steel (rated for cool, low-temperature hydrogen service) and two are 1.25 percent chrome low-alloy steel (required for hot, high-pressure hydrogen service)
  3. No labeling, tagging, or materials identification is required by BP; contractor is not informed of the material differences
  4. Contractor inadvertently installs carbon steel elbow 1 in the location of alloy steel elbow 3 (high-temperature hydrogen service position)
  5. No post-installation positive material identification (PMI) testing is performed
  6. Unit operates for approximately 3 months; HTHA progressively decarburizes and fissures the carbon steel elbow
  7. July 28, 2005: elbow ruptures; hydrogen ignites; major fire; $30 million damage
- Immediate cause: Carbon steel elbow installed in a high-temperature, high-pressure hydrogen service position where alloy steel was required; HTHA caused the elbow to fail catastrophically after approximately 3 months
- Contributing factors:
  - All three elbows were dimensionally identical to reduce construction costs; this design made inadvertent interchange possible without special controls
  - Carbon and alloy steel are visually indistinguishable; no labels or marking were applied before disassembly
  - BP did not require PMI testing during maintenance, only during new construction or warehouse receiving
  - BP did not inform the contractor that material differences existed and that special handling was required
  - The maintenance contractor did not independently control the disassembly, storage, and reassembly to prevent material mix-up
  - BP's material verification program had a gap: it applied to new materials only, not to existing materials being removed and reinstalled
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Human factors based design failure: the system was designed without considering the risk of inadvertent interchange during maintenance; design choice to make elbows dimensionally identical saved construction cost but transferred risk to maintenance operations
  - Inadequate positive material verification program: the maintenance quality control procedure did not require PMI testing for alloy steel components removed and reinstalled during maintenance, even when interchangeable with incompatible components; the gap was foreseeable and preventable
  - Communication failure between facility and contractor: critical material handling information was not passed to the maintenance contractor
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No pre-disassembly tagging or marking of elbows to record installed location
  - No PMI testing after installation to confirm correct materials
  - No requirement on BP or contractor procedures to verify alloy identity during maintenance
  - Design did not use different dimensions or connector types to make interchange physically impossible
- Key findings:
  - HTHA is a well-understood failure mechanism dating to the 1940s; the API Nelson Curves (API RP 941) define safe operating limits
  - The carbon steel elbow could withstand fewer than 3,000 hours of service in the high-temperature, high-pressure hydrogen position; it failed within that window
  - X-ray fluorescence (XRF) testing is a fast, inexpensive, non-destructive method to distinguish carbon steel from alloy steel; BP had XRF equipment but did not require its use during maintenance
  - The failure occurred at the same facility as the March 2005 Isom explosion, which killed 15 workers and injured 180
  - The BP material verification program covered new material receiving but had an explicit gap for maintenance operations
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - BP Texas City Refinery: revise maintenance quality control program to require PMI testing or other positive material verification for all critical-service alloy steel components removed and reinstalled during maintenance; inform work crews of special material handling precautions
  - JV Industrial Companies (contractor): develop or update written piping component installation quality control procedure to require PMI testing or other verification for all alloy steel piping components removed during maintenance
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical integrity (maintenance quality control, material verification), Process safety information (piping material specifications), Operating procedures (maintenance procedures), Training (contractor awareness), Management of change (human factors in design)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A contractor mixed up two pipe fittings during maintenance and the wrong one failed three months later, starting a major fire.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A design choice that saved construction cost by making three elbows of different materials physically identical, combined with a material verification program that only applied to new components and stopped at the warehouse door. The moment any of those elbows was removed for maintenance, there was no reliable way to put them back in the right places without special controls. No controls were required. The contractor was not told the materials were different. The design transferred the burden of preventing a foreseeable and catastrophic error entirely onto human memory during a routine maintenance task.
- Monday-morning checks:
  1. For any maintenance job that requires removing and reinstalling alloy steel components that are visually similar to non-compatible components, require positive material identification testing (XRF or equivalent) after reinstallation, not just at receiving.
  2. Before maintenance crews begin work, brief them explicitly on any components that have different material specifications and require them to tag each component with its installed location before removal begins.
  3. When reviewing system designs for new construction or modifications, evaluate whether dimensionally identical components of different material grades can be interchanged during maintenance; if so, either specify identical materials throughout or make the components physically different.
