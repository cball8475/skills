## 3567 - CAPECO Puerto Rico Petroleum Terminal Fire and Explosions

**CSB report:** 2010-06-I-PR (Investigation Report, 2015)
**Incident date:** October 23, 2009
**Location:** Bayamon, Puerto Rico
**Facility type:** Petroleum product bulk storage terminal
**Hazard type:** flammable vapor/VCE
**Fatalities:** 0 | **Injuries:** 3 (workers) | **Community impact:** 300+ structures damaged, 20,000+ residents evacuated, fires burned 60 hours
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, process-knowledge, operating-procedures, RAGAGEP, regulatory-gap

**What happened:** During normal operations at the Caribbean Petroleum Corporation (CAPECO) terminal, gasoline overflowed from Tank 409 due to an unreliable float gauge that indicated the tank was only 54% full when it was actually overfilling. No high-level alarm activated. Gasoline cascaded over the tank rim, generating a vapor cloud that found an ignition source and detonated in a massive vapor cloud explosion (VCE). The explosion and subsequent fires damaged 17 storage tanks and caused fires that burned for over 60 hours. Three workers sustained minor injuries. Over 300 structures in surrounding communities sustained damage.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- Tank 409's float gauge was unreliable and had a long history of malfunction; no redundant level measurement existed
- No high-level alarm or automatic shutoff was installed on the receiving tank
- CAPECO did not follow RAGAGEP for bulk liquid storage terminal design (NFPA 30, API standards)
- EPA Risk Management Program and OSHA PSM did not cover petroleum product terminals, creating a regulatory gap; the facility fell between regulatory programs
- Secondary containment dikes were not sufficient to contain product from multiple tanks
- No interlock to stop pumping when the tank approached a high level

**Key lessons:** Petroleum bulk storage terminals storing enormous quantities of flammable materials operated with none of the procedural or engineering safeguards required for PSM-covered processes. Level gauging must be reliable and redundant; single-point-of-failure gauging on large tanks storing flammable liquids is unacceptable. Regulatory gaps allowed high-hazard operations to escape systematic safety requirements.

## 3568 - NDK Crystal Explosion

**CSB report:** 2009-11-I-IL (Investigation Report, 2013)
**Incident date:** September 14, 2009
**Location:** Belvidere, Illinois
**Facility type:** Quartz crystal manufacturing
**Hazard type:** overpressure/mechanical failure
**Fatalities:** 1 | **Injuries:** 2 | **Community impact:** Debris ejected hundreds of feet; employee killed in adjacent building
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, process-knowledge, operating-procedures, RAGAGEP

**What happened:** A high-pressure autoclave used to grow synthetic quartz crystals catastrophically ruptured during normal production operations. The autoclave was operating at approximately 30,000 psi and 400 degrees C. The vessel rupture ejected heavy steel fragments hundreds of feet, killing one worker in an adjacent building and injuring two others. The vessel had recently been returned from external inspection; post-incident examination revealed pre-existing cracks at the closure threads.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- The autoclave closure threads had pre-existing stress corrosion cracks that went undetected
- The vessel inspection program was inadequate; the external inspection contractor did not use techniques capable of detecting the thread cracks
- There were no written procedures specifically addressing how to safely operate ultra-high-pressure autoclaves or how to inspect them for known failure modes
- NDK Crystal had no formal process hazard analysis for the autoclave operation
- No engineering controls (blast walls, exclusion zones) were in place to mitigate consequences of a rupture
- Industry standards for ultra-high-pressure autoclaves were limited and NDK had not adopted available guidance

**Key lessons:** Ultra-high-pressure vessels require inspection techniques matched to their known failure modes (e.g., thread cracking under stress corrosion). Written procedures and formal hazard analyses are required even for "routine" high-pressure operations. Absence of process safety management programs at non-PSM-covered facilities leaves workers exposed to uncontrolled catastrophic hazards.

## 3571 - Hot Work Safety Bulletin

**CSB report:** Safety Bulletin 2012-01-SB (2012)
**Incident date:** Multiple incidents reviewed (various dates)
**Location:** Various U.S. facilities
**Facility type:** Various industrial facilities
**Hazard type:** study/bulletin
**Fatalities:** varies across reviewed incidents | **Injuries:** varies | **Community impact:** none specific to bulletin
**PSM tags:** hot-work, safe-work-practices, operating-procedures, contractor-management

**What happened:** This CSB safety bulletin reviewed multiple explosions and fires caused by failures in hot work (welding, cutting, grinding) permit programs at industrial facilities. The bulletin examined incidents where hot work was performed in or near areas containing flammable materials, where permits were not issued, where hazard assessments were inadequate, or where atmospheric testing was not performed before or during the work.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- Hot work permits were not always required or consistently enforced across all areas
- Pre-work atmospheric testing was sometimes omitted or conducted only at floor level, missing heavier-than-air vapor accumulations
- Hot work was sometimes performed by contractors or maintenance workers who had not received facility-specific hazard information
- Monitoring during hot work was not always continuous; vapors can migrate into work areas after initial clearance
- Management oversight of the hot work permit system was insufficient; permits were sometimes signed by supervisors without field verification

**Key lessons:** A hot work permit system is only as strong as its enforcement and field verification. Continuous atmospheric monitoring is necessary when working near potential vapor sources. All workers - employees and contractors - must receive specific hazard training before performing hot work. Supervision must verify actual field conditions rather than relying on paperwork alone.

## 3572 - Tesoro Anacortes Refinery Heat Exchanger Rupture

**CSB report:** 2010-08-I-WA (Investigation Report, May 2014)
**Incident date:** April 2, 2010
**Location:** Anacortes, Washington
**Facility type:** Petroleum refinery
**Hazard type:** overpressure/mechanical failure
**Fatalities:** 7 | **Injuries:** 0 additional (all 7 victims died) | **Community impact:** Largest fatal U.S. refinery incident since BP Texas City 2005
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, PHA, RAGAGEP, operating-procedures, inherently-safer-design, regulatory-oversight

**What happened:** The E heat exchanger in the Naphtha Hydrotreater (NHT) unit at the Tesoro Anacortes Refinery catastrophically ruptured during a nonroutine startup of an adjacent heat exchanger bank. The E heat exchanger, constructed of carbon steel in 1971, had been severely damaged over decades by High Temperature Hydrogen Attack (HTHA). The rupture expelled hydrogen and naphtha at over 500 degrees F, which autoignited, killing all seven workers who were in the vicinity performing the startup. The B heat exchanger in the same bank also showed severe HTHA damage and was close to failure.

**Root causes and key findings:**
1. HTHA damaged the carbon steel E heat exchanger in the weld heat-affected zones because the welds were not post-weld heat-treated, making them high-stress regions susceptible to hydrogen diffusion and methane formation
2. The Nelson curves in API RP 941, used industry-wide to predict HTHA, were inaccurate: CSB modeling showed the ruptured section operated below the carbon steel Nelson curve yet still experienced HTHA, meaning the standard cannot reliably prevent failures
3. HTHA is extremely difficult to inspect for; damage must already exist to be detectable, and detection is highly skill-dependent
4. Tesoro's process hazard analyses failed to identify or control HTHA hazards; PHAs relied on incorrect assumptions about operating temperatures and HTHA risk
5. Heat exchanger flange leaks during startup had become normalized; the startup procedure was treated as routine despite a long history of fires and leaks
6. Tesoro did not control access during the hazardous nonroutine startup; seven workers were clustered around the heat exchangers with no defined roles
7. Neither Washington State DOSH nor OSHA had effectively identified the HTHA hazard during multiple PSM National Emphasis Program audits
8. Inherently safer design (upgrading from carbon steel to HTHA-resistant alloys) was not required by any standard or pursued by Tesoro, even though it would have eliminated the hazard

**Key lessons:** Industry inspection-based strategies cannot reliably prevent HTHA; material substitution (inherently safer design) is the only reliable control. Nelson curves in API RP 941 are empirically unreliable for carbon steel and must be revised. Normalization of hazardous conditions during nonroutine operations is a precursor to catastrophe. Regulators must develop competency to identify metallurgical damage mechanisms during PSM audits.

## 3573 - Macondo/Deepwater Horizon Explosion (BP)

**CSB report:** Joint Investigation Report (MBI/BSEE/USCG), 2011; CSB investigated independently
**Incident date:** April 20, 2010
**Location:** Gulf of Mexico, MC Block 252
**Facility type:** Offshore deepwater drilling rig
**Hazard type:** flammable vapor/VCE
**Fatalities:** 11 | **Injuries:** 17 | **Community impact:** Largest marine oil spill in U.S. history; 5 million barrels released; 87-day blowout
**PSM tags:** process-knowledge, PHA, mechanical-integrity, management-of-change, operating-procedures, emergency-response

**What happened:** A blowout occurred during the temporary abandonment of the Macondo well. During cementing and negative pressure testing operations, hydrocarbons migrated up the wellbore and onto the Deepwater Horizon rig. The crew failed to recognize the well control indicators and did not shut in the well. Hydrocarbons flowed onto the rig, ignited, and caused two catastrophic explosions that sank the rig and killed eleven workers. The blowout preventer failed to seal the well, resulting in the largest accidental marine oil spill in U.S. history.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- The negative pressure test was misinterpreted as a passed test when it actually indicated an influx
- The temporary abandonment procedure deviated from BP's own standards and industry practice by removing mud before setting a surface cement plug
- The cement design used a nitrogen-foamed slurry that was unstable; centralizers were inadequate, leading to channeling
- Halliburton and BP did not take adequate steps to test or verify cement integrity before the negative pressure test
- The blowout preventer's blind shear ram failed to seal the well due to the drill pipe being off-center and buckled
- Multiple independent barriers were bypassed or found ineffective; no single barrier failure caused the blowout
- Crew decision-making during the negative pressure test was influenced by cost and schedule pressures

**Key lessons:** Deepwater well control requires multiple independent barriers that are verified to be functional before removing any one of them. The negative pressure test is the last critical check before removing hydrostatic pressure; ambiguous results must stop operations. BOP reliability must be verified through rigorous testing, not assumed. Time and cost pressures must not override well control protocols.

## 3574 - Horsehead Corporation Monaca Zinc Refinery Explosion

**CSB report:** 2010-08-I-PA (Investigation Report, 2015, prepared by CSB-contracted expert)
**Incident date:** July 22, 2010
**Location:** Monaca, Pennsylvania
**Facility type:** Zinc refinery (secondary/recycled feed, electrothermic distillation columns)
**Hazard type:** overpressure/mechanical failure
**Fatalities:** 2 | **Injuries:** 3 | **Community impact:** Plant area significantly damaged
**PSM tags:** process-knowledge, operating-procedures, mechanical-integrity, PHA

**What happened:** A distillation column (Column B) at Horsehead Corporation's zinc refinery exploded, killing two workers and injuring three others. The column used the New Jersey distillation process in which stacked silicon carbide trays fractionate zinc at approximately 900-980 degrees C. Investigation showed the column sump partially blocked, causing liquid zinc to back-fill and flood trays up the column. With 7+ trays flooded, the zinc column acted like a pressure cooker: liquid zinc under hydrostatic pressure raised the boiling point above the column operating temperature, creating an unstable energy store. When the tray walls eventually failed, the sudden release of pressure caused explosive decompression and a surge of vapor and liquid zinc that blew out the combustion chamber wall and ignited.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- The column sump blocked (possibly by solidified zinc or accumulated dross), preventing normal bottom drainage
- Back-filling of liquid zinc into the column trays was a recognized hazard in zinc distillation but the column lacked instrumentation to detect flooding
- Operators were working from log sheets and temperature indicators, but the instruments did not clearly signal the developing abnormal condition
- The column heat-up procedure relied on experience and operator judgment without formal safeguards
- Post-incident review found that the 2007 refinery column safety survey had identified safety gaps that were not fully resolved
- The CSB expert analysis concluded that alternative scenarios (e.g., water ingress) were less likely than the sump blockage/pressure cooker mechanism

**Key lessons:** High-temperature molten metal processes can accumulate hazardous stored energy in non-obvious ways. Instrumentation must be adequate to detect abnormal liquid accumulation inside distillation columns. Known hazard scenarios (sump blockage, flooding) must be addressed in procedures and formal hazard analyses. Historical safety surveys that identify gaps must be tracked to resolution.

## 3575 - Millard Refrigerated Services Ammonia Release

**CSB report:** 2010-07-I-AL (Investigation Report, 2014)
**Incident date:** August 23, 2010
**Location:** Theodore, Alabama
**Facility type:** Cold storage and refrigerated warehousing
**Hazard type:** toxic gas release
**Fatalities:** 4 | **Injuries:** 32 | **Community impact:** Shelter-in-place for surrounding community; roads closed
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, operating-procedures, emergency-response, contractor-management, RAGAGEP

**What happened:** During cleaning operations at the Millard Refrigerated Services cold storage facility, contract workers using a high-pressure water hose struck an ammonia refrigeration pipe. The water jet cut through the 1-inch-diameter steel pipe, releasing a massive cloud of anhydrous ammonia. Four contract workers who were in the immediate area were overcome and died. Thirty-two additional workers and emergency responders were injured. Ammonia levels inside the facility reached immediately dangerous to life and health (IDLH) concentrations.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- The ammonia pipe struck by the water jet was incorrectly believed to be a water line; no hazard identification was performed before the cleaning operation
- Pipe labeling was inadequate; ammonia lines were not clearly marked or color-coded throughout the facility
- No safe work practice or hot work/line work permit procedure covered pressure washing near process piping
- Contract workers received no facility-specific hazard information about ammonia refrigeration system locations or emergency procedures
- Emergency response was hampered because responders initially had incomplete information about the chemical released
- Millard had not conducted a process hazard analysis for its ammonia refrigeration system despite being covered by PSM (>10,000 lb ammonia)

**Key lessons:** Contract workers performing routine tasks near process piping must receive facility hazard information before work begins. All process piping must be clearly and permanently labeled. Work near process piping requires a permit that includes field verification of pipe contents. PSM coverage must be exercised with rigor even in non-traditional settings such as cold storage facilities.

## 3576 - US Ink / Sun Chemical Flash Fire

**CSB report:** 2012-02-I-NJ (Case Study, 2014)
**Incident date:** April 12, 2012
**Location:** East Rutherford, New Jersey
**Facility type:** Printing ink manufacturing
**Hazard type:** flammable vapor/VCE
**Fatalities:** 0 | **Injuries:** 7 (1 critical) | **Community impact:** Shelter-in-place; community air quality concerns
**PSM tags:** PHA, mechanical-integrity, operating-procedures, safe-work-practices, RAGAGEP

**What happened:** Workers at the US Ink facility in East Rutherford were mixing a batch of ink when flammable solvent vapors from an open mixing tank ignited, causing a flash fire that injured seven workers. The mixing operation involved adding petroleum distillate solvents to resins in large open vessels. Vapors accumulated in the work area and were ignited by an unidentified ignition source. One worker sustained critical burns.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- Mixing operations with flammable solvents were conducted in open vessels, allowing vapors to accumulate at floor level
- The facility did not have adequate ventilation in the mixing area to keep solvent vapor concentrations below the lower flammability limit
- Ignition source control was inadequate; the area was not classified as a hazardous electrical area consistent with the flammable materials present
- No process hazard analysis had been conducted for the mixing operations despite the significant flammability hazards
- Workers were not provided with or required to wear appropriate flash-fire-rated personal protective equipment
- The facility had a history of near-miss vapor release incidents that had not prompted safety system improvements

**Key lessons:** Open-vessel mixing of flammable solvents requires engineering controls (closed systems, local exhaust ventilation, or inert blanketing) to prevent vapor accumulation. Hazardous area electrical classification must be applied wherever flammable concentrations can occur. Near-miss incidents involving flammable vapors must trigger PHA review and corrective action, not normalization.

## 3579 - Freedom Industries Chemical Release - Elk River, WV

**CSB report:** 2014-01-I-WV (Investigation Report, February 2017)
**Incident date:** January 9, 2014
**Location:** Charleston, West Virginia
**Facility type:** Bulk chemical aboveground storage terminal
**Hazard type:** toxic gas release
**Fatalities:** 0 | **Injuries:** 300+ sought medical treatment | **Community impact:** Do Not Use order for 300,000 residents; water supply disrupted up to 9 days
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, regulatory-gap, emergency-response, risk-communication

**What happened:** At the Freedom Industries bulk storage terminal on the Elk River, approximately 11,000 gallons of crude 4-methylcyclohexanemethanol (MCHM) and polyglycol ethers (PPH, stripped) leaked from Tank 396 through two holes (0.75 and 0.4 inches in diameter) caused by pitting corrosion on the tank floor interior. The chemical migrated through deteriorated secondary containment and an underground culvert to the Elk River, then flowed 1.5 miles downstream to the West Virginia American Water Kanawha Valley Treatment Plant intake. The water treatment process could not fully remove the chemicals. A Do Not Use order was issued to approximately 300,000 residents for up to nine days.

**Root causes and key findings:**
1. No inspection or maintenance program existed at Freedom for the storage tanks; CSB found no documentation of prior inspections of tank 396 in Freedom's or the prior owner's records that would have identified interior corrosion
2. Tank 396, constructed in the 1940s, had severe pitting corrosion on the interior floor that had never been detected because no API 653 inspection had been conducted
3. Secondary containment was in a deteriorated condition; Freedom was aware of the failing dike walls but did not repair them
4. No leak detection system was in place to provide early warning of a tank release
5. Freedom failed to disclose that a second chemical (PPH, stripped) was present in the tank until 12 days after the incident, hampering public health response
6. Crude MCHM was not regulated under any chemical facility hazard program (not PSM, not RMP); no regulatory program required AST inspection at this facility
7. West Virginia's pre-incident regulatory framework did not require periodic inspection of above-ground storage tanks
8. WVAW did not have source water protection plans adequate for responding to upstream chemical releases, and the water treatment process could not treat MCHM

**Key lessons:** Aboveground storage tanks storing chemicals near drinking water intakes require mandatory, periodic inspection programs (e.g., API 653). Secondary containment must be maintained and verified functional. Chemical facilities must immediately disclose all chemicals involved in a release to enable effective public health response. Drinking water utilities must develop source water protection and emergency response plans that account for upstream chemical release scenarios.

## 3580 - Educational Demonstrations Safety Bulletin

**CSB report:** Safety Bulletin 2014-01-SB (2014)
**Incident date:** Multiple incidents reviewed (2012-2014)
**Location:** Various educational institutions
**Facility type:** Educational institutions (schools, universities)
**Hazard type:** study/bulletin
**Fatalities:** 2 (combined across incidents) | **Injuries:** Multiple | **Community impact:** Students and faculty injured
**PSM tags:** safe-work-practices, training, inherently-safer-design

**What happened:** This CSB safety bulletin reviewed multiple incidents in which fire and explosion hazards arose from chemistry demonstrations conducted by teachers and professors for students. The "Rainbow Fire" demonstration, in which different metal salts dissolved in methanol are ignited to produce colored flames, was involved in multiple incidents where the methanol container ignited, spraying burning liquid on students. Other incidents involved energetic reactions conducted without adequate engineering controls or substitution of safer procedures.

**Root causes and key findings:**
- Demonstrations involving flammable liquids were conducted without adequate barriers between the burning material and observers (students)
- Methanol is a nearly invisible flame material; re-lighting an apparently extinguished methanol demonstration can cause the bulk supply container to ignite
- No standard of practice existed for conducting chemical demonstrations safely in educational settings
- Instructors were often performing demonstrations that had been passed down without formal hazard review
- Safer alternatives (e.g., individual small-volume demonstrations, non-flammable substitutes) were available but not uniformly adopted
- Some institutions lacked emergency response planning for chemical incidents in classrooms

**Key lessons:** Flammable liquid demonstrations must use the smallest quantity needed and must never involve bulk containers of fuel near an open flame. Safer alternatives should replace hazardous demonstrations when feasible. Educational institutions must formally review and approve demonstration procedures using hazard analysis methods. Emergency response planning must include classroom and laboratory chemical incidents.

## 3581 - DuPont La Porte Toxic Chemical Release

**CSB report:** 2015-01-I-TX (Investigation Report, June 2019)
**Incident date:** November 15, 2014
**Location:** La Porte, Texas
**Facility type:** Chemical manufacturing facility (insecticide/Lannate production)
**Hazard type:** toxic gas release
**Fatalities:** 4 | **Injuries:** 0 additional | **Community impact:** Odor complaints from surrounding community; facility ultimately closed Lannate unit
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, PHA, MOC, operating-procedures, emergency-response, process-safety-culture, safe-work-practices

**What happened:** Approximately 24,000 pounds of highly toxic methyl mercaptan released from the Lannate insecticide production unit at DuPont La Porte. The release killed four workers inside the manufacturing building (three operators and one shift supervisor) from a combination of asphyxia and acute methyl mercaptan inhalation. The release resulted from a chain of events: an inadvertent chemical dilution forced a unit shutdown; during restart attempts, a methyl mercaptan hydrate formed in feed piping; troubleshooting efforts to clear the blockage without written procedures caused liquid methyl mercaptan to flow into waste gas vent header piping; workers opened drain valves to remove accumulation, releasing methyl mercaptan into the manufacturing building, which lacked an adequate toxic gas detection system and had non-functional roof ventilation fans.

**Root causes and key findings:**
1. The NRS incinerator capital project (2011) had a flawed engineering design that did not address liquid accumulation in the waste gas vent header piping; daily instructions required workers to drain liquids inside the building without evaluating safety hazards
2. DuPont La Porte's PHA identified methyl mercaptan hydrate formation as a hazard but never implemented safeguards (e.g., heat tracing, written dissociation procedures)
3. No MOC was performed for the vent header piping alignment changes made during troubleshooting
4. The manufacturing building ventilation was designed only for flammability control (25% LEL basis), not for toxic exposure; neither rooftop fan was operational due to a delayed maintenance work order
5. The toxic gas detection system alarm setpoints were set well above safe exposure limits; alarms were communicated only verbally; no visual or audible field alarms existed
6. Emergency response was disorganized; the mini-pumper truck was not operational; search for missing workers involved entry into a potentially explosive atmosphere
7. DuPont La Porte had not formally assessed process safety culture; its Safety Perception Survey addressed personal safety only, missing significant process safety weaknesses
8. An employee incentive program may have discouraged reporting of near misses and incidents

**Key lessons:** Toxic gas detection alarm setpoints must be set at levels protective of worker health, with audible and visual alarms in the field. Building ventilation for facilities handling toxic chemicals must be designed to control toxic exposure, not just flammability. Troubleshooting under time pressure is a high-risk activity requiring written procedures, rigorous hazard assessment, and MOC review before changing process configurations. Process safety culture must be formally and independently assessed.

## 3582 - ExxonMobil Torrance Refinery FCC Unit Explosion

**CSB report:** 2015-05-I-CA (Investigation Report, May 2017)
**Incident date:** February 18, 2015
**Location:** Torrance, California
**Facility type:** Petroleum refinery
**Hazard type:** overpressure/mechanical failure
**Fatalities:** 0 | **Injuries:** 0 (near-miss with 4 workers) | **Community impact:** Catalyst dust release to community; near-miss threatened HF alkylation unit with potentially catastrophic consequences
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, PHA, operating-procedures, MOC, safe-work-practices, regulatory-oversight

**What happened:** An explosion in the electrostatic precipitator (ESP) of the fluid catalytic cracking (FCC) unit at ExxonMobil's Torrance refinery released a plume of spent catalyst dust into the surrounding community. Four workers narrowly escaped injury. Critically, a pressure slug that reached the ESP also traveled toward the nearby hydrofluoric acid alkylation unit, lifting a pressure relief device and depositing it near the unit. Had the HF alkylation unit been breached, a catastrophic release of highly toxic HF acid could have occurred, potentially affecting hundreds of thousands of nearby residents.

**Root causes and key findings:**
1. A slide valve check valve (SCSV), part of the FCC unit's catalyst transfer system, failed to seat properly, allowing hydrocarbons to back-flow through the regenerator and into the ESP
2. ExxonMobil had placed the FCC unit in "Safe Park" mode (a non-standard operating condition) but had not established safe operating limits for this mode
3. In 2012, ExxonMobil had implemented a Variance to the written operating procedure that reduced steam flow to a riser steam purge; this Variance was carried forward without adequate hazard analysis to a 2015 restart, even though it had been identified internally as increasing risk
4. No MOC was performed when the 2012 Variance was applied to the 2015 restart conditions, although process conditions differed
5. The SCSV had known reliability issues and prior failures; the inspection and reliability program for this critical valve was inadequate
6. The proximity of the HF alkylation unit to the FCC unit - and the potential for a credible scenario to threaten the HF unit - was not adequately evaluated in process hazard analyses
7. Cal/OSHA PSM regulations were less stringent than federal PSM in several key areas

**Key lessons:** Safe operating limits must be defined for all operating modes including non-standard configurations; operating without defined limits in any mode is unacceptable. Procedure variances must not be carried forward to different operational contexts without new MOC review. Critical valve reliability must be validated before placing the process in a mode that depends on valve function. PHA must evaluate credible cascading scenarios between adjacent high-hazard units, especially when one unit stores catastrophic-consequence chemicals like HF.

## 3583 - Tesoro Martinez Refinery Sulfuric Acid Releases

**CSB report:** 2014-04-I-CA (Case Study, 2016)
**Incident date:** February 12 and March 5, 2014 (two incidents)
**Location:** Martinez, California
**Facility type:** Petroleum refinery (sulfuric acid alkylation unit)
**Hazard type:** toxic gas release
**Fatalities:** 0 | **Injuries:** 4 (workers burned by sulfuric acid) | **Community impact:** Acid mist releases; shelter-in-place advisory
**PSM tags:** mechanical-integrity, operating-procedures, process-safety-culture, learning-from-incidents, RAGAGEP

**What happened:** Two sulfuric acid releases occurred at Tesoro's Martinez refinery alkylation unit within 22 days of each other, injuring four workers. The first release on February 12 occurred when 98% sulfuric acid sprayed from a leak in the acid settler unit. The second release on March 5 involved acid from a different location in the same unit. CSB investigation revealed a pattern: 12 sulfuric acid releases had occurred at the unit between 2010 and 2014, yet the underlying mechanical integrity and operational issues were never systematically addressed. The 1999 fatal Tosco Avon refinery fire (also in Martinez) had identified similar lessons, but Tesoro's Martinez refinery had not applied those lessons.

**Root causes and key findings:**
1. Tesoro Martinez had experienced 12 acid releases in four years but had not analyzed the pattern to identify systemic causes; each incident was treated independently
2. The alkylation unit's mechanical integrity program did not adequately identify, monitor, and control sulfuric acid corrosion on vulnerable components
3. Operating procedures for the acid settler and associated piping did not adequately address the hazards of working near concentrated sulfuric acid
4. Workers did not consistently use required personal protective equipment (acid suit, face shield) when working near the acid system
5. The refinery had not systematically reviewed and applied lessons from the 1999 Tosco Avon fire at the same type of alkylation unit less than 20 miles away
6. Process safety culture at Tesoro Martinez did not effectively treat process incidents as early warning signals requiring systemic investigation

**Key lessons:** A pattern of repeated releases of the same chemical from the same unit type is a serious leading indicator that demands root-cause investigation at the system level, not repeated case-by-case response. Lessons from similar facilities and similar incidents must be systematically reviewed and applied. PPE must be treated as the last line of defense, not the primary safeguard; engineering controls for acid exposure must be evaluated and implemented first. Organizational learning from incidents must be a managed, structured process.

## 3584 - Delaware City Refinery Flash Fire

**CSB report:** 2015-01-I-DE (Safety Bulletin, May 2017)
**Incident date:** November 29, 2015
**Location:** Delaware City, Delaware
**Facility type:** Petroleum refinery (alkylation unit)
**Hazard type:** fire
**Fatalities:** 0 | **Injuries:** 1 | **Community impact:** Minimal
**PSM tags:** operating-procedures, safe-work-practices, MOC, mechanical-integrity

**What happened:** A flash fire occurred at the Delaware City Refining Company (DCRC) alkylation unit during an equipment preparation for maintenance activity. Workers were attempting to de-inventory and decontaminate a depropanizer column before planned maintenance. During the operation, they discovered that isolation valves were leaking. Rather than stopping and reassessing the hazard, workers drained hydrocarbon materials containing flammable components into the sewer system. The flammable vapors ignited, causing a flash fire that injured one worker. This incident followed two previous incidents at the same facility on August 21 and August 28, 2015.

**Root causes and key findings:**
1. Equipment preparation for maintenance is a non-routine, high-risk task; the procedure used did not include specific steps for all equipment that needed to be prepared and did not address hazards introduced by leaking isolation valves
2. The isolation plan relied on a single newly installed block valve to control hazardous energy; single-valve isolation is inadequate for hazardous material service
3. When the isolation plan failed (leaking valves), workers modified the approach on the fly without initiating a formal hazard review (MOC) of the changed work scope
4. Draining materials that may contain hydrocarbons into sewers or other systems not designed for flammable materials - especially when ignition sources are present - introduced an unrecognized ignition pathway
5. Pre-planning and hazard identification were not required before initiating equipment preparation tasks; the work began without a formal assessment of hazards that might be introduced
6. The DCRC had experienced two prior incidents in the same time period, suggesting systemic safety management weaknesses

**Key lessons:** Equipment preparation for maintenance is as hazardous as the maintenance itself and requires equal rigor in hazard identification and procedure development. Double-block-and-bleed or positive isolation (blind flanging) must replace single-valve isolation for hazardous energy control. When field conditions differ from the expected isolation scenario, work must stop and a hazard reassessment must occur before continuing. Draining hydrocarbons to sewer systems with potential ignition sources is an unacceptable practice.

## Cross-Cutting Pattern Observations

1. **Normalization of deviation is a systemic precursor.** Tesoro Anacortes (3572), Tesoro Martinez (3583), and DuPont La Porte (3581) all show that when known hazardous conditions recur repeatedly without incident, they are treated as normal. In Anacortes, frequent heat exchanger leaks during startup became expected. In Martinez, twelve acid releases went without pattern analysis. In La Porte, workers smelled methyl mercaptan routinely and normalized it. The absence of immediate harm does not mean the hazard is controlled.

2. **Management of change failures precipitate catastrophic events.** ExxonMobil Torrance (3582) carried a 2012 procedure variance into a 2015 restart without a new MOC. DuPont La Porte (3581) made multiple piping reconfigurations during troubleshooting without MOC review. Tesoro Anacortes (3572) used legacy carbon steel heat exchangers without a formal reassessment of metallurgical damage mechanisms as operating history accumulated. MOC must cover not just equipment changes but procedure changes, operational mode changes, and the passage of time under changing operating conditions.

3. **Regulatory gaps leave high-hazard facilities unprotected.** CAPECO (3567) operated a petroleum terminal with enormous flammable inventories outside both PSM and RMP coverage. Freedom Industries (3579) stored chemicals near a drinking water intake with no mandatory inspection program. NDK Crystal (3568) ran ultra-high-pressure autoclaves without formal process safety requirements. The absence of regulatory coverage does not reduce hazard; it removes the systematic forcing function for hazard control.

4. **Inspection-based mechanical integrity strategies cannot replace hazard elimination.** Tesoro Anacortes (3572) shows that HTHA in carbon steel can occur below the Nelson curve and cannot be reliably detected by inspection before failure. Freedom Industries (3579) demonstrates that a tank corroding from the inside can develop through-holes with no external indication. NDK Crystal (3568) shows that thread cracking in ultra-high-pressure vessels can evade standard inspection. Material substitution and engineering design changes that eliminate the hazard are more reliable than detection-and-repair.

5. **Emergency response deficiencies compound initial releases into larger disasters.** DuPont La Porte (3581) had a non-operational emergency response vehicle, no audible field alarms, and workers entered a potentially explosive atmosphere searching for victims. CAPECO (3567) had no coordinated emergency response plan for a multi-tank fire, contributing to the 60-hour burn duration. Freedom Industries (3579) suffered delayed public health communication because Freedom did not disclose a second chemical for 12 days. Emergency response plans must be exercised, equipment must be maintained, and chemical disclosure to responders must be immediate and complete.

6. **Process safety culture failures manifest as documentation, audit, and corrective-action weaknesses.** DuPont La Porte (3581) conducted safety culture surveys focused on personal safety, missing process safety deficiencies entirely. Tesoro Martinez (3583) failed to learn systematically from 12 prior incidents. Tesoro Anacortes (3572) had prior internal damage mechanism reviews that identified HTHA concerns but did not drive corrective action. A genuine process safety culture requires formal assessment, tracking of open items, and accountability for closure.

7. **Proximity hazards between units demand cross-unit PHA.** ExxonMobil Torrance (3582) came dangerously close to a catastrophic HF acid release because the FCC unit explosion sent a pressure slug toward the alkylation unit - a scenario that was not evaluated in any PHA. CAPECO (3567) had 17 tanks damage cascaded from a single ignition. Process hazard analyses must systematically evaluate what happens if the incident scenario propagates to adjacent units or systems, especially when the adjacent system contains catastrophic-consequence chemicals.
