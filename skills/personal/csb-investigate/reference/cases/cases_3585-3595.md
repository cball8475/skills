### Enterprise Products Pascagoula Gas Plant Fires and Explosions

- Archive id | Final report date: 3585 | February 13, 2019 (Case Study, No. 2016-02-I-MS)
- Company | Location | Industry: Enterprise Products Midstream | Pascagoula, Mississippi | Natural gas liquids midstream processing
- Incident date: June 27, 2016
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Methane, ethane, propane, and other hydrocarbons (natural gas liquids stream from cryogenic separation)
- What happened: Sometime before 11:22 p.m. on June 27, 2016, a brazed aluminum heat exchanger (BAHX) in the A-Train of the Pascagoula Gas Plant suffered a catastrophic rupture due to accumulated thermal fatigue. The failure released large quantities of flammable hydrocarbons into the process area. The vapors ignited, initiating a series of 13 separate fires and explosions that shut the plant down for almost six months. Two night-shift workers were uninjured; the event occurred during low staffing.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities, 0 injuries. Property loss: $10.4 million in fire response expenses plus $7.1 million non-cash loss of damaged assets. Nearby residents self-evacuated; community members reported feeling uninformed about the hazard.
- Sequence: (1) Operators were shutting down natural gas liquids production due to a downstream pipeline problem. (2) The shutdown involved non-routine thermal cycling of the BAHX. (3) At 11:22 p.m., a cap sheet on the A-Cold Side Reboiler (ACSR) BAHX failed due to pre-existing thermal fatigue cracks. (4) A large hydrocarbon release occurred. (5) Vapors ignited, causing the first explosion and fire. (6) Cascading failures of 12 additional pieces of piping and equipment followed over the course of the incident.
- Immediate cause: Catastrophic failure of the ACSR brazed aluminum heat exchanger caused by accumulated thermal fatigue cracks, releasing flammable hydrocarbons that ignited.
- Contributing factors:
  - The BAHX had a prior history of interpass leaks repaired in 2015; that repair may have left blocked layers with unvented pressure.
  - Process conditions at PGP subjected BAHXs to repeated rapid temperature cycling from compressor shutdowns, erratic flows, and upstream curtailment events, exceeding manufacturer guidance on thermal cycling rates.
  - Temperature sensors were not optimally placed to detect early signs of thermal fatigue.
  - Industry data on BAHX failures was not shared across operators, limiting collective learning.
  - Emergency community notification relied on passive means with no social media integration, leaving nearby residents without timely information.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Absence of a reliable mechanical integrity program specifically covering BAHX thermal fatigue as a credible damage mechanism requiring proactive management.
  - Inadequate hazard assessment: PGP recognized thermal fatigue as a potential mechanism but did not translate that recognition into controlled operating procedures or inspection standards sufficient to prevent a catastrophic failure.
  - Industry-wide gap: no consensus standard required BAHX operators to track and share thermal cycling data or set enforceable inspection intervals for thermal fatigue.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No inspection protocol to detect accumulated thermal fatigue in the BAHX before failure.
  - 2015 repair did not confirm through-wall crack absence in adjacent layers.
  - No automated shutdown triggered by BAHX temperature rate-of-change anomalies.
  - Community notification plan did not include social media or proactive outreach.
- Key findings:
  - The most likely failure scenario was thermal fatigue cracks in a parting sheet between BAHX layers, leading to rapid release when pressure differential caused sudden cap-sheet separation.
  - Industry guidance recommends heating/cooling rate limits for BAHXs, but midstream operators report these limits are unrealistic in practice, indicating a gap between guidance and field conditions.
  - Four other BAHX catastrophic failure events had occurred industry-wide with little cross-operator learning due to data confidentiality norms.
  - A database for sharing BAHX operational data does not exist; CSB recommended its creation.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - API: Develop or revise ANSI/API Standard 668 to include thermal fatigue guidance, optimal sensor placement, and safe venting requirements for repaired BAHX layers.
  - GPA Midstream Association: Revise BAHX technical bulletin to include catastrophic failure risk from thermal fatigue, sensor placement guidance, and venting requirements.
  - GPA Midstream Association: Develop an anonymous BAHX operational data database for industry-wide learning and life-expectancy analysis.
  - Jackson County LEPC: Define and publish community notification methods, including social media, for chemical incident alerts.
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical integrity, process hazard analysis, process safety information, emergency response, community notification
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A heat exchanger leak triggered a fire and explosion at a gas plant.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The plant lacked a program to track cumulative thermal fatigue damage in its BAHXs. Years of normal operating cycles, compressor trips, and curtailments gradually cracked the exchanger's aluminum parting sheets. Industry norms of data confidentiality meant no operator could learn from failures at other plants. The 2015 repair closed leaks without confirming the exchanger's structural integrity, leaving a ticking failure in place.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. Does your BAHX or heat-transfer equipment maintenance program specifically address cumulative fatigue from thermal cycling, not just corrosion or single-event shock? If not, review operating records for evidence of repeated rapid temperature swings.
  2. When a vessel or heat exchanger is repaired by blocking off damaged sections, do you require a post-repair integrity test confirming no through-wall defects remain in adjacent layers before returning to service?
  3. Does your emergency notification plan include a social media or automated alert mechanism for the surrounding community, and have you tested it within the last year?

### Sunoco Nederland Terminal Flash Fire and Explosion

- Archive id | Final report date: 3586 | September 27, 2022 (Investigation Report, No. 2016-03-I-TX)
- Company | Location | Industry: Sunoco Partners Marketing and Terminals (now Energy Transfer) | Nederland, Texas | Crude oil terminal and storage
- Incident date: August 12, 2016
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Crude oil (Category 1 flammable liquid, flashpoint below 73.4 F, LEL 0.8%, UEL 8%)
- What happened: During piping modification work at Sunoco's crude oil terminal, contractor L-Con was welding on a 30-inch piping segment that had been isolated with CARBER plug tools but still contained residual crude oil. When welding began on the night shift, the crude oil inside the pipe ignited, causing a flash fire and explosion. The explosion created overpressure inside the pipe, ejecting the CARBER isolation tools and residual crude oil from both ends of the pipe segment, striking nearby workers.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities, 7 workers injured (impact and burn injuries). No off-site impact. Localized to the immediate work area.
- Sequence: (1) Sunoco hired L-Con to install new aboveground storage tanks and modify existing piping at the terminal. (2) L-Con subcontracted CARBER to cut and isolate piping sections; the agreement acknowledged that the piping would not be free of residual crude oil. (3) On August 11, CARBER cut and isolated the 30-inch pipe section using proprietary plug tools, with crude oil remaining inside. (4) On August 12, night shift welding began. (5) Residual crude oil vapors in the pipe ignited. (6) The resulting explosion ejected the isolation plugs and crude oil, injuring seven workers.
- Immediate cause: Hot work (welding) was performed on equipment containing an explosive atmosphere of crude oil vapor and air, which ignited when exposed to the welding arc.
- Contributing factors:
  - Sunoco's hot work procedure did not clearly prohibit hot work on equipment containing flammable material, nor explain how to clean or inert equipment before welding.
  - The pipe was not purged with inert gas, filled with water, or cleaned of residual crude before hot work commenced.
  - Weather conditions at the time contributed to the presence of flammable vapors inside the pipe.
  - Atmospheric monitoring was placed at the exterior welding location but did not detect the vapors confined inside the pipe.
  - CARBER's isolation tools, while isolating the work zone from the rest of the piping, confined the flammable atmosphere inside the section being welded.
  - Multiple parties (Sunoco, L-Con, CARBER) each failed to effectively implement their own procedures.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Inadequate hot work safety program at Sunoco: the procedure allowed hot work to proceed without ensuring equipment internal atmosphere was non-flammable.
  - Fragmented responsibility across three contractor tiers (Sunoco, CIS, L-Con, CARBER) without clear accountability for ensuring internal pipe conditions before hot work.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Purging with inert gas (nitrogen) before hot work was not performed.
  - Pipe was not flooded with water to eliminate flammable atmosphere.
  - Internal atmospheric testing of the confined pipe section was not performed.
  - Written procedures did not require verification of internal atmosphere before commencing hot work.
- Key findings:
  - OSHA and NFPA 51B both require hot work not be performed on equipment containing flammable material; Sunoco's procedures did not adequately reflect these requirements.
  - Purging the pipe with nitrogen would have prevented the incident.
  - Similar incidents at both Sunoco and L-Con had occurred previously, indicating a systemic pattern rather than an isolated failure.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - CSB provided key lessons to industry (no formal recommendations issued): (1) ensure internal pipe atmosphere is not flammable before hot work by purging, cleaning, or flooding with water; (2) thoroughly identify all flammable and combustible sources in the hot work zone; (3) use written permits specifying all hazards, conduct effective atmospheric monitoring including confined spaces, and provide thorough contractor training.
- PSM elements implicated: Hot work permitting, contractor management, process hazard analysis, operating procedures, pre-startup safety review
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Welders lit a fire in a pipe that still had crude oil in it.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Three-tier contractor arrangements diffused accountability. Sunoco's hot work procedure was written around exterior fire hazards, not interior confined-space flammable atmospheres. The agreement between Sunoco and L-Con acknowledged up front that the pipe would contain residual crude oil, yet no party's procedure required verifying or eliminating the internal flammable atmosphere before hot work. A technically simple fix (nitrogen purge or water fill) was never required because no procedure identified the internal pipe as the hazard.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. When issuing hot work permits for cutting or welding on any pipe or vessel that has been in flammable service, does your permit explicitly require verification of the internal atmosphere, not just the surrounding air? Physical isolation alone does not eliminate internal flammable content.
  2. In multi-contractor work, is there a single named entity responsible for confirming pre-job safety conditions (such as internal pipe cleanliness) before work begins, or is that responsibility left to assumption?
  3. Review your hot work procedure for the phrase "ensure equipment is clean or inerted before hot work" - if it is absent or vague, add it with specific verification steps (atmospheric test, certificate of cleanliness, or witness signature).

### MGPI Processing Chemical Release, Atchison, Kansas

- Archive id | Final report date: 3588 | December 2017 (Case Study, No. 2017-01-I-KS)
- Company | Location | Industry: MGPI Processing, Inc. (MGP Ingredients subsidiary) and Harcros Chemicals | Atchison, Kansas | Food-grade alcohol and starch manufacturing
- Incident date: October 21, 2016
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Sulfuric acid (30% H2SO4) and sodium hypochlorite (12.5% NaClO); reaction produced chlorine gas (Cl2) and other toxic compounds including sulfur dioxide
- What happened: A Harcros tanker driver arrived to deliver sulfuric acid and connected the discharge hose to the sodium hypochlorite fill line instead of the sulfuric acid fill line. The operator had unlocked only the correct fill line's dust cap but left the area before verifying the driver's connection. The driver connected to the first unlocked fitting he saw. Approximately 4,700 gallons of sulfuric acid were pumped into the sodium hypochlorite tank, generating a toxic chlorine-containing vapor cloud that spread into the surrounding community.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities. 6 people hospitalized (1 MGPI employee, 5 members of the public). Over 140 people sought medical attention including workers, public, and emergency responders. Thousands of community members ordered to shelter-in-place; some areas evacuated. Significant community disruption.
- Sequence: (1) Harcros driver arrived at MGPI's Mod B facility for scheduled sulfuric acid delivery. (2) MGPI operator unlocked the sulfuric acid fill line dust cap and showed the driver its location. (3) Operator returned to control room before witnessing driver's connection. (4) Driver connected hose to the first unlocked fill line he encountered, which was the sodium hypochlorite line. (5) Driver transferred approximately 4,700 gallons of sulfuric acid into the sodium hypochlorite tank. (6) Chemical reaction produced toxic gas cloud. (7) Cloud spread offsite; mass casualty response initiated.
- Immediate cause: Incorrect connection of a sulfuric acid delivery hose to the sodium hypochlorite fill line due to a human error by the tanker driver, facilitated by design and procedural deficiencies that did not prevent the wrong connection.
- Contributing factors:
  - Fill lines were not uniquely keyed or interlocked to prevent wrong connections (compatible fittings on incompatible lines).
  - MGPI's procedure allowed the operator to leave the area before the connection was completed and verified.
  - Fill line identification relied on the operator verbally communicating the correct line; no physical poka-yoke was in place.
  - The MGPI-Harcros relationship did not establish clear shared responsibility for verifying correct connections.
  - No automated process interlocks monitored for incompatible chemical receipt (e.g., pH sensor on the hypochlorite tank).
  - Emergency planning and community notification were insufficient for a release of this magnitude.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Chemical unloading transfer equipment was not designed with human factors or hierarchy of controls in mind: reliance on human memory and verbal communication rather than engineered incompatibility.
  - Neither MGPI nor Harcros had applied a formal process hazard analysis to the chemical unloading operation, even though it involved large quantities of incompatible reactive chemicals.
  - No automated safeguard (interlock, sensor, unique fitting) existed to catch the wrong-connection error before the transfer began.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Compatible cam-lever dust caps on all fill lines allowed any hose to connect to any line.
  - No process sensor to detect wrong chemical in receiving tank before or during transfer.
  - Operator did not remain to verify the connection.
  - No automated shutoff triggered by chemical incompatibility indicators.
- Key findings:
  - Chemical unloading operations are perceived as simple but involve large quantities of hazardous material and significant human error potential.
  - Applying the hierarchy of controls to chemical transfer equipment design (e.g., unique fittings, interlocks, remote shutoffs) would eliminate or substantially reduce the wrong-connection hazard.
  - Facilities with multiple incompatible chemicals delivered to a common unloading area should design the area so that only the correct connection is physically possible.
  - Industry associations and regulatory bodies provided limited specific guidance for incompatible-chemical unloading operations.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - MGPI and Harcros: Apply hierarchy of controls to transfer equipment; install safeguards such as unique incompatible fittings, process interlocks that monitor temperature/level/pH, remote emergency shutoffs, and ventilation/deluge systems activatable remotely.
  - MGPI and Harcros: Develop detailed written procedures requiring verification of correct connection before transfer begins; train all personnel on the verified procedure.
  - Industry: Associations should develop guidance specific to multi-chemical unloading operations, including human factors best practices.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, operating procedures, mechanical integrity, contractor management, emergency response, human factors
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A delivery driver hooked the hose to the wrong pipe and created a poison gas cloud.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The facility stored multiple reactive chemicals with physically identical connection points. No engineered barrier prevented the wrong connection. Procedure placed all responsibility on a brief verbal hand-off between the operator and driver, then allowed the operator to leave before verification. Neither the facility nor the delivery company had performed a hazard analysis on the unloading operation, so the severity of a wrong-connection scenario was never formally evaluated.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. At any point where incompatible chemicals are received through adjacent fill connections, are those connections physically non-interchangeable (unique fittings, colors, locks)? If they rely solely on labeling and verbal guidance, the wrong-connection scenario is a matter of when, not if.
  2. Does someone remain present to witness and verify hose connections during all chemical deliveries, or does the procedure allow the operator to leave before the connection is confirmed? If so, revise to require witnessed verification.
  3. Do you have an automated sensor on receiving tanks (pH, conductivity, temperature) that can detect the wrong chemical and trigger a shutoff before a large quantity is transferred?

### Packaging Corporation of America DeRidder Mill Explosion

- Archive id | Final report date: 3589 | April 2018 (Investigation Report, No. 2017-03-I-LA)
- Company | Location | Industry: Packaging Corporation of America (PCA) | DeRidder, Louisiana | Containerboard / pulp and paper manufacturing
- Incident date: February 8, 2017
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Turpentine vapor (flammable liquid, byproduct of kraft pulping process); non-condensable gases including hydrogen sulfide, methanol, and methyl mercaptan
- What happened: During PCA's annual planned maintenance shutdown, contractor personnel were welding on water piping on an elevated catwalk directly above a foul condensate tank. The foul condensate tank was part of the non-condensable gas (NCG) system and contained water with a layer of flammable turpentine liquid and an explosive turpentine vapor space. Air had entered the tank through a vacuum relief device during normal level cycling and also as the tank cooled during the shutdown, creating an explosive mixture. Hot work ignited the vapor, causing the tank to explode and become a projectile that traveled approximately 375 feet and over a six-story building.
- Consequences: 3 fatalities (all contractors: Jody L. Gooch, William Rolls Jr., Sedrick Stallworth). 7 injuries (all contractors). Significant property damage to surrounding process area.
- Sequence: (1) Mill entered annual maintenance shutdown; foul condensate tank contents cooled. (2) Air entered the tank through a vacuum relief device during normal cycling and during cooling, mixing with turpentine vapor to create an explosive atmosphere. (3) Contractors received a hot work permit to weld on water piping above the foul condensate tank. (4) Atmospheric testing near the tank at ground level detected no flammable gas. (5) Welding commenced above the tank. (6) Hot work ignited the explosive vapor inside the tank. (7) Tank exploded catastrophically, traveled 375 feet, and crashed into a nearby building.
- Immediate cause: Hot work on piping directly above the foul condensate tank likely ignited the explosive turpentine vapor mixture inside the tank.
- Contributing factors:
  - PCA never performed a process hazard analysis (PHA) on the foul condensate tank or the non-condensable gas system; the tank was excluded from PSM coverage by regulatory interpretation (the "Meer decision" exempting atmospheric storage tanks).
  - PCA's hot work procedure required testing the atmosphere around the work area but the test was performed near ground level, not at the tank's vapor space where the explosive concentration existed.
  - Responsibility for the foul condensate tank was unclear between pulp mill and powerhouse operations groups; few operators understood its contents or hazards.
  - Contractors were not informed that they were working in an area classified as hazardous (PCA had an electrical area classification diagram for the tank but did not communicate it to contractors).
  - Operators broadly did not understand that air could enter the tank during cycling or cooling.
  - PCA had participated in developing industry guidance (TAPPI TIP 0416-09) on NCG explosion hazards but that knowledge was not communicated to DeRidder personnel.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - PCA did not voluntarily apply its PSM program beyond the regulated boundaries, leaving the NCG system and foul condensate tank without formal hazard identification, PHA, or effective safeguards.
  - Organizational ambiguity over tank ownership meant no one was assigned to understand, document, or control its explosion hazards.
  - A learning culture was absent: PCA was aware of similar NCG explosions at other pulp and paper mills but did not use those incidents to drive improvements at DeRidder.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No PHA had been conducted for the foul condensate tank.
  - No inherently safer design options (e.g., eliminating the vacuum relief device pathway for air ingress) had been evaluated.
  - No safety instrumented system to detect explosive atmosphere inside the tank before issuing hot work permits.
  - Hot work atmospheric testing did not include the tank vapor space.
  - Contractors were not briefed on the hazardous area classification around the tank.
- Key findings:
  - Air regularly entered the foul condensate tank during normal level cycling, a known design feature that was not recognized as a hazard by site personnel.
  - The PSM regulatory framework exempts atmospheric storage tanks from coverage, creating a gap for tanks like the foul condensate tank even when connected to covered processes; the CSB had previously recommended OSHA address this gap (Motiva 2002) and reiterated that recommendation.
  - The TAPPI industry guidance on NCG explosions existed but was not effectively communicated within PCA to the DeRidder site.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - PCA DeRidder: Voluntarily extend the PSM program boundary to include the NCG system and foul condensate tank; conduct a PHA; evaluate inherently safer design options; clarify operational ownership of the tank.
  - OSHA (reiterated from Motiva 2002): Revise PSM coverage to include atmospheric storage tanks interconnected to covered processes where a catastrophic release could occur.
  - Industry (pulp and paper): Apply CCPS Guidelines for Risk Based Process Safety to NCG systems and foul condensate tanks at all facilities.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, process safety information, hot work permitting, contractor management, operating procedures, organizational ownership, inherently safer design
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A welding spark hit a tank full of flammable vapor and turned it into a missile.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The foul condensate tank was invisible to the safety system. It sat outside PSM boundaries due to a regulatory loophole, no one owned it organizationally, and operators did not understand how it could develop an explosive interior atmosphere during routine operation or cooling. Industry knowledge about this specific hazard existed, but it never reached the people making decisions about hot work near the tank. The ground-level atmospheric test gave a false all-clear while the explosive atmosphere sat in the vapor space above.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. For any tank or vessel not covered by your formal PSM program, can you identify who owns it, what hazardous atmosphere it can contain, and whether hot work is ever performed nearby? If not, conduct informal PHA-style reviews of those "orphan" units.
  2. When issuing hot work permits near vessels containing or capable of containing flammable vapors, does your atmospheric testing protocol require sampling the vapor space of all nearby vessels, not just the ambient air at ground level?
  3. Does your site have documented ownership for every significant process vessel, including tanks that sit at the boundary between two operating areas? Shared ownership often means no owner.

### ExxonMobil Baton Rouge Refinery Isobutane Release and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3590 | September 1, 2017 (Safety Bulletin, No. 2016-02-I-LA)
- Company | Location | Industry: ExxonMobil Refinery | Baton Rouge, Louisiana | Petroleum refining (sulfuric acid alkylation unit)
- Incident date: November 22, 2016
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Isobutane (highly flammable liquefied gas, alkylation unit feedstock)
- What happened: An operator attempted to remove a malfunctioning gearbox from a plug valve in the alkylation unit to manually operate the valve with a pipe wrench. The valve was one of approximately 15 older-design plug valves in the unit where the gearbox support bracket was bolted to pressure-retaining valve components. When the operator removed the bolts securing the support bracket, those bolts also secured the pressure-retaining top cap. Attempting to then open the valve with a pipe wrench caused the valve to come apart, releasing isobutane. The vapor cloud ignited within 30 seconds, severely burning four workers.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities, 4 workers seriously injured (severe burns from isobutane fire/vapor cloud).
- Sequence: (1) A gearbox on a plug valve in the alkylation unit malfunctioned (long-standing reliability issue). (2) Operator used an accepted informal practice of removing the gearbox to operate the valve with a pipe wrench. (3) Operator removed bolts securing the gearbox support bracket, which were also the bolts securing the pressure-retaining top cap. (4) Operator attempted to open the valve with a pipe wrench. (5) The valve disassembled, releasing pressurized isobutane. (6) Isobutane formed a vapor cloud. (7) Cloud ignited within 30 seconds, severely injuring four workers.
- Immediate cause: Incorrect removal of a gearbox support bracket that was bolted to pressure-retaining valve components, disassembling the plug valve under pressure and releasing isobutane.
- Contributing factors:
  - Approximately 3% of plug valves in the alkylation unit used an older design attaching the gearbox support bracket to pressure-retaining components; this was not identified as a distinct hazard class requiring a specific mitigation strategy.
  - No written procedure existed for gearbox removal from plug valves; some operators performed the task while others deferred it to maintenance.
  - Workers were not specifically trained on the hazard of gearbox removal from the susceptible valve design.
  - A long-standing practice of removing gearboxes to manually operate valves had been informally accepted without formal hazard evaluation.
  - Gearbox reliability problems were a known chronic issue not addressed through engineering controls or valve replacement.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Failure to apply human factors analysis and the hierarchy of controls to an operational difficulty: recognizing that 15 valves had a design creating inadvertent disassembly risk should have triggered engineering evaluation and a mitigation plan.
  - Absence of detailed written procedures for a potentially hazardous maintenance activity on PSM-covered equipment.
  - Organizational acceptance of an informal workaround practice (gearbox removal with pipe wrench) without a formal risk assessment.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No procedure or physical barrier preventing removal of the support bracket on susceptible valves.
  - No warning labels on susceptible valves indicating pressure-retaining bolt risk.
  - No requirement to escalate to maintenance rather than operations for gearbox removal.
  - No survey had been conducted to identify and track susceptible valve designs.
- Key findings:
  - Human factors analysis would have revealed the potential for inadvertent valve disassembly with the older plug valve design.
  - Post-incident, ExxonMobil surveyed and corrected all 15 susceptible valves in the alkylation unit and communicated the lesson to other sites.
  - The newer plug valve design avoids this hazard by attaching the gearbox bracket to non-pressure-retaining flanges.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - Industry: Survey gear-operated plug valves to identify susceptible designs; conduct hazard analysis using hierarchy of controls; consider upgrading to newer designs; develop interim mitigation plans for identified susceptible valves; provide written procedures and training for gearbox removal.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, operating procedures, training, mechanical integrity, human factors, management of change
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A worker removed the wrong bolts on a valve and caused an isobutane fireball.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A design flaw in 15 older plug valves made a routine workaround operation dangerous, but the plant never catalogued those valves as a distinct hazard class. The informal practice of removing gearboxes to manually operate valves had been used for years without incident, breeding normalization. No written procedure existed because the task was seen as a simple workaround, not a potentially catastrophic maintenance activity on pressurized equipment in a PSM-covered unit.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. Can you identify all valves and mechanical equipment in your facility where removing a mounting bracket, cover, or support could inadvertently release pressure-retaining fasteners? A survey of this type is a one-time task that can prevent a catastrophic event.
  2. Are all informal workarounds for equipment malfunctions (operating equipment with non-standard tools, bypassing devices) documented and risk-assessed, or are they passed on as tribal knowledge? If the latter, these are unmapped hazards.
  3. When chronic equipment reliability problems are reported (repeated gearbox failures, valve malfunctions), does your system require a root-cause-driven engineering response, or does the problem persist as an operational inconvenience until someone is hurt?

### Loy-Lange Box Company Pressure Vessel Explosion, St. Louis

- Archive id | Final report date: 3591 | (Investigation Report; exact publication date not captured in excerpt)
- Company | Location | Industry: Loy-Lange Box Company | St. Louis, Missouri | Cardboard box manufacturing (steam system)
- Incident date: April 3, 2017
- Hazard type: overpressure / mechanical failure
- Materials: Pressurized hot water/steam in a Semi-Closed Receiver (SCR) pressure vessel; initiated a boiling liquid expanding vapor explosion (BLEVE)
- What happened: The bottom head of a pressure vessel called a Semi-Closed Receiver (SCR), used in the steam system at Loy-Lange's box plant, catastrophically failed due to severe corrosion thinning. The failure caused a BLEVE, killing a Loy-Lange employee working near the vessel and launching the pressure vessel body into the air. The vessel traveled through the roof of a nearby business, fatally injuring three members of the public.
- Consequences: 4 fatalities (1 Loy-Lange employee, 3 members of the public killed when the pressure vessel impacted a neighboring building). Additional injuries. Significant property damage to both the Loy-Lange facility and the neighboring business.
- Sequence: (1) The SCR had experienced corrosion since at least 2004, with oxygen pitting and generalized corrosion thinning the bottom head. (2) Loy-Lange's startup practices introduced oxygenated water into the SCR daily, accelerating oxygen-pitting corrosion. (3) Water treatment was inadequate and failed to remove dissolved oxygen. (4) A 2012 repair by Kickham Boiler and Engineering replaced only a portion of the corroded bottom head, leaving substandard material in place. (5) The repair inspection agency (Arise) did not detect the inadequate repair. (6) The City of St. Louis, the regulatory inspection authority, never inspected the SCR due to manpower limitations and Loy-Lange's non-compliance. (7) On April 3, 2017, the remaining original steel in the bottom head failed, causing the BLEVE.
- Immediate cause: Catastrophic failure of the SCR pressure vessel bottom head due to chronic corrosion thinning below the minimum required wall thickness, caused by oxygen pitting and generalized corrosion.
- Contributing factors:
  - Loy-Lange had no mechanical integrity or inspection program to monitor corrosion in the SCR; aware of corrosion since 2004 but took no systematic action.
  - Startup procedures introduced oxygenated water into the SCR daily, continuously promoting oxygen pitting.
  - Inadequate water treatment chemistry: dissolved oxygen was not controlled.
  - 2012 repair by Kickham left corroded material thinner than minimum required thickness in place; the authorized inspection agency (Arise) failed to detect this.
  - The City of St. Louis, as the pressure vessel regulatory authority, never inspected the SCR.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Absence of a process safety management system: Loy-Lange did not apply process safety principles to its steam process, did not maintain a mechanical integrity program, and did not have an effective system to learn from its operational experience with corrosion.
  - Regulatory gap: The City of St. Louis's mechanical code did not require inspections by qualified inspectors using national consensus standards; its inspection program had capacity and enforcement limitations.

  - No periodic third-party inspection of the SCR during its service life (13 years since awareness of corrosion).
  - No water treatment monitoring program to control dissolved oxygen.
  - Inadequate repair inspection: Arise did not detect that Kickham left below-minimum-thickness material in place.
  - City regulatory inspection never occurred.
- Key findings:
  - The 2012 repair left a ticking failure: the corroded original steel continued degrading until the failure threshold was reached in April 2017.
  - Pressure vessel explosions cause projectile hazards that can injure people far beyond the immediate work area, including the public.
  - National inspection standards (National Board Inspection Code, NBIC) were not adopted by the City of St. Louis, creating a weaker local requirement.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - Loy-Lange: Develop and implement a comprehensive safety management system including pressure vessel mechanical integrity, water treatment monitoring, and process hazard evaluation.

  - Kickham Boiler and Engineering: Review and revise repair procedures to ensure no compromised material is left in place during partial head replacements.
  - National Board of Boiler and Pressure Vessel Inspectors: Strengthen repair inspection requirements to prevent incomplete removal of damaged material.
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical integrity, process hazard analysis, operating procedures (water treatment and startup), regulatory compliance, management systems
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): An old corroded boiler tank blew up and landed on the neighboring business.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A cascade of failed oversight stretched back 13 years. The company knew about corrosion but had no program to manage it. A repair in 2012 masked the problem without solving it because neither the repair company nor its inspector ensured all defective material was removed. The local regulator that should have caught the degradation never visited. Daily startup practices continuously fed the corrosion mechanism. Every layer of protection, from internal inspection to third-party repair certification to city oversight, was either absent or ineffective.

  1. Do you maintain documented inspection records for all pressure vessels, including atmospheric steam vessels and utility system equipment that may fall outside PSM boundaries? If a vessel's last inspection date is unknown, that is an immediate action item.
  2. When a partial repair is made to a corroded pressure vessel, does your acceptance procedure require confirming that all remaining material meets minimum thickness requirements, not just the replaced section?
  3. Do your boiler and pressure vessel startup procedures include steps to prevent or remove dissolved oxygen from feedwater? Oxygen pitting is a well-known corrosion mechanism that can be controlled with basic water treatment chemistry and monitoring.

### Didion Milling Combustible Dust Explosions, Cambria, Wisconsin

- Archive id | Final report date: 3592 | December 2023 (Investigation Report, No. 2017-07-I-WI)
- Company | Location | Industry: Didion Milling, Inc. | Cambria, Wisconsin | Dry corn milling (food manufacturing)
- Incident date: May 31, 2017
- Hazard type: combustible dust
- Materials: Corn dust (combustible particulate; the milling facility produced corn flour, corn meal, corn starch, and related products)
- What happened: At approximately 11:00 p.m., multiple combustible dust explosions occurred at Didion Milling's dry corn milling facility. Employees first noticed smoke in the milling process areas. While investigating, they discovered the source appeared to be on the first floor near rotary gap mill equipment. Shortly after, an explosion occurred with fire emerging from the rotary gap mill discharge piping. A fire and deflagration spread through interconnected dust collection systems, triggering secondary explosions throughout the facility. Buildings collapsed. Five employees were fatally injured and 14 others were hurt.
- Consequences: 5 fatalities (Duelle Block, Robert Goodenow, Carlos "Charly" Nunez Contreras, Angel Reyes, Pawel Tordoff). 14 injuries. Estimated property damage of $15.4 million. Three buildings completely collapsed (Multipurpose Building, F Mill, Boiler House). Additional structures severely damaged.
- Sequence: (1) On the day of the incident, Didion connected the discharge of two rotary gap mills to increase production (an unreviewed operational change). (2) During normal night-shift operations, employees smelled smoke and investigated. (3) The probable ignition source (not definitively determined) caused a fire in the rotary gap mill discharge area. (4) Fire propagated through interconnected dust collection ductwork systems. (5) Explosions occurred in dust collectors. (6) Secondary explosions from dispersed accumulated fugitive dust occurred throughout the facility. (7) Buildings collapsed from explosion overpressure.
- Immediate cause: A fire or ignition event in the corn milling process ignited accumulated combustible corn dust, with deflagrations propagating through interconnected dust collection systems and causing secondary explosions throughout the facility.
- Contributing factors:
  - Didion incorrectly concluded (using erroneous calculations) that its dust collectors did not contain explosive dust concentrations.
  - The day-of change connecting two rotary gap mill discharges was not evaluated for combustible dust propagation hazards through the shared dust collection system.
  - Ductwork sizing and design were inadequate, allowing combustible material to accumulate inside ducts.
  - No deflagration isolation devices were installed to prevent flame propagation between interconnected equipment.
  - No deflagration venting was installed on mill buildings or dust collectors adequate to relieve explosion pressure.
  - Mill buildings were not designed to withstand deflagration pressures; structural collapse amplified casualties.
  - Didion used food safety standards (not designed for combustible dust hazards) to manage dust, creating gaps in process safety.
  - Prior fire and smoldering incidents were not thoroughly investigated and no effective corrective actions were taken.
  - Employees could not effectively communicate the emergency via radio due to conflicting traffic.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Inadequate process hazard recognition: Didion had not performed a dust hazard analysis (DHA) on its grain handling processes and did not accurately characterize the explosion propagation hazard of its interconnected dust collection systems.
  - Weak management of change: the MOC program was food-safety focused and did not assess operational changes for combustible dust hazards.
  - Inadequate incident investigation program: prior fire events were not analyzed to identify and address root causes.
  - Weak process safety leadership and culture: no engineering controls for combustible dust prevention or mitigation were installed despite applicable NFPA guidance being available.
  - Regulatory gap: OSHA's Grain Handling Facilities Standard does not require a DHA, leaving a critical hazard recognition step to voluntary compliance.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No dust hazard analysis had ever been performed.
  - No deflagration isolation (check valves, rotary valves, chemical suppression) between interconnected equipment.
  - No deflagration venting on buildings or dust collectors adequate to relieve overpressure.
  - No engineering controls (suppression, isolation, containment) for combustible dust hazards.
  - No effective emergency notification and evacuation system; radio communications failed.
  - Prior fire events not fully investigated; no corrective actions implemented.
- Key findings:
  - Connecting two process streams through a shared dust collection system created a deflagration propagation path that was never recognized or analyzed.
  - Food safety management systems are not designed for and do not address combustible dust process safety hazards; companies should not conflate the two.
  - Regulatory standards for grain handling do not require DHAs, creating a systematic gap across the industry.
  - Building structural design must account for deflagration overpressure where combustible dust processes operate.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - Didion: Conduct a comprehensive DHA; install engineering controls for deflagration prevention, isolation, and venting; revise MOC to assess combustible dust hazards; strengthen incident investigation.
  - OSHA: Revise Grain Handling Facilities Standard to require dust hazard analyses.
  - NFPA and industry: Strengthen guidance on structural design requirements for facilities processing combustible dust.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, management of change, mechanical integrity, operating procedures, incident investigation, emergency response, process safety information, leadership and culture
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Corn dust blew up a mill and killed five workers.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The facility had never been systematically analyzed for combustible dust explosion hazards. Management focused on food safety compliance, which is a different discipline entirely. When a change connected two milling lines through shared dust collectors, no one evaluated whether that created a new fire propagation pathway. The physical design had no safeguards against deflagration spread. Previous small fires were written off rather than investigated as warning signs. The regulatory standard covering grain handling does not require the hazard analysis that would have exposed all of this.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. If your facility processes any combustible particulates (grain, wood, metal, plastic, sugar, starch), has a formal dust hazard analysis been performed covering all buildings and interconnected equipment? DHA is required by NFPA 652 and is distinct from food safety or general housekeeping programs.
  2. When two or more pieces of dust-generating or dust-collecting equipment are connected through shared ductwork, does the design include deflagration isolation devices to prevent flame front propagation from one piece of equipment to another?
  3. Do your incident investigation procedures apply to all fire and smoldering events in dust-generating areas, including minor ones? Minor combustion events in dust-handling systems are leading indicators of major explosions, not isolated nuisances.

### Midland Resource Recovery Tank Explosions, Philippi, West Virginia

- Archive id | Final report date: 3593 | December 2019 (Investigation Report, No. 2017-06-I-WV)
- Company | Location | Industry: Midland Resource Recovery (MRR) | Philippi, West Virginia | Natural gas odorant equipment decommissioning and recycling
- Incident date: May 24, 2017 (first explosion); June 20, 2017 (second explosion)
- Hazard type: reactive runaway
- Materials: Mercaptan-contaminated liquid in Peerless MP natural gas odorizer tanks; sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) used as chemical treatment; reaction products included explosive organosulfur compounds
- What happened: MRR decommissioned natural gas odorizer equipment (Peerless MP units containing mercaptans) and treated the equipment with sodium hypochlorite to remove the mercaptan odor before scrapping. On May 24, 2017, a tank exploded during draining operations, killing two MRR workers and severely injuring a third. The CSB deployed an investigation team. While the CSB was still investigating, a second explosion occurred on June 20, 2017, killing a contractor who had been hired by MRR to perform mitigation work. Both explosions occurred because reactive, unstable chemical compounds formed when sodium hypochlorite was mixed with uncharacterized mercaptan-containing liquids, and then exploded when workers attempted to drain the vessels.
- Consequences: 3 fatalities (Jan Strmen and Justin Marsh in the May explosion; contractor Scott Albertini in the June explosion). 1 serious injury (May explosion). Significant property damage. The June explosion occurred while an investigation into the first was still active.
- Sequence: (1) MRR received decommissioned Peerless MP odorizer units containing mercaptans at its Philippi site. (2) MRR treated the units with sodium hypochlorite to neutralize the mercaptan odor, without fully characterizing what chemicals were inside or what reactive chemistry could result. (3) No formal hazard identification or hazard analysis was performed before treating the unknown contents with a reactive chemical. (4) On May 24, workers attempted to drain the chemically treated tanks; an explosion occurred, killing two. (5) MRR hired SPSI to assist with investigation and mitigation following the first explosion. (6) On June 20, while the investigation was ongoing, a second treated tank exploded during draining, killing the SPSI contractor.
- Immediate cause: Reactive, unstable chemical compounds formed from the interaction of mercaptans and sodium hypochlorite exploded when workers disturbed or drained the treated vessels.
- Contributing factors:
  - MRR did not characterize what chemicals were inside the odorizer vessels before treating them with sodium hypochlorite.
  - No formal process hazard analysis or reactive chemistry evaluation was performed.
  - No overpressure protection was provided on the vessels being treated.
  - The company relied on prior successful treatments as evidence of safety, without understanding the reactive chemistry involved.
  - Physical evidence and test results confirmed the potential for explosive compound formation during sodium hypochlorite treatment of mercaptan-containing unknowns.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - MRR lacked an effective safety management system: no formal hazard identification, no reactive chemistry review, no process hazard analysis, no safeguards to prevent unexpected chemical reactions.
  - No regulatory standard (OSHA PSM, EPA RMP) covered MRR's reactive chemical operations because the specific materials and quantities did not trigger listed chemical thresholds; this regulatory gap allowed the operation to proceed without mandated safety analysis.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No chemical characterization of vessel contents before treatment.
  - No reactive chemistry evaluation before mixing sodium hypochlorite with unknown mercaptan-contaminated liquids.
  - No overpressure protection (relief devices) on tanks being chemically treated.
  - No formal hazard analysis for the decommissioning and treatment process.
- Key findings:
  - The second explosion occurred while the first was being investigated and while mitigation contractors were on site, demonstrating that the systemic hazard was not understood or controlled even after the first fatalities.
  - Sodium hypochlorite reacts unpredictably with complex organic sulfur compounds; treating "waste" or uncharacterized material with reactive chemicals is inherently hazardous.
  - Regulatory coverage of reactive hazards in the U.S. has persistent gaps where materials are reactive but not listed under PSM or RMP thresholds.
  - The CSB reiterated existing recommendations to OSHA and EPA to address reactive chemical regulatory gaps.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - OSHA (reiterated): Expand PSM coverage to include reactive chemical hazards not currently on the listed chemical threshold table.
  - EPA (reiterated): Expand RMP coverage similarly to include reactive chemical hazards.
  - MRR: Implement a robust safety management system; never treat uncharacterized chemical waste with reactive agents without full characterization and formal hazard review.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, reactive chemistry management, process safety information, mechanical integrity, management systems, regulatory compliance
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers were cleaning old gas equipment with bleach and it exploded.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): MRR treated chemically unknown material with a reactive chemical (sodium hypochlorite) without understanding the chemistry involved. Prior successes bred false confidence. No regulatory requirement forced a hazard analysis because the specific materials and volumes did not trigger PSM or RMP thresholds. The company had no safety management framework to even ask the question "what happens if these chemicals react?" The second explosion while the investigation was ongoing demonstrates how completely the underlying hazard remained unrecognized even after the first deaths.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. Does any operation at your facility involve treating, mixing, or disposing of materials whose composition is not fully known? If so, that is a reactive chemistry hazard scenario requiring characterization before chemical treatment is applied.
  2. When sodium hypochlorite or other strong oxidizers are used for cleaning, neutralizing, or treating waste, has a reactive chemistry review been completed for all materials those oxidizers may contact, including residuals and unknowns?
  3. After a serious process safety incident, does your organization suspend related operations until the hazard is understood and controlled, or does work continue around the investigation? The second MRR fatality shows that proximity to an investigation does not mean the hazard is under control.

### Arkema Crosby Organic Peroxide Fires Following Hurricane Harvey

- Archive id | Final report date: 3594 | May 2018 (Investigation Report, No. 2017-08-I-TX)
- Company | Location | Industry: Arkema Inc. (Arkema Group subsidiary) | Crosby, Texas | Organic peroxide manufacturing
- Incident date: August 31, 2017 (first fire; additional fires through September 2, 2017)
- Hazard type: reactive runaway
- Materials: Organic peroxides (Luperox products requiring continuous refrigeration; Self-Accelerating Decomposition Temperatures as low as well below ambient; specific products included those requiring storage at -20 F)
- What happened: Hurricane Harvey struck southeast Texas on August 24, 2017, producing unprecedented flooding. The Arkema Crosby facility, located entirely within the 100-year and 500-year flood plains, lost electrical power, then backup generator power, then all refrigeration capacity as floodwater inundated safety systems. All employees were eventually evacuated. Organic peroxide products requiring low-temperature storage were relocated to refrigerated trailers, but the trailers also lost refrigeration when floodwater rose above their equipment. On August 31, the first batch of organic peroxides decomposed and burned inside a refrigerated trailer. Twenty-one emergency responders and community members were exposed to toxic decomposition fumes. Over the following days, eight more trailers of organic peroxide burned in two additional fires and a controlled burn, consuming over 350,000 pounds of product. More than 200 residents could not return home for a week.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities. 21 people sought medical attention from fume exposure (first fire). Over 200 residents displaced for up to a week. Substantial property loss. Approximately 350,000 lbs of organic peroxide consumed.
- Sequence: (1) Hurricane Harvey made landfall August 24-25 with extreme flooding. (2) Floodwater rose at Arkema Crosby; ride-out crew de-energized electrical systems pre-emptively as water rose. (3) Low Temperature Warehouses lost power and refrigeration; organic peroxides relocated to refrigerated trailers on high ground. (4) Floodwater reached trailer locations, disabling trailer refrigeration and exhausting backup power. (5) By August 31, peroxides in the first trailer decomposed and burned; the burning trailer released toxic fumes across Highway 90, which had been kept open for hurricane recovery traffic. (6) Two additional fires and a Unified Command-directed controlled burn consumed the remaining trailers over the next several days.
- Immediate cause: Loss of all refrigeration capacity for temperature-sensitive organic peroxides due to flooding exceeding the design elevation of every backup safety system at the facility; peroxides exceeded their Self-Accelerating Decomposition Temperature and ignited.
- Contributing factors:
  - The Arkema Crosby facility is entirely within FEMA-designated 100-year and 500-year flood plains, a fact known since a 2007 FEMA map revision but not broadly communicated within the company or incorporated into hazard analyses.
  - All layers of protection against refrigeration loss (main systems, backup generators, liquid nitrogen, refrigerated trailers) were subject to the same flooding common-cause failure mode; no layer was elevated or flood-protected.
  - Arkema's Process Hazard Analysis for the Low Temperature Warehouses did not include flooding as a credible scenario.
  - An FM Global insurance report from September 2016 identified flood risks at the Crosby facility, but site employees (other than a past facility manager) were unaware of it.
  - Emergency responders kept Highway 90 open through the established evacuation zone for hurricane recovery purposes, exposing first responders and bystanders to fumes from the first fire.
  - Long-term employee experience set the flooding benchmark at Tropical Storm Allison (2001), which underestimated actual historic flood levels; formal flood records showed three separate 100-year events at the site since 1994.
  - No plan existed for the loss-of-all-refrigeration scenario because it was not considered credible.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Failure to identify and assess flood risk as a process safety hazard: FEMA flood plain maps were not compiled as required process safety information, and flooding was not included in the PHA.
  - Common-cause failure in layers of protection: all backup systems were vulnerable to the same flood event, violating the independence principle for layered protection.
  - Organizational knowledge gap: critical flood risk information (FEMA maps, FM Global report) existed but was not communicated to or used by site process safety staff.
  - Industry guidance on extreme weather flooding was generic and insufficient to drive adequately conservative design of safeguards.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No flood-resistant or elevated backup refrigeration system.
  - No layer of protection for temperature-sensitive materials that was independent of electrical power from the facility grid.
  - No protocol to close adjacent highways when decomposing organic peroxides created an airborne hazard in the evacuation zone.
  - Emergency response telemetry data from trailers was insufficient to determine the state of decomposition in real time.
- Key findings:
  - Extreme flooding events (100-year and beyond) have been occurring with increasing frequency; relying on employee historical experience underestimates true flood risk at many Gulf Coast sites.
  - Flood insurance rate maps and flood studies should be treated as process safety information under PSM regulations.
  - Industry guidance on natural hazard risks (flooding, hurricanes) is too generic; more prescriptive guidance is needed for chemical facilities storing materials that can cause catastrophic consequences if they lose climate control.
  - Multiple consecutive 100-year flooding events at the Crosby gauge (1994, and others) were documented but not reflected in the site's risk thinking.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - Arkema Crosby / Arkema Inc.: Incorporate FEMA flood maps into process safety information; conduct flood-risk PHA; ensure backup refrigeration systems are flood-independent; develop protocols for highway closure and emergency response around organic peroxide decomposition events.
  - AIChE Center for Chemical Process Safety: Develop more specific and conservative guidance for chemical facilities on extreme weather flooding risk assessment and safeguard design.
  - Harris County, Texas: Evaluate and improve emergency response protocols for chemical facilities in flood-prone areas, including highway routing decisions during active chemical incidents.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis, process safety information (natural hazard data), layers of protection analysis, emergency response, mechanical integrity, management systems
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A hurricane knocked out all the refrigeration and unstable chemicals caught fire.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Every safety backup at the facility was vulnerable to the same flood. The company never treated flooding as a credible process safety hazard, even though FEMA maps placed the entire facility in a floodplain and prior insurance studies flagged the risk. Employee memory set the mental benchmark at Allison (2001), a less severe flood event, preventing recognition of true historic variability. When Harvey hit, the system encountered a scenario it was not designed for, and it failed in every layer simultaneously because every layer was subject to the same mode of failure.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  1. Are FEMA flood insurance rate maps and site-specific flood history records included in your process safety information? If not, incorporate them and use them to assess whether your critical utility systems (power, cooling, suppression) have been elevated or flood-proofed to appropriate design elevations.
  2. In your layers of protection for any critical utility (refrigeration, power, water supply), have you checked for common-cause failure modes? If the same flood, earthquake, or storm event can disable all layers simultaneously, you do not have independent protection layers.
  3. If your site stores materials that can catastrophically self-react or combust upon loss of a utility (refrigeration, inert gas, power), does your emergency plan include protocols for the loss-of-all-utilities scenario, and has that scenario been included in your most recent process hazard analysis?

### Pryor Trust Gas Well Blowout and Fire, Pittsburg County, Oklahoma

- Archive id | Final report date: 3595 | June 12, 2019 (Investigation Report, No. 2018-01-I-OK)
- Company | Location | Industry: Red Mountain Operating (operator), Patterson-UTI Drilling (contractor) | Pittsburg County, Oklahoma | Onshore oil and gas drilling
- Incident date: January 22, 2018
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Natural gas (formation gas); drilling mud (water-based)
- What happened: While tripping drill pipe out of the Pryor Trust Well 1H-9, a gas influx entered the well and was not detected by the driller or other rig workers. The well had been operating in an underbalanced condition after unexpected flaring events and an improperly weighted mud system. A weighted pill intended to restore hydrostatic control was miscalculated and did not overbalance the well. During the day tour on January 22, the blowout occurred approximately 3.5 hours after tripping had begun. Drilling mud and gas ignited on the rig floor, and five workers inside the driller's cabin were trapped by fire and killed. The blowout preventer could not be closed because its hydraulic control hoses burned through.
- Consequences: 5 fatalities (all workers inside the driller's cabin from thermal burns and smoke/soot inhalation). Property loss of the rig and wellhead. Significant well fire requiring emergency response.
- Sequence: (1) Well 1H-9 was drilled as a horizontal well with near-balanced conditions planned. (2) Unexpected flaring occurred, indicating underbalanced conditions; decision was made to continue rather than stop and evaluate. (3) A weighted mud pill was spotted to restore overbalance, but calculations were incorrect and the well remained underbalanced. (4) Drill pipe was tripped out of the well from the horizontal section. (5) Gas entered the well during tripping; night driller did not detect the influx.   - Surface pressure was not identified on two prior occasions when it was apparently present, a missed influx signal.
  - The weighted pill intended to overbalance the well was miscalculated; the well remained underbalanced.
  - Both the night and day drillers turned off the alarm system, preventing automated alerts to critical well condition changes.
  - The alarm system design was not adapted to different operating states (drilling vs. tripping vs. circulating), generating excessive irrelevant alarms during tripping that drove operators to silence the system.
  - Company-required flow checks were rarely performed on this rig and on the prior well.
  - Drillers were never tested on their ability to detect simulated gas influx events.
  - The driller's cabin offered no safe escape route once the rig floor ignited.
  - No drilling-specific safety management regulation applies to onshore U.S. drilling operations.

  - Ineffective safety management system at both operator and drilling contractor: no effective management of change for operational deviations from drilling plan, no enforcement of flow check policy, no alarm management standard, no testing of safety-critical driller competencies.
  - The operating company (RMO) did not specify required barriers for the operation or define responses to barrier loss.
  - No regulatory standard governs onshore drilling safety management in the U.S., creating an industry-wide gap.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Alarm system was deactivated (not designed for state-based alarm management).
  - Flow checks were not performed per policy.
  - No safety instrumented system to automatically detect influx and close BOP.
  - BOP hydraulic hoses were exposed and burned in the fire, preventing BOP closure.
  - Driller's cabin had no emergency egress route.
  - Weighted pill failed to restore overbalance.
- Key findings:
  - Turning off an entire alarm system to avoid nuisance alarms is a known safety failure pattern; alarm management standards (like ISA 18.2) exist for industrial processes but are not required for drilling.
  - The driller's cabin design traps workers when fire occurs on the rig floor, a known industry issue.
  - No onshore U.S. drilling regulatory framework requires a safety management system comparable to offshore requirements (SEMS) or process industry PSM.
  - Multiple compounding failures, each individually potentially recoverable, combined to create an unrecoverable situation.
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - OSHA: Issue a standard requiring safety management systems for onshore drilling operations.
  - API: Develop guidance on alarm management for drilling; revise well control guidance to address underbalanced tripping hazards.
  - Patterson-UTI: Implement alarm management per ISA 18.2; test drillers on influx detection skills; enforce flow check policy; redesign driller's cabin for emergency egress.
  - Red Mountain Operating: Specify required barriers and barrier responses for all drilling operations; implement effective MOC for operational deviations.
  - IADC: Develop industry training standards for influx detection in underbalanced conditions.
  - Pason Systems Inc.: Design electronic trip sheet and alarm systems with state-based alarm management capability.
  - National Oilwell Varco: Address BOP hydraulic hose vulnerability to rig floor fires.
  - State of Oklahoma: Establish safety management requirements for onshore drilling.
- PSM elements implicated: Management systems, procedures, alarm management, training and competency, management of change, mechanical integrity (BOP), emergency response, barrier management
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Drillers silenced the alarms, missed a gas kick, and were trapped when the well caught fire.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Multiple independent barriers to a blowout were each degraded before the event: the mud system was underbalanced, the alarm system was off, flow checks were not being performed, the driller was untrained on new software, and the weighted pill that was supposed to fix the underbalance was miscalculated. The onshore drilling industry has no equivalent to the offshore SEMS regulatory requirement for a management system, meaning many of these organizational failures are common across the industry.

For any high-hazard operation with defined safety barriers (well control, pressure relief, gas detection), do your procedures require operators to confirm barrier status before starting each shift, and do you audit those confirmations? Barrier management is only effective if barriers are known to be in place.
  3. When operational conditions deviate from the approved plan (unexpected flaring, underbalanced indications, unusual readings), does your management of change system require stopping and formally reassessing before continuing, or is continuation the default?
