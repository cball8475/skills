### Husky Superior Refinery FCC Unit Explosion and Asphalt Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3597 | December 23, 2022
- Company | Location | Industry: Husky Energy (Superior Refining Company LLC) | Superior, Wisconsin | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: April 26, 2018
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Flammable hydrocarbon vapor mixture (methane, ethane, ethylene, propane, propylene, butanes, pentanes); hot asphalt (above flash point); pyrophoric iron sulfide
- What happened: During a planned turnaround shutdown of the fluid catalytic cracking (FCC) unit, operators failed to maintain correct pressure relationships between the air-side regenerator and the hydrocarbon-side main column. Air from the regenerator flowed backward through the reactor and into the gas concentration unit (GasCon), where oxygen accumulated in the primary and sponge absorbers. The oxygen reacted with pyrophoric iron sulfide inside the absorbers, igniting an explosion that shattered both vessels. Flying debris punctured a nearby asphalt storage tank, spilling about 17,000 barrels of hot asphalt, which ignited approximately two hours later and burned until extinguished before midnight.
- Consequences: 36 workers injured (11 OSHA-recordable); zero fatalities; approximately $550 million property damage; 2,507 residents evacuated from Superior; shelter-in-place advisory issued for Duluth, Minnesota; 39,000 pounds of flammable hydrocarbon released; no HF release (HF alkylation unit was nearby and at risk)
- Sequence: (1) FCC unit shutdown begins during turnaround with 800-900 people on site. (2) Operators follow shutdown procedure that incorrectly sets regenerator pressure higher than reactor pressure, reversing intended flow direction. (3) Eroded spent catalyst slide valve fails to isolate air side. (4) Reactor steam barrier never established; main column gas purge not performed. (5) Air flows through reactor into GasCon; oxygen accumulates in absorbers. (6) Oxygen reacts with pyrophoric material, igniting explosion at 9:58 a.m. (7) Absorbers shatter by brittle fracture; 100-plus metal fragments scatter up to 1,200 feet. (8) Debris punctures asphalt tank. (9) Asphalt spills outside containment and ignites at noon. (10) Fires extinguished before midnight; evacuation lifted at 6:00 a.m. next day.
- Immediate cause: Inadvertent mixing of air from the regenerator with flammable hydrocarbons in the gas concentration unit during FCC shutdown, with ignition by pyrophoric material inside the vessels.
- Contributing factors:
  - Shutdown procedure was technically incorrect, directing operators to maintain regenerator at higher pressure than the reactor, reversing safe flow direction
  - Reactor steam barrier not established; not included in the shutdown procedure
  - Main column gas purge step absent from procedure
  - Severely eroded spent catalyst slide valve provided open path for air to flow into reactor
  - Primary and sponge absorbers were made from obsolete ASTM A-212 and A-201 steels susceptible to brittle fracture
  - FCC process knowledge at the refinery was insufficient; licensor operating manual not in the PSI package
  - Operators did not understand or recognize lessons from the 2015 ExxonMobil Torrance FCC incident
  - Operating procedures not technically reviewed for at least 25 years
  - PHA did not evaluate hazards of transient (shutdown) operations or identify slide valves as inadequate isolation
  - Operator training did not cover FCC transient operation hazards or use simulators
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Inadequate process safety information: licensor operating manual and critical shutdown guidance excluded from PSI package
  - Deficient operating procedures: key safety steps (steam barrier, gas purge) omitted; procedure not technically validated
  - Incomplete process hazard analysis: transient operation scenarios not evaluated; layers of protection analysis did not identify that slide valves alone were insufficient
  - Inadequate operator training: no simulator training; training materials lacked FCC safety knowledge; trainer qualifications insufficient
  - Weak FCC industry knowledge management: no mandatory participation in industry knowledge networks; individual training did not transfer knowledge to the organization
  - No technology-specific knowledge-sharing network across Cenovus/Husky refineries
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Reactor steam barrier: not established (not in procedure)
  - Main column non-condensable gas purge: not performed (not in procedure)
  - Catalyst slide valve mechanical integrity: valve severely eroded, providing open pathway
  - Brittle fracture-resistant vessel materials: absorbers made from old, brittle-prone steel grades
  - Emergency containment for asphalt spill: response overwhelmed by unexpected spill volume and competing priorities
- Key findings:
  - Three technology-specific safeguards (steam barrier, gas purge, slide valve integrity) were all absent or ineffective simultaneously
  - The correct safe shutdown direction, with reactor pressure higher than regenerator, was specified in the licensor's 1960 guidance but contradicted by Husky's own procedure
  - Brittle fracture of old steel grades dramatically increased consequence severity by producing 100-plus dangerous projectiles
  - A similar FCC incident at ExxonMobil Torrance in 2015 was known to the industry; Husky employees did not fully learn from it
  - No industry-wide standard covers basic process safety expectations for all FCC unit types
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - Cenovus Superior Refinery: Establish reactor steam barrier, main column purge, and slide valve monitoring as codified shutdown safeguards; develop FCC transient operation training including simulator; establish emergency procedures for slide valve failure
  - Cenovus Energy: Implement FCC-specific PHA guidance for all operated refineries; create technology peer network meeting annually; incorporate licensor operating manuals into PSI at all FCC units
  - OSHA: Develop guidance for conducting PHA on operating procedures to address transient operation hazards
  - EPA: Prioritize inspections of FCC units at refineries with co-located HF alkylation units; verify transient operation safeguards
  - API: Develop guidance or standard covering common FCC process safety expectations across all FCC unit types
  - Honeywell UOP (licensor): Strengthen dissemination of critical FCC safety knowledge to licensees
- PSM elements implicated: Process Safety Information, Operating Procedures, Process Hazard Analysis, Training, Mechanical Integrity, Management of Change, Emergency Planning and Response, Industry Standards and Guidance
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Operators failed to follow the shutdown procedure correctly during a planned maintenance event, causing an explosion.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The refinery's shutdown procedure was technically wrong for at least 25 years and contradicted the licensor's guidance, but no one performed a multidisciplinary technical review because the PSM system treated procedure review as an administrative recertification exercise rather than an engineering validation. Simultaneously, three independent safety barriers (steam barrier, gas purge, slide valve integrity) were all either absent from the procedure or degraded by maintenance neglect, and operators lacked the deep technical understanding to recognize the danger because training never adequately covered FCC transient operations.
- Monday-morning checks:
  - Verify that shutdown and startup procedures for complex units (FCC, HF alky, distillation columns) have been technically reviewed against the technology licensor's guidance within the last five years, not just administratively recertified annually.
  - Check whether slide valves, check valves, or other components relied upon for process isolation during transient operations have a documented mechanical integrity program with acceptance criteria, or whether they are simply assumed to be functional.
  - Confirm that your facility's PHA methodology explicitly evaluates transient operation modes (startup, shutdown, standby, emergency) using the actual written procedures as the scenario basis, not just steady-state operation.

### Kuraray America EVAL Plant Ethylene Release and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3598 | December 16, 2022
- Company | Location | Industry: Kuraray America, Inc. | Pasadena, Texas | Specialty chemical manufacturing (ethylene vinyl alcohol copolymer)
- Incident date: May 19, 2018
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE (fire from flammable vapor cloud ignition)
- Materials: Ethylene (highly flammable gas, condensed to liquid inside reactor during startup)
- What happened: During startup of EVAL Reactor 2 following a turnaround, liquid ethylene condensed inside the reactor, lowering its temperature. Operators heated the contents to recover, vaporizing the ethylene and rapidly increasing pressure. A safety interlock had been disabled during troubleshooting. Alarm flood (about 160 alarms per hour) impaired operator response. The reactor reached the activation pressure of its emergency pressure-relief valve, which discharged approximately 2,347 pounds of ethylene vapor in less than three minutes through horizontally aimed outlet piping directly into the ambient air of a busy work area. Nearby welders, performing non-essential work during the startup, likely provided the ignition source. The resulting fire burned until pressure inside the reactor dropped below the relief valve's activation point and the valve reseated.
- Consequences: 23 workers injured (2 life-flighted, 1 critically; others with burns, falls, and sprains from escape); zero fatalities; significant property damage; no fatalities; community shelter-in-place not required
- Sequence: (1) EVAL Reactor 2 startup begins post-turnaround. (2) Ethylene vapor condenses to liquid, creating low-temperature, low-pressure condition. (3) Operators heat reactor contents; liquid ethylene vaporizes rapidly. (4) Abnormal condition safety interlock disabled during troubleshooting. (5) Alarm flood prevents effective board operator response. (6) Operators limit flare use to avoid exceeding environmental permit VOC limits. (7) Reactor pressure reaches 740 psi relief valve set point. (8) Relief valve opens and discharges ethylene horizontally toward contractor work area. (9) Welding ignites ethylene cloud; fire erupts. (10) Workers escape via falls from elevated structure; some trapped by fall protection equipment. (11) Valve reseats when pressure drops; fire extinguishes.
- Immediate cause: Emergency pressure-relief system for EVAL Reactor 2 discharged flammable ethylene vapor through horizontally aimed outlet piping into an occupied work area, where it was ignited.
- Contributing factors:
  - Relief system outlet piping was horizontally aimed rather than discharging vertically to atmosphere or to a flare
  - A 2015 PHA recommendation addressing worker safety from ethylene relief discharges was declined by Kuraray management without documented evaluation
  - Previous ethylene relief releases from this and other EVAL reactors were not treated as warning signs
  - Safety interlock was disabled during startup and startup continued without restoring it
  - Alarm management program incomplete; board operators overwhelmed with approximately 160 alarms per hour
  - Environmental VOC permit limit caused operators to restrict flare use, limiting the option to safely divert ethylene
  - Nightly operating instructions from management conflicted with written procedures (target pressure 100 psi higher than procedure)
  - Safe operating limits set at reactor mechanical design conditions rather than at levels ensuring margin below relief set point
  - Nonessential workers (including welders) allowed to work within the unit during startup without a formal exclusion zone
  - EVAL Reactor 2 had a relief activation pressure of 740 psi, 410 psi lower than the other three reactors, but operators received no special warning about this difference
  - Operator training did not cover alarm set points or alarm response actions
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Inadequate emergency pressure-relief system design: relief discharge not routed to a safe location (flare or vertical atmospheric discharge)
  - Weak management of PHA recommendations: management declined a directly relevant safety recommendation without documentation
  - Ineffective safe operating limits system: limits set at mechanical design conditions rather than at operationally meaningful limits below the relief activation pressure
  - Inadequate alarm management: high alarm rates not resolved before startup; no guidance on alarm response in procedures
  - Absent or ineffective policy for excluding nonessential workers during startup and upset conditions
  - Process safety management self-assessment audits insufficient in depth to identify accumulated systemic failures
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Vertically aimed or flare-connected relief discharge: absent for EVAL Reactor 2 (horizontal aim to atmosphere)
  - Abnormal condition safety interlock: deliberately disabled and startup continued anyway
  - Automated valve to divert reactor pressure to flare: not used because of VOC permit concern and lack of procedure
  - Nonessential personnel exclusion zone: informal, unwritten, and not enforced
  - Prior incident learning: earlier releases treated as normal operations rather than warning signs requiring corrective action
- Key findings:
  - The single most consequential design deficiency was the horizontal discharge direction of the relief outlet; had it been vertical or routed to flare, the same chain of events would have caused no worker injuries
  - A 2015 PHA team explicitly flagged this hazard and generated a recommendation; Kuraray management rejected it without documentation, a critical PSM failure
  - A desire to avoid exceeding environmental permit limits directly influenced operators' decisions during a safety-critical event, illustrating the conflict between environmental compliance metrics and process safety
  - 17 distinct safety management system weaknesses were identified in the report, spanning all major PSM elements
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - Kuraray America: Develop and implement an emergency pressure-relief system design standard requiring discharge to a safe location; develop procedures for excluding nonessential workers during startup and upset conditions; develop effective alarm management, safe operating limits, and operator training programs addressing all identified issues
- PSM elements implicated: Process Hazard Analysis, Management of Change, Safe Operating Limits, Alarm Management, Operating Procedures, Operator Training, Emergency Planning, Mechanical Integrity (equipment design), Management System Audits
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Operators let reactor pressure get too high during a tricky startup and the relief valve popped, releasing flammable gas near workers.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The plant's own safety review process had already identified the horizontal relief discharge as a hazard six years before this incident, but management rejected the fix without documentation. A chain of 17 weakly implemented management system elements, including alarm flood, a disabled safety interlock, an environmental permit constraint that interfered with safe process control, and absent nonessential worker policies, allowed a recoverable abnormal condition to escalate into a major fire.
- Monday-morning checks:
  - Walk down every emergency pressure-relief system outlet in your facility: confirm each one discharges either vertically upward (well above personnel levels) or to a contained flare or knockout system, not horizontally toward occupied areas.
  - Review open PHA recommendations from the past ten years: identify any that were declined by management, and confirm that declines were documented with engineering justification, not simply closed.
  - Check whether your operators are restricted from using flares or vents during abnormal conditions due to environmental permit limits, and if so, determine whether a documented override protocol exists for emergency situations.

### ITC Deer Park Storage Tank Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3602 | July 6, 2023
- Company | Location | Industry: Intercontinental Terminals Company, LLC (ITC) | Deer Park, Texas | Bulk liquid storage terminal
- Incident date: March 17, 2019
- Hazard type: fire
- Materials: Butane-enriched naphtha (blend stored in Tank 80-8, an 80,000-barrel atmospheric storage tank); naphtha and petrochemical products in 14 additional tanks in the same containment area
- What happened: A mechanical seal on the circulation pump serving Tank 80-8 failed while the pump continued to operate, releasing butane-enriched naphtha product for approximately 30 minutes before the vapors ignited. Because ITC had no flammable gas detection, no remote emergency isolation valves, and no formal PSM program applied to Tank 80-8 (exempted by the OSHA PSM atmospheric storage tank exclusion), the release was not detected and could not be quickly stopped. The fire spread to all 15 tanks in the containment area over three days. Secondary containment failed when a dike wall breached, releasing an estimated 470,000 to 523,000 barrels of product and contaminated water into Tucker Bayou, Buffalo Bayou, and the Houston Ship Channel.
- Consequences: Zero fatalities, zero injuries; 15 tanks (each 80,000 barrels) destroyed; property damage exceeding $150 million; Houston Ship Channel closed for seven miles; multiple shelter-in-place orders for Deer Park due to benzene air quality concerns; approximately 470,000 to 523,000 barrels of hydrocarbon, foam, and contaminated water released to waterways; parks and schools closed; major highway partially closed
- Sequence: (1) Tank 80-8 holds butane-enriched naphtha with pump continuously circulating product. (2) Pump mechanical seal fails on March 17, 2019; product releases from failed pump. (3) Flammable vapor accumulates for approximately 30 minutes undetected (no gas detection system). (4) Vapors ignite, fire erupts around Tank 80-8. (5) ITC unable to remotely isolate tank or stop release (no ROEIVs). (6) Fire spreads to adjacent tank piping manifolds and then to all 14 other tanks in the common containment area. (7) Fire burns for three days, extinguished March 20. (8) Secondary containment wall breaches. (9) Product and contaminated water flow into adjacent waterways.
- Immediate cause: Failure of the Tank 80-8 circulation pump mechanical seal released flammable butane-enriched naphtha vapor, which accumulated for approximately 30 minutes before igniting.
- Contributing factors:
  - No flammable gas detection system on or near Tank 80-8 (a 2014 hazard review recommendation for gas detection was not implemented and not documented as to why)
  - No remotely operated emergency isolation valves (ROEIVs) on Tank 80-8 or other tanks in the First and Second 80's farm
  - No formal mechanical integrity program for the Tank 80-8 circulation pump, including no condition monitoring with control limits
  - Tank farm design, including tank spacing, absence of subdivisions, pump placement within containment, and drainage design, facilitated fire spread
  - ITC was not required to apply OSHA PSM or EPA RMP to Tank 80-8 due to the atmospheric storage tank exemption (OSHA) and flammability rating exemption for the naphtha-butane mixture (EPA)
  - While ITC applied some process safety elements across the terminal, it did not apply mechanical integrity or PHA to Tank 80-8
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Absence of a comprehensive PSM program for Tank 80-8 and its equipment: no mechanical integrity, no PHA, no hazard controls specifically applied to this hazardous service
  - Regulatory gap: OSHA PSM atmospheric storage tank exemption and EPA RMP flammability exemption left Tank 80-8 outside formal regulatory safety requirements despite storing large quantities of flammable material
  - Failure to implement a prior hazard review recommendation (2014 gas detection recommendation closed without action or documentation)
  - Tank farm design standards (NFPA 30 minimum requirements) permitted configurations that allowed fire escalation; additional industry guidance existed but was not required
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Flammable gas detection and alarm: absent around Tank 80-8 and the tank farm
  - Remotely operated emergency isolation valves: absent on Tank 80-8 and all 15 tanks in the farm
  - Pump mechanical integrity program with condition monitoring: absent for Tank 80-8 circulation pump
  - Secondary containment integrity: dike wall breached under the combined load of product, firefighting water, and foam
  - Tank farm subdivision (fire barriers between tank groups): absent in the First and Second 80's farm
- Key findings:
  - None of the following safeguards existed: gas detection, remote isolation, pump condition monitoring, and tank farm subdivision; the absence of all four together created a scenario with no meaningful barriers between the initial pump failure and the three-day catastrophic fire
  - The OSHA PSM atmospheric storage tank exemption is a structural regulatory gap that has now contributed to multiple major incidents (Motiva, CAPECO, ITC)
  - A 2014 internal hazard review team recommended gas detection; ITC did not implement it and did not document the reason, a direct violation of PSM-quality hazard review closure practices even though PSM did not formally apply
  - No injuries or fatalities demonstrates that personnel warning is achievable even in major events, but the environmental and property consequences were severe and preventable
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - ITC: Develop and implement a comprehensive PSM program for all atmospheric storage tanks in highly hazardous service; implement pump condition monitoring, flammable gas detection, and ROEIVs; evaluate tank farm design against API STD 2610 and NFPA 30 current editions
  - API: Update API STD 2610 to include flammable gas detection requirements for tank terminals
  - OSHA: Eliminate the atmospheric storage tank exemption from the PSM standard (superseding earlier similar recommendations from Motiva and CAPECO investigations)
  - EPA: Expand RMP rule to cover all flammable liquids with NFPA flammability rating of 3 or higher, including mixtures (superseding CAPECO recommendation)
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical Integrity, Process Hazard Analysis, Management of Change, Hazard Recognition, Emergency Planning and Response, Regulatory Coverage (PSM/RMP applicability), Equipment Design (tank farm layout)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A pump seal failed, the vapor caught fire, and once the fire started there was no way to stop it from spreading to 14 other tanks.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Tank 80-8 and its equipment fell through multiple regulatory and management system gaps simultaneously. The PSM atmospheric storage tank exemption meant formal process safety requirements never applied. ITC's voluntary safety program did not cover the pump. A 2014 team spotted the gas detection gap but the recommendation was never implemented and never documented. And the tank farm was designed to meet only minimum NFPA 30 standards, with no fire subdivision, meaning the first tank fire had an unobstructed path to the entire farm.
- Monday-morning checks:
  - Identify all pumps serving highly flammable or toxic liquid storage at your facility and confirm each has a documented condition monitoring program with vibration or other parameter limits that trigger alarms before seal failure.
  - Walk the tank farms and identify which tanks lack flammable or toxic gas detection coverage, and which lack remote isolation capability; prioritize based on stored volume and hazard level.
  - Review your PSM/RMP applicability determinations for all storage tanks: if any atmospheric storage tanks in flammable or toxic service are excluded, confirm that voluntary safety elements (PHA, mechanical integrity) are still applied to them.

### KMCO Facility Fatal Equipment Rupture, Explosion, and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3603 | December 2023
- Company | Location | Industry: KMCO, LLC | Crosby, Texas | Specialty chemical manufacturing (tolling and lubrication additives)
- Incident date: April 2, 2019
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Liquid isobutylene (highly flammable, stored and processed at pressure); isobutylene vapor cloud
- What happened: While making a batch of sulfurized isobutylene lubrication additive, a 3-inch cast iron y-strainer in the liquid isobutylene supply piping catastrophically fractured, releasing a large isobutylene vapor cloud. The y-strainer failed from brittle overload fracture driven by internal pressure, most likely from liquid thermal expansion in a dead-leg section of piping that had no pressure protection. More than 10,000 pounds of isobutylene released. Operations staff attempted to manually stop the release by closing valves in the field, which was the site culture for responding to chemical releases. While the release was largely stopped by operators closing manual valves, workers were still within or near the vapor cloud when it suddenly exploded, most likely ignited by electrical equipment in a poorly sealed adjacent building.
- Consequences: 1 fatality (field operator James Mangum); 2 serious injuries (burn injuries to another operator and a shift supervisor); at least 28 other workers injured (5 KMCO employees and 23 contractors); 200-plus people on site; community shelter-in-place order (1-mile radius) for over four hours; portions of facility substantially damaged; KMCO subsequently filed for bankruptcy and the facility was sold
- Sequence: (1) KMCO operates sulfurized isobutylene batch process; PSM and RMP covered. (2) April 2, 2019, 10:41 a.m.: y-strainer body fractures; loud noise alerts nearby field operator trainee. (3) Operator contacts board operator; urgent response begins. (4) Operations team moves workers away from vapor cloud and closes road access. (5) Operators enter release area to manually close valves; release largely stopped but more than 10,000 pounds already released. (6) Facility alarm system never activated to notify all personnel. (7) 10:51 a.m.: vapor cloud explodes while two operators are still within it and shift supervisor is nearby. (8) Fatal and serious burn injuries result; explosion damages portions of facility. (9) Shelter-in-place issued for 1-mile radius.
- Immediate cause: Brittle overload fracture of a 3-inch cast iron y-strainer in the liquid isobutylene supply piping, driven by internal pressure from liquid thermal expansion in unprotected dead-leg piping.
- Contributing factors:
  - Cast iron y-strainer used in flammable liquid service, violating industry standards and good practice guidance that prohibit or warn against cast iron in hazardous applications
  - No pressure relief device or other high-pressure protection on the charge pump inlet piping dead-leg section where thermal expansion could occur
  - A y-strainer crack was discovered in 2015 but the investigation did not result in corrective actions addressing thermal expansion or material selection
  - Multiple PHAs (2014, 2015, 2018) did not identify the cast iron material or the thermal expansion risk; 2018 PHA drawings incorrectly showed the y-strainer as stainless steel
  - KMCO had a 2010 insurance recommendation to install remote isolation capability on isobutylene and other flammable systems; implemented for some systems but not for isobutylene
  - No remotely operated shutoff capability on the isobutylene charge pump inlet piping; operators could not stop the release from a safe location
  - KMCO plant culture expected operators to respond quickly and physically to chemical releases rather than retreat and rely on emergency response
  - KMCO's emergency response procedures did not distinguish between incidents operators should address versus those for a qualified emergency response team
  - Facility alarm system was never activated during the incident, leaving many personnel uninformed of the emergency
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Inadequate hazard evaluation program: three successive PHAs all failed to identify the cast iron y-strainer material or the thermal expansion scenario; drawings used in PHAs were inaccurate
  - Incomplete mechanical integrity standards: no material specification requirements preventing use of brittle materials in hazardous liquid service
  - Incomplete remote isolation: 2010 recommendation for remote isolation of isobutylene system not implemented despite partial implementation elsewhere
  - Deficient emergency response program: culture of manual intervention by operators in emergencies rather than retreat-and-isolate, and lack of clear policy distinguishing operator vs. ERT roles
  - Weak incident investigation program: 2015 y-strainer crack not adequately investigated to identify root cause or corrective actions
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Pressure relief device on charge pump inlet piping: absent for the dead-leg section (existed on other portions of the system)
  - Remotely operated emergency isolation valve on isobutylene system: not installed (installed on some other systems per partial implementation of 2010 recommendation)
  - Cast iron-free material specification: no requirement preventing brittle material in hazardous liquid service
  - Facility alarm system activation: not used during the incident
  - Process hazard analysis accuracy: all three PHAs missed the y-strainer material and thermal expansion hazard
- Key findings:
  - Industry knowledge that cast iron should not be used in hazardous applications has existed for decades; KMCO's PHAs consistently missed this despite using the actual equipment drawings
  - Remote isolation capability is a 50-plus-year-old concept in process safety; KMCO acknowledged this by implementing it for some systems following a 2010 insurance recommendation but never completed it for isobutylene
  - The sequence of events in which operators moved toward the hazard to manually stop the release is a predictable outcome of a culture and training program that expected manual intervention; this is a systemic program issue, not an operator error
  - KMCO filed for bankruptcy as a result of the incident; no formal recommendations were issued by the CSB because the company ceased to exist and the process was dismantled
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - No formal CSB recommendations issued (KMCO bankrupt; process dismantled by new owner Altivia Oxide Chemicals). CSB urged Altivia to review the report and address the causal factors before reinstating any process involved in the incident.
  - Key lessons disseminated for industry: avoid cast iron in hazardous applications; protect all piping against thermal expansion where applicable; provide remote isolation; do not rely on manual operator response to stop large chemical releases; use facility alarm systems in emergencies; and treat prior incidents as warning signs.
- PSM elements implicated: Process Hazard Analysis, Mechanical Integrity, Management of Change (pre-startup safety review), Emergency Planning and Response, Incident Investigation, Operating Procedures, Training
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A pipe fitting broke and released a flammable gas cloud that exploded, killing a worker who was trying to manually stop the leak.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Three consecutive process hazard analyses all missed the same two problems: the wrong material for the pipe fitting (cast iron in a flammable liquid service) and the absence of pressure protection for a trapped section of piping where thermal expansion could build dangerous pressure. A near-miss in 2015 when the same y-strainer cracked was not investigated deeply enough to find the root cause. And when the fitting finally failed, the plant's emergency culture, which expected operators to physically intervene to stop releases, sent workers into the vapor cloud rather than to safety.
- Monday-morning checks:
  - Audit piping component specifications for any flammable or toxic liquid systems: confirm that all fittings, strainers, and specialty items are specified as ductile materials (steel, stainless steel) and that cast iron is explicitly prohibited.
  - Identify all potential dead-leg piping sections in liquid hydrocarbon or high-pressure systems and verify that each is protected against liquid thermal expansion (pressure relief device or design margin documentation).
  - Review your emergency response policy and confirm it clearly states which events operators should respond to in the field versus which events require all personnel to evacuate and wait for the emergency response team.

### Philadelphia Energy Solutions Refinery HF Alkylation Unit Fire and Explosions

- Archive id | Final report date: 3604 | October 11, 2022
- Company | Location | Industry: Philadelphia Energy Solutions (PES) | Philadelphia, Pennsylvania | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: June 21, 2019
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE; toxic gas release (hydrofluoric acid)
- Materials: Carbon steel pipe elbow with high nickel and copper residual elements (corroded by hydrofluoric acid); propane (approximately 95% of released vapor cloud); hydrofluoric acid (HF, approximately 2.5% of released vapor); butylene, isobutane, butane (in V-1 vessel that ruptured)
- What happened: A pipe elbow in the HF alkylation unit ruptured due to wall thinning from accelerated HF corrosion. The elbow contained unusually high concentrations of nickel and copper (a legacy of improper double-stamping as two different steel specifications during manufacture around 1973), which caused it to corrode far faster than adjacent piping. The rupture released a large vapor cloud (approximately 95% propane, 2.5% HF) that ignited two minutes later, creating a jet flame. Attempts to activate HF water spray mitigation cannons remotely failed because the control system and backup power had been damaged by the initial explosion. Three secondary explosions followed, the largest when a vessel (V-1) containing approximately 676,000 pounds of hydrocarbons ruptured; a fragment weighing about 38,000 pounds flew across the Schuylkill River. The fire burned until the next morning. PES estimated approximately 5,239 pounds of HF were released to atmosphere. PES announced shutdown of the refinery complex five days after the incident.
- Consequences: 5 workers and 1 firefighter received minor injuries; zero fatalities; estimated property damage of $750 million (ranked third-largest refinery loss worldwide since 1974 by Marsh JLT); approximately 3,271 pounds of HF released to atmosphere; approximately 608,000 pounds of hydrocarbons combusted; refinery permanently shut down
- Sequence: (1) Pipe elbow with high Ni and Cu content corrodes faster than adjacent piping in HF alkylation unit; not detected by inspection program focused on circuit-level measurements rather than component-by-component inspection. (2) June 21, 2019 morning: elbow ruptures; large hydrocarbon and HF vapor cloud releases. (3) Vapor cloud ignites approximately 2 minutes after release; jet flame impinges on adjacent V-1 vessel. (4) Operator activates Rapid Acid Deinventory (RAD) system, successfully draining approximately 339,000 pounds of HF to a safe drum. (5) Operator attempts remote activation of HF water spray mitigation system; control system and backup power damaged, water spray not activated for approximately 40 minutes. (6) Three secondary explosions occur; V-1 vessel violently ruptures, sending a 38,000-pound fragment across the Schuylkill River. (7) Manual activation of water pump eventually achieved approximately 40 minutes into the incident. (8) Fire extinguished next morning. (9) PES announces permanent refinery shutdown June 26, 2019.
- Immediate cause: Rupture of a carbon steel pipe elbow with high nickel and copper content that had thinned from accelerated HF corrosion faster than adjacent piping components with lower concentrations of residual elements.
- Contributing factors:
  - PES and prior owner Sunoco did not inspect all individual carbon steel piping components in the HF alkylation unit; industry guidance (API RP 751) did not require component-by-component inspection at the time of the incident
  - After 2003 NACE research quantified the effect of nickel and copper on HF corrosion rates, neither Sunoco, PES, nor API RP 751 required verification that all existing piping components met the new safe composition limits
  - The failed elbow was improperly double-stamped as both YOLOY and ASTM A234 WPB during manufacture around 1973; this was made possible by the 1965 ASTM A234 standard's failure to prohibit the addition of unspecified alloy elements
  - No remotely operated emergency isolation valves (ROEIVs) on large hydrocarbon vessels adjacent to the failed elbow, allowing jet flame to impinge on V-1 continuously until manual isolation was achieved
  - HF water spray mitigation system rendered non-functional by fire and explosion damage to the control system and backup power (active safeguard failed during the event it was designed to mitigate)
  - No federal regulatory requirement for petroleum refineries to conduct inherently safer technology analysis, and no alternative alkylation technology requirements
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Inadequate mechanical integrity: inspection program did not require component-by-component wall thickness measurements of all piping in HF corrosion service; circuit-level approach missed the one anomalous elbow
  - Failure to update safety requirements after new RAGAGEP: 2003 NACE paper 03651 was seminal; neither API RP 751 nor Sunoco nor PES revised inspection protocols to ensure all existing piping met the new composition limits
  - Absence of remotely operated emergency isolation: no engineering control to stop hydrocarbon feed to the jet flame that weakened and ruptured V-1
  - Active safeguard dependency: HF mitigation relied on an active water spray system that failed when incident conditions damaged its control path, with no passive backup
  - No inherently safer design analysis required or performed; alternatives to HF alkylation not evaluated
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Component-by-component piping inspection for HF corrosion zones: absent (required by revised API RP 751 post-incident)
  - RAGAGEP verification after 2003 NACE composition limits established: not performed by Sunoco, PES, or mandated by API RP 751
  - Remotely operated emergency isolation valves on V-1 and adjacent large hydrocarbon vessels: absent
  - HF water spray mitigation system remote activation: failed due to fire and explosion damage to control system and backup power
  - Rapid Acid Deinventory (RAD) system: successfully operated (functioned as designed)
- Key findings:
  - A single elbow manufactured and improperly stamped in the early 1970s, containing residual elements known by 2003 to cause accelerated HF corrosion, was never identified or inspected because inspection programs measured piping circuits, not individual components
  - The seminal 2003 NACE research changed what was RAGAGEP for materials in HF service, but neither the industry trade group (API), the previous owner, nor PES took the required step of verifying that all existing equipment still met the updated knowledge
  - Active safeguards (requiring triggering by a person or automated system) can and did fail precisely during the major event they were designed to mitigate; reliance on active safeguards without passive redundancy is a systemic vulnerability
  - The PES incident ended the operation of the largest oil refinery on the East Coast of the United States
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - EPA: Require petroleum refineries to conduct a safer technology and alternatives analysis (STAA) as part of PHA; evaluate inherently safer technology (IST) practicability; initiate prioritization review of hydrofluoric acid under TSCA
  - API: Update API RP 751 to require all carbon steel piping circuit components and welds in HF alkylation corrosion zones to be individually inspected; update API RP 751 to address protection of control systems and backup power from fire and explosion hazards, and ability to remotely isolate large HF and hydrocarbon inventories
  - ASTM International: Revise ASTM A234 to include supplementary residual element composition requirements for fittings in HF service, consistent with NACE paper 03651 recommendations (analogous to existing ASTM A106 supplementary requirements for straight piping)
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical Integrity, Process Safety Information (RAGAGEP), Process Hazard Analysis, Safeguard Reliability, Emergency Planning and Response, Inherently Safer Design, Regulatory Coverage
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A corroded pipe elbow burst in a hydrofluoric acid unit, causing a fire and a series of explosions that destroyed the unit.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Industry knowledge updated in 2003 established that certain steel compositions corrode much faster in HF service, but no one, not the refinery, the previous owner, nor the industry standard-setting body, ever required that existing piping be checked against the new knowledge. A single pipe elbow from 1973 was effectively invisible to the inspection program because the program measured corrosion at the circuit level, not the component level. When it failed, there were no remotely operated valves to stop the jet flame, and the active HF mitigation system was knocked out by the explosion it was supposed to respond to.
- Monday-morning checks:
  - If your facility uses HF or other aggressive corrosives, verify that your inspection program samples individual piping components within each circuit, not just circuit-average measurements, to catch accelerated corrosion in anomalous components.
  - Audit whether your facility's piping specifications and inspection programs have been updated to reflect RAGAGEP changes published in the last 10 to 20 years; if new industry knowledge has been published, confirm that existing equipment was verified against it.
  - Test your major active safety systems (water deluge, HF mitigation, fire suppression) under fire and explosion simulation scenarios: determine whether their control systems and power supplies are protected from the events they are meant to respond to.

### AB Specialty Silicones Explosion and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3605 | September 24, 2021
- Company | Location | Industry: AB Specialty Silicones, LLC | Waukegan, Illinois | Specialty chemical manufacturing (silicone emulsions)
- Incident date: May 3, 2019
- Hazard type: reactive runaway
- Materials: Silicon hydride (XL 10, a silicone crosslinker with silicon-hydrogen bonds); potassium hydroxide solution (10% KOH, a strong base); TD 6/12 Blend (a silicone fluid); hydrogen gas (reaction product)
- What happened: During the manufacture of a silicone emulsion product (EM 652), an operator pumped 10% KOH solution into a batch tank that already contained XL 10 and TD 6/12 Blend. The 10% KOH was not called for in the batch recipe; it was stored in a nearly identical 55-gallon blue plastic drum next to the correct ingredient. The KOH reacted with the silicon hydride groups in XL 10 to produce hydrogen gas. The tank had an open hatch-type lid with no vent pipe; hydrogen released directly into the production building. A ventilation air mover near the batch area may have distributed the hydrogen throughout the building and mixed it with air. Workers observed the tank contents foam and overflow and saw a fog form but did not recognize the hydrogen hazard. The hydrogen ignited, causing a massive explosion that destroyed the production building and killed four employees.
- Consequences: 4 fatalities (Byron Biehn, Jeffrey Cummings, Daniel Nicklas, Allen Stevens); production building destroyed; company required to cease some operations and relocate others pending building reconstruction; no community shelter-in-place reported
- Sequence: (1) First batch of EM 652 produced without incident. (2) Second batch begins; operator goes to retrieve next ingredient. (3) Up to 11 nearly identical blue plastic drums (containing both compatible and incompatible chemicals) are present in the emulsions area. (4) Operator adds 10% KOH (likely mistaken for another ingredient due to identical drum appearance) to the tank already containing XL 10 and TD 6/12 Blend. (5) Chemical reaction produces hydrogen gas; tank contents foam and overflow; fog forms. (6) Workers observe process upset but do not recognize hydrogen hazard; do not evacuate. (7) No gas detection or alarm system in building. (8) Hydrogen ignites; massive explosion destroys production building. (9) Four employees fatally injured.
- Immediate cause: An operator added potassium hydroxide (10% KOH) to a batch tank containing silicon hydride (XL 10), triggering a chemical reaction that produced hydrogen gas, which accumulated in the production building and ignited.
- Contributing factors:
  - Incompatible chemicals stored in nearly identical blue 55-gallon drums with only small label and bung cap differences; similar appearance contributed to the operator selecting the wrong drum
  - Incompatible chemicals (10% KOH and XL 10) not required to be segregated or removed from the area after use, allowing both to be present during the second batch
  - AB Specialty's hazard analysis process (technical service request, TSR) was not designed to assess process hazards or establish safeguards; it evaluated a mix of business and safety risks
  - No process hazard analysis performed on the EM 652 batch process despite known hydrogen generation risk associated with XL 10 and a prior 2014 drum explosion
  - Double initial procedure (supervisor and operator both sign batch ticket for each step) was in place but did not prevent the wrong chemical from being added
  - Batch tank had an open hatch-type lid with no vent pipe to direct gases to a safe location
  - Ventilation air mover (blowing outside air into the production area) may have helped distribute hydrogen throughout the building rather than extracting it
  - No hydrogen or flammable gas detection and alarm system in the production building
  - Emergency preparedness training did not cover the hazard of colorless, odorless hydrogen accumulation from a process upset; workers did not recognize the need to evacuate
  - AB Specialty's safety management system did not address process safety; it addressed primarily occupational safety
  - OSHA PSM and EPA RMP did not cover AB Specialty because the chemicals involved are not individually listed as highly hazardous (a gap for reactive hazard combinations)
  - AB Specialty exhibited a weak process safety culture with heavy reliance on procedural controls and minimal engineering controls
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - No functional process hazard analysis program: TSR process was not a PHA; reactive hazards of EM 652 process never formally identified or controlled
  - Absence of a safety management system addressing process safety: AB Specialty had occupational safety programs but no PSM framework
  - Weak process safety culture: known hydrogen generation risks not addressed with engineering controls; reliance on procedure over engineering
  - Regulatory coverage gap: OSHA PSM and EPA RMP use chemical lists rather than reactivity criteria, leaving reactive hazard combinations outside mandatory coverage
  - Failure to learn from 2014 drum explosion: prior incident did not result in improved hazard analysis, storage segregation, or engineering controls
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Chemical segregation and visual differentiation: incompatible chemicals stored in identical drums without required segregation
  - Batch tank vent system: open hatch allowed hydrogen to release directly into occupied building rather than being directed to a safe location
  - Hydrogen or flammable gas detection and alarm: absent in production building
  - Extraction ventilation (local exhaust): absent; existing air mover introduced air into the area, potentially aiding hydrogen distribution
  - Emergency evacuation training for process upset recognition: workers did not recognize the hydrogen hazard when tank overflowed
  - Double initial procedure: in place but insufficient to prevent wrong chemical addition (administrative control insufficient as sole barrier for incompatibility hazard)
- Key findings:
  - A 2014 drum explosion at the same facility was a direct warning sign of the incompatible chemical and identical drum hazard; AB Specialty did not conduct a thorough hazard analysis or make significant changes after that incident
  - The chemical reactivity hazard between KOH and silicon hydrides is publicly documented in tools like the CCPS Chemical Reactivity Worksheet; a basic hazard review would have identified this
  - The PSM and RMP regulatory gaps for reactive hazard combinations represent a long-standing systemic issue the CSB has identified in multiple prior investigations; four workers died in circumstances that federal process safety regulations did not reach
  - Every engineering control that could have prevented or mitigated the explosion (ventilated batch tank, gas detection, chemical segregation, incompatible drum differentiation) was absent
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - OSHA (reiterated from prior reactive hazard study): Amend PSM standard to cover reactive hazards using objective criteria (heat of reaction, gas evolution, combinations of chemicals) rather than individual chemical lists
  - EPA (reiterated from prior reactive hazard study): Amend RMP rule to expand coverage of reactive hazard scenarios
  - AB Specialty Silicones: Develop a formal process hazard analysis program; install hydrogen gas detection and alarm; redesign batch tank ventilation to direct gases to a safe location; implement chemical segregation and visual differentiation requirements; develop and implement a process safety management system
- PSM elements implicated: Process Hazard Analysis, Process Safety Information, Mechanical Integrity (equipment design), Operating Procedures, Training, Emergency Planning, Process Safety Culture, Regulatory Coverage
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A worker grabbed the wrong chemical drum by mistake, mixed incompatible chemicals, and the resulting hydrogen gas explosion killed four people.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The facility processed chemicals capable of generating explosive hydrogen on contact, knew about this risk (the batch ticket warned about XL 10's reactivity with bases), had experienced a drum explosion in 2014, and yet had no engineering controls to prevent a wrong-drum mix from being catastrophic: identical storage drums, no ventilated batch tank, no gas detection, and no evacuation training for process upsets. The safety management system that existed was occupational safety, not process safety, and the chemicals fell through regulatory gaps in PSM and RMP because the hazard came from a reactive combination rather than a listed individual substance.
- Monday-morning checks:
  - Walk your batch chemical storage areas: if incompatible chemicals are stored in containers of similar size, shape, or color, introduce mandatory visual differentiation (different colored lids, labels, or physical separation) as an engineering or administrative requirement, not just a suggestion.
  - For every batch process involving reactive chemicals, confirm that a formal PHA has been conducted that explicitly evaluates incompatible chemical combinations, not just individual ingredient hazards; use the CCPS Chemical Reactivity Worksheet as a screening tool.
  - Audit batch tank and reactor ventilation: confirm that any gases released from open tanks during abnormal conditions are directed to a safe outdoor location or exhaust point, not into the occupied work area.

### Aghorn Operating Waterflood Station Hydrogen Sulfide Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3606 | May 2021
- Company | Location | Industry: Aghorn Operating Inc. | Odessa, Texas | Oil and gas production (waterflood injection)
- Incident date: October 26, 2019
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Hydrogen sulfide (H2S) dissolved in produced water; released from a failed pump plunger
- What happened: Aghorn employee Pumper A responded alone at night to a pump oil level alarm at Aghorn's Foster D waterflood station. The pump (Pump No. 1) was located inside a partially enclosed pump house. Pumper A worked to isolate the pump by closing its discharge valve and partially closing the suction valve, but did not first perform Lockout/Tagout to deenergize the pump, as Aghorn had no written LOTO policy or procedure. At some point, the pump automatically started, and water containing H2S released from a broken pump plunger. Pumper A was fatally injured by H2S exposure inside the pump house. His spouse subsequently gained access to the facility through an unlocked gate (per Aghorn's informal policy of leaving gates unlocked when an employee is working) and entered the pump house searching for him; she was also fatally injured by H2S exposure. The H2S detection and alarm system at the facility was nonfunctional on the night of the incident.
- Consequences: 2 fatalities (Pumper A and his spouse); no other injuries; no community evacuation or shelter-in-place reported
- Sequence: (1) Pump No. 1 crankcase oil level alarm activates; Pumper A dispatched alone at night. (2) Pumper A arrives at Foster D waterflood station; does not use personal H2S detector (not required by Aghorn policy). (3) Pumper A works to manually close pump valves without performing LOTO; pump has a broken plunger. (4) Pump auto-starts (or failure occurs before arrival); water and H2S release from broken plunger. (5) H2S detection and alarm system nonfunctional; no alarm triggered. (6) Pump house bay doors approximately 60% open; exhaust fan operability unknown; ventilation inadequate to remove H2S. (7) Pumper A fatally injured by H2S in pump house. (8) Pumper A's spouse arrives; gate unlocked per informal policy; she enters pump house searching for husband. (9) Spouse also fatally injured by H2S.
- Immediate cause: Probable cause was Aghorn's failure to enforce use of personal H2S detectors near H2S-risk equipment and failure to develop and enforce Lockout/Tagout procedures, leading Pumper A to work on an energized pump that released H2S-containing produced water. Contributing was inadequate pump house ventilation and a nonfunctional H2S detection and alarm system. Contributing to severity was deficient site security that allowed access to the hazard zone.
- Contributing factors:
  - Pumper A was not wearing his personal H2S detector; Aghorn did not require its use as mandatory PPE
  - No written LOTO policy or procedures at Aghorn; Pumper A did not deenergize the pump before working on it
  - H2S detection and alarm system was installed but nonfunctional (control panel not receiving signals from detectors)
  - Pump house ventilation was insufficient to dilute or remove H2S released inside; bay doors only partially open and exhaust fan operability unknown
  - Aghorn's formal safety management program was virtually nonexistent (the company provided only a cell phone policy, an alarm callout procedure, an H2S pamphlet, and a LOTO policy created after the incident)
  - Site security gates were left unlocked when an employee was working, with no mechanism to prevent third-party access to hazardous areas
  - Aghorn had no written operational procedures for pump maintenance or isolation
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Absence of a safety management program: Aghorn had essentially no written safety or operational policies covering equipment isolation, PPE requirements, H2S hazard management, or emergency response
  - Absence of Lockout/Tagout program: no written LOTO procedures, no training, no enforcement; created the pathway for auto-restart of an isolated pump
  - H2S detection and alarm system failure: system installed but not maintained or properly configured, providing no actual protection
  - Deficient site security design: informal policy of leaving gates unlocked created the condition for a second victim to enter the hazardous zone
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Personal H2S detector use: not required by Aghorn; Pumper A not wearing detector
  - Lockout/Tagout: no written program; pump not deenergized before work
  - H2S detection and alarm system: nonfunctional on night of incident
  - Pump house ventilation: inadequate to dilute or exhaust released H2S
  - Site security: unlocked gate allowed spouse to access hazardous area
  - Emergency response: no written emergency procedures; no apparent protocol for when a worker does not check in
- Key findings:
  - The incident involved a cascade of absent or failed safety basics, every layer of protection (personal detector, LOTO, facility detection, ventilation, site security) was either absent or failed simultaneously
  - H2S from water injection wells is a well-known hazard in oil and gas production; Aghorn's failure to manage it at any management system level represents a complete absence of basic process safety for the H2S hazard
  - The second fatality (the spouse) was a direct consequence of a single informal policy decision (leaving the gate unlocked) that had no safety analysis behind it
  - The Railroad Commission of Texas regulates oil and gas production facilities but had limited safety oversight jurisdiction; OSHA jurisdiction was also limited for agricultural and certain oil and gas field operations
- Recommendations (recipient, action):
  - Aghorn: Mandate personal H2S detectors as required PPE for all employees near H2S-risk equipment; develop site-specific LOTO program meeting 29 CFR 1910.147; commission independent analysis of pump house ventilation and mitigation systems; develop H2S safety management program; maintain and properly configure H2S detection and alarm systems; develop site security controls preventing unauthorized access to hazardous areas
  - OSHA: Improve hazard communication to operators of water injection wells regarding H2S risks
  - Railroad Commission of Texas: Develop and implement requirements for H2S safety management programs at waterflood stations where H2S exposure risk exists
- PSM elements implicated: Process Safety Information, Training, Operating Procedures, Mechanical Integrity (detection system), Emergency Planning and Response, Process Safety Culture, Regulatory Coverage (small operator gaps)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A pump released toxic gas in a building, killed a worker, and then the worker's spouse walked in and was also killed.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Aghorn was a small oil and gas operator with essentially no formal safety management program for H2S, a hazard inherent to their core operation. There were no written LOTO procedures, no required personal gas detectors, a broken alarm system, a pump house that could not ventilate H2S effectively, and a gate policy that left the facility open to anyone. The second death was the direct consequence of no site security protocol: when an employee did not return, no check-in mechanism existed, and the unlocked gate removed the one remaining barrier between his spouse and the lethal environment inside.
- Monday-morning checks:
  - For every facility where H2S is present or possible, confirm that personal H2S detection is mandatory PPE with a written policy specifying the detector requirements, calibration, and use conditions.
  - Audit your LOTO program: verify that written procedures exist for every piece of equipment where unexpected energization could expose a worker to a hazardous energy release, and confirm that field compliance is periodically checked.
  - Review your site security access controls for all facilities where a hazardous release could occur: confirm that when a hazard event is active or an employee is working alone in a hazardous area, access by unauthorized persons (including family members attempting rescue) is physically prevented.
