### BP-Husky Toledo Refinery Naphtha Release and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3620 | June 2024
- Company | Location | Industry: BP-Husky Refining LLC (BP Products North America Inc.) | Oregon, Ohio | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: September 20, 2022
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Naphtha (light virgin naphtha), refinery fuel gas
- What happened: A series of process upsets across multiple refinery units throughout the day caused liquid naphtha to overflow a series of towers and vessels, eventually filling a Fuel Gas Mix Drum that normally contained only vapor. Two workers, brothers, manually drained the drum by opening valves and a flange, releasing over 23,000 pounds of liquid naphtha to the ground. The liquid formed a vapor cloud that ignited in a flash fire at approximately 6:46 p.m., fatally injuring both men.
- Consequences: 2 fatalities (brothers, both BP employees); no off-site injuries; approximately $597 million in property damage including loss of use; largest fatal incident at a BP-operated refinery since Texas City 2005.
- Sequence: Coker Gas Plant offline, crude slate change, Crude 1 Tower unstable, absorber-stripper tower overflows naphtha through a vapor bypass line directly to the Fuel Gas Mix Drum, drum fills with liquid naphtha, drum overflows into fuel gas piping, two operators manually drain drum to ground, vapor cloud forms and ignites.
- Immediate cause: Workers opened valves and removed a flange on the pressurized Fuel Gas Mix Drum to release liquid naphtha directly to the ground, forming a vapor cloud that reached an ignition source.
- Contributing factors:
  - Refinery failed to implement effective safeguards for tower and vessel overflow; over-reliance on human intervention as the sole protection layer.
  - No procedures, written instructions, or documented corrective actions existed for responding to high liquid level in the Fuel Gas Mix Drum during upsets.
  - Board operators experienced a severe alarm flood (3,712 alarms in about 12 hours, far exceeding 10 alarms per 10 minutes); critical alarms were missed or delayed.
  - Ineffective abnormal situation management policies; no clear criteria for when to shut down a process or put it in safe state during a cascade of upsets.
  - No stop work authority was exercised despite a day-long series of escalating abnormal events.
  - Lessons from a similar 2019 incident at the same refinery were not acted upon; lessons from the 2005 BP Texas City explosion were also not effectively applied.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Failure to implement inherently safer, engineered overflow prevention; substituting human intervention where hardware protection was warranted.
  - Deficient abnormal situation management program; no structured guidance for cascading upsets.
  - Alarm management system allowed prolonged alarm floods; no recovery targets or per-event metrics.
  - Incident learning system failed to convert prior near-miss events into process changes.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Furnace safety instrumented systems and emergency pressure relief valves did not prevent liquid overflow from the absorber-stripper tower to the drum.
  - High-level instrumentation in the Fuel Gas Mix Drum was not an effective preventive safeguard.
  - No automatic isolation or interlock to stop flow into the drum under high-liquid-level conditions.
  - Stop work authority policy not effectively applied by management or workers.
  - No industry guidance existed on protective systems for Fuel Gas Mix Drums.
- Key findings:
  - The Fuel Gas Mix Drum was designed for vapor only; no safeguards existed specifically for the scenario of liquid filling the drum.
  - The 2019 incident at the same refinery involved similar overflow warning signs that were not incorporated into improved safeguards.
  - Alarm flood extended nearly 12 hours before the incident, creating conditions in which operators could not reliably respond to critical alarms.
  - The BP Texas City 2005 Baker Panel Report recommendations about process safety culture were not effectively implemented at the Toledo facility.
  - Industry (API) had no guidance on Fuel Gas Mix Drum overflow hazards at the time of the incident.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Ohio Refining Company to revise PHA safeguards for overflow scenarios to use engineered controls rather than human intervention alone.
  - Ohio Refining Company to revise abnormal situation management policy using CCPS and ASM Consortium guidance, including predictable scenarios and criteria for when to shut down.
  - Ohio Refining Company to develop a clear stop work authority policy with detailed procedures and training.
  - Ohio Refining Company to revise alarm philosophy using EEMUA guidance, including per-event alarm flood metrics for the Crude 1 board.
  - API to develop guidance on Fuel Gas Mix Drum overflow hazards and recommended safeguards.
  - API to develop guidance on preventing overflow of pressure vessels generally.
  - ISA to revise ANSI/ISA 18.2-2016 to include performance targets for alarm flood recovery.
- PSM elements implicated: process hazard analysis, mechanical integrity, operating procedures, alarm management, abnormal situation management, incident investigation and learning, stop work authority, training
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Two workers manually drained a drum full of flammable liquid and started a fatal fire.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A refinery operating with alarm floods for 12 hours, no safeguards against vessel overflow beyond human response, no procedures for an unprecedented liquid-in-vapor-drum situation, and unlearned lessons from nearly identical prior events set up a scenario where two workers had no guidance and no backup systems when the drum overflowed.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Audit your PHAs for any scenario where human intervention is the only listed safeguard for a high-consequence liquid overflow or loss of containment; replace with engineered controls where feasible.
  - Establish a per-event alarm flood metric (e.g., more than 10 alarms per 10 minutes for more than 15 minutes) that triggers a formal assessment and possible shutdown; do not rely solely on monthly average alarm rates.
  - Confirm that written procedures and trained responses exist for the specific scenario of an unusual liquid entering a vessel normally operated vapor-only; do not assume operators can improvise.

### Marathon Martinez Renewables Fired Heater Tube Rupture and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3625 | March 12, 2025
- Company | Location | Industry: Marathon Martinez Renewables (subsidiary of Marathon Petroleum Corporation) | Martinez, California | Renewable fuels / petroleum refining
- Incident date: November 19, 2023
- Hazard type: fire
- Materials: Renewable diesel (hydrotreated vegetable oil), hydrogen
- What happened: During the first startup of a renewable diesel hydroprocessing unit, a manual bypass valve upstream of a fired heater was inadvertently left open, diverting most process flow around the heater. Simultaneously, air inlet cover plates on two of four in-service burners were left in place, blocking combustion air and causing "afterburning" in the heater's upper convection section. Together, the low flow and afterburning drove tube metal temperatures to 1,710 degrees F, far above the 1,120 degrees F design limit. Convection section tubes bulged, weakened, and ruptured. A field operator standing next to the heater to manually turn off burners was engulfed in flames and suffered third-degree burns over more than 80 percent of his body.
- Consequences: 1 serious injury (field operator, third-degree burns to more than 80 percent of body, hospitalized in critical condition for over six months); approximately $350 million in property damage; unit shut down approximately one year.
- Sequence: Startup valve misaligned (left open), flow diverts around heater, safety instrumented system flow meter reads adequate flow (bypass is downstream of meter), cover plates block burner air inlets, afterburning begins in convection section, tube temperatures rise well above limits, no control room data reveals afterburning, management directs field operator to manually shut off burners next to heater, tube ruptures, renewable diesel and hydrogen ignite, fire engulfs heater and field operator.
- Immediate cause: Fired heater tubes overheated because a misaligned open valve diverted process flow away from the heater while two burners operated with blocked air inlets causing afterburning in the convection section.
- Contributing factors:
  - Safety instrumented system flow meter was upstream of the diversion point; the system showed adequate flow while the heater was running nearly dry.
  - No combustibles monitoring or flame detection capable of detecting afterburning conditions inside the heater was available to control room operators.
  - Tube metal temperature alarming did not have Not-to-Exceed (NTE) limits that would have directed operators to shut down the heater remotely and evacuate personnel.
  - Workers were directed to conduct troubleshooting tasks in close proximity to the heater while it was in an unsafe state with dangerously high tube temperatures.
  - Marathon had not identified the flow diversion scenario in PHAs or after a similar 2018 incident at a different Marathon refinery.
  - Inadequate valve labeling, walk-the-line practices, and startup procedures contributed to the misalignment going undetected.
  - Marathon did not adequately verify that the Martinez facility met corporate minimum safety standards before first startup as a renewable diesel unit.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Corporate oversight failure; facility transitioned from refinery to terminal to renewable diesel without completing a full conformance assessment against Marathon's own standards.
  - PHA did not identify the potential for flow diversion downstream of the safety interlock flow meter, even though a similar scenario had occurred elsewhere.
  - Corporate standard for fired heater NTE limits and alarms did not require adequate tube metal temperature alarming with predetermined shutdown responses.
  - Management of organizational change was insufficient when the facility changed business mode.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Safety instrumented system for low flow did not detect the actual low flow condition because the diversion occurred downstream of the instrument.
  - No effective afterburning detection available to board operators.
  - NTE limits and alarms for tube metal temperature were inadequate or absent.
  - Walk-the-line verification practices were not enforced for startup valve alignment.
- Key findings:
  - From 2020 to 2024, at least eight fired heater tube rupture incidents at multiple companies met CSB reporting criteria, indicating this is an industry-wide hazard.
  - The 2018 Marathon Detroit incident involved a similar low-flow scenario; lessons were not applied to Martinez.
  - Workers were placed in an unsafe location to do manual troubleshooting tasks that could have been done remotely or not at all (heater could have been shut down from the control room).
  - Industry guidance (API RP 556) did not adequately address prevention of afterburning or require worker exclusion zones during high tube temperature conditions.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Marathon Martinez Renewables to implement engineering safeguards (combustibles measurement, flame detection, thermocouples) for afterburning detection, accessible from the control room.
  - Marathon Martinez Renewables to implement NTE tube metal temperature alarms with predetermined shutdown procedures including personnel evacuation trigger points.
  - Marathon Martinez Renewables to improve walk-the-line practices to include pre-startup valve alignment verification.
  - Marathon Martinez Renewables to complete a comprehensive gap assessment against Marathon corporate standards.
  - Marathon Petroleum Corporation to update corporate heater NTE limits standard with tube metal temperature alarming and remote shutdown response requirements.
  - Marathon Petroleum Corporation to update heater application standard to address flow diversion downstream of flow meters and afterburning safeguards.
  - API to revise RP 556 to require response to high tube metal temperatures including personnel exclusion.
- PSM elements implicated: process hazard analysis, safe operating limits, alarm management, operating procedures, training, management of change, corporate oversight, incident investigation and learning, facility siting (worker proximity)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers put a worker right next to a heater with a valve open in the wrong position and it blew up.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A facility that changed its business mode without a rigorous safety conformance review, with PHAs that missed the specific diversion scenario, without adequate instrumentation to tell operators the heater was failing, and with no protocol that would have pulled the field operator away from the heater when tube temperatures were climbing uncontrollably.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Verify that your fired heater safety interlock flow instruments are positioned so no credible bypass or diversion can defeat them; if process flow can be diverted downstream of the instrument, add position interlocks on branch valves or a second flow measurement downstream.
  - Establish explicit tube metal temperature Not-to-Exceed limits with alarms in the control room that trigger a predetermined response including remote heater shutdown and removal of all personnel from the vicinity before troubleshooting continues.
  - Before any facility restarts after an extended idle period or change in product type, require a formal conformance review against corporate process safety standards as a condition of startup authorization.

### Honeywell Geismar Chlorine and Hydrogen Fluoride Releases (Three Incidents)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3626 | 2024 (final investigation report)
- Company | Location | Industry: Honeywell Performance Materials and Technologies | Geismar, Louisiana | Fluorochemicals / specialty chemicals manufacturing
- Incident date: October 21, 2021; January 23, 2023; June 7, 2024 (three separate incidents at same unit)
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Anhydrous hydrogen fluoride (HF), chlorine gas
- What happened: Three serious process safety incidents occurred within less than three years in the same HFC-245fa unit at the Honeywell Geismar facility. In the first (October 2021), a flanged piping connection failed during startup due to stress corrosion cracking of the gasket and acid corrosion of the stud fasteners, releasing HF and fatally injuring a field operator who was wearing inadequate PPE. In the second (January 2023), a reboiler shell that had been known to be thinning from corrosion failed catastrophically under normal operating pressure, releasing over 800 pounds of HF and over 1,600 pounds of chlorine; no injuries occurred because personnel were not in the unit. In the third (June 2024), a contract worker opened an HF-containing flanged connection during maintenance because the line had not been adequately emptied, releasing a small amount of HF and seriously injuring the worker.
- Consequences: Incident 1 (Oct 2021): 1 fatality (field operator), 39 pounds of HF released, $14 million property damage, complex-wide shelter-in-place. Incident 2 (Jan 2023): 0 injuries, over 800 pounds of HF and over 1,600 pounds of chlorine released, $4 million property damage, nearby highways closed, shelter-in-place order for two neighboring companies. Incident 3 (Jun 2024): 1 serious injury (contract worker), less than 1 pound of HF released, 2 days hospitalized.
- Sequence (Oct 2021): Attempt to start up reactor without following written startup procedures, no-flow condition develops, field operators investigate and detect wisping HF leak at flange, gasket suddenly fails catastrophically, field operator 2 exposed and fatally injured. (Jan 2023): Unit startup proceeds normally, reboiler shell (known to be near minimum thickness, replacement project abandoned after engineer departed) fails under normal operating pressure, HF and chlorine released. (Jun 2024): Maintenance workers open flange connection expecting empty line, residual trapped HF releases, worker exposed.
- Immediate cause: (Oct 2021) Catastrophic failure of a flanged piping connection with a corroded gasket and corroded stud fasteners. (Jan 2023) Catastrophic failure of a reboiler shell thinned by HF corrosion to below containment capacity. (Jun 2024) Opening of a flange connection containing residual pressurized HF.
- Contributing factors:
  - (Oct 2021) A 2007 decision to replace corroded gaskets only by attrition left known inadequate gaskets in service for 14 more years; no HF atmospheric monitors or acid-indicating paint were used to detect early leaks.
  - (Jan 2023) The only employee with working knowledge of the reboiler's deteriorating condition left the company; the capital replacement project was never reassigned under management of organizational change (MOOC) procedures. A lower-priority project was funded while the reboiler replacement was not.
  - (Jun 2024) Honeywell did not adequately verify the line was empty before turning it over for maintenance; contract workers were not informed of residual HF hazard.
  - All three incidents: Honeywell's safety management systems existed on paper but were not consistently implemented; audits were inadequate to surface gaps.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Mechanical integrity program failed to track corroded gaskets and reboiler thickness trends to replacement.
  - Change management system did not account for gradual gasket technology transitions or for personnel departures that left critical safety knowledge unattended.
  - Capital project management allowed a known safety-critical replacement project to be defunded and never reassigned.
  - Safe work practices for startup and maintenance were not consistently followed by management or by workers.
  - Organizational resilience: after the 2021 fatality, the site's attention was consumed by recovery, masking developing gaps that led to the 2023 explosion.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - HF atmospheric monitors and acid-indicating paint, both recommended by industry and by Honeywell's own guidance, were not deployed.
  - PPE worn by field operator 2 was not adequate to protect against skin and respiratory exposure to HF.
  - Reboiler thickness inspection identified the problem but the capital project to fix it was defunded and lost after a key engineer left.
  - MOOC procedures were not applied when the engineer responsible for the reboiler project departed.
  - Startup procedures were not followed, placing workers near the failing gasket unnecessarily.
- Key findings:
  - Three serious incidents in under three years at the same unit indicate that incident-by-incident fixes were insufficient; systemic gaps in management system implementation persisted.
  - Honeywell had the procedures and technical knowledge to prevent all three incidents; implementation failure was the core problem.
  - Industry and regulatory attention to HF as a hazardous substance, including potential requirements for safer alternatives, is a cross-cutting issue.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Honeywell to conduct a comprehensive third-party audit of the Geismar facility's process safety and allied management systems across mechanical integrity, MOC, MOOC, capital project management, safe work practices, and contractor management.
  - Honeywell to make the audit report available to the workforce at Geismar, Baton Rouge, and Metropolis sites.
  - EPA to initiate prioritization of hydrofluoric acid for risk evaluation under TSCA, with potential requirements for safer alternatives.
  - OSHA to amend PSM to require MOC review for organizational changes including personnel departures and budget changes.
- PSM elements implicated: mechanical integrity, management of change, process safety information, safe work practices, contractor management, pre-startup safety review, operating procedures, training, emergency planning, organizational change management
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers were killed or injured by HF leaks that Honeywell already knew about but kept deferring to fix.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Three separate failure modes (gasket corrosion, vessel thinning, incomplete line clearing) each connected to management system gaps that Honeywell had the programs to catch but did not consistently execute; after the first fatality, organizational bandwidth was consumed by recovery rather than prevention, allowing the second and third incidents to develop.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - When an engineer who owns a critical safety-related capital project departs, immediately verify that the project is formally reassigned and tracked; do not rely on informal knowledge transfer.
  - Require HF atmospheric monitors or equivalent real-time leak detection at any flange or connection in toxic acid service; do not substitute visual inspection alone for systems that can detect sub-visible leaks early.
  - Treat a technology change decision (even one decided "by attrition") as a tracked management-of-change item with a completion deadline, not an open-ended gradual process.

### Dow Louisiana Operations Glycol II Ethylene Oxide Explosions

- Archive id | Final report date: 3627 | 2025 (final report)
- Company | Location | Industry: Dow Chemical Company, Louisiana Operations | Plaquemine, Louisiana | Specialty chemicals / ethylene oxide manufacturing
- Incident date: July 14, 2023
- Hazard type: reactive runaway | overpressure / mechanical failure
- Materials: Ethylene oxide (reactive, flammable, toxic carcinogen), nitrogen (inerting gas)
- What happened: During normal operations, metal debris from portable work lights left inside a reflux drum during a turnaround caused a rupture disc to partially open, allowing ethylene oxide to enter pressure relief piping. The inerting nitrogen in that piping had slowly leaked out over time, replaced by air, so the ethylene oxide ignited inside the piping, propagated approximately 50 feet to a pressure relief valve, which lifted and allowed the flame to enter the vapor space of the ethylene oxide reflux drum. The drum's ethylene oxide vapor heated and decomposed, pressure rose until the drum catastrophically failed, releasing over 31,000 pounds of toxic ethylene oxide and causing large fires and explosions throughout the plant.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities; 0 injuries (no personnel were in the unit at time of explosion); significant damage to process equipment; local shelter-in-place order affecting hundreds of nearby residents; over 31,000 pounds of ethylene oxide released.
- Sequence: Turnaround maintenance leaves portable work lights inside reflux drum, drum closed without positive confirmation of cleanliness, unit restarted, lights degrade and create metal debris, debris punctures product cooler rupture disc, rupture disc partially opens, ethylene oxide enters pressure relief piping (now filled with air, not nitrogen), ethylene oxide ignites, flame front propagates through 50 feet of piping, pressure relief valve lifts, flame enters reflux drum vapor space, ethylene oxide decomposes, drum catastrophically fails and explodes.
- Immediate cause: Metal debris (from abandoned work lights) punctured a rupture disc, allowing ethylene oxide to contact air in the pressure relief piping, where it ignited and propagated back to the ethylene oxide reflux drum, causing a runaway decomposition and vessel rupture.
- Contributing factors:
  - Vessel closure procedures did not require positive confirmation that vessels were empty and clean before closing; work lights were sealed inside the drum.
  - Pressure relief piping inerting atmosphere (nitrogen) was not monitored during normal operation; air had filled the piping without anyone knowing.
  - Pressure relief system was designed to vent back to the reflux drum rather than to a safe location, creating a path for flame to re-enter the process.
  - The product cooler had been replaced in 2010 without modifying the relief system to eliminate the thermal expansion hazard that necessitated venting into the drum.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Inadequate vessel closure procedure: no requirement for positive confirmation of cleanliness before restart; reliance on workers' memory or informal checks.
  - Inadequate inerting system control: no requirement to monitor or maintain inert atmosphere in pressure relief piping between maintenance periods.
  - Pressure relief system design perpetuated a known hazard (ethylene oxide vented back to reflux drum) without re-evaluation when the product cooler was replaced.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No foreign material exclusion standard for vessel closure that required documented positive confirmation.
  - No monitoring of inert gas concentration in the pressure relief piping between the rupture disc and relief valve.
  - Pressure relief system discharged back to the ethylene oxide reflux drum rather than to a safe disposal location.
- Key findings:
  - The industry widely understands that nitrogen inerting is essential for ethylene oxide safety; Dow pumped nitrogen into the piping during maintenance but did not monitor it during operation.
  - The product cooler rupture disc was not designed to resist mechanical impact from debris, but no assessment considered this failure mode.
  - Dow established improved vessel closure procedures and a Global Foreign Materials Exclusion Standard after the incident; CSB found those actions sufficient to not issue a vessel closure recommendation to Dow.
  - Guidance for ensuring vessels are left clean after confined space entry is insufficient in current standards.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Dow to identify all ethylene oxide process lines that should be inerted, determine which lack continuous monitoring, and establish proper controls or eliminate the lines.
  - NFPA to update NFPA 350 to require positive confirmation of vessel cleanliness after confined space entries before startup.
  - NFPA to update NFPA 326 with requirements for cleanliness confirmation before restart.
  - ASSP to update ANSI/ASSP Z117.1 to include guidance on vessel cleanliness verification after confined space entry.
- PSM elements implicated: pre-startup safety review, operating procedures, process hazard analysis, mechanical integrity, pressure relief system design, management of change, training, incident investigation (lack of a prior learning trigger)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers forgot to take their lights out of the drum, restarted the unit, and caused an explosion months later.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A vessel closure practice with no requirement to positively confirm cleanliness, combined with unmonitored inerting piping that had silently filled with air over time, created two independent latent failure conditions that combined catastrophically; neither would have been sufficient alone.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Implement a formal foreign material exclusion checklist for all vessel closures after confined space entry; require a documented positive confirmation (verified by name and signature) that the vessel interior has been inspected and all materials removed before closing.
  - Identify every segment of process piping that relies on an inert gas blanket for safety; verify each one is continuously monitored or on a tested maintenance schedule, and document the current condition during turnarounds.
  - When replacing or modifying a piece of equipment, review whether existing pressure relief system discharge points are still appropriate; re-evaluate any relief that vents back into the same process vessel.

### TS USA Molten Salt Eruption

- Archive id | Final report date: 3629 | 2025 (final investigation report)
- Company | Location | Industry: TS USA (subsidiary of HEF Groupe, France) | Chattanooga, Tennessee | Metal parts heat treatment / liquid nitriding
- Incident date: May 30, 2024
- Hazard type: thermal | overpressure / mechanical failure
- Materials: Molten salt baths (liquid nitriding salts, operating at approximately 800 degrees F), water
- What happened: Workers processed five large cylindrical rollers with internal cavities through a liquid nitriding salt bath and subsequent water rinse. One roller's drain holes were too small to drain all the molten salt from its cavity, forming a solidified salt plug. The roller was then processed through a hot water rinse, but the plug trapped water inside the cavity. Although the roller cooled for over an hour, it remained too hot to continuously touch. Workers incorrectly believed the cavity was solid salt with no water, and decided to re-submerge all five rollers in the 800-degree-F salt bath to melt the plug. When the roller with trapped water was submerged, the water boiled and pressurized the sealed cavity until the roller's press-fit end cap blew off, releasing steam and water into the molten salt bath, causing a steam explosion. The molten salt erupted from the bath and engulfed a line operator standing nearby with second- and third-degree chemical and thermal burns over 95 percent of his body. He died later that day.
- Consequences: 1 fatality (line operator, burns over 95 percent of body); multiple fires throughout the facility; approximately $1.3 million in property damage; facility shut down approximately eight months.
- Sequence: Rollers with internal cavities submerged in molten salt bath, salt enters cavities, roller lifted with salt plug blocking drain, roller placed in hot water rinse bath, water enters plugged cavity, roller lifted (water trapped by plug), roller allowed to cool one hour but still hot, workers decide to resubmerge all rollers to melt plug, trapped water boils when heated by 800-degree-F salt, roller cavity overpressurizes, end cap blows off, steam and water contact molten salt, steam explosion, molten salt erupts, operator fatally burned.
- Immediate cause: Water trapped inside a roller cavity was introduced into the 800-degree-F oxidizing salt bath, causing a steam explosion and molten salt eruption.
- Contributing factors:
  - Workers were unaware that water could be trapped inside roller cavities and that introducing water to molten salt causes a steam explosion.
  - No procedures existed for reprocessing parts with cavities or for checking for entrapment hazards before re-submerging parts.
  - No training existed on cavity accumulation hazards in the nitriding line.
  - No hazard analysis had been performed on the nitriding process or on handling new part geometries.
  - HEF Groupe had experienced at least three similar prior incidents at other facilities; lessons were not shared with or adopted by TS USA.
  - No physical barriers isolated workers from the molten salt baths during operations.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - TS USA had quality management systems but no process safety management system at the Chattanooga facility.
  - Absence of a hazard analysis program meant no systematic identification of accumulation hazards for cavity-containing parts.
  - HEF Groupe developed corporate hazard analyses and risk assessments for the nitriding process but did not communicate them to subsidiaries or verify compliance.
  - Corporate incident investigations were not shared across the enterprise to inform subsidiary safety practices.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No protective barriers to isolate workers from molten salt eruption hazards.
  - No procedure for assessing accumulation hazards in parts before processing.
  - No protocol for verifying that cavities are drained and free of water before re-submerging in hot salt.
  - Corporate safety alert letters and risk assessments existed but were not implemented at TS USA.
- Key findings:
  - HEF Groupe had prior incidents involving the same hazard (water or other materials trapped in cavities of parts being processed in molten salt) at other facilities; this was a known corporate hazard not shared with subsidiaries.
  - The facility's lack of any process safety management structure meant that this known hazard was invisible at the facility level.
  - Simple physical barriers around salt baths were absent despite being a feasible safeguard.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - TS USA to implement physical protective barriers around all molten salt baths at all locations.
  - TS USA to develop a safety management system including hazard analysis for new and existing parts (including cavity accumulation hazards), written operating procedures, training program, and incident investigation program aligned with CCPS guidelines.
  - TS USA to establish a position with professional expertise in risk-based process safety at each facility.
  - HEF Groupe to include protective barriers as standard design for all liquid nitriding processes.
  - HEF Groupe to develop a corporate-level safety management system with hazard analysis, procedures, training, and incident investigation that applies to all subsidiaries, with verified implementation.
  - HEF Groupe to establish a mechanism to communicate prior incidents and safety alerts to all subsidiary facilities.
- PSM elements implicated: process hazard analysis, operating procedures, training, incident investigation and learning, corporate safety governance, management of change, physical safeguards
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers accidentally put water into a molten salt bath and it exploded.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A company operating a thermally hazardous process with no process safety management system, unaware that its corporate parent had encountered the exact same hazard multiple times before and had hazard assessments that were never communicated to the site.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Before processing any part with internal cavities, enclosed spaces, or restricted drain holes in a hot bath or high-temperature process, specifically assess whether liquids or gases could be trapped and what happens when they heat rapidly; require a documented answer as a condition of processing.
  - Verify that incident investigation findings from other sites in your corporate group (and from industry alerts) are systematically shared and reviewed at every facility operating similar processes; "this happened at another site" should trigger local review, not silence.
  - Evaluate whether physical barriers can isolate workers from any plausible high-energy release (eruption, flash, explosion) in thermally intensive operations; proximity to a hazard is often the biggest controllable variable in severity.

### Cuisine Solutions Ammonia Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3630 | 2025 (final investigation report)
- Company | Location | Industry: Cuisine Solutions, Inc. | Sterling, Virginia | Food processing / commercial refrigeration
- Incident date: July 31, 2024
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Anhydrous ammonia (refrigerant)
- What happened: A refrigeration process upset led to overpressure in a vessel called the Heat Exchanger 5 Surge Drum at approximately 8:20 p.m. The emergency pressure relief valve on the vessel discharged to the atmosphere adjacent to the employee parking lot, releasing approximately 275 pounds of anhydrous ammonia. Because the relief contained a high liquid fraction, much of the ammonia immediately slumped to ground level. The production manager called for site evacuation, but no ammonia sensors inside the building triggered, no sitewide evacuation alarm sounded, and no emergency shutdown buttons were activated. Workers evacuated through the ammonia cloud. When emergency responders arrived, more than ten individuals were unconscious. Thirty-three workers were transported to hospitals; four were admitted, including one in intensive care.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities; 33 injuries (4 admitted to hospital, 1 in intensive care); approximately $3 million in property damage; food production lines shut down 16 to 38 days.
- Sequence: Evening refrigeration process upset begins, overpressure develops in Heat Exchanger 5 Surge Drum, emergency pressure relief valve opens and discharges to atmosphere adjacent to employee parking lot, ammonia cloud with high liquid fraction slumps to ground level, production manager calls verbal evacuation, no sitewide alarm sounds, no ammonia sensors in production area trigger, no emergency shutdown activated, workers evacuate through cloud, multiple workers overcome by ammonia.
- Immediate cause: Overpressure in a refrigeration surge vessel caused the emergency pressure relief valve to discharge a two-phase (liquid and vapor) ammonia cloud directly at ground level near workers and evacuation routes.
- Contributing factors:
  - Emergency pressure relief was discharged to the atmosphere adjacent to the parking lot and building, directly in evacuation paths.
  - Relief discharged two-phase (liquid and ammonia aerosol) due to high liquid level in the horizontal surge drum, causing rapid ground-level accumulation.
  - Ammonia detection sensors inside the building did not trigger during the incident; no sitewide evacuation alarm system was activated.
  - Emergency Action Plan did not differentiate between indoor and outdoor ammonia releases and did not account for wind direction or provide alternate evacuation routes.
  - No written emergency shutdown procedures were available for workers to follow before or during evacuation.
  - Emergency refrigeration system shutdown buttons were not used.
  - No process data historian was in place to allow refrigeration technicians to monitor or reconstruct the process upset.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Emergency pressure relief system was designed to vent to the atmosphere next to occupied areas without a dispersion analysis to verify the discharge location was safe.
  - No engineering controls mitigated the likelihood of high liquid content in the relief discharge (e.g., knockout drum, automated emergency shutdown, emergency pressure control).
  - Emergency preparedness program was insufficient for an outdoor ammonia release scenario; drills did not simulate this scenario.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No knockout drum or vapor-liquid separation on the relief discharge to prevent liquid ammonia from slumping to grade.
  - No automated emergency shutdown system to limit ammonia release.
  - Ammonia detection system did not trigger or alert personnel effectively.
  - No sitewide alarm sounded to prompt an organized evacuation.
  - Emergency Action Plan did not address this specific release scenario or provide clear guidance.
- Key findings:
  - Horizontal surge vessels containing saturated ammonia liquid with little vapor space are at elevated risk of two-phase relief discharge; standard refrigeration guidelines (IIAR) did not adequately address this scenario.
  - Discharging emergency relief next to the parking lot and building entrance was inherently unsafe; no dispersion analysis had been conducted to verify the location.
  - Four of the 33 injured workers required hospital admission, showing the potential for a mass casualty event even from a relatively small ammonia quantity when liquid aerosol forms a ground-level cloud near many workers.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - IIAR to update ANSI/IIAR 2 to include guidance preventing or mitigating liquid or two-phase atmospheric discharges, and to require assessment of whether relief devices discharge to a safe location.
  - Cuisine Solutions Sterling to implement engineering controls to reduce the likelihood of two-phase relief discharge and mitigate consequences, including identification of liquid-phase scenarios, engineering controls to prevent overfill or boiling overpressure, and mitigative controls such as knockout drums or automatic shutdown.
  - Cuisine Solutions to implement a process data historian for refrigeration system monitoring.
  - Cuisine Solutions to update the Emergency Action Plan to address indoor and outdoor releases separately, include alternates for evacuation routes, address wind direction, provide emergency shutdown procedures, and conduct annual drills for all personnel.
- PSM elements implicated: process hazard analysis, pressure relief system design, emergency planning and response, alarm management, training, operating procedures, facility siting
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A food plant ammonia relief valve vented into the parking lot and gassed thirty-three workers because there was no alarm and no one knew where to go.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A relief system designed and sited without a dispersion analysis, combined with a refrigeration system that could produce two-phase (liquid-bearing) relief discharge slumping to grade, together with an Emergency Action Plan that did not address outdoor ammonia scenarios and an alarm system that never activated, turned a manageable pressure upset into a mass exposure event.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Confirm that every atmospheric ammonia (or other toxic gas) relief valve discharge point has been evaluated by a dispersion analysis to verify that IDLH concentrations cannot reach occupied areas, evacuation routes, or fence lines; relocate or add mitigation if they can.
  - For any horizontal surge drum or other vessel with high liquid inventory and limited vapor space, assess whether the emergency relief discharge could be two-phase; if so, add a knockout drum or equivalent vapor-liquid separator before the atmospheric vent.
  - Conduct an unannounced or tabletop drill specifically for an outdoor toxic gas release, testing whether all personnel know alternate routes, how to shelter in place, and how to activate emergency shutdown; make sure the drill scenario matches your highest-probability outdoor release location.

### PEMEX Deer Park Refinery Hydrogen Sulfide Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3632 | 2025 (final investigation report)
- Company | Location | Industry: PEMEX Deer Park Refinery (PEMEX) | Deer Park, Texas | Petroleum refining
- Incident date: October 10, 2024
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Hydrogen sulfide (H2S)
- What happened: Contract workers from Repcon were assigned to open specific flanged piping connections in an amine unit undergoing turnaround maintenance. The intended flange was marked with an identification tag placed on a platform railing above the flange, out of the workers' line of sight. The workers instead opened an identical-looking flange five feet away in an operational unit containing pressurized hydrogen sulfide, releasing approximately 27,000 pounds of H2S. The H2S cloud traveled downwind to an adjacent unit where another contractor (from ISC Constructors), unaware of any release, was overcome and killed. The release continued for nearly an hour until responders reassembled the flange. Thirteen additional workers were transported to medical facilities.
- Consequences: 2 fatalities (1 Repcon contract worker at the release point; 1 ISC Constructors worker downwind); 13 additional workers transported for medical evaluation; shelter-in-place orders for Deer Park and Pasadena, Texas; $12.3 million in property damage from loss of use.
- Sequence: Repcon workers reassigned from isolated turnaround unit to adjacent operational unit containing H2S, workers not briefed on hazards of new unit, workers issued broad work permit without clear hold points, workers search for unlocked flange-locking devices as informal identification method, workers open wrong flange (5 feet from intended), 27,000 pounds of H2S released, H2S travels downwind to adjacent unit, ISC worker overcome, release continues for nearly one hour, responders reassemble leaking flange.
- Immediate cause: Workers opened incorrect piping (containing pressurized H2S) because the correct piping was not clearly and unambiguously identified before work was authorized.
- Contributing factors:
  - PEMEX Deer Park did not establish a reliable method to identify the specific equipment to be opened; the identification tag for the correct flange was placed above and out of sight of the workers.
  - Workers commonly used the presence or absence of flange-locking devices as an informal identifier of the intended flange; this was not a reliable method.
  - The broad work permit covered multiple jobs with different hazards and did not include clear hold points requiring workers to stop and confirm with a PEMEX operator before opening H2S piping.
  - Workers were abruptly reassigned from an isolated, shutdown turnaround unit to an active, operational unit containing H2S, without receiving specific hazard orientation for the new unit.
  - PEMEX Deer Park had documented policies aligned with industry guidance but management and operations personnel frequently deviated from required practices; conduct of operations was deficient.
  - The hazard to downwind workers in the adjacent unit from a release in the amine unit was not evaluated in the work permit.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - No industry standard for positive equipment identification applicable to chemical processing or refining existed at the time of the incident.
  - Work permitting system was too broad and did not control the specific hazard of opening pressurized toxic piping in an operational unit.
  - Contractor management failed to ensure newly assigned workers received unit-specific hazard briefings when work environments changed mid-shift.
  - Conduct of operations program did not enforce adherence to written policies; the gap between documented requirements and actual practice was large and unaudited.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - No reliable positive identification method for the specific flange to be opened.
  - No mandatory stop and verify step with a PEMEX operator before opening H2S-containing equipment.
  - No hazard evaluation of the downwind impact on workers in adjacent units.
  - Workers were not informed of the H2S hazard when transferred to the operational unit.
- Key findings:
  - Accidental releases from opening wrong equipment are a recurring industry hazard; at the time of the report, no industry standard for positive equipment identification existed for chemical processing or refining.
  - Other industries (healthcare, utilities) have established marking standards that have reduced wrong-equipment incidents; a similar standard is absent in refining.
  - The proximity of two units (one shutdown, one operational and containing H2S) with workers reassigned between them without re-briefing was a key contributor.
  - PEMEX Deer Park improved equipment identification procedures after the incident; CSB did not issue a separate recommendation on that specific point.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - PEMEX Deer Park to label all piping in the amine unit in accordance with ANSI/ASME A13.1.
  - PEMEX Deer Park to develop procedures requiring that any craftworker introduced to or removed from a unit receives clear communication on unit hazards, control measures, and requirements before commencing work in a new area.
  - PEMEX Deer Park to establish a conduct of operations system with behavioral and performance metrics, employee input on procedures, regular audits, and management visibility, following CCPS guidance.
  - ASME to develop written guidelines for marking equipment for opening, including a standard marking practice for equipment identification and means to remove markings at conclusion of work.
- PSM elements implicated: safe work practices, work permitting, contractor management, conduct of operations, hazard communication, operating procedures, process hazard analysis (inter-unit impact evaluation), training
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers opened the wrong pipe in a refinery, released a toxic gas cloud, and two people died.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): No reliable way existed to tell which specific flange to open; workers were abruptly moved from a safe area to a dangerous one without being told it was dangerous; a permit that covered multiple jobs without hold points let them proceed unverified; and a culture gap between documented policies and actual practice had never been systematically closed.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Establish a positive equipment identification standard for any planned pipe or equipment opening: tag the specific flange to be opened with a unique, unambiguous identifier that the executing worker confirms directly before breaking containment, with a required sign-off from the issuing authority.
  - Require that any worker reassigned to a new unit mid-shift, or any contractor entering a unit that differs from their onboarding briefing, receives a short unit-specific hazard briefing before starting work in the new area.
  - Design work permits for line-break or pipe-opening tasks to include an explicit, mandatory hold point requiring face-to-face confirmation with a plant representative before breaking containment on any toxic or flammable service.

### Givaudan Sense Colour Reactor Explosion

- Archive id | Final report date: 3633 | 2025 (final investigation report)
- Company | Location | Industry: Givaudan Sense Colour (acquired from D.D. Williamson) | Louisville, Kentucky | Caramel coloring / food ingredient manufacturing
- Incident date: November 12, 2024
- Hazard type: reactive runaway | overpressure / mechanical failure
- Materials: Caramel coloring sugar ingredient (invert sugar), carbon dioxide (decomposition product)
- What happened: During routine manufacturing of Product 484 (a caramel coloring), the vent valve on Batch Reactor 6 failed in the closed position, blocking venting of gases produced by the normal controlled decomposition of the sugar ingredient. Blocked in, the temperature and pressure in the reactor rose above set points, causing the sugar decomposition reaction to accelerate. The cooling system could not remove heat fast enough, and the emergency pressure relief system was too small to handle the gas production rate. Pressure rose exponentially until Reactor 6 catastrophically ruptured at approximately 237 psig, three times its 75 psig maximum allowable working pressure. The blast wave destroyed the adjacent control room, which was not blast-resistant and was located just 40 feet away, killing two employees inside. Three others were seriously injured. Reactor fragments and debris were ejected up to 400 feet, damaging nearby homes and businesses. A shelter-in-place order was issued for a 1-mile radius.
- Consequences: 2 fatalities (employees inside adjacent control room); 3 serious injuries; approximately $30 million property damage to facility; approximately $10 million in damage to nearby homes and businesses; shelter-in-place for 1-mile radius; facility has since been demolished.
- Sequence: Vent valve (pressure control valve) fails closed 18 minutes before rupture, gases cannot be vented, temperature and pressure rise above set points, accelerating exothermic sugar decomposition begins, cooling system activated (9 minutes before rupture) but insufficient to control runaway, emergency pressure relief system activates but is undersized for the accelerated decomposition (would need to be four times larger), pressure rises to 237 psig (over three times MAWP of 75 psig), reactor catastrophically ruptures, blast wave collapses adjacent control room, two employees inside fatally injured.
- Immediate cause: The vent valve failed closed, causing the normal sugar decomposition reaction to accelerate into an uncontrolled runaway. The emergency pressure relief system was undersized by a factor of four for this scenario and could not prevent overpressure.
- Contributing factors:
  - Facility management did not understand that caramel coloring sugar ingredients could undergo a hazardous, runaway exothermic decomposition producing high temperature and pressure.
  - Emergency pressure relief system for Reactor 6 was sized only for normal operations, not for the accelerated decomposition scenario; a 2012 testing event that revealed similar reactivity in another product was not investigated to its root cause.
  - Reactor 6 was relocated from a closed facility and reinstalled in 2021 without conducting a full hazard review of the relief system design for the product being made.
  - Institutional knowledge about the reactive hazards discovered in 2012 was lost over time; no tracking system or process safety information system preserved it.
  - Safe operating limit procedures required evacuation if limits were exceeded, but operators did not understand the urgency or potential consequences; no automatic alarms triggered when limits were exceeded.
  - The control room was 40 feet from the reactor with no blast resistance; no facility siting analysis was performed.
  - The facility was not subject to OSHA PSM or EPA RMP (caramel coloring manufacturing does not trigger coverage), so formal process hazard analysis, management of change, and process safety information requirements did not apply.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Fundamental lack of understanding of the reactive hazard potential of sugar ingredients used in caramel coloring manufacturing.
  - No systematic hazard review when Reactor 6 was relocated and reinstalled; process safety policies were not fully implemented.
  - Loss of institutional knowledge about product reactivity hazards after 2012 discovery; no requirement to maintain or transfer such knowledge.
  - OSHA PSM and EPA RMP regulatory gap: reactive hazards in non-covered processes are not subject to systematic PHA, MOC, or relief sizing requirements.
  - Facility siting analysis never performed; control room located in proximity to hazardous reactor without blast protection.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Emergency pressure relief system was undersized by a factor of four for the runaway decomposition scenario.
  - No alarm or automatic indicator alerted operators when safe operating limits were exceeded.
  - Cooling system was inadequate to control the accelerating runaway reaction.
  - Control room was not blast-resistant and was too close to the reactor.
  - Vent valve had no redundant venting path if it failed closed.
- Key findings:
  - No industry guidance specifically addresses the safe manufacturing of caramel coloring, including the decomposition hazards of sugar ingredients.
  - Sugar ingredient safety data sheets varied widely; the SDS provided to Givaudan by their supplier did not warn of decomposition hazard, while another supplier's SDS for the same type of material did.
  - The relief system would have needed to be four times larger, or alternatively, a 4-inch rupture disc alone could have provided adequate relief capacity.
  - OSHA PSM and EPA RMP coverage does not adequately address process-specific reactive hazards; regulatory gap remains after decades of prior recommendations.
  - A 2,000-pound reactor shell was launched 245 feet into a residential yard.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - Givaudan caramel coloring facilities to contract a third party for calorimetry testing of all caramel coloring product types to characterize their reactivity.
  - Givaudan caramel coloring facilities to contract a third party for a hazard analysis of each facility incorporating reactivity data.
  - OSHA to amend PSM to achieve comprehensive control of reactive hazards using objective criteria including reactive hazard classifications and catastrophic potential.
  - EPA to revise RMP to explicitly cover catastrophic reactive hazards.
  - Previously issued recommendations (from 2002 Reactive Hazard Study) to OSHA and EPA reiterated.
- PSM elements implicated: process hazard analysis, reactive chemistry assessment, pressure relief system design, facility siting, safe operating limits, alarm management, process safety information, management of change, training, regulatory coverage of reactive hazards
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A control valve stuck closed, a chemical reaction ran away, and a food factory reactor exploded and killed two workers in the next room.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A manufacturing operation where no one understood that the sugar ingredient could undergo a self-accelerating decomposition reaction, where a 2012 near-miss was never investigated to root cause, where the replacement reactor was installed with an undersized relief system, and where the control room was placed adjacent to the reactor without any blast analysis, all operating under no regulatory requirement for formal process hazard analysis because caramel coloring manufacturing falls outside current PSM/RMP coverage.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - For any exothermic reaction process, confirm that the emergency pressure relief system has been explicitly sized for the worst-case runaway or blocked-in scenario, not just for normal operations; if calorimetry data is absent, obtain it before concluding the relief is adequate.
  - Confirm that the location and blast resistance of your control room (and any occupied building near reactors or high-pressure vessels) has been evaluated by a formal facility siting study; "close is convenient" is not a safety argument.
  - When an unusual result appears during testing or a near-miss occurs (elevated temperature, unexpected pressure, abnormal color, unexpected gas generation), treat it as a required investigation trigger for reactive hazards; do not set it aside because it was isolated.

### Acetylene Services Company (ASCO) Gas Explosion

- Archive id | Final report date: 3641 | January 2006 (Safety Bulletin)
- Company | Location | Industry: Acetylene Services Company (ASCO) | Perth Amboy, New Jersey | Acetylene manufacturing and packaging
- Incident date: January 25, 2005
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Acetylene (colorless, highly reactive, extraordinarily flammable; flammable range 2.5 to 82 percent in air; can detonate)
- What happened: ASCO employees started up the calcium carbide acetylene generator after a period of filling with purchased acetylene. During startup, the city water supply was closed before the recycled water system was placed into service, leaving the generator pressurized with acetylene but with no water seal to prevent backflow. A check valve on the recycled water supply line failed to prevent reverse flow (the valve design was prone to misalignment and buildup of lime scale). Acetylene flowed backward through the recycle water line and out through a drain valve that was normally left open in winter for freeze protection, entering the adjacent unventilated wooden lime shed. The shed contained a propane space heater with a combustion chamber surface at approximately 1,100 degrees F (above acetylene's autoignition temperature of 580 degrees F), as well as general-purpose (non-explosion-proof) electrical equipment. Acetylene accumulated in the unventilated shed, ignited, and detonated. The explosion killed three workers and seriously injured a fourth.
- Consequences: 3 fatalities (two workers killed instantly outside the shed, one died shortly after arriving at hospital); 1 serious injury; shed completely destroyed; adjacent manufacturing building heavily damaged; debris ejected up to 450 feet; no fire following the explosion.
- Sequence: Generator started up with purchased acetylene depleted, city water supply closed before recycled water system started, generator pressurized with acetylene but no water-seal backflow prevention, check valve fails to prevent reverse flow (guide pin hung up on pipe nipple), acetylene flows through open winter drain valve into unventilated lime shed, acetylene accumulates in shed, ignites on 1,100-degree-F heater combustion chamber surface, detonation occurs, shed destroyed, three workers outside shed killed by blast wave.
- Immediate cause: Acetylene backflowed through a defective check valve and a routinely left-open drain valve into an unventilated shed containing an ignition source.
- Contributing factors:
  - Freeze-protection practice of leaving a drain valve open in winter created an unrecognized pathway for acetylene to reach the shed.
  - The Rexarc check valve design was inherently prone to malfunction; the guide pin could become hung up on the pipe nipple, allowing the valve to leak or fail to seat.
  - The startup sequence left the generator pressurized before the recycled water system was re-engaged, creating the driving pressure for backflow.
  - No written procedures existed for starting up the recycled water system or for the consequences of incorrect sequencing.
  - The 1996 PHA did not identify the hazard of the drain valve location inside the shed; the PHA was overdue for its 2001 update.
  - The lime shed was not designed or built for acetylene service: unventilated, equipped with general-purpose electrical gear, and heated by a surface-ignition propane heater.
  - Operator training was not adequately documented; different workers operated the process inconsistently.
- Root causes (management-system level):
  - Process hazard analysis did not identify the drain valve in the shed as a potential hazard and was not updated on schedule (2001 update was not performed).
  - No written, step-by-step startup procedures for the recycled water system; operators had no guidance on sequence or consequences of deviation.
  - Check valve design selection and maintenance: valve was susceptible to fouling by lime particles and was not on a testing or inspection schedule; sole reliance on a single check valve for backflow prevention of an explosive gas.
  - Facility design placed acetylene-connected drain piping inside an unventilated enclosure with ignition sources, violating NEC and NFPA 51A requirements.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing:
  - Check valve failed to prevent backflow (design deficiency, lime fouling, guide pin misalignment).
  - No secondary positive isolation (double-block-and-bleed) on the recycled water line to prevent backflow.
  - Drain valve routinely left open as freeze protection created an uncontrolled pathway into the shed.
  - Lime shed was not built or equipped to NFPA 51A or NEC Class I, Division 2, Group A requirements for acetylene service.
  - No mechanical ventilation in the shed to prevent accumulation of leaked acetylene.
  - PHA outdated and did not identify this hazard pathway.
- Key findings:
  - Acetylene is uniquely hazardous among flammable gases: it is the only material in NEC Group A, capable of detonation over a very wide concentration range.
  - The combination of the open drain valve, the failing check valve, and the unventilated shed created a latent scenario that workers and management were unaware of.
  - Post-incident testing confirmed acetylene could flow through the water recycle line into the shed; the check valve leaked significantly even in the closed position.
  - OSHA's acetylene standard (1910.102) was outdated, referencing unavailable CGA pamphlets rather than current NFPA 51A.
- Recommendations (recipient to action):
  - ASCO to improve isolation on acetylene generator water lines with double-block-and-bleed and vent to a safe location.
  - Rexarc Inc. to inform existing users of the check valve backflow failure and provide interim actions; recommend replacing with more reliable valve designs.
  - Rexarc Inc. to replace check valves of this design with valves reliable in recycle water service.
  - OSHA to update the acetylene standard (1910.102) to replace references to obsolete CGA pamphlets with current NFPA 51A.
  - All acetylene facilities to implement PSM per OSHA 1910.119 including written procedures, training, PHA, and documentation.
- PSM elements implicated: process hazard analysis, operating procedures, training, mechanical integrity (check valve inspection and testing), facility design and layout, management systems (PSM compliance), RAGAGEP (NEC and NFPA 51A compliance)
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A freeze-protection drain valve was left open, gas leaked backward into a shed, and three workers were killed in the explosion.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A small facility operating a genuinely hazardous process (acetylene generation) with no written startup procedures, an outdated PHA that missed the drain-valve pathway, a check valve that had a known history of leaking and was not on an inspection schedule, and a shed that was not designed to NFPA 51A requirements, creating a latent pathway that workers did not know existed.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable, plant-agnostic):
  - Identify every drain, vent, or low-point connection on piping that contains or could contain flammable or toxic gases, and assess whether leaving that valve open (for freeze protection, draining, or any other routine purpose) could create a pathway to an occupied space or ignition source; require physical closure or positive isolation for any such valve.
  - Do not rely on a single check valve as the only barrier against backflow of a flammable or toxic gas; require a positive isolation method (double block and bleed, blinds, or equivalent) in addition to the check valve for high-hazard services.
  - Ensure that any enclosure that drains, vents, or receives process lines connected to flammable gas service is ventilated and equipped with electrical equipment rated for the flammable gas classification (Group A for acetylene); a general-purpose space heater or industrial switch is not an acceptable ignition-source safeguard.
