### Allied Terminals Fertilizer Tank Collapse
- Archive id | Final report date: 3492 | May 2009 (Report No. 2009-03-I-VA)
- Company | Location | Industry: Allied Terminals, Inc. | Chesapeake, Virginia | Liquid bulk chemical storage / fertilizer terminal
- Incident date: November 12, 2008
- Hazard type: overpressure / mechanical failure
- Materials: Urea ammonium nitrate (UAN 32) liquid fertilizer, approximately 2.1 million gallons
- What happened: A 2-million-gallon riveted petroleum storage tank (Tank 201, originally built in 1929) had its vertical riveted joints replaced with butt-welds in 2006 to allow higher fill levels for the denser liquid fertilizer it now stored. On November 12, 2008, while workers were filling the tank to its calculated maximum level and welding leaking rivets on the exterior, a defective vertical weld seam failed midway up the shell. The tank split open nearly instantaneously, releasing all 2.1 million gallons. The collapsing shell injured two G&T Fabricators contractors working on the tank; liquid fertilizer overtopped secondary containment, flooded an adjacent residential neighborhood of 43 homes, and released an estimated 200,000 gallons into the Elizabeth River.
- Consequences: 2 workers seriously injured (welder and helper); community evacuation lasting several days; major environmental release into Elizabeth River; property damage to neighboring homes.
- Sequence: (1) 1929-era riveted steel tank purchased by Allied and repurposed for liquid fertilizer. (2) 2006: G&T Fabricators replaces vertical riveted joints with butt-welds to allow higher fill levels; no authorized inspector or qualified weld procedures used. (3) HMT calculates new "safe fill height" of 27.01 ft using average wall thickness and assuming radiographed welds, but no radiography is ever done on Tank 201. (4) November 11-12, 2008: Allied fills tank to test for leaks and paint exterior, directing contractors to seal weeping rivets while filling. (5) At 26.72 ft fill, a defective weld seam fails; tank collapses catastrophically.
- Immediate cause: Defective butt-welds on the vertical joint insert plates failed due to lack of penetration, porosity, and undercut, compromising joint strength far below design assumptions.
- Contributing factors: Inspection firm (HMT) used average rather than minimum wall thickness in calculating safe fill height, and assumed spot radiography had been performed; post-modification spot radiography was never done on Tank 201; no safe work policy existed prohibiting personnel from working on or near tanks during initial fill after major modification.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) Allied did not ensure welds met accepted quality standards for tank fabrication. (2) Allied did not perform post-welding radiographic inspection required for the calculated maximum liquid level. (3) Allied had no safety procedures for work around tanks being filled for the first time after major modification.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Post-modification radiographic weld inspection (never performed); authorized inspector review of modification (not done); safe work exclusion zone during first fill (no policy existed); secondary containment capacity was insufficient to hold entire tank contents when released rapidly.
- Key findings: (1) Allied unaware of Fertilizer Institute recommended inspection guidelines. (2) HMT's safe fill height calculation assumed radiographed welds that did not exist, and used average rather than minimum wall thickness. (3) Tank failed below its calculated safe fill height. (4) Eleven of 16 similar tank failures at other facilities between 1995 and 2008 also resulted from defective welds. (5) No federal or Virginia regulatory program specifically covered non-petroleum aboveground storage tanks.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): EPA: revise CEPPO Chemical Safety Alert on tank rupture hazards to include Allied case and first-fill risk. Virginia Governor/Legislature: enact state regulation of large fertilizer storage tanks or authorize local jurisdictions to do so. Allied: hire independent reviewer to verify all tank maximum liquid levels per API 653; develop worker safety procedures for initial fills. HMT Inspection: incorporate Fertilizer Institute guidelines into inspector training; verify radiography before using assumptions that require it. Fertilizer Institute: formally recommend members incorporate inspection guidelines into fertilizer storage contracts.
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical integrity; management of change; process hazard analysis; safe work practices; contractor safety management.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): An old tank was welded improperly and collapsed when they filled it up.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A cascade of paper compliance replaced real compliance. Allied hired contractors without qualifying their welders or weld procedures, skipped the radiography required by API 653 but allowed an inspection firm to calculate fill heights as if the radiography had been done, and had no policy preventing workers from being on the tank during what is statistically the highest-risk moment in a tank's life. Every safeguard that should have caught the weld defects was skipped or circumvented, and no regulator had authority to intervene because the stored product was fertilizer, not petroleum.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) Before returning a tank to service after modification, verify that every inspection step assumed in calculating the safe fill height (especially radiography) has actually been completed. (2) Establish a written exclusion zone policy: no workers inside secondary containment during initial or first-fill hydrostatic test after major modification. (3) When tank service changes from a lighter to a heavier fluid, treat the change-in-service as a full engineering review, not a simple fill-height recalculation.

### Texas Tech University Chemistry Laboratory Explosion
- Archive id | Final report date: 3500 | October 2011 (Case Study No. 2010-05-I-TX)
- Company | Location | Industry: Texas Tech University, Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry | Lubbock, Texas | Academic research / energetic materials
- Incident date: January 7, 2010
- Hazard type: reactive runaway
- Materials: Nickel hydrazine perchlorate (NHP), approximately 10 grams synthesized; hexane used as wetting agent
- What happened: A fifth-year chemistry graduate student and a first-year student synthesized an energetic compound (NHP) for explosives-detection research. Concerned about reproducibility between small batches, the senior student scaled up to approximately 10 grams without consulting his principal investigators (PIs) and without any formal hazard evaluation. He transferred about half the product into a mortar, used a pestle to break up clumps, removed his goggles, then returned and stirred the compound "one more time." The NHP detonated, causing the student to lose three fingers, injure one eye, and sustain burns to his hands and face.
- Consequences: 1 graduate student seriously injured (three fingers amputated, eye perforated, burns to hands and face); research group issued a 4-month stop-work order; broader stop-work at all ALERT partner universities for up to 10 months.
- Sequence: (1) October 2008: Texas Tech subcontracts with Northeastern University for DHS-funded ALERT program on explosives detection. (2) Student learns NHP synthesis with no formal energetic-materials training. (3) Safety limits (100 mg) communicated verbally and inconsistently; students believe limit is 200-300 mg. (4) December 2009: students decide to scale up to ~10 g for analytical batch. (5) No PI consultation; no hazard evaluation for scale-up. (6) Student observes clumps, transfers ~5 g to mortar, uses pestle, removes goggles, returns to stir. (7) Detonation occurs.
- Immediate cause: Mechanical impact from pestling approximately 5 grams of shock-sensitive NHP after removing personal protective equipment.
- Contributing factors: No written safety limits or protocols for energetic materials synthesis; no management-of-change requirement for scale-up decisions; PPE use left to individual discretion; two prior near-miss incidents in the same lab not formally communicated; grant funding agency (DHS) prescribed no safety provisions specific to energetic materials research.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) Physical hazards of energetic materials research were not effectively assessed or controlled at Texas Tech. (2) The university's Chemical Hygiene Plan followed OSHA's Laboratory Standard, which addresses health hazards from chemical exposure but not physical hazards such as detonation. (3) No comprehensive hazard evaluation guidance existed for academic research laboratories working with energetics. (4) Previous incidents and near-misses were not documented or formally communicated across the research group. (5) EH&S had no authority or organizational path to enforce safety improvements; it reported to finance, not to the Vice President for Research who had authority over faculty. (6) DHS missed an opportunity to condition research funding on safety requirements.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Written synthesis quantity limits (verbal only, inconsistently communicated); PI approval requirement for scale-up (none existed); required PPE during energetic compound handling (optional); formal hazard evaluation before changing synthesis scale (none); near-miss reporting system (none); organizational authority for EH&S to shut down unsafe labs (none).
- Key findings: (1) OSHA Laboratory Standard (29 CFR 1910.1450) does not address physical hazards of chemicals, only health/exposure hazards. (2) Texas Tech modeled its CHP on that standard and inherited the gap. (3) Two prior incidents in the same research groups involved similar scale-up and energetics hazards; neither was formally communicated. (4) DHS cooperative agreement included no safety provisions for energetic materials research. (5) More than 110,000 graduate students work in U.S. academic labs with no nationwide near-miss reporting system.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): OSHA: issue Safety and Health Information Bulletin explicitly communicating that the Laboratory Standard does not address physical hazards and that programs are needed to control them. American Chemical Society: develop good-practice guidance for hazard assessment methodologies in research laboratories. Texas Tech University: revise CHP to address physical hazards; develop incident/near-miss reporting system with single accountability; implement a verification program.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis; operating procedures; training; incident investigation; management of change; management systems / organizational oversight.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A student scaled up a dangerous experiment without goggles and it exploded.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): An entire regulatory and institutional framework focused on chemical health hazards simply had no language, requirements, or authority to address physical hazards like detonation. The lab followed OSHA's Laboratory Standard; that standard was designed for health exposures; nobody at any level, from the student to the PI to the department to EH&S to the federal funding agency, had a framework that required them to evaluate or control energetics risks. Two prior near-misses that could have triggered system changes were instead treated as isolated learning moments and never formally documented.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) If your laboratory safety program is modeled on OSHA 1910.1450, explicitly audit whether it covers physical hazards (shock sensitivity, detonation, reactive scale-up) as a separate category. (2) Establish a written management-of-change requirement for any scale-up of energetic or reactive synthesis, requiring PI sign-off before proceeding. (3) Create a near-miss reporting database accessible to the whole research group and treat documented near-misses as mandatory reading for anyone joining the lab.

### Universal Form Clamp Co. Explosion and Fire
- Archive id | Final report date: 3514 | April 2007 (Case Study No. 2006-08-I-IL)
- Company | Location | Industry: Universal Form Clamp, Inc. (UFC) | Bellwood, Illinois (suburb of Chicago) | Concrete product manufacturing / chemical mixing
- Incident date: June 14, 2006
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Heptane and mineral spirits mixture (approximately 6,000 lb heptane, 3,000 lb mineral spirits), "Super Clean and Tilt" concrete release compound; approximately 9,000 lb batch in 2,200-gallon open-top tank
- What happened: An operator was mixing and heating a flammable heptane-mineral spirits mixture in an open-top 2,200-gallon tank equipped with steam heating coils. A malfunctioning temperature controller allowed the mixture to overheat and boil, generating a large flammable vapor cloud that accumulated on the floor and spread to adjacent work areas. A contracted delivery driver entered the building while talking on a cell phone, did not hear warnings from evacuating employees, and walked into the vapor cloud as it ignited. The driver died several days later from burns. A secondary pressure blast injured a temporary employee nearby.
- Consequences: 1 killed (contract delivery driver, burns); 1 seriously injured (second-degree burns, hospitalized 3 days); 1 minor injury (evacuation fall); fire burned 3.5 hours in adjacent resin storage; operations suspended indefinitely for flammable mixing.
- Sequence: (1) 2002-2003: UFC adds chemical mixing area; hires chemist and engineers through temp agency. (2) Plans not stamped by registered design professional; local building permit review inadequate. (3) Mixing room built with broken local exhaust fans and no floor-level ventilation. (4) Temperature controller installed incorrectly (dry thermowell, kinked capillary tube). (5) June 14, 2006 morning: operator heats batch; temperature controller malfunctions; mixture boils. (6) Operator sees vapor "dense fog," shuts down, alerts nearby workers; most evacuate. (7) Delivery driver enters building, ignores warnings (on cell phone); vapor ignites within seconds of his entry. (8) Pressure wave injures adjacent employee.
- Immediate cause: Temperature controller malfunction allowed mixture to boil; combined with failed ventilation systems, produced a large flammable vapor cloud that found ignition sources in unclassified adjacent areas.
- Contributing factors: Both local exhaust fan belts were broken before the incident; area exhaust registers at ceiling level unable to remove heavy vapors accumulating on floor; no floor-level exhaust ventilation; facility not subject to Village of Bellwood's adequate code review; UFC did not implement PSM standard requirements (PHA, mechanical integrity, emergency action plan) despite triggering flammable quantity thresholds; no facility-wide emergency alarm; no emergency action plan or evacuation drills conducted.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) UFC designed and constructed a flammable liquid heating operation without adequate safety controls; facility managers did not follow regulatory requirements or good engineering practices. (2) Village of Bellwood did not require or verify compliance with adopted BOCA and NFPA 30 fire codes, and did not require a registered design professional to stamp plans. (3) UFC did not implement PSM-required elements (PHA, mechanical integrity program, emergency action plan) for a process that met PSM thresholds.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Functioning local exhaust ventilation (broken); floor-level exhaust registers (not installed); temperature alarm or backup steam shutoff (not installed); correct temperature controller installation (improperly installed); facility-wide emergency alarm (absent); emergency action plan and evacuation drills (absent); building permit compliance review (inadequate).
- Key findings: (1) Mixture was a PSM-covered quantity but UFC had no PSM program. (2) Temperature controller was installed and maintained out of compliance with manufacturer specs. (3) Mixing room design violated NFPA 30 and OSHA 1910.106 requirements for ventilation and hazardous area classification. (4) OSHA 1910.106 (Flammable and Combustible Liquids) did not require emergency action plans, creating a regulatory gap. (5) The driver's death may have been preventable with a facility-wide alarm system.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): OSHA: amend 1910.106 to require Emergency Action Plans for facilities handling flammable/combustible liquids. OSHA: amend 1910.38 to require annual evacuation drills.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis; mechanical integrity; emergency planning and response; operating procedures; management of change; safe work practices.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A delivery driver walked into a vapor cloud that workers hadn't cleared because they had no alarm system.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The mixing operation was built without required ventilation, without correct instrumentation, and without a process hazard analysis, all because neither UFC's management nor the local building department enforced applicable codes. The regulatory gap (OSHA flammable liquids standard did not require emergency action plans) meant that even if OSHA had inspected, the most critical missing safeguard would not have been cited. The driver's death was the end of a chain that started with engineers designing a flammable process without floor-level ventilation.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) For any process that heats or mixes flammable liquids, verify that local exhaust ventilation includes floor-level sweeps to capture vapors heavier than air. (2) Confirm that temperature control instruments for heated flammable processes are installed, calibrated, and maintained to manufacturer specs, with a backup high-temperature alarm independent of the primary controller. (3) Require a facility-wide alarm and tested evacuation plan before allowing contractor or delivery traffic near a building with open flammable liquid processes.

### Hayes Lemmerz Dust Explosions and Fire
- Archive id | Final report date: 3528 | September 2005 (Report No. 2004-01-I-IN)
- Company | Location | Industry: Hayes Lemmerz International-Huntington, Inc. | Huntington, Indiana | Aluminum alloy wheel casting / metal manufacturing
- Incident date: October 29, 2003
- Hazard type: combustible dust
- Materials: Fine aluminum dust generated during chip drying and scrap remelting; aluminum alloy wheel scrap chips
- What happened: Hayes operated a scrap remelting system that recycled aluminum wheel chips. The chip drying process generated fine aluminum dust that was collected by an outdoor dust collector. At approximately 8:30 pm on October 29, 2003, a fume hood duct fire inside the building preceded a restart of the chip feed system. Shortly after restart, a fireball erupted at furnace 5. The explosion completely destroyed the outdoor dust collection equipment, lifted a portion of the building roof, and ignited a fire in insulation and combustible materials that burned for several hours. One employee engulfed in the fireball died within hours; two others were burned, one critically.
- Consequences: 1 killed; 2 seriously burned (1 critical); 4 minor injuries (3 Hayes employees, 1 contractor); dust collector destroyed; building roof damaged; fire burned several hours.
- Sequence: (1) Hayes installs scrap remelting system supplied by Premelt Systems; chip dryer generates fine aluminum dust. (2) Excess dust accumulates; no formal investigation or root-cause review conducted when dust problems are identified. (3) Dust collector designed and installed without following NFPA 651 explosion protection guidance (no explosion vents or suppression). (4) October 29 evening: fume hood duct fire occurs; system briefly shut down. (5) Chip feed restart ignites accumulated aluminum dust in the dust collector; explosion propagates back to building, fireball at furnace 5. (6) Explosion lifts roof section; fire burns for hours.
- Immediate cause: Ignition of accumulated fine aluminum dust in an inadequately protected dust collector, with the explosion propagating into the building.
- Contributing factors: Employees did not wear flame-retardant clothing during routine work near the melt furnace; housekeeping and maintenance in the chip-processing and dust collector areas were inadequate; no formal training for facility employees on operating and maintaining chip-processing and dust collection systems; management of change procedures (which existed for product quality) were never applied to the chip-processing system.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) Hayes did not conduct a review to determine why the chip system was releasing excess dust; the hazards of aluminum dust were neither identified nor addressed. (2) Hayes did not ensure proper design of the dust collector system. (3) Hayes did not ensure that the dust collector design and installation followed NFPA 651 guidance (explosion vents, suppression). (4) Hayes had no formal documented program to investigate and implement corrective action for fires in the foundry area, especially aluminum dust fires.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Dust collector explosion venting and/or suppression (not installed, NFPA 651 not followed); management-of-change review for chip handling system (quality MOC existed but was not applied to safety-critical support systems); incident investigation program for dust fires (absent); employee training on aluminum dust hazards (absent); flame-retardant clothing PPE program (absent); routine housekeeping and maintenance program for dust collection areas (inadequate).
- Key findings: (1) Aluminum dust is an explosive hazard that Hayes management did not recognize or address. (2) The dust collector was a known problem area, but no formal root-cause review was ever triggered. (3) MOC procedures existed but were scoped only to product quality, not to safety-critical support systems like scrap processing. (4) Premelt Systems, the system supplier, did not communicate the explosive dust hazard to customers.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): Hayes: develop aluminum chip handling that minimizes dust; regular training on aluminum dust fire/explosion hazards; written operating procedures for chip processing; flame-retardant PPE program; formal incident investigation program. Premelt Systems: communicate findings to all customers with similar chip-melting systems, specifically noting that chip drying produces explosive dust. Aluminum Association and Fire Protection Research Foundation: jointly research improved explosion protection for dust collectors in aluminum dust service.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis; mechanical integrity; management of change; incident investigation; training; operating procedures; management systems.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A dusty dust collector exploded because nobody knew aluminum dust was explosive.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The chip drying process was generating explosive dust continuously, but because the scrap remelting system was treated as a support function rather than a hazardous process, it was excluded from every management system that existed at the facility. The quality management system (MOC, audits) explicitly applied only to systems affecting wheel quality. The incident investigation program existed but was never triggered for aluminum dust fires. The equipment vendor never disclosed the explosive dust hazard. Every layer that should have caught the risk either was not applied or did not exist for this class of equipment.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) Map all processes that generate combustible dust, including secondary processes like chip drying, scrap handling, and conveying; confirm each has explosion protection designed to NFPA 651 or equivalent. (2) Extend your MOC process to all utility and support systems that contact combustible materials, not just production equipment. (3) Investigate every dust fire and near-miss as a potential precursor to a dust explosion, not as a routine maintenance event.

### Honeywell Chemical Incidents (Three Incidents)
- Archive id | Final report date: 3529 | August 2005 (Report No. 2003-13-I-LA)
- Company | Location | Industry: Honeywell International, Inc. | Baton Rouge, Louisiana | Specialty chemicals / refrigerant and chemical manufacturing
- Incident date: July 20, 2003 (chlorine release); July 29, 2003 (antimony pentachloride fatality); August 13, 2003 (hydrogen fluoride release)
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Chlorine gas; contaminated antimony pentachloride (mislabeled as R-22 refrigerant); hydrogen fluoride
- What happened: Three distinct incidents at the same Honeywell Baton Rouge plant occurred within 25 days. (1) July 20: chlorine cooler tubes in the G-143a process failed, releasing chlorine gas; seven workers were injured and a shelter-in-place advisory was issued for a 0.5-mile radius. (2) July 29: a mislabeled one-ton cylinder shipped from another Honeywell facility arrived labeled as R-22 refrigerant but actually containing antimony pentachloride; when a worker attempted to vent the cylinder, it released its contents and fatally exposed the worker. (3) August 13: during post-incident maintenance cleanup, workers attempted to drain liquid hydrogen fluoride from a vaporizer using a venturi stick, causing a release that injured one worker.
- Consequences: Incident 1 (Cl2): 7 workers injured, community shelter-in-place. Incident 2 (SbCl5): 1 worker killed. Incident 3 (HF): 1 worker hospitalized, 1 exposed. Overall: 1 killed, 8 injured, community impact.
- Sequence: Incident 1: Chlorine cooler tubes fail; chlorine enters coolant system; escapes to atmosphere; 31-minute delay before community shelter-in-place alert. PHA had not identified tube failure as a credible scenario. Incident 2: Cylinder mislabeled at El Segundo; shipped to Baton Rouge as R-22; received without weighing (gross weight discrepancy not caught); placed in empty cylinder area; worker attempts to vent full cylinder; fatal exposure. Incident 3: Vaporizer left with liquid HF during emergency G-143a shutdown; worker uses venturi stick to drain it; nonroutine task lacks hazard analysis; HF release injures worker.
- Immediate cause: (1) Chlorine cooler tube failure from corrosion with materials incompatible with chlorine. (2) Fatally mislabeled hazardous chemical cylinder accepted without verification of contents. (3) Nonroutine HF draining task performed without hazard analysis or proper procedures.
- Contributing factors: Hazard analyses were generic and did not cover all credible failure scenarios; nonroutine situations were not routinely triggered for hazard review before work proceeded; workers did not consistently follow written operating procedures; no systematic verification of received cylinder contents; community notification system had a 31-minute activation delay.
- Root causes (management-system level): Incident 1: PHA did not identify chlorine leaking into coolant system as a hazard; MOC review of chlorine feed system expansion did not integrate railcar shutdown with G-143a shutdown procedures. Incident 2: No program to positively identify and verify cylinder contents when rejected by receiving facility; no hazard analysis for the ton-cylinder area. Incident 3: No nonroutine job planning requirement that triggered a hazard analysis for the HF draining task; procedures did not address abnormal-state vaporizer draining.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Adequate PHA coverage of all credible failure scenarios (generic PHAs missed key scenarios); positive verification of cylinder contents on receipt (not done); weigh-in check to catch weight discrepancies on received cylinders (not done); hazard analysis for nonroutine jobs (not required); integrated shutdown procedures linking chlorine railcar to cooler shutdown (not in place); timely community shelter-in-place notification (31-minute delay).
- Key findings: (1) All three incidents shared common deficiencies: incomplete hazard analyses, inadequate nonroutine work controls, and inconsistent procedure adherence. (2) No guidance or industry standard existed for design and maintenance of positive-pressure control room systems. (3) The 31-minute delay in community shelter-in-place activation exposed off-site residents to unnecessary risk. (4) Cylinder weight check would have caught the mislabeled antimony pentachloride shipment at receipt. (5) Industry best practices for HF draining operations were not shared or captured in site-specific procedures.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): Honeywell: revise PHAs to identify credible hazard scenarios for all covered processes; implement weigh-in verification for received cylinders; require hazard analysis before nonroutine tasks. American Society of Heating, Refrigeration, and Air Conditioning Engineers: develop guidance for positive-pressure control room design. East Baton Rouge Parish OHSEP and Baton Rouge Fire Department: reduce shelter-in-place activation time. Hydrogen Fluoride Industry Practices Institute: develop and share best practices for HF draining and maintenance operations.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis; operating procedures; management of change; mechanical integrity; nonroutine work planning; emergency response; employee training.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Three accidents in a month at the same plant, each involving a different toxic chemical.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The three incidents were separate chains of failure but shared a common thread: hazard analyses that were too generic to identify specific failure modes, and no organizational trigger requiring a hazard review before nonroutine work. The chlorine incident followed an incomplete PHA; the antimony fatality followed a complete absence of a hazard analysis for the cylinder area combined with no receipt verification; the HF incident followed a nonroutine draining task performed with no hazard review. The plant's management systems were structured for routine operations and had gaps everywhere work deviated from the routine.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) Audit your PHA process: are hazard analyses specific enough to identify scenarios like "what happens if a corrosive leaks into a non-corrosion-resistant coolant system?" or are they generic? (2) Establish a nonroutine work trigger: any task not covered by an existing SOP should require a documented mini-hazard review before work begins. (3) Verify received chemical cylinders and vessels by weight and visual inspection; flag discrepancies before connecting to any process.

### First Chemical Corp. Reactive Chemical Explosion
- Archive id | Final report date: 3537 | October 2003 (Report No. 2003-01-I-MS)
- Company | Location | Industry: First Chemical Corporation (FCC) | Pascagoula, Mississippi | Specialty chemical manufacturing / mononitrotoluene (MNT) production
- Incident date: October 13, 2002
- Hazard type: reactive runaway
- Materials: Mononitrotoluene (MNT), approximately 1,200 gallons; para-MNT in adjacent storage tank; anhydrous ammonia and other hazardous materials in nearby tanks
- What happened: First Chemical Corporation operated a 145-foot MNT distillation column that was shut down in early September 2002 with approximately 1,200 gallons of MNT left inside. The column was thought to be fully isolated from steam, but aging manual steam valves were leaking, allowing steam to slowly heat the MNT. Over five weeks, the hot MNT began to decompose; operators did not monitor the column's internal temperature. On October 12, a high liquid-level alarm activated but no action was taken. At 5:25 am on October 13, the column exploded violently. The upper 35 feet of the column was blown off; fragments were hurled up to a mile away. A 6-ton section landed near a crude oil storage tank at an adjacent refinery. Flying debris punctured an MNT storage tank, igniting a fire that burned for nearly 3 hours. Three employees in the nearby control room were injured by broken glass.
- Consequences: 3 workers injured (minor, from broken glass); significant potential for catastrophic offsite consequences (fragments missed ammonia, chlorine, and crude oil tanks by narrow margins); MNT storage tank fire; community shelter-in-place ordered; potential for mass casualty event averted by chance.
- Sequence: (1) September 2002: MNT distillation process shut down; 1,200 gallons MNT left in column; steam supply valve closed. (2) October 5: steam system restarted; aging steam valves leaking; MNT begins to heat again. (3) Temperatures inside column climb above 400°F for days; no monitoring in place for shutdown column. (4) October 12: high liquid-level alarm activates; no action taken. (5) October 13, 5:00 am: workers hear rumbling; take shelter in control room 50 feet from column base. (6) 5:25 am: column explodes, blowing off top 35 feet; debris field extends one mile.
- Immediate cause: Steam leaked through leaking manual valves into the supposedly isolated MNT distillation column; MNT was heated above its thermal decomposition temperature and underwent runaway thermal decomposition leading to explosion.
- Contributing factors: 1996 hazard analysis of a separate batch MNT process at the same facility identified thermal decomposition risk and established temperature limits with interlocks, but results were never applied to the continuous process column; facility lacked a system to share lessons learned across similar processes; control room sited 50 feet from the base of a reactive chemical distillation column without hazard review; community notification system was inadequate.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) FCC had no adequate management system for evaluating the hazards of processing MNT; lessons learned from hazard analyses of similar on-site processes were not applied. (2) No system to ensure MNT column had sufficient layers of protection (alarms, safety interlocks, overpressure protection). (3) System for ensuring consistent work practices when isolating equipment was ineffective (leaking steam valves were not identified as a credible failure mode). (4) Mechanical integrity program for steam isolation valves was inadequate.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Temperature monitoring of shutdown MNT column (none); interlock or alarm on MNT column temperature during shutdown (none, even though these existed on similar batch process from 1996 analysis); effective steam isolation (leaking aging valves); high liquid-level alarm response (alarm activated but ignored); layers of protection against runaway (none); protective control room siting (control room 50 feet from base of reactive chemical column); effective community notification (inadequate).
- Key findings: (1) A 1996 PHA of a similar on-site batch MNT process identified the thermal decomposition risk and resulted in temperature limits and interlocks; this knowledge was never transferred to the continuous process column. (2) A Responsible Care self-audit in 2001 asserted full compliance; CSB found the actual situation substantially different. (3) Debris from the explosion could have caused catastrophic releases from adjacent tanks containing ammonia, chlorine, sulfuric acid, and crude oil. (4) MNT temperatures had exceeded 400°F for days before the explosion, but no monitoring existed for the shutdown column.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): DuPont (which acquired facility post-incident): audit facility to address all identified issues; communicate findings to workforce. FCC Pascagoula: establish reactive hazard PHA program; evaluate and install layers of protection including alarms and interlocks; revise isolation procedures and train workers; survey pressure vessels for overpressure protection; improve facility siting for control rooms. Jackson County Emergency Management: update community notification system for immediate, clear, and all-clear alerts. American Chemistry Council / SOCMA: amend Responsible Care guidance to explicitly require identifying lessons learned from hazard analyses and applying them across similar processes.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis; mechanical integrity; operating procedures; layers of protection; incident investigation; facility siting; emergency response; lessons-learned transfer across processes.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A chemical tower that was supposed to be shut down exploded because someone didn't know it was still being heated.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The plant had already done the hard work in 1996: a hazard analysis of a batch MNT process correctly identified thermal decomposition risk and installed temperature limits and interlocks. But that knowledge lived in one corner of the facility and was never applied to the larger continuous-process column running the same chemistry. The explosion was not caused by ignorance of MNT's reactive hazards, it was caused by a management system failure to share and apply lessons learned from hazard analyses across similar processes. A 2001 Responsible Care self-audit asserted full compliance with process safety code; the actual state of the facility told a different story.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) After completing a PHA on any reactive chemical process, require a formal review of whether lessons learned apply to other processes at the facility that involve the same or similar chemicals. (2) Establish a written shutdown checklist for any column or vessel containing reactive materials: confirm isolation, verify no leakage paths from energy sources, begin periodic temperature monitoring, and do not abandon the shutdown without sign-off on each step. (3) Review your community notification procedures to confirm you can issue a shelter-in-place advisory within minutes of incident detection, not hours.

### Environmental Enterprises Hydrogen Sulfide Release
- Archive id | Final report date: 3539 | September 2003 (Case Study No. 2003-02-C-OH; note: this is a CSB Case Study, not a full Investigation Report)
- Company | Location | Industry: Environmental Enterprises, Inc. (EEI) | Cincinnati, Ohio | Hazardous waste treatment, storage, and disposal
- Incident date: December 11, 2002
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Hydrogen sulfide (H2S), generated by reaction of sodium sulfide (Na2S) flake with polyaluminum chloride (PAC); approximately 2 pounds released
- What happened: An experienced wastewater treatment (WWT) operator at EEI's hazardous waste facility was treating water-based waste containing mercury in the facility's WWT clarifier, an open-top settling vessel not designed for chemical treatment. To remove mercury, he added 50 pounds of sodium sulfide (Na2S) flake to the clarifier, then added three 55-gallon drums of polyaluminum chloride (PAC) to adjust pH and flocculate the mercury salts. He used a temporary air hose for mixing, which provided insufficient agitation. The strongly acidic PAC reacted locally with undissolved Na2S flake at the clarifier bottom, generating H2S. When a maintenance mechanic entered the room to retrieve a tool, he was overcome by H2S and collapsed unconscious. A supervisor and other employees pulled him to safety; he recovered.
- Consequences: 1 worker seriously injured (H2S asphyxiation, hospitalized, recovered); no fatalities; approximately 2 pounds H2S released.
- Sequence: (1) December 10: WWT operator receives mercury-contaminated waste water; tests show treatment needed. (2) December 11: operator adds Na2S flake to clarifier to precipitate mercury. (3) Uses air hose for mixing (clarifier has no agitator); insufficient mixing leaves undissolved Na2S flake at bottom. (4) Adds PAC (acidic, pH 2-3) to flocculate and adjust pH. (5) PAC contacts undissolved Na2S; H2S evolves from open-top clarifier. (6) Compliance coordinator notices H2S odor; goes to get gas detector. (7) Mechanic enters room to retrieve a tool; overcomes H2S, collapses. (8) Compliance coordinator returns, finds mechanic unconscious; other employees drag mechanic to safety.
- Immediate cause: Chemical treatment was conducted in the clarifier, a vessel not designed for chemical addition, lacking the mixing, enclosure, and scrubbing needed for safe treatment; insufficient mixing caused localized H2S generation.
- Contributing factors: No written operating procedures for WWT area; operator was unaware that reacting Na2S with PAC could produce H2S; hazard communication inadequate (staff not trained on H2S dangers from WWT process); fixed H2S detector was not functioning (sensor not calibrated for 2-3 months, had a failed sensor); no management system to investigate and communicate lessons from previous incidents; no warning signs or controlled access to WWT area during treatment operations; H2S detector not maintained per any formal mechanical integrity program; a July 2001 city enforcement order requiring Na2S to be dissolved before addition was never communicated to WWT personnel.
- Root causes (management-system level): (CSB Case Study; contributing factors only, no formal root causes stated) Inadequate process safety management practices: no written procedures; inadequate training; inadequate hazard communication; deficient incident investigation; absent mechanical integrity program for safety-critical detection equipment; insufficient management oversight to ensure safe treatment methods were used.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: H2S gas detector (installed but failed due to uncalibrated sensor); written WWT operating procedures (none existed); warning signs at WWT room entry (none); access control during chemical treatment (none); adequate mixing for treatment chemistry (no agitator in clarifier); scrubber on open-top clarifier (none); training on H2S reaction hazards for WWT operators (inadequate); management oversight of treatment protocol decisions (absent).
- Key findings: (1) Treatment was done in the wrong vessel: the clarifier was designed to settle solids, not to mix reactive chemicals. (2) The H2S detector was installed but had a failed, uncalibrated sensor; it did not alarm on the day of the incident. (3) A prior incident and a July 2001 city enforcement order regarding the same chemical hazard (Na2S + acid producing H2S) were never communicated to WWT personnel. (4) The operator was experienced but had no formal chemistry training and no knowledge of the Na2S-acid reaction producing H2S. (5) Employees throughout the facility were accustomed to H2S odors and did not recognize them as immediately dangerous.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): CSB Case Studies do not make formal recommendations, but the report outlines management system elements EEI should implement: written WWT operating procedures; formal training on H2S hazards and reaction chemistry; incident investigation and communication program; mechanical integrity program for detectors with calibration records; management oversight guidelines; access control during hazardous treatment operations. EEI subsequently implemented all of these voluntarily.
- PSM elements implicated: Operating procedures; training; hazard communication; mechanical integrity; incident investigation and lessons learned; management oversight.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A worker was overcome by toxic gas from a chemical reaction nobody knew was happening.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): EEI had a skilled, experienced workforce, but experience is not a substitute for documented procedures and hazard knowledge. The operator knew how to do waste treatment; he did not know that combining Na2S with an acidic reagent in an unventilated open vessel would produce lethal gas. A city enforcement order from 2001 described exactly this risk and required specific steps to prevent it, but the order was never communicated to the people doing the work. The fixed H2S detector that should have provided a safety net had a failed sensor and no calibration program to catch the failure. Every link in the hazard chain from process to detection to communication was broken.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) Maintain a master list of all regulatory orders, enforcement actions, and compliance agreements at your facility; verify each one has a documented procedure update, training record, and ongoing compliance check. (2) Require formal hazard compatibility review before any new chemical combination is introduced into a treatment vessel; do not rely on operator experience to know what reactions are possible. (3) Implement a calibration and functional test schedule for all toxic gas detectors with written records; treat a failed calibration as a process deviation requiring investigation, not a routine maintenance task.

### Georgia-Pacific Corp. Hydrogen Sulfide Poisoning
- Archive id | Final report date: 3541 | January 2003 (Report No. 2002-01-I-AL)
- Company | Location | Industry: Georgia-Pacific Corporation (Fort James Operating Company subsidiary) | Pennington, Alabama | Pulp and paper manufacturing / kraft process
- Incident date: January 16, 2002
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Hydrogen sulfide (H2S), generated by reaction of sodium hydrosulfide (NaSH) with sulfuric acid in a process sewer
- What happened: The Naheola mill unloaded 15 tank trucks of sodium hydrosulfide (NaSH) over the 24 hours preceding the incident. A construction crew from Burkes Construction was working near the NaSH truck unloading station. To allow the crew to work in the adjacent oil pit, a mill operator drained the pit contents (a mixture of water and accumulated NaSH from unloading spills) into a process sewer that was simultaneously receiving sulfuric acid for pH control of mill effluent. NaSH and acid reacted in the sewer to generate H2S gas, which vented through a leaking fiberglass manhole cover directly next to the construction crew. Ten people were exposed. Three workers were immediately overcome and fell. Other workers attempted rescue without protective equipment, exposing themselves. Two Burkes employees died of H2S poisoning; eight others were injured, including some paramedics who transported victims.
- Consequences: 2 killed (Burkes Construction employees); 8 injured (7 Burkes employees, 1 Davison Transport driver); 6 county paramedics reported H2S exposure symptoms.
- Sequence: (1) 1995: Plant engineers connect the oil pit drain directly to the acid sewer without hazard review or MOC. (2) January 15-16: 15 NaSH trucks unload; NaSH accumulates in oil pit (approximately 5 gal/truck offload spill). (3) January 16: Burkes crew needs to work in oil pit; operator opens drain valve for 3-5 minutes to empty pit. (4) NaSH from pit enters acid sewer; immediate reaction produces H2S. (5) H2S vents through leaking fiberglass manhole cover near workers. (6) Workers overcome; rescue attempts without PPE cause additional exposures. (7) Two fatalities; emergency response further contaminated by off-gassing victims.
- Immediate cause: NaSH drained from the oil pit contacted sulfuric acid in the adjacent process sewer, producing H2S that vented through an inadequately sealed fiberglass manhole cover next to the construction workers.
- Contributing factors: (1) Fiberglass manhole cover was not adequately designed or sealed to maintain an airtight seal; prior leaks of chlorine dioxide from the same cover had been repaired but never investigated as safety incidents. (2) Construction workers had inadequate training on H2S hazards; they did not evacuate or put on protective equipment before attempting rescue.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) Good engineering and process safety practices were not followed in 1995 when the oil pit drain was connected to the acid sewer: no identification of chemicals that could enter the sewer, no hazard review, no MOC analysis, no identification of H2S generation scenarios, no warning devices installed. (2) No management system existed to incorporate hazard warnings about mixing NaSH with acid into process safety information: design information, operating procedures, and training for NaSH unloading did not identify the H2S generation hazard.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Hazard analysis of sewer connections (never done in 1995); management-of-change review when sewer systems were connected (not conducted); warning labels or procedures for NaSH unloading identifying acid-sewer incompatibility (absent); H2S gas monitors in the unloading area (not installed because the area was not identified as an H2S risk); proper manway seal (leaking and inadequately repaired); near-miss investigation for prior chlorine dioxide leaks from the same manhole (never done); H2S safety training for contractors (inadequate).
- Key findings: (1) The 1995 connection of the oil pit drain to the acid sewer was made without any hazard review or MOC. (2) The plant did not identify the NaSH unloading area as an H2S risk zone and installed no gas detection. (3) Prior leaks of chlorine dioxide from the same fiberglass manhole cover were repaired but never reported or investigated as incidents. (4) NaSH material safety data sheets and operating procedures did not warn of H2S generation when mixed with acid. (5) Paramedics suffered exposure from off-gassing victims, indicating inadequate communication of patient contamination hazard to emergency responders.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): Georgia-Pacific: conduct periodic safety audits of all mills to ensure management systems evaluate process sewers where chemicals may interact; install H2S monitoring at NaSH unloading areas; ensure incident investigation includes near-misses. Pulp & Paper Safety Association: communicate findings and recommendations to membership. (Specific recommendations also made regarding H2S safety training for emergency responders and contractor hazard communication.)
- PSM elements implicated: Management of change; process hazard analysis; process safety information; incident investigation; training; contractor safety management; mechanical integrity (sewer system design); hazard communication.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): Workers were overcome by toxic gas from a drain that nobody knew was dangerous.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): In 1995, an engineer connected two sewer systems that should never have been connected. The decision was made without any analysis of what chemicals could be in the oil pit or what would happen if those chemicals met the acidic contents of the acid sewer. That single unremedied engineering decision, made seven years before the incident, created a hidden hazard. Operating procedures and training for NaSH unloading were written in a world where the oil pit drain simply didn't lead anywhere dangerous. The prior leaks from the same manhole cover, which could have triggered a broader review, were treated as a sealing problem, not a safety signal.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) Audit your site's sewer and drain systems: map where every drain goes, what chemicals could reach it, and what reactions could occur if those chemicals mix in a confined space. (2) Before opening any sewer system or drain valve in an industrial area, identify what chemicals are currently in the upstream line and downstream sewer; require a brief hazard check before draining. (3) Review near-miss records for any incident involving gas leaks from sewer access points; treat repeated leaks from the same point as a system design failure requiring engineering, not just a recurring repair.

### Motiva Enterprises Sulfuric Acid Tank Explosion
- Archive id | Final report date: 3542 | October 2002 (Report No. 2001-05-I-DE)
- Company | Location | Industry: Motiva Enterprises LLC | Delaware City Refinery, Delaware City, Delaware | Petroleum refining / sulfuric acid alkylation process
- Incident date: July 17, 2001
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Spent sulfuric acid (H2SO4) containing hydrocarbon residues from alkylation; flammable light hydrocarbons vaporizing above liquid surface in Tank 393; approximately 415,000-gallon carbon steel tank
- What happened: Tank 393 was one of six 415,000-gallon sulfuric acid storage tanks at Motiva's Delaware City Refinery. The tank had been converted from fresh to spent acid service without an adequate management-of-change review. Spent acid contains hydrocarbon residues that vaporize and create a flammable atmosphere in the tank headspace. A carbon dioxide inerting system was installed but was poorly designed and provided insufficient CO2 flow to maintain an inert atmosphere. The tank had experienced repeated localized corrosion and shell leaks since 1998; tank inspectors repeatedly recommended an internal inspection, which Motiva repeatedly deferred from its 1996 scheduled date. By July 2001, the tank had additional unreported holes in the shell and roof. A Washington Group International (WGI) crew was welding grating on a catwalk above the tank farm. Sparks from the hot work entered the vapor space of Tank 393 through the holes, igniting the flammable atmosphere. The tank separated from its floor, instantaneously releasing its contents. Other tanks in the dike also released, creating a major acid spill. The Delaware River was reached, causing significant aquatic damage. Jeffrey Davis, a boilermaker working above the tank, was killed.
- Consequences: 1 killed (Jeffrey Davis, Washington Group International); 8 injured; significant offsite environmental damage: spent sulfuric acid reached the Delaware River, damaging aquatic life; major property damage.
- Sequence: (1) 1979: Tanks built for fresh H2SO4. (2) 1996 and later: Motiva converts tanks to spent acid service without formal MOC; CO2 inerting system improperly designed. (3) 1996: Internal inspection of Tank 393 scheduled; deferred due to inventory constraints. (4) 1998-2001: Repeated shell leaks on Tank 393; inspectors repeatedly recommend internal inspection; management defers each time. (5) May 2001: Shell leak discovered but not repaired; tank level lowered below leak point and left in service. (6) June 27, 2001: Unsafe Condition Report filed identifying tank hazards; not resolved before incident. (7) July 2001: Motiva authorizes hot work on catwalk above tank farm knowing tanks have holes; no continuous monitoring required. (8) July 17: Sparks from welding ignite flammable vapor in Tank 393 through shell holes; catastrophic explosion.
- Immediate cause: Hot work sparks ignited flammable hydrocarbon vapors inside Tank 393 through known holes in the tank shell and roof; the CO2 inerting system did not maintain an inert atmosphere.
- Contributing factors: (1) Motiva's system for investigating Unsafe Condition Reports and tracking resolution was inadequate; the June 27 report was not resolved before the incident. (2) Motiva Enterprises LLC corporate management oversight failed to detect and hold the refinery accountable for deficiencies in mechanical integrity, engineering management, and MOC programs.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) Motiva had no adequate mechanical integrity management system: tank inspector recommendations to pull Tank 393 for inspection were repeatedly overridden by management citing inventory constraints; a May 2001 leak was managed by lowering tank level rather than repair; holes in the shell and roof were unreported. (2) Engineering management and MOC systems were inadequate for the fresh-to-spent acid conversion: CO2 inerting was designed without engineering calculations and provided insufficient flow; no PHA, process hazard analysis, or pre-startup safety review was done for the conversion. (3) Hot work program was inadequate: Motiva authorized and permitted hot work above tanks containing flammable vapors with known holes in the roof and shell, without requiring continuous atmospheric monitoring.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: Adequate CO2 inerting system sized by engineering calculation (not done; system undersized); internal tank inspection per scheduled maintenance (deferred six times from 1996 to 2002); repair of identified shell holes and leaks (deferred); hot work atmospheric monitoring requirements for work above tanks with flammable vapors (not required); resolution of Unsafe Condition Reports before work proceeds (not tracked or enforced); PSM coverage of spent acid tanks (regulatory ambiguity excluded them); corporate management oversight of mechanical integrity deficiencies (absent).
- Key findings: (1) Tank inspectors recommended internal inspection of Tank 393 annually from at least 1996 through 2001; management deferred every time citing inventory constraints. (2) The CO2 inerting system was installed via a temporary hose with no engineering calculations; it could not maintain inert conditions. (3) Hot work was authorized above tanks with known holes containing flammable vapors. (4) An Unsafe Condition Report filed three weeks before the incident was not resolved. (5) A regulatory ambiguity under OSHA's PSM standard meant Motiva did not include the spent acid tanks in its PSM program; had they done so, mechanical integrity, MOC, and hot work requirements would have applied. (6) Spent H2SO4 with hydrocarbon content creates a flammable atmosphere that was not adequately managed after the conversion from fresh to spent acid.
- Recommendations (recipient -> action): Motiva: establish adequate mechanical integrity program for all tank farm equipment; apply proper MOC process to tank service changes; revise hot work program to require atmospheric monitoring above tanks containing flammable vapors; improve Unsafe Condition Report tracking. OSHA: clarify PSM coverage for atmospheric storage tanks connected to PSM-covered processes to close the regulatory ambiguity. (Additional recommendations to industry associations regarding tank inspection standards and inerting system design.)
- PSM elements implicated: Mechanical integrity; management of change; hot work permits; process hazard analysis; pre-startup safety review; management systems; regulatory compliance (PSM scope ambiguity).
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): A welder sparked an explosion in a tank that everyone knew was leaking but nobody had fixed.
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): This incident is almost entirely a story of deferred maintenance overriding engineering judgment. Tank inspectors documented the hazard and recommended action every year from 1996 forward. Management said no, every time, citing inventory constraints. When the tank was eventually converted to a service that created flammable headspace vapors, the conversion was done without engineering review, without a proper inerting system, and without updating the hot work program to reflect the new hazard. The regulatory system that should have backstopped the mechanical integrity failures did not cover the tank farm due to a PSM exemption for atmospheric storage. The June 27 Unsafe Condition Report was the last chance; it was filed, documented, and unresolved when the explosion occurred three weeks later.
- Monday-morning checks: (1) Maintain a formal log of all open mechanical integrity findings and inspector recommendations; require written management sign-off with documented justification for any deferral, and set hard expiration dates on deferrals. (2) Any tank converted from one service to another should be treated as a new process requiring a full PHA and engineering review of all safeguards, including inerting system sizing calculations, before restart. (3) Do not authorize hot work on or above tanks that have known holes, leaks, or are in degraded condition; require the work to stop until the tank can be proven safe, or the hot work location moved out of the hazard zone.
