### Barton Solvents Static Spark Explosion and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3552 | 2008 (Case Study No. 2007-06-I-KS, published 2008)
- Company | Location | Industry: Barton Solvents | Valley Center (Wichita), Kansas | Chemical distribution / solvent storage
- Incident date: July 17, 2007
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: VM&P naphtha (NFPA Class IB flammable liquid, nonconductive, conductivity 3 pS/m); subsequent involvement of ~20,000 gallons of mixed flammable liquids in the tank farm
- What happened: During routine transfer of VM&P naphtha from a tanker-trailer into a 15,000-gallon above-ground storage tank, static electricity accumulated in the liquid due to stop-start filling, air pockets, and sediment. A loose-linkage float gauge inside the tank likely sparked, igniting the ignitable vapor-air mixture in the tank headspace. The initial explosion launched the tank approximately 130 feet and triggered a cascading fire that destroyed the entire 43-tank farm and sent steel projectiles into the surrounding community.
- Consequences: 0 fatalities; 11 residents and 1 firefighter received medical treatment; entire tank farm destroyed; approximately 6,000 residents evacuated from Valley Center; steel tank tops, vent valves, and pipe fragments struck a mobile home 300 feet away and a neighboring business 400 feet away; major business disruption to Barton.
- Sequence: (1) Supervisor begins pumping final compartment of naphtha tanker into storage tank. (2) Stop-start filling and air pockets rapidly accumulate static charge in the nonconductive liquid. (3) Loose linkage on the internal liquid-level float gauge separates under turbulence, creating a spark. (4) Spark ignites the ignitable vapor-air mixture in the tank headspace. (5) Tank explodes, launches 130 feet, and lands trailing burning liquid. (6) Two adjacent tanks rupture and release contents into containment fire. (7) Remaining tanks overpressurize, launch steel parts off-site; fire burns for extended period.
- Immediate cause: Static electric spark from a loose-linkage tank level float ignited an ignitable vapor-air mixture inside the naphtha storage tank.
- Contributing factors: (1) Nonconductive naphtha (3 pS/m) accumulates static far more than conductive liquids; bonding and grounding alone are insufficient. (2) Stop-start filling and air pockets introduced into piping exacerbated charge generation. (3) Tank had never been cleaned; sediment and water likely suspended during filling added to turbulence and static buildup. (4) The tank was approximately 30% full, near the maximum static voltage condition during filling. (5) MSDS for the naphtha did not warn that the material can form an ignitable vapor-air mixture inside tanks or that bonding and grounding may be insufficient.
- Root causes (management-system level): Inadequate hazard communication at the industry level (MSDSs omitting critical static-accumulation warnings and conductivity data). Lack of facility awareness that additional precautions beyond standard bonding and grounding are required for nonconductive flammable liquids. No manufacturer or regulatory requirement to include conductivity testing data or specific precautions in safety data sheets.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) Bonding and grounding were present but insufficient for nonconductive liquid. (2) The tank level float was not designed to prevent sparking; no bonding wire on the float linkage. (3) Tank head space was not inerted. (4) MSDS did not prompt additional precautions. (5) No reduced pumping velocity guidance was followed.
- Key findings: (1) VM&P naphtha at 77F (25C) forms a readily ignitable vapor-air mixture inside tanks. (2) A loose-linkage float in the tank likely produced the igniting spark. (3) 97% of MSDSs reviewed warned of ignitable vapors, but only 1 of 62 specifically warned of the vapor-air mixture formation inside storage tanks. (4) Only 3 MSDSs (all from one manufacturer) included conductivity data. (5) The October 2007 Barton Des Moines fire also involved static and flammable liquids, reinforcing the pattern.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): OSHA to revise hazard determination guidance to require MSDS preparers to evaluate static accumulation potential and include conductivity data. OSHA to communicate to the GHS Sub-Committee the need to amend GHS classification criteria for static-accumulating flammable liquids. ANSI Z400.1 Committee to revise MSDS preparation standard to require conductivity data and specific precautions. American Chemistry Council, American Petroleum Institute, National Association of Chemical Distributors, National Paint and Coatings Association, National Petrochemical and Refiners Association, Society for Chemical Hazard Communication to recommend to member companies that they update MSDSs accordingly.
- PSM elements implicated: Hazard communication and process safety information; process hazard analysis (inadequate recognition of static ignition hazard); operating procedures (no procedures for handling nonconductive liquids beyond standard bonding/grounding); training and performance.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Tank exploded because of a static spark during routine liquid transfer."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The entire industry's hazard communication system failed to convey that nonconductive flammable liquids require a different category of protection than standard bonding and grounding. Neither the MSDS, the facility procedure, nor the operator training recognized that VM&P naphtha's low conductivity makes static sparks inside tanks a credible ignition scenario. A defect in the float design was the proximate spark source, but the absence of inerting, reduced fill velocity, or float modification was the systemic gap.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) For every flammable liquid you transfer into storage tanks, check whether it is nonconductive (conductivity below 100 pS/m). If it is, standard bonding and grounding are not enough. (2) Inspect all internal tank fittings (level floats, dip pipes) to confirm they cannot produce a spark inside the tank headspace during filling. (3) Review your MSDSs or SDS sheets for static-accumulating liquids. If they do not mention tank headspace ignition risk or reference NFPA 77 and API 2003, request updated information from the manufacturer before the next transfer.

### ConAgra Foods Natural Gas Explosion and Ammonia Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3557 | September 2009 (Safety Bulletin No. 2009-12-I-NC; final investigation report not yet published at time of bulletin; urgent recommendations issued February 2010)
- Company | Location | Industry: ConAgra Foods (Slim Jim plant) | Garner, North Carolina | Meat processing / food manufacturing
- Incident date: June 9, 2009
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Natural gas (methane); anhydrous ammonia (refrigeration system, secondary release)
- What happened: During installation and commissioning of a new industrial gas-fired water heater, a contractor from Energy Systems Analysts (ESA) purged air from a new 120-foot steel gas supply line by venting natural gas directly into an interior utility room over a period of up to 2.5 hours. No combustible gas detectors were used; workers relied on smell. Natural gas accumulated above the lower explosive limit inside the building, found an ignition source around 11:25 am, and exploded. The blast caused extensive structural collapse of prefabricated concrete roof panels (double-tees weighing 11 to 13.5 tons each), killing workers by crushing. The explosion also ruptured ammonia refrigeration piping, releasing approximately 18,000 pounds of anhydrous ammonia.
- Consequences: 4 fatalities (3 killed by structural collapse, 1 additional); 67 total injuries including 3 firefighters exposed to ammonia; 3 critical life-threatening burn injuries; 1 amputation; approximately 100,000 square feet of plant damaged; 37% of roof collapsed; ammonia contaminated local surface waters; plant eventually closed with loss of hundreds of jobs.
- Sequence: (1) ESA worker opens gas supply valve to purge air from new piping by releasing gas into interior utility room. (2) Difficulties lighting water heater lead to repeated purging over 2.5 hours. (3) Exhaust fan proves inadequate to clear accumulating gas. (4) Workers smell gas but consider it normal for the startup process; no combustible gas detector used. (5) Gas exceeds LEL throughout building. (6) Gas finds ignition source (believed to be unclassified electrical devices near boiler room). (7) Explosion collapses double-tee roof panels. (8) Ammonia refrigeration piping ruptures; ~18,000 lb released, hampering rescue.
- Immediate cause: Natural gas intentionally purged into an enclosed building interior accumulated above the lower explosive limit and found an ignition source.
- Contributing factors: (1) Indoor gas purging was common industry practice not explicitly prohibited by code at the time. (2) Reliance on smell to detect gas, combined with odor fade in new piping. (3) No combustible gas detectors deployed during purging. (4) No assessment of ventilation adequacy relative to gas release rate. (5) Over 200 non-essential personnel were in the building and were not informed or evacuated. (6) Building construction (precast concrete double-tees on pillars) was susceptible to collapse under even modest overpressure. (7) Utility room contained multiple unclassified ignition sources.
- Root causes (management-system level): ConAgra had no uniform gas-line purging procedure and did not require contractors to vent purged gases outdoors. ESA considered indoor purging normal practice. National fuel gas codes (NFPA 54, International Fuel Gas Code) at the time did not prohibit indoor purging. No MOC review governed the water heater installation commissioning activities or identified the gas accumulation hazard.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) No written purging procedure requiring outdoor venting. (2) No combustible gas detectors. (3) No isolation of ignition sources before purging. (4) Non-essential personnel not evacuated. (5) Ventilation fan not sized or verified against gas release rate. (6) Gas code did not prohibit indoor purging (remedied by NFPA emergency code change in August 2010).
- Key findings: (1) Indoor gas purging is inherently hazardous and was common practice. (2) Reliance on smell is unreliable due to olfactory fatigue and odor fade in new piping. (3) The building's structural design amplified death toll. (4) Ammonia release complicated rescue significantly. (5) Multiple similar incidents existed nationally (1997 Cary NC fitness center, 2007 Cheyenne hotel, 2008 San Diego hotel, 2005 Porterville school).
- Recommendations (recipient to action): NFPA to amend National Fuel Gas Code to prohibit indoor purging of industrial gas lines (emergency code change approved August 2010). International Code Council to require outdoor venting. Companies to develop written purging procedures requiring outdoor venting, combustible gas monitoring, and evacuation of non-essential personnel.
- PSM elements implicated: Operating procedures (no purging procedure); contractor management (ESA not required to follow safe practices); MOC (water heater installation not reviewed for gas accumulation hazard); training; process hazard analysis; emergency planning.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Gas leak from commissioning work exploded inside the plant."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Decades of accepted industry practice treated indoor gas purging as routine. Neither the company nor the contractor had a procedure requiring outdoor venting. No code prohibited it. The building's precast concrete structure was selected for cost without evaluating its explosive vulnerability. The absence of combustible gas detection and the reliance on smell created a system where accumulation above the LEL could go undetected for hours.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) Require any gas line purging or commissioning activity to vent purged gas to a safe outdoor location via hose or permanent vent piping; never rely on indoor venting even with an exhaust fan. (2) Deploy combustible gas detectors during any fuel gas commissioning or purging operation and set alarm setpoints well below LEL. (3) Evacuate all non-essential personnel from the affected building or area before beginning any operation that could release flammable gas, and establish an ignition-source control perimeter.

Note: No published final investigation report was present in the folder; the safety bulletin and urgent recommendations documents are the primary sources.

### Removal of Hazardous Material from Piping Systems (Huntsman Petrochemical)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3559 | July 2004 (Safety Bulletin No. 2004-03-B)
- Company | Location | Industry: Huntsman Petrochemical Corporation (anchor incident) | Port Neches, Texas | Petrochemical manufacturing
- Incident date: January 2004 (Huntsman investigation)
- Hazard type: study/bulletin
- Materials: Organic peroxide / alcohol mixture (thermally reactive); flammable vapor released on decomposition; steam (heat source)
- What happened: This CSB Safety Bulletin uses a January 2004 explosion at Huntsman Petrochemical as its anchoring case. During a steam purge to prepare piping for hot work (cutting and welding), a thermally reactive peroxide/alcohol mixture remained trapped in a low-point segment of a 900-foot, 6-inch process pipe. The nitrogen purge that was supposed to clear the pipe beforehand was ineffective because the low point was not identified or drained. Steam at over 380F heated the trapped peroxide above its decomposition temperature, causing exothermic decomposition and pipe rupture. Flammable vapor vented and ignited, producing a flash fire that injured two employees. The bulletin generalizes this to generic guidance on opening piping safely.
- Consequences (Huntsman anchor case): 2 serious injuries; ruptured pipe and valve; damage to nearby equipment; fire suppressed by automatic deluge system and fire brigade. The bulletin documents the broader hazard across chemical and refinery facilities.
- Sequence (Huntsman): (1) Modification work order issued for an infrequently used startup line, requiring hot work. (2) Nitrogen gas purge performed to clear peroxide/alcohol mixture per procedure. (3) Nitrogen purge is ineffective at removing liquid from an unidentified 300-foot-long low-point segment (volume over 350 gallons). (4) Steam purge begins as prerequisite to hot work. (5) Steam temperature (380F+) exceeds peroxide decomposition temperature. (6) After 1.5 hours, trapped peroxide decomposes exothermically. (7) Pipe overpressurizes, valve gasket fails, then pipe upstream bursts. (8) Flammable vapor ignites; flash fire injures two employees.
- Immediate cause: Steam purge heated thermally reactive peroxide trapped in an unidentified low-point segment above its decomposition temperature.
- Contributing factors: (1) Nitrogen purge procedure was not effective at removing liquid from the low-point trap. (2) As-built drawings split the low-point piping across two separate isometric sheets, making the trap difficult to recognize. (3) No field walkdown of the entire pipe was required by the modification work order. (4) Generic steam-purge procedure was applied to a line containing thermally unstable liquid without considering the specific hazard. (5) Revised procedure failed to require identification of low points or use of low-point drains.
- Root causes (management-system level): (Bulletin level) Failure to perform complete pipe walkdowns before opening or modifying piping. Reliance on as-built drawings without physical verification. Generic procedures applied to nonroutine work without unit-specific hazard review. MOC process did not require evaluation of full pipe routing for reactive material retention.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) Low-point drains were present but not used (identified in as-built drawings but missed). (2) Nitrogen purge was not verified as effective at removing all liquid. (3) No unit-specific procedure addressing thermally reactive liquids in low points. (4) MOC review did not identify the low-point hazard.
- Key findings (bulletin-level): (1) Piping modification and opening activities should never be treated as routine. (2) A full walkdown of all piping between isolation devices is essential before any line-opening work. (3) As-built drawings must be verified in the field; pipe routing in overhead racks frequently creates hidden low points. (4) Generic purge procedures must be reviewed against unit-specific hazards before application. (5) Gas purging does not reliably remove trapped liquid; draining low points is necessary. (6) Nonroutine work activities require facility-wide unit-specific written procedures.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): Chemical and refinery facilities to: (a) perform complete physical walkdowns of all piping before opening, using as-built drawings to identify all branch connections, valves, low-point drains, and high-point vents; (b) prepare specific written procedures for removing, flushing, and purging hazardous material from any system; (c) update unit-specific procedures when modifications are involved; (d) identify applicable constraints when generic procedures are used and require additional inspection steps; (e) consider whether steam or inert gas purging is appropriate for lines containing thermally reactive materials.
- PSM elements implicated: Operating procedures; process hazard analysis; mechanical integrity and process safety information (accurate as-built drawings); management of change; pre-startup safety review; safe work practices (line breaking).
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Pipe exploded during a steam purge before welding work."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A modification work order was issued for a line that had never required hot work before. The nitrogen purge that was supposed to clear it followed a generic procedure that was never verified against the actual pipe geometry. The as-built isometrics split the 300-foot low point across two sheets, so nobody recognized the trap. When steam was applied following what seemed like standard practice, it heated a reactive chemical left sitting in a 350-gallon blind pocket. The system had the right tools (low-point drains, MOC process) but they were not exercised adequately for a nonroutine task.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) Before any line-opening or hot-work job, physically walk the entire length of piping from isolation point to isolation point looking for low points, branch connections, and drains. Do not rely solely on drawings. (2) If the piping has ever contained thermally reactive, reactive, or highly hazardous material, require a unit-specific written procedure that explicitly addresses whether liquid can remain trapped and what temperature the purge medium will reach. (3) Verify that low-point drains are open and draining before declaring a line clear, and confirm their effectiveness before steam or other high-temperature purge media are applied.

### Sodium Hydrosulfide: Preventing Harm

- Archive id | Final report date: 3561 | November 2004 (Safety Bulletin No. 2003-03-B, reprinted November 2004)
- Company | Location | Industry: Multi-industry bulletin (anchor cases include Georgia-Pacific, Whitehall Leather, Horween Leather, Prime Tanning, Powell Duffryn, Westvaco) | Various U.S. locations | Pulp and paper, leather tanning, chemical manufacturing, mining
- Incident date: Bulletin covers incidents from 1971 through 2004
- Hazard type: study/bulletin
- Materials: Sodium hydrosulfide (NaHS) solution; hydrogen sulfide (H2S) generated on reaction with acids or exposure to heat; secondary: sulfur dioxide from combustion of H2S
- What happened: This CSB Safety Bulletin synthesizes 45 NaHS-related incidents from 1971 to 2004 that resulted in 32 fatalities, 176 injuries, and at least 10 evacuations. The core hazard is that NaHS reacts violently with acidic solutions or high heat to produce large volumes of deadly hydrogen sulfide gas. Most fatal incidents involved delivery drivers inadvertently transferring NaHS into acid storage tanks, NaHS mixing with acids in shared sewer systems, or failure of engineering controls and PPE during spills. The bulletin reviews anchor cases across leather tanning, pulp and paper, transportation, and manufacturing sectors and provides prevention and emergency response guidance.
- Consequences (aggregate): 32 fatalities, 176 injuries, 351 medical evaluations, at least 10 plant or community evacuations across 45 documented incidents. Anchor cases: Prime Tanning 1971 (6 killed), Horween Leather 1978 (8 killed, 43 injured), Powell Duffryn Savannah 1995 (337 medical evaluations, community evacuation for 30+ days), Georgia-Pacific 2002 (2 killed, 8 injured), Whitehall Leather 1999 (1 killed, 1 injured).
- Sequence (typical mechanism): (1) NaHS is spilled, mis-transferred, or leaks into contact with an acidic solution (pH below 7). (2) Acid-base reaction rapidly generates large volumes of H2S gas. (3) H2S exceeds IDLH (100 ppm) in the vicinity. (4) Workers exposed experience olfactory fatigue, lose ability to smell the gas, and are overcome. (5) Emergency responders without proper PPE attempt rescue and become secondary casualties.
- Immediate cause (typical): Inadvertent mixing of NaHS with acidic solutions due to mislabeling, driver unfamiliarity, improper sewer design, or spill containment colocating incompatible materials.
- Contributing factors: (1) Olfactory fatigue: H2S deadens odor-detecting nerves at concentrations above 50 ppm, removing the only warning most workers relied on. (2) Absent or inadequate H2S detection systems at transfer, storage, and offloading areas. (3) Delivery drivers not trained or informed about site-specific NaHS hazards. (4) Sewer systems and spill containment designed without evaluating compatibility between NaHS and acidic wastes. (5) Fill couplings or connections not uniquely designed to prevent misconnection. (6) No supervision of deliveries by qualified plant personnel.
- Root causes (management-system level): (1) Failure to identify and mitigate NaHS acid-incompatibility hazards during process system design and engineering. (2) Failure to manage hazards not eliminated by design through ongoing operational controls. (3) NaHS not covered by OSHA PSM despite its capacity to generate large quantities of deadly H2S when reacted. (4) Industry-wide gaps in hazard communication to drivers, contractors, and emergency responders.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) Separate containment for NaHS and acid-containing materials. (2) H2S detection and alarm at offloading and storage areas. (3) Unique fill couplings to prevent misconnection. (4) Adequate ventilation. (5) Trained supervision of product deliveries. (6) Sewer compatibility analysis. (7) Emergency responder training and PPE.
- Key findings: (1) H2S olfactory fatigue is rapid and predictable; never rely on smell as the sole H2S warning method. (2) Sewers must be treated as process extensions; acid and NaHS waste streams must not be comingled without H2S controls. (3) Separate spill containments for incompatible materials are essential. (4) NaHS is not covered by OSHA PSM, representing a regulatory gap for a chemical capable of catastrophic outcomes. (5) Emergency responders rushing to help fallen workers without PPE have become secondary fatalities in multiple incidents.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): Facilities using NaHS to: design process systems to prevent NaHS from contacting acidic solutions; install H2S detection and alarm systems at storage and transfer areas; install unique fill couplings to prevent misconnection; train employees and delivery drivers on NaHS hazards; communicate emergency procedures to first responders; separately contain NaHS and acid storage. OSHA to expand PSM to include chemicals with reactive characteristics similar to NaHS. CSB previously recommended OSHA expand PSM coverage for reactive chemicals (Hazard Investigation, 2002).
- PSM elements implicated: Process safety information; process hazard analysis; operating procedures; contractor and driver management; training; emergency planning and response; mechanical integrity (containment and detection systems); hazard communication.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Worker overcame by hydrogen sulfide from a chemical spill."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Across dozens of incidents spanning industries and decades, the same pattern repeated: NaHS reached an acid-containing space (a tank, a sewer, a shared containment) through a combination of absent engineering barriers, driver unfamiliarity, and sewer systems never analyzed for chemical compatibility. The H2S generated then overcame workers whose only warning mechanism, smell, had been removed by olfactory fatigue. Emergency responders entering without PPE extended the casualty count. The regulatory gap (NaHS not covered by PSM) meant facilities had no compelled baseline to analyze the hazard systematically.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) Map every sewer line and spill containment that receives NaHS-containing waste and verify that no acidic waste stream can enter the same space. If segregation is not absolute, install H2S monitoring with alarms on the sewer. (2) Install fixed H2S detectors with audible alarms at every NaHS offloading and storage location, and confirm that delivery drivers are briefed on site-specific hazards before they connect any hose. (3) Never trust smell as an H2S warning at concentrations that matter: deploy personal H2S detectors on all personnel working in NaHS areas, and train emergency responders that fallen workers in H2S environments require supplied-air SCBA before any rescue attempt.

### Veolia Environmental Services Flammable Vapor Explosion and Fire

- Archive id | Final report date: 3563 | July 21, 2010 (Case Study No. 2009-10-I-OH)
- Company | Location | Industry: Veolia ES Technical Solutions, LLC | West Carrollton, Ohio (near Dayton) | Hazardous waste treatment, storage, and disposal (TSDF) / solvent recovery
- Incident date: May 4, 2009 (12:07 a.m.)
- Hazard type: flammable vapor / VCE
- Materials: Tetrahydrofuran (THF), a Class IB flammable liquid (flammable range 2.3 to 11.8% in air); mixed waste solvents (aliphatic and chlorinated hydrocarbons, esters, ketones, alcohols); THF peroxides (potential contributor)
- What happened: At midnight, an operator was shutting down a THF solvent recovery process on Unit 3. To clear the piping, he back-blew nitrogen through the circulation piping into dirty tank 14 (D-14). Shortly after starting the nitrogen flow, a massive vapor cloud of THF released from the dirty tank farm, likely through the atmospheric relief system or an overpressured tank. The heavy-than-air vapor cloud drifted the 80 feet to the lab/operations building, where three natural gas-fired boilers provided ignition. The explosion severely injured two employees and damaged every structure on the 20-acre site; approximately 20 residences and businesses off-site also sustained damage.
- Consequences: 2 severely injured (1 first-degree burns, 1 broken pelvis); 2 slightly injured; all 8 on-site structures damaged; approximately 20 residences and 5 neighboring businesses damaged; 1 local business extensively damaged with cracked masonry, shifted foundation, fallen ductwork; total costs estimated at approximately $27 million. Fire declared under control at 10:38 a.m.
- Sequence: (1) Night shift operator begins shutdown of THF azeotrope recovery run on Unit 3. (2) Operator initiates nitrogen blowback through circulation piping into D-14 dirty tank. (3) Overpressure event occurs; investigators believe either peroxide-containing THF residue reacted with oxygen, or piping was mis-manifolded causing a nearby dirty tank to pressurize. (4) Large vapor cloud of THF released from the dirty tank farm. (5) Vapor cloud drifts to the lab/operations building 80 feet away; employees smell strong THF odor. (6) THF vapor finds ignition in the unclassified boiler room. (7) Initial explosion blows open boiler room doors. (8) Second, larger explosion in dirty tank farm. (9) Multiple subsequent explosions over 45 minutes; pool fires from spilled solvents extend burn time.
- Immediate cause: Flammable THF vapor released from the dirty tank farm ignited at natural gas-fired boilers in the adjacent lab/operations building.
- Contributing factors: (1) Atmospheric relief systems (pressure/vacuum vent devices and long-bolt manway covers) vented directly to atmosphere rather than to a closed system or flare, allowing uncontrolled vapor release to accumulate. (2) Lab/operations building located only 30 feet from operating units, well within the explosion zone. (3) Lab/operations building not classified under NFPA 70 National Electrical Code, despite housing multiple ignition sources (boilers). (4) No process hazard analysis existed evaluating building siting relative to the operating plant. (5) The nitrogen blowback procedure carried potential for peroxide concentration or mis-manifolding (root cause of the release remains uncertain). (6) OSHA PSM citations after the incident: no compliance audits in 3 years, inadequate mechanical integrity testing, insufficient written operating procedures, deficient training records.
- Root causes (management-system level): Absence of siting analysis for occupied buildings relative to operating flammable liquid processing areas. Open atmospheric relief systems with no closed capture or flare. Inadequate PSM program implementation (no compliance audits, inadequate PHA, inadequate operating procedures). No NFPA or industry standard specific to TSDF facilities to prescribe required separation distances or relief system design.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) Closed relief system to capture or flare released vapors. (2) Electrical classification of the lab/operations building. (3) Safe separation distance between occupied building and operating plant. (4) Remote shutdown capability for unit operators. (5) PHA evaluating building siting.
- Key findings: (1) Atmospheric venting of highly flammable vapors directly to air is inherently hazardous when ignition sources exist within the vapor dispersion range. (2) Mixed-use occupied buildings adjacent to flammable chemical processing create severe exposure risk. (3) NFPA 30 guidance for control room siting was followed for stable combustible liquids, but THF exhibited characteristics (heavy vapor, congestion, peroxide formation) not captured in that guidance. (4) No occupancy standard existed for TSDFs. (5) Previous CSB recommendations to ETC (Environmental Technology Council) from a 2006 Apex NC fire had not been implemented.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): Veolia to restrict occupancy of non-essential personnel in buildings near operating plants, and to install a closed relief system for hazardous vapors. Veolia to conduct PHA on all PSM-covered processes to ensure building siting and electrical classification comply with NFPA 70. NFPA to revise NFPA 30 to require written engineering analysis for separation distances of occupied buildings from operating areas. CCPS to revise control room siting guidelines to reflect Class IB liquid characteristics (heavy vapor, congestion). Environmental Technology Council to petition NFPA to develop an occupancy standard specific to TSDFs.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis; operating procedures; mechanical integrity; compliance audits; training; building siting (facility design); emergency planning.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Solvent recovery plant exploded when vapors reached a boiler."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The facility was designed with open atmospheric relief systems that vented flammable vapors into the air, and with an occupied building full of ignition sources only 30 feet away. No engineering analysis was ever performed to evaluate whether these two facts together created an unacceptable explosion risk. The NFPA siting guidance for control rooms was formally followed (for stable Class IB liquids) but was inadequate for the actual materials and equipment configuration at this facility. Industry standards for hazardous waste TSDFs did not exist.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) Audit the siting of every occupied building relative to your operating units processing flammable materials. If the building lacks electrical classification and is within the dispersion radius of a credible release, it is an uncontrolled ignition source. (2) Atmospheric relief vents on tanks and process equipment that handle Class IB or more volatile flammable liquids should discharge to a closed capture system or flare, not directly to open air near buildings. (3) Verify that your PHA explicitly addresses building siting, and that any occupied structures within the potential vapor cloud zone are part of the hazard evaluation.

### After Katrina: Special Precautions Needed During Oil and Chemical Facility Startup

- Archive id | Final report date: 3565 | September 2005 (Safety Bulletin No. 2005-01-S)
- Company | Location | Industry: Not a specific incident. Multi-facility advisory bulletin issued in response to Hurricane Katrina disruption of Gulf Coast petrochemical facilities | Gulf Coast region, USA | Oil refining, petrochemicals
- Incident date: September 2005 (bulletin issued following Hurricane Katrina, August 29, 2005)
- Hazard type: study/bulletin
- Materials: General: flammable hydrocarbons, process chemicals, corrosive materials, fuels; not specific to one material
- What happened: This CSB Safety Bulletin was issued to advise Gulf Coast oil refineries and chemical plants returning to operation after Hurricane Katrina about process safety risks specific to post-disaster restart. The bulletin draws on the CSB's investigation history: of 38 significant process incidents investigated since 1998, three catastrophic incidents occurred during startup of continuous process equipment (22 deaths, over 170 injuries). The bulletin emphasizes that startup hazards are amplified after a hurricane because equipment, instrumentation, and procedures may all be compromised simultaneously while the workforce is under stress and may be operating with incomplete staffing.
- Consequences: No single incident. The bulletin references prior CSB-investigated startup disasters as context, without specifying all cases. General framing: 22 deaths and 170+ injuries attributed to startup incidents in the CSB's investigated cases since 1998.
- Sequence (general startup risk scenario): (1) Hurricane disrupts facility, causing flooding, wind damage, and power loss. (2) Personnel begin restart under pressure to restore fuel supply; staffing may be reduced; some staff may be dealing with personal losses. (3) Damage to instrumentation, insulation, electrical systems, and process equipment is incomplete or undiscovered before restart. (4) MOC processes for hurricane-driven changes may be skipped or rushed. (5) Startup proceeds with compromised equipment, degraded procedures, or absent key personnel, creating conditions for a serious incident.
- Immediate cause: N/A (bulletin; no single incident). The recognized hazard is that post-disaster startup combines the inherent complexity of startup operations with compromised equipment, degraded safety systems, and stressed personnel.
- Contributing factors (general): (1) Pressure to restore operations quickly after a disaster overrides normal pre-startup review. (2) Equipment damage from flooding, wind, and debris may be latent and not apparent before startup. (3) Instrumentation, emergency systems, and electrical equipment may be water-damaged. (4) Staffing may be reduced or consist of personnel unfamiliar with the unit. (5) Procedures may not account for post-hurricane-specific conditions (e.g., water-saturated insulation, debris in fireboxes, grounding integrity).
- Root causes (management-system level): (Bulletin level) The systemic lesson is that crisis conditions create pressure to bypass or abbreviate safety management steps (MOC, PSSR, pre-startup checklists) that are most needed when conditions are abnormal.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing (general): Pre-startup safety reviews; management of change for hurricane-driven equipment, procedure, or staffing changes; complete equipment inspection checklists; adequate staffing and expertise; removal of non-essential personnel from startup zones.
- Key findings: (1) Startup is inherently one of the most hazardous operational phases. (2) Post-hurricane conditions amplify every startup hazard simultaneously. (3) Key pre-startup activities: comprehensive equipment inspection checklists (tanks for floating/foundation damage, pressure vessels for flood leakage, furnaces for debris and refractory damage, motors for ground faults, instrumentation for water damage). (4) MOC processes must apply to hurricane-driven changes in equipment, procedures, and staffing. (5) Human performance is compromised under crisis conditions; allow for reduced throughput rather than rushing to full capacity. (6) Nonessential personnel, including trailer occupants, must be removed from the vicinity of process units during startup.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): Facilities to: follow established startup procedures and checklists; complete thorough pre-startup safety reviews; apply MOC processes to any changes made because of hurricane damage; ensure adequate staffing and expertise before restart; use current, updated startup procedures; evacuate non-essential personnel from startup zones. Specific inspection checklists cover large bulk storage tanks, pressure vessels, insulation, sewers, furnaces, electric motors, switchgear, instrumentation, emergency warning systems.
- PSM elements implicated: Pre-startup safety review; management of change; operating procedures; employee training; mechanical integrity (post-hurricane inspection); process safety culture; emergency response and personnel protection.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Chemical plant restarts after hurricane disaster and something goes wrong."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): The bulletin's core insight is that the organizational dynamics of a disaster recovery, urgency, reduced staffing, compromised awareness, and disrupted procedures, are precisely the conditions under which process safety management steps are most likely to be skipped and most urgently needed. The hurricane physically damages equipment and simultaneously degrades the management system's ability to detect and correct that damage before restart.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) After any event that disrupts normal operations (storm, power outage, extended shutdown), do not bypass the pre-startup safety review even under time pressure. Use a written checklist that explicitly covers the specific types of damage the event was likely to cause. (2) Apply your MOC process to every change, including personnel, procedure, or equipment changes driven by the emergency, before restarting. (3) Remove non-essential personnel from the process unit area for the duration of startup, regardless of whether the startup is expected to be routine.

### Williams Olefins Plant Explosion and Fire (Geismar)

- Archive id | Final report date: 3578 | October 2016 (Case Study No. 2013-03-I-LA)
- Company | Location | Industry: Williams Olefins LLC (Williams Companies subsidiary) | Geismar, Louisiana | Olefins/petrochemical manufacturing (ethylene and propylene production)
- Incident date: June 13, 2013
- Hazard type: overpressure / mechanical failure (liquid thermal expansion leading to BLEVE)
- Materials: Liquid propane mixture (approximately 95% propane, balance propylene and C4 hydrocarbons); hot quench water (heat source, approximately 185F)
- What happened: During nonroutine troubleshooting of reduced quench water flow, an operations supervisor opened the quench water inlet and outlet valves on an offline propylene fractionator reboiler (EA-425B, offline 16 months, blocked-in with liquid propane in the shell). The reboiler was isolated from its pressure relief valve by the same block valves. The hot quench water (~185F) caused rapid thermal expansion of the confined liquid propane. Within approximately three minutes, the reboiler shell pressure reached an estimated 674 to 1,212 psig (maximum allowable working pressure: 300 psig). The shell catastrophically ruptured, causing a boiling liquid expanding vapor explosion (BLEVE) and fire. Two employees died; 167 reported injuries.
- Consequences: 2 fatalities (1 operator died at scene, 1 operations supervisor died from burns the next day); 167 reported injuries (3 Williams employees, 164 contractors working on expansion project); plant shutdown for 18 months; restart January 2015; over 30,000 pounds of flammable hydrocarbons released; fire lasted approximately 3.5 hours; portion of reboiler piping launched into overhead pipe rack 30 feet above.
- Sequence: (1) 2001: Williams installs block valves on reboiler shell-side and tube-side to enable single-reboiler operation. This change inadvertently allows reboilers to be isolated from pressure relief devices. (2) MOC and PSSR for the 2001 valve installation failed to identify the overpressure hazard when a blocked-in reboiler could receive heat while isolated from relief. (3) PHA action items generated to address this scenario were not effectively implemented. (4) June 13, 2013 morning meeting: operations supervisor identifies reduced quench water flow. (5) Operations supervisor decides to investigate in the field rather than follow change management protocols. (6) At 8:33 am, supervisor opens quench water valves on offline Reboiler B. (7) Hot quench water enters reboiler containing blocked-in liquid propane, isolated from its relief valve. (8) Approximately 3 minutes later, at 8:36 am, Reboiler B catastrophically ruptures, BLEVE, massive fireball.
- Immediate cause: Opening quench water valves on Reboiler B introduced heat to a blocked-in liquid propane-filled vessel that was isolated from its pressure relief device, causing liquid thermal expansion to overpressure and rupture the shell.
- Contributing factors: (1) The 2001 valve installation, without adequate hazard analysis, created the configuration that allowed a reboiler to be simultaneously heated and isolated from overpressure protection. (2) No operating procedure governed the quench water valve operation performed on the day of the incident. (3) The operations supervisor acted unilaterally without involving maintenance or following established management protocols. (4) PHA had previously identified an overpressure scenario for Reboiler B but the action item was closed with an administrative control (procedure) rather than an engineering safeguard, and that procedure did not exist. (5) Pre-startup safety review for the 2001 modification did not catch the gap. (6) Block valves are subject to leakage and inadvertent opening; more robust isolation (blinding) would have been safer.
- Root causes (management-system level): Twelve years of weaknesses in Williams Geismar's process safety management culture, including: deficient MOC and PSSR implementation for the 2001 valve installation; inadequate PHA action item closure (administrative control accepted where engineering control was needed); insufficient operating procedure development and maintenance; lack of a hierarchy-of-controls approach; absence of a hazard analysis for the specific nonroutine activities performed on the day of the incident. Gaps in API guidance on overpressure protection for standby and out-of-service equipment also contributed at the industry level.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) Engineering control (second relief device or interlock to prevent heating of isolated vessel) never installed. (2) Procedure for quench water valve operation on offline reboiler never written. (3) Effective PHA action item closure verifying field implementation. (4) Effective PSSR for the 2001 valve installation. (5) Management authorization for nonroutine field activities.
- Key findings: (1) Overpressure protection is essential for all pressure vessels; relief devices are not optional safeguards. (2) Block valves leak and can be inadvertently opened; robust isolation for standby equipment requires blinds. (3) MOC and PSSR must be conducted as multidisciplinary group reviews before field implementation, not as paperwork exercises. (4) PHA action items must be field-verified before closure. (5) Operating procedures must have sufficient detail for critical nonroutine steps. (6) Process safety culture weakness at the facility allowed 12 years of management system deficiencies to persist without correction. (7) API guidance on overpressure protection for standby equipment was incomplete.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): Williams Geismar to implement a continual improvement program for process safety culture including safety culture assessments, process safety leading and lagging indicators, and detailed PSM program assessments. Williams to strengthen MOC, PSSR, PHA, and operating procedure programs. API to revise guidance to require relief devices for thermal expansion and blocked-flow scenarios, to eliminate inconsistencies in guidance suggesting administrative controls suffice, and to add guidance on overpressure protection for standby and out-of-service equipment.
- PSM elements implicated: Management of change; pre-startup safety review; process hazard analysis; operating procedures; mechanical integrity; process safety culture; hierarchy of controls; overpressure protection.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Pipe burst during maintenance troubleshooting, causing an explosion."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): A 2001 valve installation changed the plant configuration to enable single-reboiler operation, a genuine operational improvement, but no one recognized that the new valves also created a scenario where hot quench water could be introduced to a liquid-filled vessel isolated from its relief device. The MOC that should have caught this was inadequate. The PHA that did identify the scenario generated an action item that was closed as complete, but the required procedure was never written. For 12 years, the hazard existed in the plant while multiple layers of process safety management passed over it without detection. The day of the incident, an experienced supervisor performed an unplanned field action with no procedure and no authorization, under time pressure from a routine operational problem.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) Identify every vessel in your plant that can be blocked in while also receiving heat from a connected source (utility fluid, steam, solar, process fluid). Confirm each has an active, unblockable path to a pressure relief device, or an interlock that prevents heat introduction when isolated. (2) When a valve installation or piping modification changes what can be isolated and from what, require the MOC to specifically address every combination of open/closed valve positions and what happens to each vessel in each configuration. (3) Before closing any PHA action item, require field verification that the safeguard actually exists and is functional, not just that a commitment was made on paper.

### Airgas Facility Fatal Nitrous Oxide Explosion

- Archive id | Final report date: 3587 | February 2017 (Investigation Report No. 2016-04-I-FL)
- Company | Location | Industry: Airgas (Air Liquide subsidiary) | Cantonment, Florida | Industrial gas manufacturing (nitrous oxide production and distribution)
- Incident date: August 28, 2016 (approximately 12:10 pm)
- Hazard type: reactive runaway
- Materials: Nitrous oxide (N2O, liquid and gas); nitrous oxide undergoes exothermic decomposition to nitrogen and oxygen when subjected to excessive heat, contamination, or mechanical shock above safe operating limits
- What happened: During the initial loading of a nitrous oxide trailer truck, the transfer pump apparently heated nitrous oxide above its safe operating limits. This triggered a nitrous oxide decomposition reaction (N2O yields N2 plus oxygen, highly exothermic, self-sustaining above threshold) that propagated from the pump into the trailer, causing a catastrophic explosion. The single Airgas employee present was killed. The explosion heavily damaged the Cantonment facility, permanently halting nitrous oxide production there.
- Consequences: 1 fatality (Jesse Folmar, the only on-site Airgas employee); facility destroyed; nitrous oxide manufacturing at Cantonment halted indefinitely. Industry context: since 1973, the nitrous oxide industry has averaged one major decomposition explosion approximately every 7 years, killing 6 workers and injuring 21 others. Since 2001, frequency increased to approximately one every 4 years.
- Sequence: (1) Airgas employee begins loading Trailer 182, which had not been loaded in this session and needed to be loaded. (2) The ACD transfer pump, which had a history of heating issues and run-dry events that could overheat nitrous oxide, initiates transfer. (3) The pump heats liquid nitrous oxide above safe operating limits. (4) Nitrous oxide decomposition reaction initiates at or near the pump. (5) Decomposition reaction propagates through piping into the trailer. (6) Trailer explodes catastrophically at approximately 12:10 pm. (7) Employee killed; facility destroyed.
- Immediate cause: The transfer pump heated nitrous oxide above safe operating limits, initiating a self-propagating decomposition reaction that caused the trailer to explode.
- Contributing factors: (1) Airgas installed a new pump (ACD) without performing an MOC safety review; the new pump design increased explosion likelihood without evaluation. (2) The flame arrestor (explosion prevention device) installed in the system had never been tested or inspected to confirm it could prevent nitrous oxide decomposition propagation. (3) The automatic run-dry safety interlock required the employee to be physically present adjacent to the trailer (the most dangerous location during a potential explosion) to bypass the interlock during startup; the interlock design was not evaluated per ISA-84 safety instrumented system standards. (4) No process hazard analysis was ever conducted on the Cantonment nitrous oxide manufacturing process. (5) Airgas lacked an effective process safety management system. (6) Inherently safer design alternatives (avoiding the pump entirely) were never evaluated. (7) Lessons from six prior industry nitrous oxide explosions (1973, 1980, 1987, 2001, 2007, 2012) were not applied. (8) OSHA PSM and EPA RMP standards do not apply to nitrous oxide facilities; no federal regulatory requirement for a safety management system existed.
- Root causes (management-system level): Absence of a process safety management system at Airgas/Air Liquide for nitrous oxide manufacturing. No hazard analysis of the Cantonment process. Failure to apply hierarchy of controls (inherently safer design not evaluated; safeguards not analyzed for effectiveness). Inadequate application of industry standards (CGA guidelines not fully followed; ISA-84 not applied to safety interlocks). Regulatory gap: nitrous oxide is not covered by OSHA PSM or EPA RMP despite repeated industry fatalities. Failure to learn from six prior industry explosions over 43 years.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) Effective flame arrestor (installed but never tested; may not have been designed for nitrous oxide decomposition). (2) Run-dry interlock designed per applicable safety standards. (3) Process hazard analysis. (4) Safety management system. (5) MOC review for pump change. (6) Inherently safer design (gravity or pressure-differential loading without a pump). (7) Protective physical barrier between loading area and employee work location.
- Key findings: (1) Nitrous oxide is a reactive hazard capable of catastrophic decomposition explosions; this has been known since at least 1973. (2) Federal regulations (PSM, RMP) did not apply to this facility despite its hazard profile, leaving safety management entirely voluntary. (3) Airgas had no process safety management system, conducted no hazard analysis, and did not apply the hierarchy of controls. (4) The two key safeguards (flame arrestor, run-dry interlock) were both ineffective: the flame arrestor was untested and the interlock required the employee to be in the danger zone to function. (5) Air Liquide, the parent company, had knowledge of nitrous oxide decomposition hazards but did not ensure Cantonment was adequately managed. (6) The global industry (CGA, pump manufacturers) had insufficient standards and warnings to address the hazard.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): Air Liquide (Airgas) to complete post-incident actions and implement a comprehensive process safety management system for all nitrous oxide operations. Compressed Gas Association (CGA) to develop a safety management system standard for nitrous oxide manufacturing, ensure effective flame arrestor design guidance, and require pump run-dry interlocks meet ISA-84. Pump manufacturers ACD LLC and Cryostar USA to provide effective warnings about nitrous oxide decomposition hazards associated with their pumps.
- PSM elements implicated: Process hazard analysis; management of change; process safety information; mechanical integrity; hierarchy of controls; inherently safer design; safety management system; contractor management; regulatory coverage gap.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Gas plant explosion killed a worker during a routine loading operation."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): For 43 years, the nitrous oxide industry had experienced recurring decomposition explosions, but no federal regulation required facilities to implement systematic process safety management. Airgas had no hazard analysis, installed a new pump without MOC review, and relied on two safety devices (a flame arrestor and an interlock) that were never verified to work as intended for this specific hazard. The employee had to stand next to the trailer at the moment explosion risk was highest in order to operate the safety system. No inherently safer alternative was ever considered. The risk was known, the precautions were absent or ineffective, and the regulatory framework provided no backstop.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) For any reactive gas or liquid capable of self-propagating decomposition (nitrous oxide, acetylene, ethylene oxide), verify that your safety interlocks have been designed and tested per ISA-84 and that they do not require personnel to be in the danger zone to operate. (2) Test and document the effectiveness of any explosion prevention or suppression device (flame arrestor, explosion vent, suppression system) specific to the material handled, not just for generic flammable vapor service. (3) Before installing or replacing transfer equipment (pumps, compressors, loading arms) in reactive chemical service, conduct a formal MOC and hazard analysis that includes pump-specific failure modes and heat generation rates relative to the reactive limits of the material.

### Wacker Polysilicon Chemical Release

- Archive id | Final report date: 3614 | June 2023 (Investigation Report No. 2021-01-I-TN)
- Company | Location | Industry: Wacker Polysilicon North America LLC | Charleston, Tennessee | Polysilicon manufacturing / semiconductor materials
- Incident date: November 13, 2020
- Hazard type: toxic gas release
- Materials: Hydrogen chloride (HCl) gas, released from a cracked graphite heat exchanger in the HCl regeneration process; hydrochloric acid (30% HCl / 70% water) was the process fluid being converted to anhydrous HCl gas
- What happened: Contractors from Jake Marshall LLC were performing a post-maintenance torque check on flange bolts of a heat exchanger (AW234) vapor outlet pipe on the fifth floor (approximately 70 feet above ground) of a five-story equipment access structure. The pipe contained live HCl under process conditions. Workers inadvertently over-torqued bolts on a PTFE-to-graphite connection, fracturing the graphite outlet piping and releasing gaseous HCl. Four Pen Gulf insulation contractors working on the same platform in standard FR clothing (not chemical-resistant suits) and three Jake Marshall workers were on the platform. The white HCl cloud obscured the staircase (the only exit). Three Pen Gulf workers climbed down the exterior piping to escape, fell approximately 70 feet, and were fatally or seriously injured. One worker (Jesus Jared Aguilar Montes) was fatally injured from the fall. Two others sustained serious injuries from falls. The four remaining workers stayed on the platform until the release stopped and used the staircase to exit.
- Consequences: 1 fatality (Jesus Jared Aguilar Montes, Pen Gulf contractor, fell from approximately 70 feet while escaping HCl cloud); 2 serious injuries (falls); chemical burns to at least 1 worker; $214,000 property damage.
- Sequence: (1) Night shift (November 12): Wacker restarts HCl regeneration unit after maintenance. (2) 7:00 am November 13: Unit reaches normal operating conditions. (3) 8:00 am: Wacker issues work permit to Jake Marshall to "hot torque AW234" (check torque on all bolts of heat exchanger vapor outlet piping). (4) 8:24 am: Wacker issues separate work permit to Pen Gulf to repair insulation on nearby equipment on the same fifth-floor platform. (5) Work proceeds simultaneously with 7 workers on the 5th floor platform (3 Jake Marshall, 4 Pen Gulf). Jake Marshall workers wear full-body chemical-resistant suits; Pen Gulf workers wear only FR clothing. (6) Jake Marshall workers use a click-type torque wrench on the bolts. The pipe had bolts with differing torque requirements; Wacker gave verbal instructions only. (7) Bolts with PTFE-to-graphite connections are inadvertently over-torqued. (8) Graphite outlet pipe cracks; HCl releases as white cloud. (9) Cloud obscures visibility and the single staircase. (10) Three Pen Gulf workers attempt to climb down exterior piping; all three fall. (11) Remaining workers shelter on platform until release stops; exit via staircase.
- Immediate cause: Inadvertent over-torquing of flange bolts on a live HCl piping connection fractured a graphite heat exchanger outlet pipe, releasing HCl gas in the vicinity of seven workers, three of whom fell from 70 feet while attempting to escape.
- Contributing factors: (1) No written procedure for the torque check; Wacker relied on the manufacturer's equipment manual, which did not include torque requirements for the over-torqued bolt configuration. (2) Wacker did not treat torquing on live equipment containing hazardous chemicals as a line-break or hazardous energy control activity requiring risk assessment and isolation. (3) No simultaneous operations (SIMOPs) program or policy to evaluate risks of co-located work tasks. (4) Pen Gulf insulation workers, who were not involved in the torquing, were permitted on the platform without evaluation of exposure to the torquing task hazard. (5) The fifth-floor platform had only a single staircase for egress; building code for "unoccupiable equipment platforms" did not require a second egress point. (6) Three months before the incident, a PHA identified the need for a second egress point on this structure, but no action was taken before the incident.
- Root causes (management-system level): Absence of written procedures for torquing operations on live equipment with hazardous material. Failure to treat torquing bolts on operating equipment as a hazardous energy control activity. No SIMOPs program to manage co-located work groups with different PPE levels and different hazard exposure. Single point of egress designed for building code compliance without evaluating the chemical hazard context. PHA recommendation for second egress not acted upon. General absence of industry and regulatory guidance on SIMOPs and means of egress from open-air industrial structures.
- Safeguards that failed or were missing: (1) Written torquing procedure with annotated torque specifications for each bolt configuration. (2) Hazardous energy control (either de-inventory of equipment or restriction of non-essential personnel) before torquing live equipment. (3) SIMOPs evaluation to separate or protect Pen Gulf workers from Jake Marshall hazard zone. (4) Second means of egress from the fifth-floor platform. (5) Appropriate PPE (chemical-resistant suits) for all workers on the platform, or exclusion of workers not in proper PPE. (6) Action on PHA recommendation for second egress.
- Key findings: (1) Verbal instructions for torque requirements on equipment with multiple differing specifications are inadequate; written procedures with visual differentiation are necessary. (2) Torquing bolts on live equipment containing hazardous material is a hazardous energy control activity and must be treated as such regardless of whether it involves intentional line opening. (3) SIMOPs create compounded exposure risk, especially when work groups have different PPE levels. (4) Building codes for "unoccupiable equipment platforms" do not adequately address chemical release egress requirements. (5) A PHA had identified the egress gap but the recommendation was not acted upon before the incident. (6) Regulatory and industry guidance on SIMOPs is largely absent for non-confined-space and non-construction settings.
- Recommendations (recipient to action): OSHA to promulgate a standard or modify existing standards to require coordination of SIMOPs involving multiple work groups, including contractors, applicable to both general industry and construction (not limited to confined spaces). OSHA to develop safety guidance on SIMOPs coordination. Wacker to develop detailed maintenance procedures for torquing activities with clear, annotated torque specifications for differing bolt configurations; to develop policy for hazardous energy control during torquing on live equipment; to implement a formalized SIMOPs program; and to improve means of egress from elevated work platforms used for hazardous equipment access.
- PSM elements implicated: Operating procedures (written procedures for maintenance tasks); control of hazardous energy; simultaneous operations management; means of egress from process structures; process hazard analysis (PHA action item follow-through); contractor management; PPE and worker protection.
- Easy story (headline / lazy explanation): "Chemical leak on high platform caused workers to fall trying to escape."
- Systemic reframe (what actually set it up): Workers were permitted to perform a torque check on live HCl equipment without a written procedure specifying torque values for each bolt configuration. At the same time, a second group of workers in inadequate PPE was permitted on the same small elevated platform under a separate work permit, with no one evaluating what would happen if the first task went wrong. The platform had a single escape route that a chemical cloud could immediately obscure. A PHA three months earlier had identified the egress gap, but no action was taken. The regulatory framework had no standard requiring SIMOPs coordination or adequate egress from open-air industrial structures at elevation. Every layer of prevention was missing.
- Monday-morning checks (2-3 transferable): (1) Before any maintenance task on live equipment containing corrosive, toxic, or flammable material, require a written procedure that specifies the exact torque, force, or energy values for each component. Verbal briefings are not sufficient when different fasteners in the same assembly have different specifications. (2) When issuing simultaneous work permits for co-located tasks, require an explicit evaluation of whether the hazard from one task can reach the workers doing the other. If the PPE levels differ or egress would be shared, the tasks need to be separated in time or space, or all workers need the higher-level PPE. (3) For any elevated platform used for accessing hazardous equipment, verify that there are at least two independent egress paths and that both remain usable during a credible release scenario. Do not rely on building code minimums designed for buildings without chemical hazards.
