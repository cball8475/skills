### Valero Meraux Refinery, April 10, 2020 (Vol 1, #1)
- Location | Industry: Meraux, Louisiana | Petroleum refining
- Hazard type: Flammable gas release, explosion, fire
- What happened: A malfunctioning emergency pressure-relief valve remained open, venting hydrogen and hydrocarbon gas to the flare system. Operators attempted to reseat the valve by closing the downstream 20-inch outlet valve instead of an upstream inlet valve. The outlet valve was rated for only 275 psi but was subjected to roughly 2,100 psi, causing it to fail and release approximately 49,000 pounds of flammable gas, which ignited. One operator was seriously injured and property damage was approximately $5.15 million.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: closure of the low-pressure-rated 20-inch outlet valve instead of the high-pressure-rated upstream 6-inch inlet valves
  - The hazard of using the wrong valve was not recognized by the operations team
- Transferable lesson: When reseating a relief valve, verify the pressure rating of every valve in the proposed isolation path. A documented management-of-change process does not substitute for engineering review of which valve is safe to close.

### LACC, April 11, 2020 (Vol 1, #2)
- Location | Industry: Westlake, Louisiana | Petrochemical manufacturing
- Hazard type: Corrosive liquid release (spent caustic)
- What happened: An operator was clearing a chemical hose connected to a nearly full frac tank of spent caustic. When the valve was opened, pressurized fluid surged into the tank and ejected caustic through the unsecured top hatch, splashing the operator with corrosive liquid. No safety shower was within 25 feet of the tank, meaning the operator spent about two minutes reaching decontamination, worsening chemical burns.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: pressurized fluid entering a nearly full tank whose top hatch was unsecured
  - Inadequate procedure: no step requiring the hatch to be secured before opening the valve
  - PPE did not protect against caustic splash; safety shower was too far away
  - Underlying driver: ongoing reliability problems with the oxidizing reactor led to prolonged use of temporary frac tanks
- Transferable lesson: Temporary workarounds that persist become de facto permanent operations; procedures and PPE requirements must match the actual hazard, not the original design intent.

### Wacker Polysilicon, July 24, 2020 (Vol 1, #3)
- Location | Industry: Charleston, Tennessee | Specialty chemical manufacturing
- Hazard type: Corrosive liquid release (silicon tetrachloride)
- What happened: Contract workers disassembled a flange to reinstall cleaned piping. The piping had not been effectively isolated, flushed, and drained, so silicon tetrachloride that had leaked past an isolation valve pressurized the section. When the flange was opened, corrosive liquid splashed both workers, causing serious chemical burns.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: failure to effectively isolate, flush, and drain the piping before handing it over for disassembly
  - Contractors did not wear chemical suits and rubber boots required by Wacker's permit
  - Wacker's investigation scope was too narrow; it focused only on contractor behavior and did not evaluate Wacker's own authorization and verification failures
- Transferable lesson: The facility owner, not just the contractor, bears responsibility for verifying that line-opening prerequisites (isolation, flush, drain, PPE confirmation) are met before work begins.

### Formosa Plastics Corporation, December 3, 2020 (Vol 1, #4)
- Location | Industry: Point Comfort, Texas | Petrochemical manufacturing
- Hazard type: Toxic gas release (chlorine)
- What happened: Workers disconnecting a "supposedly empty" one-ton chlorine container found it still held 1,250 pounds of chlorine. The disconnection released approximately 10 pounds of chlorine gas. An employee who was not wearing respiratory protection (because the container was believed empty) was life-flighted to hospital with serious respiratory injuries.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: disconnecting process equipment from a pressurized chlorine container
  - No instrumentation (e.g., a container scale) to confirm actual chlorine inventory
  - Use of chlorine rather than a safer alternative such as bleach as a biocide
- Transferable lesson: "Assumed empty" is not a safe basis for opening a chemical container. Verify actual inventory with instrumentation; consider inherently safer substitutes for toxic materials.

### Lubrizol Chemtool, June 14, 2021 (Vol 1, #5)
- Location | Industry: Rockton, Illinois | Lubricant/specialty chemical manufacturing
- Hazard type: Flammable liquid release, fire (mineral oil)
- What happened: A contractor raising a scissor lift struck a half-inch threaded piping assembly connected to a four-inch pipe carrying mineral oil above 500 degrees Fahrenheit. The impact created a leak, releasing hot mineral oil as an aerosol cloud. Electrostatic conditions ignited the mist, starting a fire that destroyed the facility and caused $380 million in property damage. The facility was permanently closed.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: piping damage from force applied by the scissor lift
  - Hot mineral oil ignited by static electricity
  - No remote isolation capability to shut off flow after the leak developed
- Transferable lesson: Mobile equipment (lifts, cranes, vehicles) near live process piping creates struck-by risks. Map overhead and adjacent piping hazards before raising equipment. Ensure remote isolation for hot flammable systems.

### ConocoPhillips, October 6, 2021 (Vol 1, #6)
- Location | Industry: Carlsbad, New Mexico | Oil and gas production
- Hazard type: Flammable liquid/vapor release, flash fire
- What happened: A contractor vacuum truck driver was removing an emulsion layer from a pressurized heater treater using system pressure rather than the truck's vacuum pump. After closing a process valve, the driver disconnected the hose while it still contained hydrocarbon fluid; the contents flowed to atmosphere and ignited in a flash fire, seriously injuring the driver.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: disconnecting the hose while it contained hydrocarbon material and the truck's isolation valve was open
  - Burner on the heater treater was inadvertently left online; static electricity also not eliminated as ignition source
  - No pre-job hazard analysis; no grounding/bonding protocol for the vacuum truck; procedures did not effectively control the hazards
- Transferable lesson: Vacuum truck loading from pressurized vessels requires the vessel to be depressured first, with the truck using its own vacuum. Grounding and bonding must be specified in the procedure, not left to the driver's judgment.

### Rubicon (Huntsman/Lanxess), January 15, 2022 (Vol 1, #7)
- Location | Industry: Geismar, Louisiana | Chemical manufacturing (MDI)
- Hazard type: Hot chemical release (methylene diphenyl diisocyanate, MDI)
- What happened: A rupture disc activated prematurely at about 13 psi (design activation 30 psi), discharging 201 pounds of 400-degree Fahrenheit MDI through vertical piping that ended eight inches above the floor. Two contract maintenance workers installing a pump nearby were sprayed with hot MDI and hospitalized with thermal burns.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: activation of the rupture disc emergency pressure-relief device
  - Discharge piping directed hot MDI toward the work area rather than to a safe location
  - Process hazard analysis had identified this consequence but the hazard had not been mitigated
- Transferable lesson: Identifying a hazard in a PHA without acting on it provides no protection. Relief-device discharge paths must be designed (or redirected) to avoid occupied areas.

### Suncor Energy, December 24, 2022 (Vol 1, #9)
- Location | Industry: Commerce City, Colorado | Petroleum refining
- Hazard type: Flammable liquid/vapor release, explosion, fire
- What happened: A steam turbine pump had been isolated for maintenance for 190 days, creating a temporary dead-leg with stagnant hydrocarbon and water. During a cold-weather event (temperatures below freezing from December 21 to 24), water in the dead-leg froze. When temperatures rose on December 24, ice damaged a valve flange, releasing approximately 1,400 pounds of light straight-run gasoline. The vapor cloud drifted to a fired heater and ignited; two operators performing emergency response tasks in the area were seriously injured and property damage was $40 million.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: release of flammable hydrocarbons through the flange of an ice-damaged valve
  - Dead-leg management program failed to identify this temporary dead-leg during maintenance
  - Emergency response procedure allowed operators to approach a flammable vapor cloud to eliminate ignition sources, placing them in harm's way
- Transferable lesson: Temporary dead-legs (created during maintenance, not just permanent ones) must be entered in the dead-leg management program and winterized. Emergency procedures should keep responders out of flammable vapor hazard zones.

### Dow St. Charles Operations (Union Carbide), January 17, 2023 (Vol 1, #11)
- Location | Industry: Hahnville, Louisiana | Chemical manufacturing
- Hazard type: Hot water release, burn injury
- What happened: After control valve maintenance, operators restarted the boiler feedwater system without using the operational readiness checklist to verify valve positions. Two bleed valves that had been opened for maintenance remained open, and a temporary drain hose was still attached. Hot boiler feedwater continued to release for about two hours, pooling in a depression. An operator investigating the leak stepped into the pooled hot water and suffered serious burns to the lower leg and foot.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: startup of the feedwater system with open bleed valves and an open-ended hose
  - Operational readiness checklist was not used, allowing the temporary configuration to remain
- Transferable lesson: Operational readiness checklists exist to catch exactly this scenario. Making their use mandatory (not optional) before any restart prevents "state drift" from maintenance to operation.

### Phillips 66 Refinery, January 17, 2023 (Vol 1, #12)
- Location | Industry: Borger, Texas | Petroleum refining (natural gas liquid storage)
- Hazard type: Flammable liquid/vapor release, fire
- What happened: Contractors were tightening lockdown screws on a natural gas liquid cavern wellhead using a battery-operated impact wrench inadvertently left in reverse. One screw was accidentally removed instead of tightened, releasing natural gas liquid at 400 psi. The material ignited, fatally injuring one contractor and seriously injuring another.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: inadvertent removal of a lockdown screw due to the impact wrench being set to reverse
  - Pressurized natural gas liquid remained in the snubbing unit during the work
  - Post-incident action: Phillips 66 required hand tools (not power tools) for lockdown screws and venting of the snubbing unit before work
- Transferable lesson: Power tools on pressure-retaining fasteners create an accidental removal risk. Specify the correct tool direction or prohibit power tools where accidental reversal would release pressurized material.

### CITGO Petroleum Refinery, January 21, 2023 (Vol 1, #14)
- Location | Industry: Sulphur, Louisiana | Petroleum refining
- Hazard type: Flammable gas release, fire (hydrogen)
- What happened: A shell-and-tube heat exchanger assembled in 2013 had undergone 43 thermal cycles. The bolts had relaxed over time to a point where the flange could no longer contain hydrogen. During a unit startup, approximately 30 pounds of hydrogen leaked and caught fire, causing over $1.5 million in property damage.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: under-torqued bolts that had relaxed through 43 thermal cycles
  - Assembly instructions listed incorrect (too low) torque values due to wrong bolt sizes being specified
  - Incorrect torque from a nearby similar heat exchanger may also have been applied
- Transferable lesson: Torque specifications must be verified against actual bolt sizes, not assumed from adjacent equipment. Flanges on hydrogen service require periodic re-torquing programs that account for thermal cycling.

### Kinder Morgan Gas Plant, January 31, 2023 (Vol 1, #15)
- Location | Industry: Snyder, Texas | Natural gas/CO2 processing
- Hazard type: Vessel overpressure, catastrophic rupture (CO2)
- What happened: A dump valve on a high-pressure CO2 scrubber stuck open in cold weather (22 degrees Fahrenheit outside), allowing high-pressure CO2 to flow into a low-pressure drain system. The Joule-Thompson effect chilled the CO2 to as low as negative 30 degrees Fahrenheit, causing ice or hydrate to block the drain vessel's relief valve outlet. Pressure in the drain vessel built to 550 to 700 psig, well above its 125 psig rating, causing a catastrophic brittle fracture. Fragments flew up to 1,200 feet, heavily damaging the control room. Property damage exceeded $14 million. Over 5.6 million pounds of CO2 and other gases were released.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: overpressure of the drain vessel after the dump valve stuck open and ice/hydrate blocked the relief path
  - Drain system had no pressure, temperature, or level instrumentation; the developing upset was invisible to operators
  - Prior PHAs identified gas blow-through as an operational (not safety) concern; did not evaluate ice/hydrate formation risk in the drain system
  - The low-pressure drain system was not designed to handle high-pressure CO2 blow-through
- Transferable lesson: Low-pressure utility systems connected to high-pressure processes can be exposed to full upstream pressure through a single stuck valve. PHA scope must include downstream drain systems, and thermodynamic properties of the fluid (CO2 Joule-Thompson cooling, hydrate formation) must inform the design.

### Kinder Morgan Crude Oil Production Facility, February 11, 2023 (Vol 1, #16)
- Location | Industry: Snyder, Texas | Crude oil production
- Hazard type: Flammable liquid release, fire (crude oil)
- What happened: Excessive wear and a loose bearing lock nut allowed a crude oil pump shaft to move and contact internal components. The resulting metal-to-metal contact released crude oil and likely provided the ignition source. A fire damaged nearby equipment; over 1,500 barrels of hydrocarbons and more than 1,200 pounds of nitrogen oxide were released. Property damage was approximately $1.5 million.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: failure of a crude oil pump from excessive wear; heat from metal-to-metal friction likely ignited the released crude oil
  - Loose bearing lock nut contributed to the mechanical failure
- Transferable lesson: Mechanical integrity for rotating equipment must include bearing lock nut inspection. Pump failure modes that release flammable liquids and simultaneously generate an ignition source (friction heat) require robust vibration monitoring.

### Pemex Refinery, February 23, 2023 (Vol 1, #18)
- Location | Industry: Deer Park, Texas | Petroleum refining
- Hazard type: Flammable liquid release, fire (hydrocarbons)
- What happened: During heat exchanger startup after cleaning, operators walked down the energy isolation drawing and removed locks, but a bleed valve locked open on top of the exchangers was missed. The valve's location made it hard to see, especially in the dark. Hot hydrocarbons at 562 degrees Fahrenheit flowed through the open bleed valve into ambient air and autoignited (autoignition temperature 482 degrees Fahrenheit), causing a fire and $2.3 million in property damage.

  - The energy isolation drawing may not have shown the bleed valve; it was destroyed in the fire

### ConocoPhillips, February 24, 2023 (Vol 1, #19)
- Location | Industry: Watford City, North Dakota | Oil and gas production
- Hazard type: Toxic/asphyxiant gas release (hydrocarbons, hydrogen sulfide)
- What happened: A lone worker in an 8-foot-tall, 90-square-foot insulated building in negative 26-degree Fahrenheit weather manually drained a vessel into a bucket inside the building. The hydrocarbons vaporized and displaced oxygen. When the employee returned to the building, he was fatally asphyxiated; a knocked-over bucket likely released additional hydrogen sulfide, completing the oxygen depletion. The employee's gas monitor was in his truck, turned off.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):

  - No company procedure for safely draining liquids inside buildings
  - No installed gas detectors, alarms, or ventilation; personal gas monitor not in use
- Transferable lesson: Enclosed buildings in oil and gas production facilities that contain process equipment are effectively confined spaces. Gas detection, ventilation, and lone-worker check-in systems are essential.

### Pemex Refinery, March 14, 2023 (Vol 1, #21)
- Location | Industry: Deer Park, Texas | Petroleum refining
- Hazard type: Flammable liquid release, fire (hydrocarbons)
- What happened: A centrifugal pump cavitated eight times during a unit startup because of low liquid level in upstream equipment. The cavitation damaged the pump bearings. Five days later the damaged bearings caused the mechanical seal to fail, releasing approximately 1,000 pounds of hydrocarbons at 590 degrees Fahrenheit, which autoignited (autoignition temperature 484 degrees Fahrenheit). A "High Priority" vibration alarm had triggered two days before the incident but no spare pump was available; a "Critical High High" alarm never activated because it was set incorrectly.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: hot hydrocarbon release and autoignition from a failed pump damaged by cavitation during startup
  - Incorrectly programmed vibration alarms; employees also mistrusted alarms due to past nuisance alarms
  - Startup conditions that led to repeated cavitation were not adequately controlled
- Transferable lesson: Alarm credibility is as important as alarm coverage. Nuisance alarms breed normalization; correctly programmed, tested alarms and a robust spare-pump protocol are essential to catching developing equipment failures.

### Domtar Paper Mill, March 29, 2023 (Vol 1, #22)

- Hazard type: Toxic gas generation in situ (hydrogen sulfide)
- What happened: Three operators were adding solid sulfamic acid to a tank for a cleaning procedure. An upstream valve had been left open, allowing a "weak wash" stream containing sodium sulfide to enter the tank first. The reaction between sulfamic acid and sodium sulfide generated approximately 25 pounds of hydrogen sulfide gas. One operator standing over the tank opening lost consciousness; another was seriously injured after losing consciousness, falling, rolling through a guardrail, and dropping 11 feet to a lower level.

  - Written procedure did not specify that the weak wash valve must be closed before adding sulfamic acid
- Transferable lesson: Procedures for adding reactive chemicals must explicitly specify all upstream isolation steps. Chemical compatibility reviews should identify unexpected reaction pathways when process streams contact cleaning or treatment chemicals.

### Flint Hills Resources Refinery, April 8, 2023 (Vol 1, #23)

- What happened: A tube ruptured in the waste heat boiler of the sulfur recovery unit, releasing approximately 68 pounds of hydrogen sulfide and 999 pounds of sulfur dioxide and causing $2.235 million in property damage. ### Polycarbon Industries, May 4, 2023 (Vol 1, #24)
- Location | Industry: Newburyport, Massachusetts | Specialty chemical manufacturing
- Hazard type: Reactive chemical decomposition, vessel explosion, fire
- What happened: PCI was drying a chemical called Dekon 139 in a nitrogen-inerted pressure Nutsche filter vessel. An agitator inside the vessel had loosened and was rubbing a bottom plate, generating frictional heat. The heat triggered a self-accelerating decomposition of Dekon, releasing flammable gases (hydrogen, methane, carbon monoxide). Pressure rapidly built, the discharge door opened, and then the rupture disc opened in a second larger explosion. One operator was fatally injured; property damage was approximately $48 million and the facility closed permanently.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: self-accelerating decomposition of Dekon, ignited by frictional heat from the rubbing agitator
  - PCI had incomplete knowledge of Dekon's thermal hazards, specifically its self-accelerating decomposition temperature (280 degrees Fahrenheit) and flammable gas release
- Transferable lesson: Before processing a chemical in new or unfamiliar configurations, full thermal hazard characterization (self-accelerating decomposition temperature, adiabatic calorimetry) is essential. Agitator mechanical integrity is a process safety issue in reactive chemistry operations.

### Darling Ingredients, June 22, 2023 (Vol 1, #25)
- Location | Industry: Wadesboro, North Carolina | Rendering/specialty ingredients
- Hazard type: Reactive chemical release, vessel rupture (hydrogen chloride)
- What happened: An employee refilling a 5,200-gallon polyethylene tank with aluminum chloride added approximately 80 gallons of 93 percent sulfuric acid by mistake. ### BASF TOTALEnergies (BTP), September 5, 2023 (Vol 1, #26)
- Location | Industry: Port Arthur, Texas | Petrochemical manufacturing
- Hazard type: Pyrophoric material fire, distillation tower collapse
- What happened: BTP shut down its Pyrolysis Gasoline extractive distillation tower for maintenance. Before manways were opened, iron sulfide (a pyrophoric material) had accumulated inside from a corrosion reaction involving iron, hydrogen sulfide, and water. When manways were opened and air entered, the iron sulfide oxidized exothermically, heating the structured packing to 1,300 to 1,800 degrees Fahrenheit, weakening the tower walls and causing it to collapse. Property damage was approximately $194 million.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: smoldering iron sulfide oxidation inside the tower upon air entry
  - A 2016 incident had identified iron sulfide accumulation risk in this tower; that knowledge was not effectively transferred to current site personnel
  - Equipment leak a month before shutdown allowed water into the unit, accelerating iron sulfide formation
- Transferable lesson: Lessons from past incidents involving pyrophoric materials (iron sulfide) must be institutionalized in procedures and maintained through personnel changes. Opening any equipment with a history of or conditions conducive to pyrophoric material formation requires purging with inert gas before air entry.

### Cyanco, May 10, 2020 (Vol 2, #1)

### BASF, October 1, 2020 (Vol 2, #2)
- Location | Industry: Geismar, Louisiana | Chemical manufacturing (MDI/isocyanates)
- Hazard type: Toxic gas release (chlorine)
- What happened: Four workers (two BASF employees, two contractors) were retightening a leaking chlorine drain valve bonnet. The bolt retightening by hand did not stop the leak, so a contractor used an impact wrench. The vibrations from the impact wrench caused catastrophic failure of already severely corroded bonnet bolts, separating the top half of the drain valve and releasing approximately 554 pounds of chlorine. Workers evacuated but one BASF employee removed his respirator before switching to the escape bottle, resulting in chlorine exposure and hospitalization.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Probable cause: catastrophic failure of bolts corroded by prolonged exposure to hydrochloric acid (from chlorine reacting with condensed water in insulation)
  - Mechanical integrity program had not inspected the valve; three other similarly corroded valves were found post-incident
  - A contractor invoked stop work authority, but miscommunication resulted in work continuing
  - Respirator training did not ensure workers could effectively switch to escape bottles during an emergency
- Transferable lesson: Attempting to repair a severely corroded valve in chlorine service is high-risk; the mechanical integrity program should have identified and scheduled replacement during a planned shutdown. Stop work authority is only effective when communication channels are unambiguous.

### Chevron Phillips, February 15, 2021 (Vol 2, #3)
- Location | Industry: Port Arthur, Texas | Petrochemical manufacturing (ethylene)
- Hazard type: Flammable gas release, fire (hydrogen/hydrocarbon)
- What happened: Winter Storm Uri caused the facility's boilers to shut down, reducing steam and water supply.   - Fuel gas isolation valve failed to close because instrument air water had frozen (inadequate winterization)

### Chevron Phillips, February 15, 2021 (Vol 2, #4)
- Location | Industry: Sweeny, Texas | Petrochemical manufacturing (ethylene)
- Hazard type: Flammable gas release, explosion (hydrogen/hydrocarbon)
- What happened: A second Chevron Phillips facility was also affected by Winter Storm Uri. Fired heaters were shut down without decoking (removing carbon deposits from tube interiors). A valve between a fired heater and downstream equipment remained open, allowing flammable gas to flow backward into the heater. Approximately 30 minutes later, a tube ruptured (thermal stress from rapid shutdown plus coke fouling hotspots), releasing gas into the firebox.
