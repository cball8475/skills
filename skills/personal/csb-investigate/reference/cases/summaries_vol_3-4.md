### Pixelle, April 15, 2020 (Vol 3, #1)
- Location | Industry: Jay, Maine | Pulp and Paper
- Hazard type: Pressure vessel catastrophic failure, caustic chemical release
- What happened: A 260,000-gallon pulp digester exploded at a paper mill, releasing approximately 260,000 gallons of hot corrosive chemicals and wood fibers. The explosion caused an estimated $350 million in property damage and resulted in the permanent closure of the mill, which employed 515 people. No serious injuries occurred because most employees were on a lunch break.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Extensive caustic stress corrosion cracking along weld seams in the vessel's carbon steel shell
  - Failure-initiating crack had breached roughly 70 percent of the vessel's wall thickness before rupture
  - Ineffective mechanical integrity program; inspections failed to identify and repair preexisting cracks
  - Poor historical welding practices and inadequate post-weld heat treatment produced high-hardness welds susceptible to cracking
- Transferable lesson: Aging pressure vessels in corrosive chemical service require targeted inspection for known damage mechanisms (here, caustic stress corrosion cracking near welds). Ownership changes, as happened 66 days before this incident, are a critical trigger for a fresh mechanical integrity review.

### Tyson Foods, July 19, 2020 (Vol 3, #2)
- Location | Industry: Enid, Oklahoma | Food Processing, Refrigeration
- Hazard type: Toxic ammonia release, pressurized equipment opening
- What happened: A refrigeration technician was injured when pressurized anhydrous ammonia sprayed from a strainer assembly being opened on the roof of a facility. The technician had attempted to drain residual ammonia through the drain valve, but the drain valve plug was damaged and could not be removed, leading to an improvised and unsafe opening approach.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Equipment opened while still pressurized with anhydrous ammonia
  - Damaged drain valve plug prevented proper depressurization before opening
  - Technician did not wear required respiratory protection
  - No supervisor or designee was available to authorize the required ammonia equipment opening work permit
- Transferable lesson: Damaged equipment that prevents the standard depressurization path must be treated as a stop-work condition, not a workaround opportunity. Work permit authorization requirements exist precisely for situations where normal safe practices cannot be followed.

### Chevron, August 18, 2020 (Vol 3, #3)
- Location | Industry: Pasadena, Texas | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Storage tank overpressure, fire
- What happened: A 3.4-million-gallon vacuum residuum storage tank was overpressured and caught fire after a power outage during a storm led to an emergency shutdown. When the unit restarted, a flow restriction caused hotter and lighter hydrocarbon material to enter the heavy-service tank, generating vapor pressure that tore open the frangible roof seam and ignited.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Lighter and hotter hydrocarbons (crude oil) were combined with heavy hydrocarbons (vacuum residuum) in a tank not designed to contain them
  - Generated vapor exceeded the tank's atmospheric venting capacity
  - Hazard analysis had not identified the need for safeguards to prevent incompatible materials from entering this tank
- Transferable lesson: Abnormal startup scenarios following power outages can create off-spec material routings into tanks with mismatched design parameters. Hazard analyses should explicitly cover upset and restart conditions, not just normal operations.

### PBF Energy, September 10, 2020 (Vol 3, #4)
- Location | Industry: Delaware City, Delaware | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Flammable hydrocarbon release, fire
- What happened: During unit startup after maintenance, a board operator opened a control valve intended to supply nitrogen for oxygen purging. Instead, the valve directed flammable hydrocarbons (primarily butane) into piping with six valves open to the atmosphere. The hydrocarbons were ignited by a fired heater roughly 100 feet away, resulting in a fire lasting about five minutes.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Inadvertent valve alignment directed flammable hydrocarbons into an open-vented system rather than nitrogen
  - Startup procedure did not specify steps needed for oxygen-freeing the equipment
  - Opened control valve was not needed for the nitrogen purge; its manual isolation valve should have been closed
- Transferable lesson: Startup procedures following maintenance must explicitly define all valve positions, not assume operators will identify which valves are unnecessary. Every opening to atmosphere during nitrogen purging must be treated as a potential flammable gas release point.

### Silver Eagle, September 19, 2020 (Vol 3, #5)
- Location | Industry: Evanston, Wyoming | Petroleum Refining, Truck Loading
- Hazard type: Flammable vapor release, explosion, fire
- What happened: While loading diesel into a tanker truck inside an enclosed building at a refinery, high pressure built up in one compartment because the air-operated vapor recovery valve to the flare was closed. The truck's emergency pressure-relief system discharged flammable vapor into the enclosed building, where it was ignited by overhead electrical lighting. Two truck drivers were seriously burned.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Air-operated vapor recovery valve closed because the truck's compressed air system had insufficient pressure to hold it open
  - Low compressed air pressure resulted from extended loading duration, air system leaks, and the truck engine being shut off per refinery policy
  - High wind speeds had repeatedly extinguished the flare pilot, further extending the loading time
  - Building's electrical classification was based on outdoor (well-ventilated) criteria rather than indoor requirements
- Transferable lesson: Engineered vapor control systems dependent on utility pressure (air, power) must have interlocks or monitoring to stop loading operations when that utility pressure drops below the threshold needed to keep protective valves open. Enclosed loading structures require indoor-appropriate electrical area classification.

### Tyson Foods, November 20, 2020 (Vol 3, #6)
- Location | Industry: Hutchinson, Kansas | Food Processing, Refrigeration
- Hazard type: Toxic ammonia release, equipment disassembly
- What happened: Two employees were seriously injured when they disassembled an out-of-service heat exchanger in an ammonia refrigeration system that still contained pressurized ammonia. Downstream valves had not been closed, and a check valve had likely failed, allowing ammonia to backflow into the heat exchanger. Air-purifying respirators were overwhelmed by the concentration of ammonia released.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Heat exchanger opened while still containing anhydrous ammonia due to incomplete isolation
  - Downstream valves not closed; failed check valve allowed backflow
  - Equipment opening permit not obtained; system not verified as depressurized
  - Air-purifying respirators saturated, providing inadequate protection
- Transferable lesson: Equipment deemed "out of service" must be independently verified as isolated, drained, and depressurized before disassembly. Relying on a single remote pressure indicator rather than local verification is insufficient for ammonia system line breaks.

### FutureFuel, May 5, 2021 (Vol 3, #7)
- Location | Industry: Batesville, Arkansas | Specialty Chemicals
- Hazard type: Corrosive liquid release (oleum), hose failure
- What happened: A flexible polymer hose ruptured while an operator was transferring oleum (fuming sulfuric acid) from a railcar, spraying the operator with corrosive liquid. The operator suffered chemical burns and was airlifted to a burn center. Oleum continued releasing for 73 minutes because no remotely operated isolation valve was installed.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Hose's fluoropolymer inner liner was damaged (possibly from seven years of storage or a manufacturing defect), allowing oleum to degrade the structural layers until failure
  - Hose rupture was not identified as a credible hazard in the facility's hazard analysis; PPE requirements did not account for this scenario
  - No remotely operated isolation valve available to stop the release quickly
- Transferable lesson: Flexible hoses in highly corrosive chemical service must have a documented service life limit and pre-use inspection protocol. Hazard analyses for chemical transfer operations must explicitly consider hose failure as a credible scenario and ensure both PPE and remote isolation capability exist.

### PBF Energy, November 23, 2021 (Vol 3, #8)
- Location | Industry: Oregon, Ohio | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Flammable LPG release, explosion, fire
- What happened: Severely corroded drain piping in a refinery catastrophically failed while sealant was being injected into a temporary leak repair clamp, releasing approximately 36,000 pounds of LPG that formed a vapor cloud and exploded. The piping had been identified as leaking five days earlier, and a management of change was issued for the temporary clamp.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Corroded piping broke under sealant injection pressure; the clamp's horizontal support had been removed during installation to make it fit, leaving the pipe without adequate support
  - Accelerated corrosion from two mechanisms: internal corrosion from residual water and acids (compounded by online water washes that were not flushing low points), and external corrosion under insulation from seasonal winterization insulation that was never removed
  - Mechanical integrity program did not identify the severe corrosion between the 2011 and 2021 inspections
- Transferable lesson: Temporary leak repairs on highly corroded piping are high-risk operations. The integrity of the underlying piping must be assessed before any repair attempt. Seasonal winterization practices (adding insulation to deadlegs) must include a corresponding de-winterization step.

### ExxonMobil, December 23, 2021 (Vol 3, #9)
- Location | Industry: Baytown, Texas | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Piping rupture, flammable naphtha release, fire
- What happened: While contractors performed emergency hot-bolting on a leaking flange in a hydrodesulfurization unit, an elbow and adjacent piping catastrophically ruptured, releasing 41,000 pounds of hot naphtha vapor that autoignited. Four contract workers were seriously injured. Post-incident inspection found both the 14-inch elbow and 24-inch piping were extremely thin from sulfidation corrosion.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - A hydraulic torque wrench applied external force to a severely corroded 14-inch elbow (wall thickness as low as 0.028 inches) at an operating temperature well above autoignition temperature
  - Low-silicon piping components (below 0.10 weight percent silicon) are known to suffer accelerated sulfidation corrosion; the 24-inch piping was a low-silicon component
  - Prior inspection failed to identify the dangerously thin piping; no causal analysis was performed after the initial flange fire two days before the rupture
- Transferable lesson: Before any emergency hot-work on refinery piping operating above autoignition temperatures, a thorough inspection of the piping's wall thickness is essential. Low-silicon carbon steel components in high-temperature hydrocarbon service require complete inspection and replacement as recommended by prior CSB investigations.

### Pilgrim's, January 19, 2022 (Vol 3, #10)
- Location | Industry: Canton, Georgia | Food Processing, Refrigeration
- Hazard type: Toxic ammonia release, pressure relief activation
- What happened: While restarting an ammonia refrigeration compressor following routine sanitation, high-pressure conditions caused a pressure-relief valve to activate and discharge toxic ammonia outside the compressor building. Workers evacuating the facility were exposed to the ammonia cloud. Two employees were admitted to a hospital.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Compressor slide valve stuck in the fully loaded position while condenser fans were off, generating excessive system pressure
  - Compressor's high-pressure alarm and automatic shutdown had set points higher than the pressure-relief valve's activation pressure, rendering them ineffective
  - Safety device settings were never configured correctly when the compressor was installed months earlier
  - Pressure-relief discharge piping directed ammonia near compressor room exit doors rather than to a safe, elevated location
- Transferable lesson: Safety device set points must be verified correct at commissioning and confirmed again at startup. Pressure-relief discharge systems must direct releases away from evacuation routes and occupied areas.

### WR Meadows, January 21, 2022 (Vol 3, #11)
- Location | Industry: Hampshire, Illinois | Construction Products Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Flammable vapor explosion (acetone), static ignition
- What happened: An employee vacuuming spilled acrylic resin powder from the top of an open mixing tank containing flammable acetone vapor was fatally injured when the vacuum cleaner's ungrounded hose generated a static spark that ignited the tank's contents. A second employee sustained serious chemical and thermal burns.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Ungrounded, unbonded vacuum cleaner hose generated static discharge near the open tank, igniting acetone vapor
  - No engineering controls to prevent the open tank from containing an explosive concentration of flammable vapor
  - Room was electrically classified as hazardous, but an extension cord routed power in from outside the classified area
- Transferable lesson: Cleaning operations near or above open tanks containing flammable solvents must use explosion-proof, grounded, and bonded vacuum equipment. Engineering controls (inert blanket, closed tank design, LEV) should make flammable vapor accumulation inside mixing tanks impossible during housekeeping tasks.

### Westlake, January 26, 2022 (Vol 3, #12)
- Location | Industry: Westlake, Louisiana | Chlorinated Chemical Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Storage tank explosion, flammable vapor ignition
- What happened: A one-million-gallon ethylene dichloride storage tank exploded and caught fire when contract workers used a battery-powered reciprocating saw to cut corroded bolts during maintenance on the tank's nitrogen inerting system. Sparks from the saw ignited flammable vapor escaping through holes in the tank's roof, and the flame propagated into the tank.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Tank had developed a flammable atmosphere from a two-week piping misalignment that directed ethylene and oxygen from a connected process unit into the tank
  - Flammable vapor was ignited by sparks from a battery-powered saw not authorized by the work permit
  - Work permit did not authorize spark-producing tools; this was not enforced or caught before work began
- Transferable lesson: Work permits on or adjacent to storage tanks must explicitly list authorized and prohibited tools, and the authorization must be verified in the field before work starts. Piping connections between process units and storage tanks should be audited periodically to confirm alignment matches the intended configuration.

### Eastman, January 31, 2022 (Vol 3, #13)
- Location | Industry: Kingsport, Tennessee | Chemical Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Piping elbow failure, high-pressure steam release, asbestos release
- What happened: An 18-inch carbon steel piping elbow carrying steam at 600 psig and 750 degrees Fahrenheit ruptured from creep damage, causing $25 million in property damage and releasing asbestos fibers into the surrounding community from the pipe's insulation.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Elbow failed from creep damage after 53 years; a thinner-walled substandard elbow (0.375 inches instead of the required 0.5 inches) was installed in 1968 or 1969
  - Company had no mechanical integrity inspection program for the steam piping
  - The thinner elbow's expected service life was dramatically shorter than a code-compliant elbow would have been
- Transferable lesson: High-energy steam piping systems require mechanical integrity programs that include inspection for creep, especially when components are aged and may not meet the original design specification. Legacy installations should be audited against the as-designed specification.

### Blues City Brewery, February 1, 2022 (Vol 3, #14)
- Location | Industry: Memphis, Tennessee | Brewing, Food and Beverage
- Hazard type: Incompatible chemical mixing, toxic chlorine gas generation
- What happened: An operator inadvertently transferred a nitric/phosphoric acid solution into a bleach solution tank, generating a toxic chlorine gas cloud that seriously injured three workers and required hospital evaluation of ten. The acid and bleach solution totes had nearly identical trade names differing by only one letter.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):

### Dyno Nobel, February 17, 2022 (Vol 3, #15)
- Location | Industry: Waggaman, Louisiana | Ammonia Production, Fertilizer
- Hazard type: High-temperature piping failure, hydrogen/process gas fire
- What happened: During startup of an ammonia production plant, a transition pipe between a secondary reformer and a waste heat boiler ruptured from creep damage, releasing hot process gas above 1,600 degrees Fahrenheit that autoignited. The resulting jet fire caused a fish-mouth rupture and a large fire with an estimated $128 million in property damage.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Creep crack initiated along a circumferential weld in the transition pipe's outer shell; lack of post-weld heat treatment shortened component life
  - Transition pipe's inner liner was damaged, insulation layer was degraded, and the water jacket operated with a partially filled liquid level, all of which elevated shell temperatures and accelerated creep damage
  - Jet fire from the initial release further overheated the shell, causing the full fish-mouth rupture
- Transferable lesson: Multi-layer specialty piping in very high-temperature service requires monitoring of all protective subsystems (liner, insulation, cooling jacket). Degradation of any one layer shifts thermal load to the outer shell, accelerating creep beyond design life assumptions.

### ONEOK, July 9, 2022 (Vol 3, #16)
- Location | Industry: Medford, Oklahoma | NGL Processing
- Hazard type: Corrosion-induced piping rupture, flammable vapor explosion, fire
- What happened: Approximately 450 barrels of an ethane and propane mixture were released when a 12-inch carbon steel pipe elbow ruptured due to localized internal corrosion. The resulting vapor cloud ignited and exploded, causing fires and approximately $930 million in property damage.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Internal bottom-of-pipe corrosion from an acidic water layer (carbonic and sulfuric acids formed from CO2 and H2S in the hydrocarbon feed) caused wall thinning to failure
  - Low fluid velocity allowed water to settle and form a secondary liquid layer, concentrating corrosive species at the pipe bottom
  - New hydrocarbon feed sources introduced in 2020 increased water content, accelerating the corrosion rate beyond what the 2019 inspection had predicted
  - Mechanical integrity program did not trigger a re-inspection after the change in feed composition and water content
- Transferable lesson: When process feed sources change (new supply contracts, additional well connections), the impact on internal corrosion rates must be reassessed and inspection frequencies updated accordingly. Bottom-of-pipe corrosion in wet gas service is a known, predictable mechanism that demands targeted inspection.

### SABIC, December 26, 2022 (Vol 3, #19)
- Location | Industry: Mt. Vernon, Indiana | Polymer (ULTEM Resin) Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Pump explosion, reactive chemical release
- What happened: A magnetically driven PTFE-lined nitric acid pump exploded during plant restart after a winter storm shutdown, releasing more than 15,000 pounds of nitric acid inside a processing building. Post-incident analysis showed the PTFE liner had degraded from prolonged exposure to nitric acid and nitrogen oxides, allowing a violent reaction between nitric acid, lubrication oil, and cast iron.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - PTFE containment liner degraded from prolonged exposure to nitric acid and NOx; industry guidance recommended limiting such pumps to an 18-month service life, but this was unknown to the procurement team
  - Reactive combination of nitric acid, lubricating oil, and iron was not foreseen and not captured in reactive chemical hazard assessments
  - Pump had been operating for nearly two years, well past the recommended service life
- Transferable lesson: Process safety information must include vendor and industry knowledge on materials compatibility and service life limits. Procurement of equipment for corrosive service requires explicit review of chemical compatibility between the process fluid, lining material, and structural components.

### Delek, April 17, 2023 (Vol 3, #21)
- Location | Industry: Tyler, Texas | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Hot hydrocarbon liquid release, burn injury
- What happened: An operator collecting a vacuum tower bottoms (VTB) sample removed a glove while waiting for the viscous, 650-degree Fahrenheit material to flow. The material suddenly surged from the open-ended sample tubing, contacting the ungloved hand and causing third-degree burns.

### Delek, May 8, 2023 (Vol 3, #23)
- Location | Industry: El Dorado, Arkansas | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Storage tank explosion, lightning ignition, splash filling
- What happened: An 850,000-gallon fixed-roof storage tank exploded and caught fire after a lightning strike. The tank had been splash-filled with clarified oil (falling 30 feet to the liquid surface), creating a flammable mist and aerosol atmosphere inside the tank. The grounding cable routed near atmospheric vents created an ignition arc when dissipating the lightning strike.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Splash filling created a flammable aerosol above the liquid surface; the hazard of splash filling has been documented in industry literature since at least 1970
  - All other tanks at the facility in similar service used bottom filling; this was the only tank using splash filling
  - Neither atmospheric vent was equipped with a flame arrestor to prevent flame propagation into the tank
  - Grounding cable routed near the vents brought an ignition source close to flammable vapor exiting the vents
- Transferable lesson: Splash filling of flammable liquids into atmospheric tanks should be prohibited. Flame arrestors on atmospheric vents are a low-cost safeguard that can prevent a range of ignition scenarios from propagating into tanks.

### CITGO, July 31, 2023 (Vol 3, #24)
- Location | Industry: Lemont, Illinois | Petroleum Refining, Railcar Loading
- Hazard type: Switch loading, static ignition, flash fire
- What happened: An employee loading mineral spirits into a railcar compartment that previously held heptane was seriously burned when a static discharge from a metal sampling device ignited flammable residual heptane vapor. The danger of switch loading has been documented in industry standards since at least 1960.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Switch loading (loading a low-vapor-pressure material into a compartment that held a high-vapor-pressure material) created flammable residual vapor
  - Sampling was performed while the pump was still running, without the required 10-minute waiting period for static dissipation
  - Company had not identified switch loading as a recognized hazard; training did not cover it adequately
  - Higher-flow-rate pump at this loading station generated more static charge than other stations
- Transferable lesson: Switch loading must be prohibited or controlled with thorough tank cleaning and purging. Relaxation time requirements to allow static dissipation before sampling are critical and must be enforced; they should be interlocked or procedurally controlled, not left to individual compliance.

### Georgia Pacific, January 16, 2025 (Vol 3, #30)
- Location | Industry: Monticello, Mississippi | Pulp and Paper
- Hazard type: Equipment overpressure, hot water release, burn injury
- What happened: After a restart, operators suspected a clog in refiner equipment and flushed with hot water. A misaligned valve (outlet closed) created a dead-head condition; the hot water overpressurized a refiner casing with insufficiently tightened bolts, cracking it and spraying an employee with hot water. The valve misalignment was missed because a recent control system upgrade changed the color coding for equipment status.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Operator misread the refiner's operating status because a recent computer control system upgrade replaced intuitive colors (red/green) with less obvious colors (gray/white), leading to incorrect troubleshooting
  - Hot water injected into a closed (no-flow) system overpressurized the refiner
  - High-pressure shutdown set point exceeded the manufacturer's recommended limit; it did not activate
  - Seven refiner casing bolts were not adequately tightened (mechanical integrity deficiency)
- Transferable lesson: Changes to human-machine interface color schemes and status indicators are safety-critical and must be treated as management of change. Operator fatalities and injuries from HMI misinterpretation are preventable by following established human factors design standards.

### ArcelorMittal, July 16, 2020 (Vol 4, #1)
- Location | Industry: Burns Harbor, Indiana | Steel Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Pressure vessel catastrophic failure, blast furnace stove dome rupture
- What happened: The dome of a blast furnace stove catastrophically separated and exploded at a steel mill. The dome failed at a repair patch where the wall was approximately one-tenth of its original thickness from high-temperature oxidation corrosion. Multiple prior repairs used improper welds, dissimilar steel grades, and undersized patch plates. The incident caused approximately $22 million in property damage.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Dome failed at a repair patch that was improperly welded and did not provide the minimum required thickness (0.25-inch patch versus 0.56-inch minimum required)
  - Wall in the failure area was only about 0.10 inches thick, 10 percent of the original 1-inch wall
  - Multiple historical repairs used dissimilar steel grades and non-code-compliant welding
  - Mechanical integrity program lacked effective inspection and weld repair practices
- Transferable lesson: Repairs to pressure vessels must comply with code requirements for weld quality, material matching, and minimum thickness. Cumulative repair history must be tracked; each repair patch adds complexity and potential failure initiation sites if not done to code.

### Eastman, November 29, 2020 (Vol 4, #2)
- Location | Industry: Kingsport, Tennessee | Chemical Manufacturing (Coal Gasification)
- Hazard type: Piping failure, flammable process gas release, autoignition fire
- What happened: A pipe support near a coal gasifier water supply control valve failed from fatigue and corrosion, tearing a large hole in the water supply piping. Loss of water flow allowed hot, flammable product gases to backflow into the water piping and rupture two water supply connections, releasing gas that autoignited. The fire was stopped when the gasifier was shut down.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Fatigue cracking at a severely corroded pipe support weld caused the initial water supply failure
  - A check valve had been stuck open for an extended period and was never inspected or tested; it did not close, allowing water supply loss to cascade
  - Process hazard analysis incorrectly assumed a loss of water flow would trigger an automatic gasifier shutdown, so no additional safeguards had been implemented
- Transferable lesson: Check valves in safety-critical service must be periodically tested, not just visually inspected. PHA assumptions about automatic safety responses must be field-verified; untested assumptions can eliminate what appear to be adequate safeguards on paper.

### Phillips 66, March 22, 2021 (Vol 4, #3)
- Location | Industry: Wilmington, California | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Residual flammable hydrocarbon in piping, ignition during pipe cutting
- What happened: During turnaround maintenance, contractors cutting a section of isolated FCC unit piping with an electric angle grinder triggered an explosion and fire when residual gasoline in the piping ignited. The piping was supposed to have been flushed and cleared, but a plugged drain had prevented full clearing. A contract worker had previously invoked stop-work authority over concerns about liquid in the pipe; those concerns were dismissed rather than escalated.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Piping not effectively flushed and drained; a plugged drain prevented clearing as intended
  - Flammable gas testing at the work area did not detect flammable vapor inside the piping
  - A worker's valid stop-work authority invocation was not escalated to operations and maintenance supervisors for independent review before work resumed
- Transferable lesson: Stop-work authority invocations must be formally documented and resolved at a supervisory level before work resumes; dismissing them in the field without escalation defeats the purpose of the program. Verification of piping clearance must confirm the piping is empty at the actual work point, not just at a remote test location.

### AdvanSix, December 20, 2021 (Vol 4, #4)
- Location | Industry: Chester, Virginia | Nylon Resin Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Pressurized molten polymer release, fire, burn injury
- What happened: During rupture disc maintenance on a molten polymer pump, a maintenance worker using a propane torch to heat and melt polymer had removed most flange bolts when the rupture disc and remaining bolt were forcefully ejected, spraying 500-degree Fahrenheit molten polymer that ignited. The pump's outlet isolation valve had been locked in the open position (not closed as required), allowing pressurized polymer to remain in the system.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Outlet isolation valve locked in the open position rather than the closed position; workers did not verify valve position, only confirmed locks were present
  - Equipment not effectively isolated and drained before disassembly
  - Denim jeans provided inadequate protection compared to the aluminized jacket worn; PPE standards did not specify full-body aluminized protection for this task
- Transferable lesson: Lockout/tagout must include physical verification that each valve is in the safe (correct) position, not merely confirmation that a lock is attached. Verifying locked equipment actually isolates the hazardous energy is the essential step that visual lock presence alone cannot provide.

### Diamond Green Diesel, February 14, 2022 (Vol 4, #5)
- Location | Industry: Norco, Louisiana | Renewable Diesel Production
- Hazard type: Steam and hot condensate release, burn injury
- What happened: A contract worker was sprayed with steam and hot condensate while disconnecting a steam hose from a railcar after unloading. A broken isolation valve on the steam supply line had changed how the hose was isolated and drained, trapping condensate in the steam hose that was not apparent before disconnection.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Known broken isolation valve (Valve A, stuck open) was not repaired and stop-work authority was not used to take the equipment out of service
  - Alternate isolation method trapped condensate in the steam hose; no pressure gauge or other means existed to confirm the hose was depressurized before disconnection
  - Written procedure did not describe how to verify pressure-free condition before disconnecting steam or condensate hoses
- Transferable lesson: Equipment with known defects (broken valves) that are required for standard safe operating procedures must be repaired or taken out of service. Operating around known defects with informal workarounds creates uncharted hazards.

### Marathon, February 21, 2022 (Vol 4, #6)
- Location | Industry: Garyville, Louisiana | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Vacuum ejector overpressure, flammable gas release, jet fire
- What happened: During hydrocracker unit startup after maintenance, a vacuum ejector ruptured and released approximately 7,000 pounds of flammable methane and hydrogen, igniting in a large jet fire. The ejector, rated for 360 psi, was subjected to 1,465 psi of process gas because the startup procedure did not include steps to isolate the ejector before pressurizing the unit.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Startup procedure did not include isolating the vacuum ejector from high-pressure process gas after the air removal step
  - Process hazard analysis had not identified the potential for overpressurizing the vacuum ejector
  - The ejector was no longer in active service and could have been removed or permanently blinded to eliminate the risk entirely
- Transferable lesson: Inactive but connected process equipment (unused ejectors, bypassed vessels) must be positively isolated from operating process pressure, or removed. Startup procedures must account for every piece of connected equipment, including equipment that is not in active use.

### Northrop Grumman, January 30, 2023 (Vol 4, #7)
- Location | Industry: Magna, Utah | Aerospace and Defense Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Oxygen-deficient atmosphere (argon displacement), asphyxiation, two fatalities
- What happened: Two employees asphyxiated in a basement after argon leaked from a pressurized autoclave into the poorly ventilated space. The employees had ended their shift and removed their personal oxygen monitors, but returned to the basement before meeting the oncoming shift. They were found unconscious and were later declared deceased at the hospital.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Argon leaking from autoclave seals accumulated in the approximately 4,500-cubic-foot basement while the exhaust ventilation system was off
  - No fixed oxygen monitoring or alarm in the basement; workers had removed personal monitors at shift end
  - No engineering controls to prevent entry into the basement when oxygen concentration was unsafe, ventilation was off, or the vessel was pressurized
- Transferable lesson: Any enclosed space where inert gas accumulation is possible must be classified as a confined space and equipped with continuous fixed oxygen monitoring, forced ventilation interlocks, and entry controls tied to safe atmospheric conditions. Personal oxygen monitors must not be removed while the hazard source remains active.

### Atalco, February 6, 2023 (Vol 4, #8)
- Location | Industry: Gramercy, Louisiana | Aluminum Refining
- Hazard type: Compressed air injection into open-top caustic slurry tank, geyser eruption, burn injuries
- What happened: Operators clearing a blocked drain pipe at the bottom of an open-top hot caustic slurry tank used compressed air to blow through the blockage. Because no isolation device was installed between the drain piping and the tank interior, compressed air entered the tank and caused hot caustic slurry at 195 degrees Fahrenheit to erupt from the open top, seriously burning two operators.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - No isolation device between the air injection point and the tank, allowing air to pressurize the tank's contents
  - Steam control valve was leaking, heating the contents beyond the target temperature
  - No written procedure for clearing blocked drain piping; some operators were not wearing required chemical suits
- Transferable lesson: Drain-clearing procedures using compressed air or similar pressurizing methods must incorporate isolation to prevent the air from backflowing into the vessel. Any task involving pressurizing a line that connects to an open vessel or tank must be explicitly prohibited or controlled by procedure.

### SABIC, December 10, 2023 (Vol 4, #9)
- Location | Industry: Mt. Vernon, Indiana | Polymer (PBT Resin) Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Organic peroxide formation and explosion, reactive chemistry, fire
- What happened: During pre-startup heating of a polymer reactor system, tetrahydrofuran (THF) vapor evolved from residual polymer in outlet piping and entered an interconnected heat exchanger that had a nozzle open to air. THF reacted with oxygen over approximately 12 hours to form explosive organic peroxide, which energetically decomposed and exploded. The first explosion ignited flammable THF in the reactor, triggering a second explosion.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Residual hydrocarbon material (including THF) was routinely left in the reactor's outlet piping during shutdown; the hazard of THF plus oxygen forming peroxide was not recognized
  - A nozzle on the heat exchanger remained open to ambient air, providing the oxygen source
  - Management of change for a modified leak-test procedure did not assess how the change affected simultaneous pre-startup tasks; nitrogen flow through the reactor system was omitted as a result
- Transferable lesson: Residual reactive chemicals left in equipment during shutdowns can create hazards that take hours or days to develop. Shutdown and pre-startup procedures must evaluate what materials remain in equipment, their potential to react with air or other materials, and how procedural changes interact with simultaneous tasks.

### PBF Energy, February 22, 2024 (Vol 4, #10)
- Location | Industry: Chalmette, Louisiana | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Valve material incompatibility, hot VTB hydrocarbon release, fire
- What happened: A stainless steel globe valve with Teflon (PTFE) bonnet gasket and packing, installed six days before the incident, leaked hot vacuum tower bottoms material at 650 degrees Fahrenheit. The Teflon components were rated to only 450 degrees Fahrenheit and were incompatible with the process temperature. Approximately 12,000 pounds of VTB material were released and ignited.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Valve supplied with Teflon components instead of required graphite components; a third-party supplier's tag incorrectly identified the valve as having graphite components, conflicting with the manufacturer's tag
  - Refinery personnel accepted and installed the valve based on order documentation and the refinery valve number, without verifying the manufacturer's tag showed compatible materials and temperature ratings
- Transferable lesson: Incoming materials inspection must include verifying that the manufacturer's nameplate or tag matches the purchase specification for all critical parameters, especially temperature ratings and sealing material. Where conflicting tags exist, the manufacturer's original tag controls; the discrepancy must be resolved before installation.

### Formosa, October 8, 2024 (Vol 4, #11)
- Location | Industry: Baton Rouge, Louisiana | Plastics Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Liquefied gas cylinder BLEVE, anhydrous ammonia explosion, burn injuries
- What happened: Four employees were seriously injured when a 150-pound liquefied anhydrous ammonia cylinder exploded (BLEVE) after workers wrapped the cylinder with a steam hose at approximately 70 psi and 300 degrees Fahrenheit to prevent ice formation during vaporization. The cylinder's outlet valve was closed during heating, trapping pressure with no relief path.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Cylinder heated while the outlet valve was closed, with no pressure relief device installed on the cylinder
  - Procedure did not address the critical safety requirement to never heat a closed liquefied gas cylinder
  - Process safety management systems did not effectively evaluate and control the hazards of this anhydrous ammonia treatment operation
- Transferable lesson: Heating a closed liquefied gas cylinder is an inherently catastrophic scenario. Any procedure involving heat application to cylinders or portable vessels containing liquefied gases must explicitly prohibit heating while the outlet is closed, and a pressure relief device must be installed as a hardware safeguard.

### PBF Energy, February 1, 2025 (Vol 4, #12)
- Location | Industry: Martinez, California | Petroleum Refining
- Hazard type: Wrong flange opened on active process, flammable hydrocarbon release, major fire
- What happened: Contract workers installing a maintenance blind opened the wrong flange (Flange B, connected to an active hydrotreater unit operating above 600 degrees Fahrenheit) rather than the intended isolation point (Flange A). Pressurized flammable hydrocarbon was released and ignited, causing a fire that burned for three days and caused approximately $924 million in property damage.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Contract workers hired for turnaround had not received training on the refinery's equipment tagging system for identifying which flange to open
  - Operator required to be present for "first break" work was not present when contractors began work; a miscommunication caused the operator to leave the area
  - Contractors did not apply personal locks or tags to isolation valves, missing an opportunity to participate in and understand the isolation scheme
  - Workers apparently believed Flange B was the correct location despite the pre-work walkdown
- Transferable lesson: Workers opening equipment during turnarounds must be confirmed to understand the specific isolation scheme before work begins, especially when the correct and incorrect flanges are in close proximity. The permitting system must ensure a qualified person is physically present at the work site for the first break, not just available somewhere in the facility.

### Olin, May 20, 2025 (Vol 4, #13)
- Location | Industry: Freeport, Texas | Chlor-Alkali Chemical Manufacturing
- Hazard type: Toxic chlorine release, rupture disc replacement on live system, injury
- What happened: Contract maintenance workers began disassembling a rupture disc holder in an operating chlorine system because operations personnel had mistakenly prepared a nearly identical adjacent system (E-209B, rupture disc RD-217S) instead of the intended system (E-209A, rupture disc RD-217N). Approximately 8,000 pounds of liquid chlorine at 100 psi were released, triggering a community shelter-in-place order. One emergency responder was hospitalized after a 30-minute escape respirator became saturated.
- Cause / key issue (as stated by CSB):
  - Operations team isolated and tagged a different but nearly identical parallel system; the error was not caught because no pre-job field walkthrough was performed
  - Contract workers did not review or sign the equipment isolation plan or the identification tag on the rupture disc holder
  - No field verification step in the work process that would have confirmed the targeted equipment had been prepared for maintenance
- Transferable lesson: When two nearly identical parallel systems exist, a physical field walkthrough by both operations and maintenance personnel is essential to confirm the correct system was prepared. Maintenance workers must independently verify equipment tags and isolation plans before breaking containment on any toxic chemical system.
